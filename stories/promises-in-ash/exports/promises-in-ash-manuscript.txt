# Chapter 1

# Chapter 1: The Raid

The smell came first.

Garicus was helping Mother sort the autumn turnips when the smoke slipped through the cracks in the shutters. Not the good smoke from cooking fires or the forge. Wrong smoke. Bitter.

"Inside," Mother said. Not loud. Quiet, the way she spoke when Father came home angry.

He dropped the turnip. It rolled under the table.

Mother crossed to the window, one hand on the shutter. She went very still. Garicus knew that stillness. Rabbits did it when hawks circled.

"Garicus." Her voice came out strange. Too calm. "The cellar. Now."

"But—"

"Now."

She grabbed his wrist and pulled him across the room. The cellar door was beside the hearth, a heavy wooden square set into the floor. Mother yanked it open. The ladder disappeared into darkness.

"Down."

"Mother, what's—"

A scream cut through the air outside. High and sharp and suddenly silent.

Mother shoved him toward the ladder. Garicus caught the top rung, descended three steps, looked up. She knelt at the opening, her face backlit by firelight from the hearth.

"Listen to me." Her hands shook. She gripped the edge of the door to steady them. "You stay down here. You stay quiet. You don't come up until the sun comes through the cracks. Do you understand?"

"I don't—"

"Promise me, Garicus."

He'd never heard her voice break like that.

"I promise."

She pulled something from her apron. The small iron key Father used for the lockbox. She pressed it into his palm, closed his fingers around it.

"Keep this. Don't lose it. Your father will—" She stopped. Started again. "Just keep it safe."

More screams now. Closer. Men shouting in a language Garicus didn't know.

Mother kissed his forehead. Her lips were cold.

Then she slammed the cellar door shut.

Darkness swallowed him whole.

Garicus clung to the ladder, heart hammering against his ribs. Above, Mother's footsteps crossed the floor. Something heavy scraped—the table, dragged over the cellar door. Hiding it.

Then her footsteps ran toward the front of the house.

The door crashed open.

"Please," Mother said. "We have nothing, we're not—"

A man's voice, rough and indifferent. Words Garicus couldn't understand.

Mother screamed.

Garicus pressed his forehead against the ladder rung. The wood bit into his skin. He wanted to climb up, to push through the door, to—

To what? He was eight years old. Small for his age. He had no weapon, no strength, no—

Metal on metal. A wet sound. Mother's scream cut short.

Something heavy hit the floor above him.

Garicus bit his lip until he tasted blood. Don't make a sound. Don't make a sound. She said stay quiet. She made him promise.

Footsteps crossed overhead. Floorboards creaked. Someone kicked over a chair. The sound of drawers yanked open, contents spilling out.

Then the footsteps moved away. The door slammed. Voices outside, growing distant.

But not silent.

Garicus descended the rest of the ladder on shaking legs. The cellar floor was hard-packed dirt, damp and cold. He couldn't see anything. The darkness was absolute, thick as water.

He found the wall with his hands and pressed his back against it. Drew his knees up to his chest. Wrapped his arms around them.

And waited.

Screams echoed through Thornhaven. Men and women both. Children crying for their mothers. The crackle of fire grew louder, closer. Heat seeped through the floorboards above. The cellar filled with smoke.

Garicus pulled his shirt up over his nose and mouth. His eyes burned. He squeezed them shut, but tears came anyway.

Father was at the fields today, checking the late harvest. He wouldn't be back until evening. Would he run when he saw the smoke? Would he try to fight?

Would he come for Garicus?

No. Don't think about it. Mother said wait until morning. Mother said—

Another scream, this one so close Garicus felt it in his bones. He recognized the voice.

Mara. The baker's daughter. She was nine. She'd given him a honey cake last market day, laughed when he got it all over his face.

The scream ended.

Garicus shoved his fist into his mouth to keep from making a sound.

Hours crawled past. Or maybe minutes. Time stopped making sense in the dark. The screaming gradually faded. The fire sounds changed—less crackling, more the deep roar of buildings fully ablaze. The smoke thickened. Garicus could barely breathe.

He thought about climbing the ladder. Just to see. Just to check if Mother—

No. He promised.

So he stayed.

Eventually, the sounds changed again. Hoofbeats, leaving. The thunder of many horses riding away at speed. Shouts in the distance, orders given. Then, slowly, quiet.

The fire kept burning. Garicus could hear it eating through Thornhaven, timber by timber. Sometimes something collapsed with a crash. But no more screams.

Silence was worse than screaming.

Because silence meant no one was left to scream.

Garicus pressed his face against his knees and tried not to think. Tried not to imagine what was up there. Tried not to remember the wet sound Mother made when she stopped screaming.

He must have slept. When he opened his eyes, thin gray light filtered through the cracks around the cellar door. Dawn.

Mother said wait for morning.

It was morning.

Garicus climbed the ladder on numb legs. At the top, he pressed his shoulder against the door and pushed. It didn't budge. The table. Mother had moved the table.

He pushed harder. The door lifted an inch. He got his fingers into the gap, braced his feet on the ladder rungs, and shoved with everything he had.

The table scraped aside. The cellar door fell open.

Smoke poured down. Garicus choked, scrambled up and out, fell to his hands and knees on the floor.

The house was gone.

Not collapsed. Not burned to the foundation. Just—hollowed. The walls still stood, blackened and skeletal. The roof had caved in. Gray morning light filtered through the gaps, illuminating ash and char.

The hearth still stood. The table was overturned beside the cellar door, scorched but intact.

And there, near the front door—

Garicus looked away.

He knew he should check. Should see if maybe, somehow—

But he knew. The stillness told him. The silence.

He stumbled to the door, or where the door had been. The frame remained. The door itself lay in the yard, kicked off its hinges.

Thornhaven was ash.

The buildings that hadn't burned to the ground were burning still, sluggish flames eating through what remained. Bodies lay in the street. Too many to count. Garicus recognized some: Master Torven the blacksmith, face-down outside his forge. Old Willem who told stories at the tavern, sprawled in the mud.

Others he couldn't identify. Didn't want to.

Garicus walked into the street. His feet moved without thought. The morning air was thick with smoke and the smell of burned meat. He should feel something—horror, grief, rage. But there was only a strange, distant numbness.

Everyone was dead.

Father wouldn't come home. Mother was—

He didn't finish the thought.

A sound broke through the silence. Quiet. Almost a whimper.

Garicus stopped. Listened.

There. To the right, near the baker's shop—what was left of it.

He picked his way through the rubble. The bakery had collapsed completely, just a pile of blackened beams and shattered stone. The oven chimney still stood, impossibly intact amid the destruction.

The sound came again. Weak. Barely human.

Garicus climbed over a fallen beam. His hands came away black with soot.

A boy lay half-buried in the rubble. Smaller than Garicus, maybe seven years old. Blond hair matted with blood and ash. Eyes closed. Breathing in shallow gasps.

Solaire. The baker's son. Garicus knew him by sight—they'd never spoken. Solaire was always trailing after his mother, asking questions, laughing at everything.

He wasn't laughing now.

Garicus knelt beside him. "Can you hear me?"

No response.

Garicus grabbed Solaire's shoulders and pulled. The boy came free of the rubble with a scrape of stone. He was light, too light. Garicus dragged him away from the collapsed building, into the street.

Solaire's eyes flickered open. Blue, pale as winter sky. They stared at nothing for a moment, unfocused. Then found Garicus.

"Mother?" Solaire's voice came out cracked, barely a whisper.

Garicus looked back at the bakery ruins. At the bodies in the street. At Thornhaven, burning and silent and dead.

"No," he said.

Solaire's face crumpled. He tried to sit up, fell back. "Father? Mara?"

"No."

"Anyone?"

Garicus met his eyes. "Just us."

Solaire stared at him for a long moment. Then he started to cry. Not loud, hysterical sobs. Quiet, broken sounds that barely made it past his lips.

Garicus sat beside him in the ash and said nothing. What was there to say?

They were eight and seven years old. Alone in a graveyard that used to be home.

Eventually, Solaire's crying faded to hiccups, then silence. He sat up slowly, wincing. Blood crusted the side of his head. More on his arm. But he could move. That was something.

"Why?" Solaire asked. His voice was small. Lost. "Why did they do this?"

Garicus didn't answer. How could he? He was a child. He didn't understand why men killed, why they burned villages, why Mother had to die.

He only understood one thing, sitting there in the ruins: he was alive, and he shouldn't be.

Everyone else was dead. Mother. Father. All of Thornhaven. Dead because Garicus hid in a cellar while they screamed.

The numbness cracked. Guilt poured in like water into a drowning man's lungs.

"I hid," Garicus said. His voice sounded strange. Flat. "Mother told me to hide, and I did."

Solaire looked at him. "You're alive."

"Everyone else is dead."

"We're alive," Solaire said. Like it mattered. Like being alive meant something when everyone you loved was ash.

But then Solaire reached out and grabbed Garicus's hand. His fingers were small and cold and covered in soot.

"Don't leave me," Solaire whispered. "Please. Don't leave me alone."

Garicus looked at their joined hands. Black with ash. Shaking.

He thought about the cellar. The darkness. The screams. The hours waiting alone, not knowing if anyone else survived, if anyone would ever come.

He thought about Solaire, waking up buried in rubble, calling for his family, finding only silence and bodies.

Alone was worse than death.

"I won't," Garicus said.

It wasn't enough. Not for what they'd lost. Not for the emptiness that used to be home.

So he said it again, and this time he meant it like Mother meant her final words. Like a promise that mattered more than fear or sense.

"I won't let you die alone," Garicus said. "Ever. I promise."

Solaire's hand tightened on his. Tears cut clean lines through the ash on his face.

"I promise too," Solaire said. "We stay together. No matter what. We don't—" His voice broke. He tried again. "We don't let each other die alone."

Garicus nodded. Found Solaire's other hand and gripped both tight.

"We stay together," Garicus repeated.

They sat like that as the sun rose higher, two boys in the ash and ruin of everything they'd known, holding on to the only thing left: each other.

The fires burned low. Smoke drifted across Thornhaven's corpse. Somewhere in the distance, a raven called. The sound echoed through empty streets.

Garicus didn't let go.

Neither did Solaire.

They would wait for someone to come. A survivor, a traveler, anyone. And when they came—if they came—Garicus and Solaire would still be together.

Because that was the promise.

The only promise that mattered anymore.

Dawn light filtered through the smoke, pale and cold. The ashes settled. The fires died.

And two boys sat vigil over the grave of their world, bound by an oath they didn't yet understand, holding each other in the silence that followed the screaming.

Together.

Always together.

No matter what came next.



# Chapter 2

# Chapter 2: The Knight

The first thing Solaire knew was pain.

It bloomed behind his eyes like fire, sharp and immediate. He tried to move and couldn't—his body felt distant, disconnected. Heavy. Something pressed against his face, rough and gritty. Stone? No. Ash.

He opened his eyes to gray.

Gray sky above, choked with smoke. Gray dust coating everything—his hands, his clothes, the rubble surrounding him. And sitting beside him, knees drawn up, face streaked with soot and tears, was Garicus.

"You're awake." Garicus's voice was small. Broken.

Solaire tried to sit up. The world tilted violently, and Garicus's hands were suddenly on his shoulders, steadying him. "Slow. Don't... don't move too fast."

"What—" Solaire's throat burned. He coughed, tasted ash and blood. "What happened?"

Garicus didn't answer. His eyes slid away, focusing on something past Solaire's shoulder. Following his gaze, Solaire saw it: the ruin of his village. Collapsed houses. Charred timber. Bodies—

He looked away, bile rising.

"They came at night," Garicus whispered. "Men in armor. They were looking for... I don't know. Something. Someone. They burned everything."

Fragments surfaced in Solaire's memory. His mother's voice, urgent and afraid. The sound of boots on cobblestones. A shout. Then impact—something striking his head—and darkness.

"My mother," Solaire said. The words scraped out of him. "Where is—"

"Gone." Garicus's voice was flat. Final. "Everyone's gone. It's just us."

The words didn't make sense. Couldn't make sense. Solaire looked around wildly, searching for movement, for anyone else alive in the ruins. There was nothing. Only ash and silence and the two of them, small and fragile amid the devastation.

"We made a promise," Garicus said suddenly. His hands gripped Solaire's shoulders tighter. "Last night. Do you remember? We promised we'd never let each other die alone."

Solaire didn't remember. But he nodded anyway, because Garicus looked so desperate, so terrifyingly alone, and Solaire understood with sudden clarity that he felt exactly the same.

"I remember," he lied.

Garicus's shoulders sagged with relief. "Good. Because we're all we have now."

They sat together in the ash, two boys who had barely known each other before the world ended, clinging to a promise neither of them fully understood. The smoke drifted. The sun climbed higher, revealing more of the destruction. And still, no one came.

Until the hoofbeats.



# Chapter 3

# Chapter 3: Forged in Fire

Eight years.

I count them sometimes, when the nightmares wake me—eight winters since the cellar, eight springs since the promise. Eight years of Aldric's voice barking corrections, of wooden swords becoming steel, of calluses layering over softer skin until my hands barely remembered what it meant to hold anything but a weapon.

The training yard at Greystone Keep smells like iron and sweat. Always has. I stand in its center now, sixteen years old and harder than I was at eight, waiting for the trial that will make us wards of the Order. The morning sun cuts through the mist, catching on the practice dummies that line the yard's edge—straw men we've beaten to pieces and sewn back together a hundred times.

Solaire shifts beside me. He's taller than me now, has been for two years. I stopped growing at fourteen. He didn't.

"Nervous?" he asks.

"No."

"Liar." He grins, but it doesn't reach his eyes. His fingers tap against his thigh—one, two, three, pause, repeat. Old habit. He does it when he's trying not to think about something.

I don't ask what. I already know. Same thing I'm trying not to think about: what comes after we pass. The missions. The villages. The things Aldric doesn't talk about when he thinks we're sleeping.

The keep's gates grind open. Three knights emerge, crimson cloaks heavy despite the summer heat. Master Edran leads them—older than Aldric, with a scar that splits his face from temple to jaw. The other two I don't recognize. Don't need to. They're here to watch us bleed.

"Garicus." Edran's voice carries like a blade scraping stone. "Solaire. You stand before the Order of the Crimson Vigil as supplicants. For eight years you have trained. Today you prove yourselves worthy."

The ritual words. We knew they were coming. Doesn't make them feel less like a noose tightening.

"The trial is simple," Edran continues. "Defeat your opponent with live steel. First blood, or yield. The Order does not suffer the weak."

A fourth knight steps from the gate. Younger than Edran, older than us. Maybe twenty. His sword is already drawn.

"Garicus," Edran says. "You first."

I step forward. The yard goes quiet except for the wind and Solaire's breathing behind me. I draw my sword—the one Aldric gave me on my fourteenth birthday, plain crossguard and leather-wrapped hilt, edge sharp enough to split hair. It feels lighter than it did two years ago. Feels like part of my arm.

The knight doesn't introduce himself. Doesn't need to. This isn't about names.

He comes at me fast. Not testing, not teaching—trying to end it. His blade sweeps low, aiming for my knee. I pivot, let it hiss past, bring my sword down where his neck will be in half a second.

He's not there. He's already moving, blade rising to catch mine with a sound like a bell cracking. The impact shudders up my arm but I don't let go. Never let go. That was the first lesson.

We circle. He's better than me. I can see it in how he moves—effortless, like he's done this a thousand times and will do it a thousand more. I've done it eight years. It's not the same.

But I've spent eight years learning how to win when I shouldn't.

He lunges. I don't dodge. I step in, inside his guard, too close for his blade to bite. My shoulder drives into his chest and I feel his breath leave in a rush. His sword arm swings wide, trying to recover, and I'm already dropping low, sweeping his legs.

He goes down hard. My blade finds his throat before he can roll away.

"Yield," I say.

He looks at Edran. Edran nods.

"Yield," the knight repeats.

I step back. Offer him a hand. He doesn't take it. Just stands, sheathes his blade, walks back to the others without a word.

My hand is shaking. I don't let them see it.

"Solaire," Edran calls.

Solaire steps forward. His fingers have stopped tapping. That's how I know he's terrified—when he goes still, when he stops filling silence with movement. I want to tell him it's fine, that he's ready, that I've watched him beat me in practice more times than I can count.

I don't say anything. He wouldn't believe me anyway.

The knight facing him is different—smaller, faster. She doesn't wait for Edran to finish speaking. She just attacks.

Solaire barely gets his blade up in time. Steel screams against steel and he stumbles back, off-balance. She presses the advantage, raining blows like she's trying to break him through sheer force. He blocks, deflects, retreats step by step across the yard.

He's losing.

I know the moment he realizes it too. I see it in how his shoulders tighten, how his grip shifts on his sword. He's thinking too much. Always does when it matters.

The knight feints high, cuts low. Solaire jumps back but not fast enough—her blade kisses his thigh, drawing a thin line of red through his trousers.

First blood.

"Yield," the knight says.

Solaire doesn't move. His eyes find mine across the yard, wide and desperate. I shake my head once.

He doesn't yield.

Instead he surges forward, sword cutting in a wild arc that makes my teeth ache. It's too much, too reckless—the kind of move that gets you killed in a real fight. But this isn't a real fight. This is a trial. And sometimes trials aren't about winning clean.

The knight blocks, but she has to give ground. Solaire keeps coming, pressing her back toward the fence, every strike a little wilder than the last. She's better than him. But she's not expecting him to fight like someone with nothing to lose.

Her foot catches on uneven ground. She stumbles.

Solaire's blade stops an inch from her ribs.

"Yield," he says. His voice doesn't shake. Doesn't gloat. Just states fact.

The knight looks at him for a long moment. Then she smiles—brief, sharp—and sheathes her sword.

"Yield."

Solaire steps back, breathing hard. The cut on his thigh is bleeding through his trousers but he doesn't seem to notice. When our eyes meet again I nod. Just once.

He grins. This time it reaches his eyes.



# Chapter 4

# Chapter 4: First Blood

Two years changes everything and nothing.

I'm eighteen now, tall enough that Aldric doesn't have to look down when we speak. The scar on my thigh from the initiation trial has faded to a thin white line. My sword feels like breathing—natural, necessary, mine. I've trained for this my whole life, or at least the part of my life that matters. Ten years of drills and sparring and lessons in how to read danger in a stranger's eyes.

Ten years to prepare for my first real mission.

And I'm not ready.

The village is called Millbrook. Three days' ride from Greystone Keep, nestled in a river valley where the current runs too fast for ice even in winter. Population maybe two hundred. Aldric gave us the briefing last night: reports of blood magic, livestock found drained and marked with symbols, a traveler who swore he heard chanting from the forest at midnight.

"Probably nothing," Aldric said, reading the parchment without looking at us. "Provincial paranoia. But we check anyway."

Garicus asked the question I didn't dare: "And if it's not nothing?"

Aldric finally met our eyes. "Then we do what the Order does. We stop it."

Now we're riding through Millbrook's main street—if you can call it that—while shutters crack open and faces peer out at our crimson cloaks. Fear. That's what I see in those faces. Not relief. Not hope. Fear.

I've never been on this side of it before.

Aldric dismounts first, smooth despite his age. Fifty-two this winter, though he moves like a man ten years younger. Garicus and I follow his lead, tying our horses to a post outside what looks like the village elder's house. It's larger than the others, with glass windows instead of oiled parchment. Wealth is relative.

The door opens before Aldric can knock. A woman stands there—maybe forty, gray threading through her dark hair, eyes sharp and wary. "Ser Aldric," she says. Not a question.

"Elder Marten?" Aldric inclines his head, the bare minimum of courtesy.

"Yes." She doesn't invite us in. Doesn't step aside. Just stands there like a wall between us and whatever she's protecting. "We weren't expecting the Order."

"You sent a report."

"I sent a question. I didn't ask for an inquisition."

"Good thing we're not here for one, then." Aldric's voice is mild, but there's steel underneath. "We're here to investigate. If there's no problem, we'll leave. If there is..." He lets the sentence hang.

Elder Marten's jaw tightens. Then she steps aside. "Come in."



# Chapter 5

# Chapter 5: The Merchant's Tale

Five years since the oath. Seven since the trial. Fifteen since the ashes.

I don't count time the way Garicus does—obsessively, like tallying debts. But some numbers stick anyway. Twenty-three years old. Five missions with the Order. Fifteen years since I last saw my father's face, though I can barely remember it now. Just fragments: tall. Kind eyes. Hands that smelled like incense.

The rest is ash and silence.

Market day at Crossroads—a trading post where three roads meet and merchants hawk everything from Ashtari spices to northern furs. We're here on routine business: supplies for Greystone, a few reports to deliver to the regional commander, nothing that requires drawn steel. Easy work. The kind Aldric assigns when he thinks we need a break from the dark missions.

Garicus hates it. Calls it errand-running beneath our station. But I don't mind. Markets are loud, chaotic, alive. They remind me that the world contains more than blood and oaths.

"Potatoes," Garicus says, consulting Aldric's list with the grim intensity of a man planning an execution. "Six bushels. Salted pork, three barrels. And—" He squints at the handwriting. "Is that 'candles' or 'candies'?"

"Knowing Aldric? Candles." I grin. "Though wouldn't it be something if the great Ser Aldric had a sweet tooth?"

"Focus, Solaire."

"I am focused. On imagining Aldric eating honey cakes like a normal person."

Garicus doesn't smile, but his mouth twitches. Close enough.

We split the list—he takes the practical items, I handle the oddities. My half includes candles (it was candles), a set of replacement buckles for armor, and something Aldric circled twice: "whetstone, Mirean quality if available."

Mirean whetstones. Of course. Only the best for the Order's blades.

I wander through the stalls, half-listening to merchants' pitches. A woman selling ribbons catches my eye—not for the ribbons themselves, but because she's arguing with a customer in three different languages, switching mid-sentence like a bard changing keys. Impressive.

The whetstone merchant is an old man with gaps in his teeth and hands stained perpetually gray with stone dust. He sizes me up, sees the crimson cloak, and names a price that would buy three whetstones in any reasonable market.

"Mirean quality," he insists when I raise an eyebrow. "Ground from the quarries outside Mirendel. Best in the realm."

"And I'm the lost prince of Velathis," I say pleasantly. "Half that, or I walk."

"Two-thirds, and you walk with the whetstone."

We settle at three-fifths. He wraps the stone in oilcloth, still muttering about unreasonable knights and their unreasonable demands. I tuck it into my pack and turn—

—and nearly collide with a man carrying a crate of glass vials.

"Apologies," I say, stepping back.

"No harm." He's middle-aged, weathered in the way of traveling merchants, with road dust in every crease of his clothes. He sets the crate down carefully, then pauses, eyes caught on something.

On me.

More specifically: on my chest.

I follow his gaze. The medallion. It hangs outside my shirt today—bronze compass rose on a leather cord, tarnished with age but still clear in its markings. I've worn it since childhood. Don't remember where it came from. Just woke up one day at the Keep with it in my hand, and Aldric said I'd had it when they found me. Part of the life before. I wear it because it's the only piece of that life I have left.

"That symbol," the merchant says slowly. His voice has gone strange. Careful. "Where did you get it?"

"It was my father's." The answer comes automatically. I don't know if it's true. It feels true.

The merchant's face does something complicated. "Your father."

"He died. Fifteen years ago." Why am I still talking? There's something in the man's expression—recognition, or shock, or—

"What was his name?"

I open my mouth. Close it. The truth is I don't know. Mother called him 'love.' Mara and the other children called him 'Solaire's da.' I was seven when he died. Did I ever know his name?

"I don't remember," I admit. It feels like a confession.

The merchant reaches into his coat, pulls out a leather-bound journal. He flips through pages covered in cramped writing, then stops. "Was he tall? Dark hair going gray at the temples? Spoke softly but carried authority?"

My heart is doing strange things in my chest. "I... maybe. I don't remember. Why?"

"Because six months ago, I met a man wearing that symbol." He taps the page. "Here. In Ashford, three days north. He was asking about a boy who survived a raid years back. Said the boy would be a young man now. Said his son died, but he couldn't let go."

The market sounds fade. The noise becomes distant, muffled, like I'm underwater.

"That's not possible," I hear myself say. "My father is dead. Everyone died."

"He wore priest's robes," the merchant continues. "Crimson Vigil colors, but older, faded. Like he'd been wearing them a long time. And he had that symbol—" He gestures to my medallion. "—burned into his palm. A brand, or maybe a scar shaped deliberate."

Priest's robes. The Order investigates forbidden rituals. Priests who turn informant sometimes join them, trade their knowledge for protection. It's rare, but it happens. Aldric mentioned it once.

Could he have—

No. Impossible. Everyone died. Garicus and I were the only survivors. Aldric found us in the ruins. There was no one else.

"What did he look like?" My voice doesn't sound like mine. "Exactly."

The merchant describes him: tall, yes. Weathered. Eyes like old grief. Hands that moved when he talked, like he was conducting an invisible choir. And the symbol, always the symbol, burned into his right palm.

I reach for my medallion. Turn it over in my fingers. On the back, so worn I'd stopped noticing it, is an inscription in script I never learned to read: _per aspera ad astra_.

"Did he say anything else?" I ask. "A name? Where he was going?"

"Just that if I ever saw a boy with the symbol, I should tell him..." The merchant consults his journal again. "...'I'm sorry. I thought you were dead. I looked for you.' That's all. He paid me ten silver to remember it."

Ten silver. Enough to feed a family for a month. Enough that the merchant would remember, would write it down, would recognize the symbol years later in a crowded market.

My hands are shaking.

"Which way did he go?"

"North. Said he was heading to Blackwood, but that was half a year ago. Could be anywhere now."

Blackwood. The name catches in my mind like a thorn. There's something there, some significance I can't quite grasp. Aldric's maps, late-night conversations about old ruins—

The Blackwood Monastery. Burned years ago, right around the time—

"What's your name?" the merchant asks gently.

"Solaire."

His expression shifts. "He mentioned that name. Said his son was called Solaire."

The world tilts.

I thank him. Don't remember what I say. Give him coin for the information—too much, probably. He presses a scrap of paper into my hand: the page from his journal with the description, the location, the message. Evidence. Proof that I'm not imagining this.

I walk through the market in a daze. Find Garicus near the salt pork barrels, arguing with a vendor about preservation methods. He sees my face and stops mid-sentence.

"What happened?"

"Nothing. I—nothing."

"Solaire." He steps closer, voice low. "What happened?"

I should tell him. We don't keep secrets. Haven't in fifteen years. But something stops me—the look in his eyes, already wary, already afraid. Like he knows whatever I'm about to say will change everything.

"Can we talk? Somewhere private?"

He nods once. We collect our purchases, load them onto the cart Aldric sent with us, and walk to the edge of town where the old well stands. No one comes here—the water went bad years ago, and they built a new well closer in. It's quiet. Safe.

I tell him everything.

The merchant. The description. The symbol. The message. The possibility—impossible, insane, but there nonetheless—that my father survived.

Garicus listens without interrupting. His face goes through a series of expressions I can't read. When I finish, silence stretches between us like a chasm.

"It's not real," he says finally.

"The merchant saw him—"

"The merchant saw someone. Not your father."

"He knew my name. He wore the symbol."

"Scavengers pick through ruins all the time. Could have taken the medallion from a body. Could be a con artist who heard about the raid, figured he'd—"

"Why would a con artist wait fifteen years? Why would he—"

"I don't know!" Garicus's voice cracks like a whip. "But your father is dead, Solaire. They're all dead. We were there. We saw."

"We saw ruins. We saw bodies. We didn't see everyone."

"Who survives a massacre and doesn't come back for their child?"

The question lands like a blow. I don't have an answer. Can't imagine a reason good enough. But the merchant's words echo: _I thought you were dead. I looked for you._

"Maybe he looked in the wrong places," I say quietly. "Maybe he thought I died. Maybe—"

"Maybe he abandoned you." Garicus's eyes are hard. "Maybe he ran when the raiders came and left you to burn. Maybe finding out he's alive is worse than thinking he's dead."

"You don't know that."

"Neither do you." He grabs my shoulders, and there's something desperate in his grip. "Listen to me. Whoever that man is, whatever he wants—it's not worth it. You have a life here. You have the Order. You have—"

"You." I finish for him. "I know. But Garicus, what if it's true? What if he's alive and I do nothing?"

"What if it's true and you go looking and he's exactly who I think he is—someone who left you to die?" His voice drops to almost a whisper. "What if you find him and it breaks you?"

I want to argue. Want to insist I'm stronger than that. But the fear in Garicus's eyes isn't for me finding the truth. It's for what happens after.

He's afraid of losing me.

The realization sits heavy in my chest.

"I have to know," I say. "I'm sorry. I know you think it's a mistake. But I have to."

"Then I'm coming with you."

"Garicus—"

"No." His grip tightens. "Fifteen years ago we made a promise. Whatever comes. However long it takes. You're not doing this alone."

I search his face. Find only stubborn loyalty and bone-deep dread.

"Okay," I say. "Together."

He nods. Doesn't let go.

We stand there as the sun sinks lower, two brothers bound by a promise made in ashes, facing the possibility that everything we thought we knew was a lie. The Order will want us back soon. Aldric will ask where we've been. We'll have to decide whether to tell him.

But for now, it's just us and the silence and the terrible, fragile hope that maybe—maybe—the dead aren't as dead as we thought.

And maybe that's the worst possibility of all.



# Chapter 6

# Chapter 6: Ashes and Truth

The investigations started small.

A question here, a bribe there. Merchants who traveled the northern routes, innkeepers who remembered faces, scribes who kept records of travelers passing through. Weeks slipped by in fragments—mornings spent training with Garicus, afternoons running errands for the Order, evenings walking dusty roads to taverns where strangers might know something I didn't.

I told myself it was curiosity. Just wanting to know if the merchant's words held truth—if somewhere, impossibly, my father still drew breath.

I didn't tell Garicus.

That was the first betrayal. Small, but it counted. We'd shared everything for fifteen years. Every scar, every nightmare, every secret thought. But this... this was mine. A fragile, desperate thing I couldn't bear to expose to his pragmatic gaze.

He'd ask why it mattered. Why I needed to know. And I didn't have an answer that wouldn't sound like I was abandoning what we'd built together.

So I kept quiet and kept searching.

Three weeks into my investigation, I found a priest who remembered a man matching the description. Tall, hollow-eyed, wearing robes that marked him as clergy from the Ashen Reach. The priest had seen him at a monastery months ago, praying for hours in the ruins.

"What monastery?" I asked.

"Blackwood. Burned twenty years past. Nothing there now but ghosts and ash."

I paid him and left before he could ask why I cared.

That night I lay awake in the room I shared with Garicus, listening to his steady breathing, memorizing the location on mental maps. Blackwood Monastery. Two days' ride northwest. I could make an excuse—scouting mission, personal errand, anything. Just go and see. Confirm it was nothing.

Confirm my father was still dead, like he'd been for fifteen years.

Before I could plan further, the letter arrived.



# Chapter 7

# Chapter 7: The Weight of Silence

I waited three days before seeking Aldric out.

Three days of going through the motions—training with Garicus in careful silence, eating meals I couldn't taste, sleeping in fragments between nightmares that now had my father's face. Three days of carrying Theron's confession like a stone in my chest, growing heavier with each passing hour.

The dragon cult. The ritual. The Order's raid.

My father's words circled endlessly: *I led them there. I thought I was saving people. I killed them instead.*

But there was another question beneath that one, sharper and more urgent: Did Aldric know?

The man who found us in the ashes, who took us in when he could have left us to die, who raised us for fifteen years—did he know what the Order had done? Did he know what Theron had told them?

I needed to hear him say it. Needed to look him in the eye and ask the question I'd been too afraid to voice for a decade and a half.

On the third day, I found him in the chapel.



# Chapter 8

# Chapter 8: Blood and Duty

I count the days since Solaire came back from Blackwood.

Seven.

Seven days of watching him move through training like a ghost piloting a corpse. Seven days of stilted conversations and careful silences. Seven days of pretending I don't know exactly what broke inside him when he met his father.

He won't tell me. Not the details. Just that Theron confirmed everything—the cult, the ritual, the Order's response. That Aldric knew. Has always known.

And yesterday, Solaire confronted him.

I wasn't there. Solaire went alone, found Aldric in his quarters after evening meal, and they spoke for two hours behind a closed door. When Solaire emerged, his face was ash-pale and his hands were shaking. He walked past me without a word, went to our room, and hasn't spoken since.

Aldric, for his part, looks ten years older. I saw him this morning in the training yard, watching the younger initiates with the expression of a man attending his own funeral.

He knows I'm coming. Has to know. If Solaire confronted him, I will too. That's how it works—we're a matched pair, two halves of the same oath. What one does, the other follows.

Except this time, I'm not following to support. I'm following to understand.

Because unlike Solaire, I don't need to know if the raid was justified. I need to know why Aldric lied about it for fifteen years.



# Chapter 9

# Chapter 9: The Fracture

## Garicus

I find the letter by accident.

It's tucked inside Solaire's kit bag, the leather satchel he carries on missions. I'm not looking for it—just trying to find the whetstone he borrowed three days ago and hasn't returned. My blade needs sharpening, and I know he has the good one, the Mirean stone he bargained for at Crossroads.

The paper falls out when I lift the bag. Unfolds on the floor between my boots.

I shouldn't read it. Know I shouldn't even as I'm bending to pick it up.

But I do anyway.

*Solaire,*

*Meet me at the oak grove, two miles east of the Keep. Tomorrow at midday. There's more you need to know about the ritual, about what the elders were planning before I intervened.*

*Come alone. The Order has eyes everywhere.*

*—T*

T. Theron.

The paper crumples in my fist before I can stop myself.

He's been meeting him. After everything—after the confession, after the ride back in silence, after I thought he'd realized the truth was poison—he's been meeting him.

How many times? How long has this been going on?

I smooth the letter out. Check the date. Three days ago. The same day Solaire volunteered for a solo patrol. Said he needed space to think. I believed him.

I was a fool.

The door opens. Solaire walks in, still wearing his riding cloak, face flushed from cold or exertion. He sees me. Sees the letter in my hands.

Goes very still.

"Garicus—"

"How long?" My voice sounds strange. Flat. Like all the anger has burned down to ash and this is what's left.

"It's not what you think."

"How. Long."

He closes the door. Doesn't meet my eyes. "Two weeks. Maybe three."

"Three weeks." I hold up the letter. "You've been sneaking off to meet your father for three weeks and you didn't tell me."

"I was going to—"

"When? When were you going to tell me you were choosing him over everything we built?" The anger cracks through now, hot and jagged. "Over the oath? Over us?"

"I'm not choosing him!" Solaire's hands fist at his sides. "I'm trying to understand! There's more to this than what he told me that first night. The cult, the ritual, what the Order did—it's all connected and I need to know how deep it goes!"

"Why?"

The question stops him.

"Why do you need to know?" I step closer. "Will it bring back the dead? Will it change what happened? We survived, Solaire. We made something from the ashes. Why isn't that enough?"

"Because it's built on lies!" His voice rises to match mine. "Everything—the Order, Aldric's training, the oaths we swore—all of it stands on the graves of people who died for no reason! Don't you want to know why?"

"I know why." I throw the letter at his feet. "Evil men made evil choices. The Order tried to stop them and failed. Aldric took us in because he felt guilty. There's your truth. There's your understanding. Does it help?"

Solaire stares at the crumpled paper. "You talked to Aldric."

"Of course I talked to Aldric! You came back from Blackwood looking like the world had ended and wouldn't tell me what happened! So yes, I confronted him. I made him tell me everything—about the cult, about the dragon, about why they burned the village instead of just arresting the guilty." My throat is raw. When did I start shouting? "He told me. All of it. And you know what I realized?"

"What?"

"That it doesn't matter." The words come out hard as stone. "The Order made a choice. A bad choice, maybe. But the cult had already won. The dragon had already come and gone. Everyone was dying anyway. What difference does it make if they burned fast or slow?"

"The difference is intent!" Solaire is shouting now too. "The difference is that the Order chose murder over mercy! Chose covering up their failure over admitting they were too late! Don't you see? We've been serving the same people who killed our families!"

"I see someone desperate for his father's approval." The words are vicious. I know it even as I'm saying them. Can't stop. "I see someone who'd rather believe in conspiracy and injustice than accept that sometimes the world is just cruel and random. Your father failed you, Solaire. He ran instead of fighting. He trusted the wrong people. And now he's trying to make you complicit in that failure by—"

"He's trying to make amends!"

"By poisoning you against the only family you have left!" I grab his shoulders, force him to look at me. "I'm your family! Me! Not some man who abandoned you and spent fifteen years hiding in shame! I was there when you woke up in the ruins! I was there every nightmare, every training session, every mission! Where was he?"

Solaire wrenches free. "You think I don't know that? You think I don't remember?" His eyes are bright with unshed tears. "But he's my father, Garicus. My blood. And he's offering me answers to questions I've had my whole life!"

"And I'm offering you the same thing I've always offered—to stand beside you no matter what. Why isn't that enough anymore?"

The question hangs between us like a sword suspended by thread.

Solaire's face does something complicated. Pain and frustration and something that looks horribly like pity. "Because you're asking me to stay ignorant. To stop questioning. To accept that the people who raised us are the same ones who orphaned us and just... live with that."

"Yes." I don't flinch. "I'm asking you to survive. Because that's what we do. We survive."

"By forgetting?"

"By not letting the past destroy the present!" My hands are shaking. I clench them into fists. "Every moment you spend with Theron is a moment you're not here. Not with me. Not with the life we built. You're choosing ghosts over—"

"Over what? Over you?" Solaire's laugh is bitter. "You keep saying that like I have to choose. Like I can't have both."

"You can't." The certainty in my voice surprises even me. "Because one of us is asking you to move forward and one is dragging you backward into ash and questions that have no good answers. And you're so focused on understanding the why that you can't see you're losing the what."

"What am I losing?"

"Us."

The word falls like an executioner's blade.

Solaire stares at me. "That's not fair."

"It's true." I turn away. Can't look at him anymore. "We swore an oath, Solaire. Never alone. Never apart. But you've been alone for weeks now. Meeting him in secret. Keeping things from me. Breaking the promise a little more each day."

"I was protecting you—"

"From what? The truth? I don't need protecting!" I spin back. "I need my brother. The one who swore we'd face everything together. Where is he? Because all I see now is someone who's already chosen which side of the fracture he's standing on."

Silence. Just our breathing, harsh and uneven.

"I need to understand," Solaire says finally. Quiet. Almost broken. "I need to know if there's something more than just tragedy and bad timing. If there's meaning in what happened. Purpose."

"There's no purpose in massacre, Sol."

"Then what was the point?" His voice cracks. "What was the point of surviving if all we do is serve the people who made us orphans? If we enforce the same ruthless logic that killed our families?"

And there it is. The real question. The one underneath all the others.

I don't have an answer that won't hurt us both.

"The point," I say slowly, "is that we're alive. We have each other. We made something from nothing. That's enough for me. If it's not enough for you..." I stop. Start again. "If you need more than that, then go. Keep meeting your father. Keep searching for meaning in ash. But don't expect me to wait here while you do."

"What are you saying?"

"I'm saying make a choice." The words taste like blood. "Him or me. The past or the present. Because I can't—" My voice breaks. I force it steady. "I can't watch you die slowly from the inside while you chase a truth that will only hurt more."

Solaire's face is white. "You're asking me to abandon my father."

"I'm asking you to remember who was there when he wasn't."

"That's not fair."

"None of this is fair!" I'm shouting again. "You think I want to say this? You think I want to watch everything we built fall apart because some man you barely remember decided his guilt was more important than your peace? But here we are, Sol. You with your letters and secret meetings, me with the certainty that this ends badly no matter what we do. So yes, I'm asking you to choose. Because we can't go on like this."

The silence that follows is so complete I can hear my own heartbeat.

Solaire reaches down. Picks up the crumpled letter. Smooths it out with careful fingers.

"I'm meeting him tomorrow," he says. Doesn't look at me. "At the oak grove. There's something about the ritual he needs to tell me. Something about why the Order really burned the village."

"Solaire—"

"I have to go, Garicus. I have to know."

"And if I ask you not to? If I ask you to stay here with me instead?"

He finally meets my eyes. And what I see there breaks something in my chest I didn't know could break.

"I'm sorry," he whispers. "I'm so sorry. But I have to."

I nod once. Turn away so he won't see my face.

"Then go," I say. "But don't come back expecting things to be the same."

I hear him move toward the door. Hear it open.

"Garicus—"

"Go, Solaire."

The door closes. His footsteps fade down the corridor.

And for the first time in fifteen years, I'm alone in a room that used to be ours.



# Chapter 10

# Chapter 10: The Choice

Six days.

Six days since Solaire walked out of Greystone Keep and I let him go.

Six days of training alone, eating alone, sleeping in a room that echoes with absence. Six days of telling myself I made the right choice, that forcing him to decide was necessary, that clean breaks heal better than slow tears.

Six days of knowing I was lying to myself.

The messenger arrives on the seventh day.



# Chapter 11

# Chapter 11: Somewhere No One's Burning

The chains on my father's wrists are too heavy for what he is now.

He shuffles between Garicus and me as we walk the long road back to Greystone Keep, his priest's robes torn and stained with mud from the scuffle last night. We stopped him. Garicus and I, together, stopped him from burning another village on nothing but paranoid conviction and fifteen years of guilt transformed into crusade.

The village was innocent. We found the proof—no cult, no ritual texts, just farmers frightened by their own shadows and Theron's accusations. My father, broken by what he did to Thornhaven, had been seeing evil everywhere. Creating it where it didn't exist, trying to prevent horrors that lived only in his mind.

He doesn't resist. Hasn't spoken since Garicus restrained him while I searched the evidence. Since I looked him in the eye and said, "No more."

The worst part is understanding. I understand how guilt can calcify into paranoia. How trying to atone for one massacre might drive you to justify another. How the man who tried to save Thornhaven by betraying it could become the man who'd burn innocents again because the first betrayal never stopped hurting.

I understand.

And I'm still bringing him to the Order for judgment.

Garicus hasn't said much either. We're not fighting anymore—the argument that split us dissolved last night when he tracked me to the village Theron was targeting. I was already there, already stopping my father, already making the choice Garicus feared I wouldn't make.

He showed up ready to drag me home. Found me binding Theron's hands instead.

"You came back," was all he said.

"I never left," I replied. "Not really."

It was a lie. We both knew it. But it was the lie we needed to move forward.

Now we walk in careful silence, three men carrying different weights toward the same gray stones.
