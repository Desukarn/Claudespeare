# Chapter 1: The Raid

The smell came first.

Garicus was helping Mother sort the autumn turnips when the smoke slipped through the cracks in the shutters. Not the good smoke from cooking fires or the forge. Wrong smoke. Bitter.

"Inside," Mother said. Not loud. Quiet, the way she spoke when Father came home angry.

He dropped the turnip. It rolled under the table.

Mother crossed to the window, one hand on the shutter. She went very still. Garicus knew that stillness. Rabbits did it when hawks circled.

"Garicus." Her voice came out strange. Too calm. "The cellar. Now."

"But—"

"Now."

She grabbed his wrist and pulled him across the room. The cellar door was beside the hearth, a heavy wooden square set into the floor. Mother yanked it open. The ladder disappeared into darkness.

"Down."

"Mother, what's—"

A scream cut through the air outside. High and sharp and suddenly silent.

Mother shoved him toward the ladder. Garicus caught the top rung, descended three steps, looked up. She knelt at the opening, her face backlit by firelight from the hearth.

"Listen to me." Her hands shook. She gripped the edge of the door to steady them. "You stay down here. You stay quiet. You don't come up until the sun comes through the cracks. Do you understand?"

"I don't—"

"Promise me, Garicus."

He'd never heard her voice break like that.

"I promise."

She pulled something from her apron. The small iron key Father used for the lockbox. She pressed it into his palm, closed his fingers around it.

"Keep this. Don't lose it. Your father will—" She stopped. Started again. "Just keep it safe."

More screams now. Closer. Men shouting in a language Garicus didn't know.

Mother kissed his forehead. Her lips were cold.

Then she slammed the cellar door shut.

Darkness swallowed him whole.

Garicus clung to the ladder, heart hammering against his ribs. Above, Mother's footsteps crossed the floor. Something heavy scraped—the table, dragged over the cellar door. Hiding it.

Then her footsteps ran toward the front of the house.

The door crashed open.

"Please," Mother said. "We have nothing, we're not—"

A man's voice, rough and indifferent. Words Garicus couldn't understand.

Mother screamed.

Garicus pressed his forehead against the ladder rung. The wood bit into his skin. He wanted to climb up, to push through the door, to—

To what? He was eight years old. Small for his age. He had no weapon, no strength, no—

Metal on metal. A wet sound. Mother's scream cut short.

Something heavy hit the floor above him.

Garicus bit his lip until he tasted blood. Don't make a sound. Don't make a sound. She said stay quiet. She made him promise.

Footsteps crossed overhead. Floorboards creaked. Someone kicked over a chair. The sound of drawers yanked open, contents spilling out.

Then the footsteps moved away. The door slammed. Voices outside, growing distant.

But not silent.

Garicus descended the rest of the ladder on shaking legs. The cellar floor was hard-packed dirt, damp and cold. He couldn't see anything. The darkness was absolute, thick as water.

He found the wall with his hands and pressed his back against it. Drew his knees up to his chest. Wrapped his arms around them.

And waited.

Screams echoed through Thornhaven. Men and women both. Children crying for their mothers. The crackle of fire grew louder, closer. Heat seeped through the floorboards above. The cellar filled with smoke.

Garicus pulled his shirt up over his nose and mouth. His eyes burned. He squeezed them shut, but tears came anyway.

Father was at the fields today, checking the late harvest. He wouldn't be back until evening. Would he run when he saw the smoke? Would he try to fight?

Would he come for Garicus?

No. Don't think about it. Mother said wait until morning. Mother said—

Another scream, this one so close Garicus felt it in his bones. He recognized the voice.

Mara. The baker's daughter. She was nine. She'd given him a honey cake last market day, laughed when he got it all over his face.

The scream ended.

Garicus shoved his fist into his mouth to keep from making a sound.

Hours crawled past. Or maybe minutes. Time stopped making sense in the dark. The screaming gradually faded. The fire sounds changed—less crackling, more the deep roar of buildings fully ablaze. The smoke thickened. Garicus could barely breathe.

He thought about climbing the ladder. Just to see. Just to check if Mother—

No. He promised.

So he stayed.

Eventually, the sounds changed again. Hoofbeats, leaving. The thunder of many horses riding away at speed. Shouts in the distance, orders given. Then, slowly, quiet.

The fire kept burning. Garicus could hear it eating through Thornhaven, timber by timber. Sometimes something collapsed with a crash. But no more screams.

Silence was worse than screaming.

Because silence meant no one was left to scream.

Garicus pressed his face against his knees and tried not to think. Tried not to imagine what was up there. Tried not to remember the wet sound Mother made when she stopped screaming.

He must have slept. When he opened his eyes, thin gray light filtered through the cracks around the cellar door. Dawn.

Mother said wait for morning.

It was morning.

Garicus climbed the ladder on numb legs. At the top, he pressed his shoulder against the door and pushed. It didn't budge. The table. Mother had moved the table.

He pushed harder. The door lifted an inch. He got his fingers into the gap, braced his feet on the ladder rungs, and shoved with everything he had.

The table scraped aside. The cellar door fell open.

Smoke poured down. Garicus choked, scrambled up and out, fell to his hands and knees on the floor.

The house was gone.

Not collapsed. Not burned to the foundation. Just—hollowed. The walls still stood, blackened and skeletal. The roof had caved in. Gray morning light filtered through the gaps, illuminating ash and char.

The hearth still stood. The table was overturned beside the cellar door, scorched but intact.

And there, near the front door—

Garicus looked away.

He knew he should check. Should see if maybe, somehow—

But he knew. The stillness told him. The silence.

He stumbled to the door, or where the door had been. The frame remained. The door itself lay in the yard, kicked off its hinges.

Thornhaven was ash.

The buildings that hadn't burned to the ground were burning still, sluggish flames eating through what remained. Bodies lay in the street. Too many to count. Garicus recognized some: Master Torven the blacksmith, face-down outside his forge. Old Willem who told stories at the tavern, sprawled in the mud.

Others he couldn't identify. Didn't want to.

Garicus walked into the street. His feet moved without thought. The morning air was thick with smoke and the smell of burned meat. He should feel something—horror, grief, rage. But there was only a strange, distant numbness.

Everyone was dead.

Father wouldn't come home. Mother was—

He didn't finish the thought.

A sound broke through the silence. Quiet. Almost a whimper.

Garicus stopped. Listened.

There. To the right, near the baker's shop—what was left of it.

He picked his way through the rubble. The bakery had collapsed completely, just a pile of blackened beams and shattered stone. The oven chimney still stood, impossibly intact amid the destruction.

The sound came again. Weak. Barely human.

Garicus climbed over a fallen beam. His hands came away black with soot.

A boy lay half-buried in the rubble. Smaller than Garicus, maybe seven years old. Blond hair matted with blood and ash. Eyes closed. Breathing in shallow gasps.

Solaire. The baker's son. Garicus knew him by sight—they'd never spoken. Solaire was always trailing after his mother, asking questions, laughing at everything.

He wasn't laughing now.

Garicus knelt beside him. "Can you hear me?"

No response.

Garicus grabbed Solaire's shoulders and pulled. The boy came free of the rubble with a scrape of stone. He was light, too light. Garicus dragged him away from the collapsed building, into the street.

Solaire's eyes flickered open. Blue, pale as winter sky. They stared at nothing for a moment, unfocused. Then found Garicus.

"Mother?" Solaire's voice came out cracked, barely a whisper.

Garicus looked back at the bakery ruins. At the bodies in the street. At Thornhaven, burning and silent and dead.

"No," he said.

Solaire's face crumpled. He tried to sit up, fell back. "Father? Mara?"

"No."

"Anyone?"

Garicus met his eyes. "Just us."

Solaire stared at him for a long moment. Then he started to cry. Not loud, hysterical sobs. Quiet, broken sounds that barely made it past his lips.

Garicus sat beside him in the ash and said nothing. What was there to say?

They were eight and seven years old. Alone in a graveyard that used to be home.

Eventually, Solaire's crying faded to hiccups, then silence. He sat up slowly, wincing. Blood crusted the side of his head. More on his arm. But he could move. That was something.

"Why?" Solaire asked. His voice was small. Lost. "Why did they do this?"

Garicus didn't answer. How could he? He was a child. He didn't understand why men killed, why they burned villages, why Mother had to die.

He only understood one thing, sitting there in the ruins: he was alive, and he shouldn't be.

Everyone else was dead. Mother. Father. All of Thornhaven. Dead because Garicus hid in a cellar while they screamed.

The numbness cracked. Guilt poured in like water into a drowning man's lungs.

"I hid," Garicus said. His voice sounded strange. Flat. "Mother told me to hide, and I did."

Solaire looked at him. "You're alive."

"Everyone else is dead."

"We're alive," Solaire said. Like it mattered. Like being alive meant something when everyone you loved was ash.

But then Solaire reached out and grabbed Garicus's hand. His fingers were small and cold and covered in soot.

"Don't leave me," Solaire whispered. "Please. Don't leave me alone."

Garicus looked at their joined hands. Black with ash. Shaking.

He thought about the cellar. The darkness. The screams. The hours waiting alone, not knowing if anyone else survived, if anyone would ever come.

He thought about Solaire, waking up buried in rubble, calling for his family, finding only silence and bodies.

Alone was worse than death.

"I won't," Garicus said.

It wasn't enough. Not for what they'd lost. Not for the emptiness that used to be home.

So he said it again, and this time he meant it like Mother meant her final words. Like a promise that mattered more than fear or sense.

"I won't let you die alone," Garicus said. "Ever. I promise."

Solaire's hand tightened on his. Tears cut clean lines through the ash on his face.

"I promise too," Solaire said. "We stay together. No matter what. We don't—" His voice broke. He tried again. "We don't let each other die alone."

Garicus nodded. Found Solaire's other hand and gripped both tight.

"We stay together," Garicus repeated.

They sat like that as the sun rose higher, two boys in the ash and ruin of everything they'd known, holding on to the only thing left: each other.

The fires burned low. Smoke drifted across Thornhaven's corpse. Somewhere in the distance, a raven called. The sound echoed through empty streets.

Garicus didn't let go.

Neither did Solaire.

They would wait for someone to come. A survivor, a traveler, anyone. And when they came—if they came—Garicus and Solaire would still be together.

Because that was the promise.

The only promise that mattered anymore.

Dawn light filtered through the smoke, pale and cold. The ashes settled. The fires died.

And two boys sat vigil over the grave of their world, bound by an oath they didn't yet understand, holding each other in the silence that followed the screaming.

Together.

Always together.

No matter what came next.

# Chapter 2: The Knight

The first thing Solaire knew was pain.

It bloomed behind his eyes like fire, sharp and immediate. He tried to move and couldn't—his body felt distant, disconnected. Heavy. Something pressed against his face, rough and gritty. Stone? No. Ash.

He opened his eyes to gray.

Gray sky above, choked with smoke. Gray dust coating everything—his hands, his clothes, the rubble surrounding him. And sitting beside him, knees drawn up, face streaked with soot and tears, was Garicus.

"You're awake." Garicus's voice was small. Broken.

Solaire tried to sit up. The world tilted violently, and Garicus's hands were suddenly on his shoulders, steadying him. "Slow. Don't... don't move too fast."

"What—" Solaire's throat burned. He coughed, tasted ash and blood. "What happened?"

Garicus didn't answer. His eyes slid away, focusing on something past Solaire's shoulder. Following his gaze, Solaire saw it: the ruin of his village. Collapsed houses. Charred timber. Bodies—

He looked away, bile rising.

"They came at night," Garicus whispered. "Men in armor. They were looking for... I don't know. Something. Someone. They burned everything."

Fragments surfaced in Solaire's memory. His mother's voice, urgent and afraid. The sound of boots on cobblestones. A shout. Then impact—something striking his head—and darkness.

"My mother," Solaire said. The words scraped out of him. "Where is—"

"Gone." Garicus's voice was flat. Final. "Everyone's gone. It's just us."

The words didn't make sense. Couldn't make sense. Solaire looked around wildly, searching for movement, for anyone else alive in the ruins. There was nothing. Only ash and silence and the two of them, small and fragile amid the devastation.

"We made a promise," Garicus said suddenly. His hands gripped Solaire's shoulders tighter. "Last night. Do you remember? We promised we'd never let each other die alone."

Solaire didn't remember. But he nodded anyway, because Garicus looked so desperate, so terrifyingly alone, and Solaire understood with sudden clarity that he felt exactly the same.

"I remember," he lied.

Garicus's shoulders sagged with relief. "Good. Because we're all we have now."

They sat together in the ash, two boys who had barely known each other before the world ended, clinging to a promise neither of them fully understood. The smoke drifted. The sun climbed higher, revealing more of the destruction. And still, no one came.

Until the hoofbeats.

---

Garicus heard them first. He went rigid, eyes wide with sudden fear. "Hide," he hissed, pulling Solaire behind a collapsed wall.

"Maybe they're here to help—"

"Maybe they're the ones who did this." Garicus's voice was sharp, brooking no argument. They pressed against the rubble, breathing shallow, as the sound grew closer.

Three riders emerged from the smoke. Knights, armored in steel plate, crimson cloaks billowing behind them. The lead rider sat tall in his saddle, gray-bearded and grim-faced, his gaze sweeping the ruins with practiced efficiency. He raised a hand, and the others stopped.

"Spread out," he commanded. His voice carried despite its quietness—a voice used to being obeyed. "Search for survivors. And search for the altar."

The altar? Solaire glanced at Garicus, confused, but Garicus was staring at the knights with something like hatred.

The knights dismounted, moving methodically through the rubble. One poked at a collapsed doorway with his sword. Another bent to examine something on the ground—a scrap of fabric, or perhaps bone. The leader stood apart, scanning the horizon as if expecting something.

"Please," Solaire breathed, so quietly only Garicus could hear. "They might help us."

"They might kill us."

"Why would they—"

"I don't know!" Garicus's whisper cracked. "I don't know anything anymore. But I'm not taking chances."

Solaire wanted to argue. Wanted to believe that armored men with noble bearing had come to save them, to make sense of the senseless. But Garicus's fear was infectious, and beneath it, Solaire felt his own terror—what if these were the same men who had come in the night? What if they had unfinished business?

One of the knights called out. "Ser Aldric! Bodies in the square. Signs of ritual."

The gray-bearded man—Aldric—moved toward the voice, his expression darkening. Solaire watched him go, this stranger who might be salvation or doom, and made a choice.

He stood up.

"Solaire—" Garicus grabbed for him, but Solaire was already stumbling forward, legs unsteady, voice cracking as he called out.

"Please! Help us!"

All three knights turned. Hands went to sword hilts. Aldric's eyes locked onto Solaire, sharp and assessing, and for a moment Solaire was certain he'd made a fatal mistake.

Then Aldric's expression shifted—just slightly, a crack in the stern facade. "Lower your weapons," he said quietly. "He's a child."

Garicus appeared beside Solaire, moving to place himself between his friend and the knights. Protective even in terror. Aldric noticed. His gaze moved between the two boys, lingering on their faces, their ash-covered clothes, the way they stood together like the last two stones in a collapsing wall.

"What are your names?" Aldric asked.

"Solaire." His voice was barely a whisper.

"Garicus." Defiant, despite everything.

Aldric dismounted slowly, deliberately non-threatening. He approached them with the careful movements of someone gentling a frightened animal. When he was close enough, he knelt—bringing himself to their eye level. Solaire saw his face clearly then: weathered, scarred, but not cruel. Just tired. So very tired.

"How many others?" Aldric asked.

"None," Garicus said. "Just us."

"Your families?"

"Dead." The word fell like a stone.

Aldric closed his eyes briefly. When he opened them again, something had changed in his expression—grief, perhaps, or guilt. "I am sorry," he said, and it sounded like he meant it. "No child should see what you have seen."

One of the other knights approached, younger, eager. "Ser, we found the altar. Bloodstains. Ritual markings consistent with—"

"Not now, Loras." Aldric's voice was steel.

The young knight blinked, confused, but stepped back.

Aldric turned his attention back to the boys. "Can you ride?"

Solaire nodded hesitantly. Garicus said nothing.

"The village is gone," Aldric said. "There is nothing left for you here. The Order will send someone to investigate, to document, to..." He trailed off, as if realizing how hollow it all sounded. "You cannot stay here. Do you understand?"

"Where would we go?" Solaire asked.

Aldric was quiet for a long moment. His fellow knights were watching now, waiting. Clearly, this was not standard procedure. Clearly, whatever came next would break protocol.

"With me," Aldric said finally. "I will take you to Greystone Keep. You will be wards of the Order."

"Ser Aldric," the older knight began, "that's not—"

"I know what it is and isn't, Marcus." Aldric rose to his feet, his hand resting on the pommel of his sword—not a threat, just a statement. "These boys are survivors of a massacre we failed to prevent. I will not leave them to die of exposure or be taken by slavers. If the commander takes issue, he may address it with me."

Marcus looked like he wanted to argue. Instead, he sighed. "Your conscience, Ser. Your burden."

"Yes," Aldric said quietly. "It is."

---

They left within the hour.

Garicus refused to ride with any of the knights, so Aldric fashioned a makeshift seat behind his own saddle. Solaire rode with Marcus, who said nothing but whose grip on the reins was steady and sure. The third knight, Loras, ranged ahead, scouting.

Solaire looked back once as they rode away. The village—his village, though it felt like someone else's life now—was already fading into the smoke. He tried to remember his mother's face and found the memory slipping, replaced by ash and ruin.

He turned forward and didn't look back again.

They rode in silence. The countryside was barren, winter-dead fields stretching in all directions. Occasionally they passed other travelers—merchants, farmers—who gave the crimson-cloaked knights a wide berth. No one asked about the two ash-covered children.

As the sun began to set, Aldric called a halt. They made camp in a clearing beside a frozen stream, the knights moving with practiced efficiency to set up tents and start a fire. Solaire and Garicus sat together on a fallen log, watching the flames. Neither of them spoke. There were no words for what they'd lost.

Aldric approached with two bowls of stew. He handed them over without comment, then settled himself across the fire. The silence stretched.

"Why did you take us?" Garicus asked finally. His voice was hard, suspicious.

Aldric was quiet for a moment. "Because you needed help."

"That's not an answer."

"It is the only answer I can give you."

Garicus glared at him. Aldric met his gaze steadily, without anger or defensiveness. Just acceptance.

Solaire, emboldened by the warmth of the fire and the food in his belly, asked the question that had been gnawing at him. "The knight—Loras—he mentioned an altar. What did he mean?"

Aldric's jaw tightened. "Something that no longer concerns you."

"Our village burned and you say it doesn't concern us?"

"Your village burned because evil men made evil choices." Aldric's voice was measured, careful. "That evil is being dealt with. You need only concern yourselves with surviving."

"How do we do that?" Solaire pressed. "We have nothing. No family. No home. You say we're wards, but what does that even mean?"

Aldric regarded him for a long moment. Then he said, "It means you will be fed. Clothed. Trained, if you have the stomach for it. It means you will learn to protect yourselves and others. And it means—" He paused, seeming to choose his words with care. "—it means you will have a place in this world, when so many would cast you aside."

"At what cost?" Garicus asked.

"Discipline. Obedience. Service." Aldric's gaze was unflinching. "The Order does not take without expecting something in return. But neither does it abandon those under its protection."

It wasn't a reassuring answer. But it was honest, and somehow that mattered more.

They finished their meal in silence. The other knights settled into their own routines—Marcus sharpening his blade, Loras checking the perimeter. Aldric sat by the fire, staring into the flames with an expression that might have been regret.

Solaire leaned against Garicus, exhausted beyond words. Garicus's arm came around him automatically, protective even in sleep.

"We'll be okay," Solaire whispered. Trying to convince himself as much as Garicus.

Garicus said nothing. But his arm tightened, and that was answer enough.

---

They reached Greystone Keep three days later.

It rose from the barren landscape like a fist of stone—gray walls, gray towers, gray battlements under a gray sky. No banners flew. No color broke the monotony. It looked, Solaire thought, like a place where hope went to die.

"Welcome home," Marcus said dryly as they rode through the gates.

The courtyard was filled with activity—knights training, stablehands leading horses, servants carrying supplies. Everyone wore variations of the same crimson and gray. Everyone moved with purpose. It was overwhelming after the silence of the ruins.

Aldric dismounted and helped the boys down. He gestured to a severe-looking woman in gray robes. "Matron Elys will see to your immediate needs. Food, baths, beds. Tomorrow we'll discuss your training."

"Training for what?" Solaire asked.

Aldric's expression was unreadable. "For survival. For justice. For whatever path the world sets before you." He paused. "The boy you were died in that village. The man you will become is forged here."

It should have sounded inspirational. Instead, it sounded like a sentence.

Matron Elys led them away, through cold stone corridors that echoed with their footsteps. She showed them to a small room with two narrow beds, a washbasin, and a single window overlooking the training yard. Austere. Functional. Safe.

"Rest," she commanded. "Tomorrow begins your new life."

She left, closing the door behind her with a soft click.

Solaire and Garicus stood in the center of the room, two boys in a strange place, everything familiar stripped away. Then, moving in silent agreement, they pushed the beds together. They climbed onto the makeshift pallet and lay side by side, staring at the ceiling.

"Do you think we'll ever go back?" Solaire whispered.

"There's nothing to go back to," Garicus said.

"I know. But... do you think we'll want to? Someday?"

Garicus turned his head to look at Solaire. His eyes were old beyond his years, haunted by things no child should remember. "I think we'll spend the rest of our lives trying not to."

Solaire wanted to argue. Wanted to believe they could heal, could move forward, could become something other than victims of a massacre. But exhaustion dragged him down, and the words wouldn't come.

He closed his eyes. Beside him, Garicus's breathing slowly evened out.

Outside, the sounds of the Keep continued—metal on metal, shouted commands, the rhythms of a place built on discipline and violence. This was their world now. These gray stones and crimson cloaks. This man Aldric, with his careful words and haunted eyes.

Solaire's last thought before sleep took him was a question: What had they been saved for?

Only time would tell.

And in the morning, the answer would begin.

# Chapter 3: Forged in Fire

Eight years.

I count them sometimes, when the nightmares wake me—eight winters since the cellar, eight springs since the promise. Eight years of Aldric's voice barking corrections, of wooden swords becoming steel, of calluses layering over softer skin until my hands barely remembered what it meant to hold anything but a weapon.

The training yard at Greystone Keep smells like iron and sweat. Always has. I stand in its center now, sixteen years old and harder than I was at eight, waiting for the trial that will make us wards of the Order. The morning sun cuts through the mist, catching on the practice dummies that line the yard's edge—straw men we've beaten to pieces and sewn back together a hundred times.

Solaire shifts beside me. He's taller than me now, has been for two years. I stopped growing at fourteen. He didn't.

"Nervous?" he asks.

"No."

"Liar." He grins, but it doesn't reach his eyes. His fingers tap against his thigh—one, two, three, pause, repeat. Old habit. He does it when he's trying not to think about something.

I don't ask what. I already know. Same thing I'm trying not to think about: what comes after we pass. The missions. The villages. The things Aldric doesn't talk about when he thinks we're sleeping.

The keep's gates grind open. Three knights emerge, crimson cloaks heavy despite the summer heat. Master Edran leads them—older than Aldric, with a scar that splits his face from temple to jaw. The other two I don't recognize. Don't need to. They're here to watch us bleed.

"Garicus." Edran's voice carries like a blade scraping stone. "Solaire. You stand before the Order of the Crimson Vigil as supplicants. For eight years you have trained. Today you prove yourselves worthy."

The ritual words. We knew they were coming. Doesn't make them feel less like a noose tightening.

"The trial is simple," Edran continues. "Defeat your opponent with live steel. First blood, or yield. The Order does not suffer the weak."

A fourth knight steps from the gate. Younger than Edran, older than us. Maybe twenty. His sword is already drawn.

"Garicus," Edran says. "You first."

I step forward. The yard goes quiet except for the wind and Solaire's breathing behind me. I draw my sword—the one Aldric gave me on my fourteenth birthday, plain crossguard and leather-wrapped hilt, edge sharp enough to split hair. It feels lighter than it did two years ago. Feels like part of my arm.

The knight doesn't introduce himself. Doesn't need to. This isn't about names.

He comes at me fast. Not testing, not teaching—trying to end it. His blade sweeps low, aiming for my knee. I pivot, let it hiss past, bring my sword down where his neck will be in half a second.

He's not there. He's already moving, blade rising to catch mine with a sound like a bell cracking. The impact shudders up my arm but I don't let go. Never let go. That was the first lesson.

We circle. He's better than me. I can see it in how he moves—effortless, like he's done this a thousand times and will do it a thousand more. I've done it eight years. It's not the same.

But I've spent eight years learning how to win when I shouldn't.

He lunges. I don't dodge. I step in, inside his guard, too close for his blade to bite. My shoulder drives into his chest and I feel his breath leave in a rush. His sword arm swings wide, trying to recover, and I'm already dropping low, sweeping his legs.

He goes down hard. My blade finds his throat before he can roll away.

"Yield," I say.

He looks at Edran. Edran nods.

"Yield," the knight repeats.

I step back. Offer him a hand. He doesn't take it. Just stands, sheathes his blade, walks back to the others without a word.

My hand is shaking. I don't let them see it.

"Solaire," Edran calls.

Solaire steps forward. His fingers have stopped tapping. That's how I know he's terrified—when he goes still, when he stops filling silence with movement. I want to tell him it's fine, that he's ready, that I've watched him beat me in practice more times than I can count.

I don't say anything. He wouldn't believe me anyway.

The knight facing him is different—smaller, faster. She doesn't wait for Edran to finish speaking. She just attacks.

Solaire barely gets his blade up in time. Steel screams against steel and he stumbles back, off-balance. She presses the advantage, raining blows like she's trying to break him through sheer force. He blocks, deflects, retreats step by step across the yard.

He's losing.

I know the moment he realizes it too. I see it in how his shoulders tighten, how his grip shifts on his sword. He's thinking too much. Always does when it matters.

The knight feints high, cuts low. Solaire jumps back but not fast enough—her blade kisses his thigh, drawing a thin line of red through his trousers.

First blood.

"Yield," the knight says.

Solaire doesn't move. His eyes find mine across the yard, wide and desperate. I shake my head once.

He doesn't yield.

Instead he surges forward, sword cutting in a wild arc that makes my teeth ache. It's too much, too reckless—the kind of move that gets you killed in a real fight. But this isn't a real fight. This is a trial. And sometimes trials aren't about winning clean.

The knight blocks, but she has to give ground. Solaire keeps coming, pressing her back toward the fence, every strike a little wilder than the last. She's better than him. But she's not expecting him to fight like someone with nothing to lose.

Her foot catches on uneven ground. She stumbles.

Solaire's blade stops an inch from her ribs.

"Yield," he says. His voice doesn't shake. Doesn't gloat. Just states fact.

The knight looks at him for a long moment. Then she smiles—brief, sharp—and sheathes her sword.

"Yield."

Solaire steps back, breathing hard. The cut on his thigh is bleeding through his trousers but he doesn't seem to notice. When our eyes meet again I nod. Just once.

He grins. This time it reaches his eyes.

---

The oath ceremony happens at sunset.

They lead us to the keep's chapel, a stone room with no windows and crimson candles burning in niches along the walls. The candles smell wrong—sweet and rotten at once. Aldric told us once what they're made from. We didn't ask again.

Edran stands at the altar, the other knights forming a half-circle behind him. Aldric is there too, off to the side, his face unreadable in the flickering light.

"Kneel," Edran commands.

We kneel. The stone is cold even through our trousers. My knee finds a crack and settles into it like it was made for this.

"Garicus. Solaire. You have proven your strength. Now you will prove your commitment. Repeat the words I speak."

He begins the oath. We repeat it.

"I pledge my sword to the Order of the Crimson Vigil."

The words taste like ash.

"I will stand against the darkness, no matter the cost."

Solaire's voice wavers on 'cost.' Mine doesn't. I stopped wavering years ago.

"I will hunt those who summon forbidden powers, who threaten the innocent, who would bring ruin upon the realm."

My mother's screams, distant and half-remembered, echo under the words. I push them down.

"I will be the blade in the night, the vigil that never sleeps, the fire that purges corruption."

Fire. Always fire. The Order loves its fire.

"This I swear, on blood and honor, until death claims me."

Edran draws a knife from his belt—small, ceremonial, sharp. He cuts his palm, then holds it out to me. I take the knife. Cut my own palm. The pain is bright and clean. I clasp his hand, our blood mixing.

He does the same with Solaire.

"Rise, wards of the Order."

We stand. My hand throbs in time with my pulse. Beside me, Solaire is still gripping his bleeding palm, staring at it like he's trying to memorize the shape of the wound.

"You are dismissed," Edran says. "Training begins at dawn."

The other knights file out. Aldric lingers, watching us, but when I meet his gaze he turns and follows the others.

We're alone in the chapel. Just us and the candles and the oath still hanging in the air like smoke.

"Come on," I say quietly.

We walk out together, through the keep's corridors, past the training yard, to the place we always go when we need to be away from everything else: the wall overlooking the forest. The sun is below the horizon now, the sky streaked with red and gold. Promises in ash, I think. That's what this looks like.

Solaire sits on the wall's edge, legs dangling over empty air. I sit beside him. We don't talk for a long time.

Finally, he speaks.

"Do you remember what we promised? That night?"

"Yes."

"Say it."

I close my eyes. I can still smell the smoke, still hear the silence after the screaming stopped.

"Never let each other die alone."

"Never let each other die alone," he echoes. His hand finds mine—the one that's still bleeding from the oath. He squeezes it. I squeeze back.

"I don't care what oath they made us swear in there," Solaire says. His voice is quiet but steady. "That one doesn't count. Not really. The only oath that matters is the one we made in the ashes."

"I know."

"To each other. Not to them."

"I know."

He turns to look at me, and in the dying light he looks so much like the boy from eight years ago—small and scared and desperate for something to hold onto—that my chest aches.

"Promise me again," he says. "Right now. No ceremony, no blood, no knights watching. Just us."

I turn to face him fully. The forest below us is darkening, the keep behind us is lighting its torches, and somewhere in the chapel Edran is probably cleaning blood off the altar. None of it matters.

"I promise," I say. "Never alone. Whatever comes. However long it takes."

"However long it takes," Solaire repeats.

We sit there as night falls, two boys who survived fire once and swore they'd survive it again. The Order has our blood now. Our swords. Our mornings and our evenings and the years stretching ahead.

But they don't have this. They'll never have this.

---

Aldric stands in the chapel long after the candles should have been extinguished.

He tells himself he's checking that the altar is clean, that the ceremonial knife has been returned to its proper place, that everything is ready for tomorrow's training. He tells himself a lot of things these days.

The truth is simpler: he can't bear to go back to his quarters yet. Can't bear to lie down and pretend to sleep when all he'll see is Garicus's face during the oath, blank and hard as stone. Solaire's face, trying so hard to believe the words mattered.

They're good boys. He raised them to be good. Taught them to fight, to survive, to question nothing. And they learned. God help him, they learned so well.

He should feel proud.

Instead he feels like a man lighting a pyre and calling it warmth.

The knife is still on the altar. He picks it up, turns it over in his hands. Eight years ago he stood in their burning village and made a choice: take them, raise them, teach them to be weapons. He thought it was mercy. Thought if he trained them hard enough, loved them carefully enough, they'd be strong enough to survive when the truth finally came.

Now he's not sure there's any strength in the world that will be enough.

"I'm sorry," he says to the empty chapel. To the boys who aren't there. To the man he was before he learned that righteousness and ruin wear the same cloak.

The candles gutter. He doesn't relight them.

He leaves the knife on the altar and walks out into the dark.

---




# Chapter 4: First Blood

Two years changes everything and nothing.

I'm eighteen now, tall enough that Aldric doesn't have to look down when we speak. The scar on my thigh from the initiation trial has faded to a thin white line. My sword feels like breathing—natural, necessary, mine. I've trained for this my whole life, or at least the part of my life that matters. Ten years of drills and sparring and lessons in how to read danger in a stranger's eyes.

Ten years to prepare for my first real mission.

And I'm not ready.

The village is called Millbrook. Three days' ride from Greystone Keep, nestled in a river valley where the current runs too fast for ice even in winter. Population maybe two hundred. Aldric gave us the briefing last night: reports of blood magic, livestock found drained and marked with symbols, a traveler who swore he heard chanting from the forest at midnight.

"Probably nothing," Aldric said, reading the parchment without looking at us. "Provincial paranoia. But we check anyway."

Garicus asked the question I didn't dare: "And if it's not nothing?"

Aldric finally met our eyes. "Then we do what the Order does. We stop it."

Now we're riding through Millbrook's main street—if you can call it that—while shutters crack open and faces peer out at our crimson cloaks. Fear. That's what I see in those faces. Not relief. Not hope. Fear.

I've never been on this side of it before.

Aldric dismounts first, smooth despite his age. Fifty-two this winter, though he moves like a man ten years younger. Garicus and I follow his lead, tying our horses to a post outside what looks like the village elder's house. It's larger than the others, with glass windows instead of oiled parchment. Wealth is relative.

The door opens before Aldric can knock. A woman stands there—maybe forty, gray threading through her dark hair, eyes sharp and wary. "Ser Aldric," she says. Not a question.

"Elder Marten?" Aldric inclines his head, the bare minimum of courtesy.

"Yes." She doesn't invite us in. Doesn't step aside. Just stands there like a wall between us and whatever she's protecting. "We weren't expecting the Order."

"You sent a report."

"I sent a question. I didn't ask for an inquisition."

"Good thing we're not here for one, then." Aldric's voice is mild, but there's steel underneath. "We're here to investigate. If there's no problem, we'll leave. If there is..." He lets the sentence hang.

Elder Marten's jaw tightens. Then she steps aside. "Come in."

---

The inside of her house smells like woodsmoke and old herbs. We sit at a rough-hewn table while she pours water from a clay pitcher. No one touches the cups.

"Tell me about the livestock," Aldric says.

"Three goats, two chickens. Found them over the course of a week. Drained of blood, symbols carved into the dirt around them."

"What kind of symbols?"

"I don't know. I'm not a scholar of forbidden arts." Her tone is sharp. "That's why we asked for guidance, not an armed party."

Aldric ignores the jab. "The traveler who heard chanting. Is he still in the village?"

"Left two days ago. Didn't want trouble."

"Smart man." Aldric leans back in his chair. "Is there anyone in Millbrook who might practice blood magic? Anyone new to the village, anyone with... unusual knowledge?"

Elder Marten's hesitation is brief but telling. "There's a woman. Elara. She came here five months ago, fleeing the war in the north. She has some skill with healing herbs, helps with births. Some of the older folk say she has a gift."

"A gift," Aldric repeats. The word sounds like an accusation.

"People talk. Doesn't mean anything."

"Where can we find her?"

Elder Marten stands. "I'll take you."

---

Elara lives on the edge of the village, in a cottage so small it barely qualifies as a structure. Smoke rises from the chimney. Herbs hang in bunches from the eaves, drying in the cold air. A garden plot beside the door, winter-dead but carefully tended.

It looks... ordinary. Harmless.

Garicus sees it differently. I can tell by how his hand drifts to his sword hilt, by the way his eyes scan the tree line. He's looking for threats. Finding them everywhere.

Elder Marten knocks. "Elara? It's Marten. I've brought... visitors."

The door opens. The woman who emerges is younger than I expected—maybe twenty-five, with auburn hair and eyes the color of forest moss. She looks at Elder Marten, then at us, and something in her expression closes off.

"The Order," she says flatly.

"Knights of the Crimson Vigil," Aldric confirms. "We're investigating reports of blood magic in the area."

"And you thought to start with the stranger." She doesn't sound surprised. Doesn't even sound angry. Just tired. "Of course."

"We're simply gathering information," Aldric says. "May we come in?"

"Do I have a choice?"

"Yes."

Elara studies him for a long moment. Then she steps aside. "Fine. But I have work to do, so make it quick."

The cottage interior is even smaller than it looks from outside. A pallet in one corner, a table with two chairs, shelves lined with clay jars and dried plants. A pot simmers over the fire, filling the air with the scent of something medicinal. Clean. Poor but proud.

Aldric doesn't sit. Neither do we. "You practice healing," he states.

"I help where I can. Midwifery. Poultices for fever. Nothing more."

"Nothing magical?"

"Magic?" Elara laughs, bitter and short. "I know plants, Ser Knight. Which roots ease pain, which leaves stop bleeding. My mother taught me. Her mother taught her. If that's magic, then every grandmother in the realm is a witch."

Garicus speaks for the first time. "The animals. The symbols. You know anything about that?"

"Nothing." She meets his gaze without flinching. "I'm a healer, not a butcher."

"Blood magic often starts with animals," Aldric says quietly. "Small sacrifices. Practice. Then it escalates."

"Then find someone who butchers animals." Elara's voice is sharp now, fear bleeding through. "I've done nothing wrong."

Aldric is silent for a moment. Thinking. Weighing. I can see the calculation in his eyes.

"We'll need to search your home," he says finally.

"What?"

"Standard procedure. If you've done nothing wrong, you have nothing to hide."

"I have nothing to hide because I've done nothing wrong." Elara's hands are shaking now. "You can't just—"

"We can." Aldric's voice is gentle, which somehow makes it worse. "We are the Order. This is what we do."

I want to say something. Want to point out that she's just a woman trying to survive, that healing isn't the same as corruption, that fear of strangers isn't evidence of guilt.

I don't say anything.

Garicus moves first, systematically searching the shelves. I follow because that's what we do—we follow orders. We check jars and bundles, looking for... what? Blood? Ritual components? I don't even know what I'm looking for.

I find herbs. Willow bark. Chamomile. Things my mother might have used if she'd lived long enough to teach me their names.

Aldric searches the pallet, the small chest beside it. Elara stands frozen by the fire, watching us violate her space, and I can see in her face the exact moment she stops being a person and becomes a suspect.

I've seen that look before. In the mirror, when I remember the raiders.

Garicus finishes first. "Nothing."

I shake my head. "Nothing here either."

Aldric straightens. "Thank you for your cooperation," he says, as if Elara had a choice. "We may have more questions later."

"Am I free to go about my work?" Her voice is ice over rage.

"For now."

We leave. Elder Marten is waiting outside, her expression unreadable. "Satisfied?" she asks.

"We're rarely satisfied," Aldric replies. "But we're thorough."

---

That night we make camp outside the village. Aldric doesn't trust their hospitality, and I don't blame him—we're about as welcome as plague rats. The fire crackles between us, shadows dancing across Aldric's weathered face.

"She's hiding something," Garicus says.

I look up, startled. "What makes you say that?"

"She was too defensive. Too quick to explain." He pokes at the fire with a stick. "Innocent people don't react like that."

"Innocent people react exactly like that when knights show up and start accusing them of blood magic." The words come out sharper than I intended.

Garicus frowns. "You think she's clean?"

"I think we found nothing. No evidence. No ritual components. Just herbs."

"The symbols on the ground—"

"Could be anything. Could be some kid playing with sticks. Could be completely unrelated to her."

"Could be carefully hidden," Garicus counters. "We searched for an hour. She's lived there for months."

Aldric has been silent, staring into the flames. Now he speaks. "It doesn't matter."

We both turn to him.

"What do you mean it doesn't matter?" I ask.

"Evidence would make it cleaner. Easier. But the absence of evidence is not evidence of absence." He meets my eyes. "She's a stranger in a village where strange things are happening. She admits to knowledge of herbs and healing—which is how blood magic often begins. And she's alone, with no family to vouch for her character."

My stomach drops. "You can't be serious."

"I'm always serious, Solaire."

"We found nothing!" I'm on my feet now, though I don't remember standing. "You searched her home. There was nothing there!"

"We found nothing today," Aldric corrects. "How long until we find something tomorrow? Next week? How many more animals need to die? How long until it's not animals anymore?"

"You don't know that will happen—"

"I know what happens when we do nothing." His voice is quiet, final. "I've seen it. You haven't."

"So what, we just execute her on suspicion? On fear?"

"On preventive justice." Aldric stands, meeting me eye-to-eye now. "That's what the Order does, Solaire. We don't wait for the body count to justify action. We prevent it."

"That's murder."

The word hangs between us like drawn steel.

Garicus stands too, placing himself slightly between us. Not taking sides. Just present. "Solaire—"

"Don't." I step back. "Don't tell me this is necessary. Don't tell me this is what we do. We're supposed to hunt the guilty, not—not terrorize healers because they're convenient."

"Convenient?" Aldric's voice hardens. "You think I find this convenient? You think I enjoy condemning people on insufficient evidence? This is the burden of the vigil, Solaire. We make the hard choices so others don't have to. We carry the guilt so villages can sleep at night."

"Villages like ours?" The question escapes before I can stop it.

Aldric goes very still.

"Villages like Thornhaven?" I press. "Is that what happened? Did knights show up and decide preventive justice was necessary? Did they carry the guilt while everyone burned?"

"That was different," Aldric says quietly.

"How?"

He doesn't answer. Can't answer. I can see it in his face—the confirmation I've never wanted, the truth I've always suspected.

Garicus's hand is on my arm now. "Solaire. Not here. Not now."

I shake him off. "When, then? When do we talk about this? When do we ask why we're about to do the exact same thing that was done to us?"

"It's not the same," Garicus says, but his voice lacks conviction.

"Isn't it? Strange people in a village. Reports. Suspicion. And then—" I make a gesture encompassing the camp, the village, everything. "Then knights in crimson cloaks decide who lives and who doesn't."

Aldric's face is stone. "Go to your tent, Solaire."

"I'm not a child—"

"Go. To. Your. Tent." Each word falls like an axe blow. "We'll discuss this in the morning when you've had time to think clearly."

"I'm thinking clearly now!"

"No, you're thinking emotionally. There's a difference." He turns away from me, addressing Garicus instead. "Make sure he stays in camp tonight."

"Aldric—" Garicus starts.

"That's an order."

Silence. The fire pops. Somewhere in the village, a dog barks.

I walk to my tent because the alternative is saying something I can't take back. Garicus follows, hovering just outside while I collapse onto my bedroll.

"He's wrong," I say to the canvas roof.

Garicus sits at the tent's entrance. His outline blocks the firelight. "Maybe."

"Maybe?"

"I don't know, Sol. I've never done this before either."

"But you think he might be right."

He's quiet for a long moment. "I think... I think if we're wrong and we do nothing, people die. And if we're wrong and we act, one person dies. The math isn't complicated."

"One innocent person."

"We don't know she's innocent."

"We don't know she's guilty!"

Garicus turns to look at me. In the dim light, he looks older than eighteen. Looks like Aldric. "Would you rather wait to find out? Risk it? Bet the village that she's exactly what she claims?"

I want to say yes. Want to believe in proof and justice and waiting for certainty. But I remember Thornhaven. Remember the bodies. Remember waking up to ash and silence and Garicus's hand in mine, the only thing tethering me to life.

Would I have traded Elara's life for that not happening? For my mother to still be alive?

The thought makes me sick.

"I don't know," I whisper.

Garicus nods. "Yeah. Me neither."

He stays there until I pretend to sleep, then quietly returns to the fire. I lie awake listening to him and Aldric talking in low voices, making plans I don't want to hear.

And I think about a woman in a small cottage, brewing medicine over her fire, completely unaware that tomorrow three men in crimson cloaks will decide whether she deserves to see another sunrise.

---

Dawn comes too soon.

Aldric wakes us with his usual efficiency. We eat in silence—dried meat, hard bread, water from our skins. Then we pack up camp and ride back into Millbrook.

Elara is in her garden when we arrive. She looks up at the sound of hooves, and I watch understanding dawn in her eyes. She knows. Somehow, she knows.

She doesn't run. Just stands there, dirt on her hands, watching us approach.

Aldric dismounts. "Elara of Millbrook, by the authority of the Order of the Crimson Vigil, you are accused of practicing blood magic within the borders of—"

"I'm innocent," she says. Not pleading. Just stating fact.

"The evidence suggests otherwise."

"What evidence?" Her voice rises. "You found nothing! I've done nothing!"

"The pattern suggests—"

"The pattern suggests you needed someone to blame and I'm convenient!" She's backing away now, toward her cottage. "I'm a stranger. I'm alone. I'm easy."

"Garicus," Aldric says quietly.

Garicus dismounts. Draws his sword.

My breath stops.

"Please," Elara says. Looking at me now. Why is she looking at me? "Please, I've done nothing. I just want to help people. I just want to—"

"I'm sorry," Aldric says. And the terrible thing is, he sounds like he means it. "This is preventive justice. For the safety of the realm."

Elara runs. Not toward the forest, where she might have a chance. Toward her cottage. Toward home. Like sanctuary still means something.

Garicus catches her before she reaches the door. His hand closes on her arm and she screams—not words, just pure terror—and I'm moving before I can think, my hand on my own sword.

"Solaire." Aldric's voice cuts through the chaos. "Stand down."

"She's unarmed!"

"She's guilty."

"You don't know that!"

"I know enough!" For the first time in ten years, Aldric raises his voice at me. "I know what happens when we hesitate! I know what indecision costs! And I will not—I will NOT—let another village burn because we were too afraid to act!"

He's not talking about Millbrook anymore.

Garicus has forced Elara to her knees. She's stopped struggling. Just crying now, quiet and broken. Like she's already a ghost.

"Do it," Aldric orders.

Garicus raises his sword.

I should stop him. Should draw my own blade, get between them, refuse the order. Should do something other than stand here frozen, watching my brother become an executioner.

But I don't move.

Because Aldric might be right. Because Thornhaven burned. Because I don't know enough about magic or patterns or preventive justice to be certain. Because I'm eighteen years old and I'm scared and I don't want to be responsible for the next massacre.

So I watch.

Garicus's blade falls.

It's quick. Clean. One stroke. Elara makes a sound—small, surprised—and then nothing.

Silence.

Garicus steps back. His sword is red. His face is blank.

Aldric closes Elara's eyes with gentle fingers. "May your soul find peace in the next world," he murmurs. The ritual words for the executed. As if prayers change anything.

I can't breathe. Can't look away. Can't reconcile the body on the ground with the woman who brewed medicine yesterday, who told us she just wanted to help people.

Aldric turns to me. "This is the work, Solaire. This is what we swore to do."

"This is murder," I hear myself say.

"This is war." His eyes are haunted but certain. "And in war, we don't wait for perfect intelligence. We act on the best information we have, and we live with the consequences."

"What if you're wrong?"

"Then I'll answer for it when I stand before whatever god judges knights." He wipes blood from his hands with a cloth. "But I won't answer for inaction. Not again."

Again.

The word confirms everything I've suspected and feared. Thornhaven wasn't random. Wasn't bandits or bad luck. It was this—preventive justice, hard choices, the greater good measured in corpses.

Garicus sheathes his sword. Looks at me. Waiting for... what? Condemnation? Absolution?

I can't give him either.

We bury Elara in her own garden. Aldric says it's procedure—can't leave bodies in villages, causes talk. I think it's guilt, trying to dress itself up as mercy.

Elder Marten watches from her doorway as we work. When we're done, she approaches. "I hope you're satisfied, Ser Knight."

"I'm never satisfied," Aldric replies. "But the village is safe."

"Is it?" She looks at the fresh-turned earth. "Or did you just teach us that strangers aren't welcome, that being alone is dangerous, that the Order cares more about suspicion than truth?"

Aldric has no answer.

We ride out before noon. Millbrook's gates close behind us with finality.

---

We camp that night in silence. I sit apart from the fire, unable to stomach food or company. Garicus tries to approach once, but I shake my head and he retreats.

Aldric lets me be.

I think about Elara. About her cottage and her herbs and her desperate claim of innocence. I think about the villagers who will remember her execution, who will whisper about the Order's justice. I think about the next stranger who arrives in Millbrook, and how welcome they'll be.

I think about Garicus's face when he raised that sword—blank, dutiful, already halfway to not-feeling. And I wonder if that's what I'll look like in a year, in five, in ten. If this is how you survive the vigil: by turning off the part of you that questions, that doubts, that sees people instead of threats.

Garicus sits down beside me. Doesn't speak. Just sits.

After a while, I find words. "Do you think she was guilty?"

He's quiet for a long time. "I think Aldric believes she was. And I think he's seen more than we have."

"That's not an answer."

"I don't have one." He looks at his hands. They're clean now, but I know he can still see the blood. "I did what I was trained to do. What we both were trained to do. And I'll probably have to do it again."

"How can you be okay with that?"

"I'm not okay with it." His voice is raw. "I'm just... accepting it. Because the alternative is what? Abandoning the Order? Going rogue? We made an oath, Sol."

"We made two oaths," I remind him. "One to the Order. One to each other. Which one matters more?"

He meets my eyes. "You know which one."

"Do I?" I gesture toward the village behind us. "Because back there, you chose the Order. You did what Aldric said without question."

"I trust Aldric."

"Even when he might be wrong?"

Garicus is quiet. Then: "Especially then. Because if I don't trust someone who's done this longer than we've been alive, how am I supposed to trust myself?"

It's not a bad point. But it doesn't make the weight in my chest lighter.

"I saw her face," I whisper. "Right before. She wasn't guilty. I know she wasn't."

"Maybe." Garicus leans back, looking at the stars. "Or maybe you wanted her to be innocent so badly that you convinced yourself. Either way, it's done now."

"That's supposed to make me feel better?"

"No. Nothing's supposed to make you feel better." He turns his head toward me. "This is the work, Sol. Aldric's right about that. It's ugly and uncertain and sometimes we'll get it wrong. But we do it anyway because the alternative is worse."

"What if the alternative isn't worse?" The question feels like betrayal just asking it. "What if we're the ones making things worse?"

Garicus doesn't have an answer for that.

We sit together under stars that don't care about justice or innocence, two brothers bound by oaths they're starting to question, and I feel the first crack form in the foundation of everything I thought I knew.

Aldric was right about one thing: this is the work.

I just don't know if I can do it.

---




# Chapter 5: The Merchant's Tale

Five years since the oath. Seven since the trial. Fifteen since the ashes.

I don't count time the way Garicus does—obsessively, like tallying debts. But some numbers stick anyway. Twenty-three years old. Five missions with the Order. Fifteen years since I last saw my father's face, though I can barely remember it now. Just fragments: tall. Kind eyes. Hands that smelled like incense.

The rest is ash and silence.

Market day at Crossroads—a trading post where three roads meet and merchants hawk everything from Ashtari spices to northern furs. We're here on routine business: supplies for Greystone, a few reports to deliver to the regional commander, nothing that requires drawn steel. Easy work. The kind Aldric assigns when he thinks we need a break from the dark missions.

Garicus hates it. Calls it errand-running beneath our station. But I don't mind. Markets are loud, chaotic, alive. They remind me that the world contains more than blood and oaths.

"Potatoes," Garicus says, consulting Aldric's list with the grim intensity of a man planning an execution. "Six bushels. Salted pork, three barrels. And—" He squints at the handwriting. "Is that 'candles' or 'candies'?"

"Knowing Aldric? Candles." I grin. "Though wouldn't it be something if the great Ser Aldric had a sweet tooth?"

"Focus, Solaire."

"I am focused. On imagining Aldric eating honey cakes like a normal person."

Garicus doesn't smile, but his mouth twitches. Close enough.

We split the list—he takes the practical items, I handle the oddities. My half includes candles (it was candles), a set of replacement buckles for armor, and something Aldric circled twice: "whetstone, Mirean quality if available."

Mirean whetstones. Of course. Only the best for the Order's blades.

I wander through the stalls, half-listening to merchants' pitches. A woman selling ribbons catches my eye—not for the ribbons themselves, but because she's arguing with a customer in three different languages, switching mid-sentence like a bard changing keys. Impressive.

The whetstone merchant is an old man with gaps in his teeth and hands stained perpetually gray with stone dust. He sizes me up, sees the crimson cloak, and names a price that would buy three whetstones in any reasonable market.

"Mirean quality," he insists when I raise an eyebrow. "Ground from the quarries outside Mirendel. Best in the realm."

"And I'm the lost prince of Velathis," I say pleasantly. "Half that, or I walk."

"Two-thirds, and you walk with the whetstone."

We settle at three-fifths. He wraps the stone in oilcloth, still muttering about unreasonable knights and their unreasonable demands. I tuck it into my pack and turn—

—and nearly collide with a man carrying a crate of glass vials.

"Apologies," I say, stepping back.

"No harm." He's middle-aged, weathered in the way of traveling merchants, with road dust in every crease of his clothes. He sets the crate down carefully, then pauses, eyes caught on something.

On me.

More specifically: on my chest.

I follow his gaze. The medallion. It hangs outside my shirt today—bronze compass rose on a leather cord, tarnished with age but still clear in its markings. I've worn it since childhood. Don't remember where it came from. Just woke up one day at the Keep with it in my hand, and Aldric said I'd had it when they found me. Part of the life before. I wear it because it's the only piece of that life I have left.

"That symbol," the merchant says slowly. His voice has gone strange. Careful. "Where did you get it?"

"It was my father's." The answer comes automatically. I don't know if it's true. It feels true.

The merchant's face does something complicated. "Your father."

"He died. Fifteen years ago." Why am I still talking? There's something in the man's expression—recognition, or shock, or—

"What was his name?"

I open my mouth. Close it. The truth is I don't know. Mother called him 'love.' Mara and the other children called him 'Solaire's da.' I was seven when he died. Did I ever know his name?

"I don't remember," I admit. It feels like a confession.

The merchant reaches into his coat, pulls out a leather-bound journal. He flips through pages covered in cramped writing, then stops. "Was he tall? Dark hair going gray at the temples? Spoke softly but carried authority?"

My heart is doing strange things in my chest. "I... maybe. I don't remember. Why?"

"Because six months ago, I met a man wearing that symbol." He taps the page. "Here. In Ashford, three days north. He was asking about a boy who survived a raid years back. Said the boy would be a young man now. Said his son died, but he couldn't let go."

The market sounds fade. The noise becomes distant, muffled, like I'm underwater.

"That's not possible," I hear myself say. "My father is dead. Everyone died."

"He wore priest's robes," the merchant continues. "Crimson Vigil colors, but older, faded. Like he'd been wearing them a long time. And he had that symbol—" He gestures to my medallion. "—burned into his palm. A brand, or maybe a scar shaped deliberate."

Priest's robes. The Order investigates forbidden rituals. Priests who turn informant sometimes join them, trade their knowledge for protection. It's rare, but it happens. Aldric mentioned it once.

Could he have—

No. Impossible. Everyone died. Garicus and I were the only survivors. Aldric found us in the ruins. There was no one else.

"What did he look like?" My voice doesn't sound like mine. "Exactly."

The merchant describes him: tall, yes. Weathered. Eyes like old grief. Hands that moved when he talked, like he was conducting an invisible choir. And the symbol, always the symbol, burned into his right palm.

I reach for my medallion. Turn it over in my fingers. On the back, so worn I'd stopped noticing it, is an inscription in script I never learned to read: _per aspera ad astra_.

"Did he say anything else?" I ask. "A name? Where he was going?"

"Just that if I ever saw a boy with the symbol, I should tell him..." The merchant consults his journal again. "...'I'm sorry. I thought you were dead. I looked for you.' That's all. He paid me ten silver to remember it."

Ten silver. Enough to feed a family for a month. Enough that the merchant would remember, would write it down, would recognize the symbol years later in a crowded market.

My hands are shaking.

"Which way did he go?"

"North. Said he was heading to Blackwood, but that was half a year ago. Could be anywhere now."

Blackwood. The name catches in my mind like a thorn. There's something there, some significance I can't quite grasp. Aldric's maps, late-night conversations about old ruins—

The Blackwood Monastery. Burned years ago, right around the time—

"What's your name?" the merchant asks gently.

"Solaire."

His expression shifts. "He mentioned that name. Said his son was called Solaire."

The world tilts.

I thank him. Don't remember what I say. Give him coin for the information—too much, probably. He presses a scrap of paper into my hand: the page from his journal with the description, the location, the message. Evidence. Proof that I'm not imagining this.

I walk through the market in a daze. Find Garicus near the salt pork barrels, arguing with a vendor about preservation methods. He sees my face and stops mid-sentence.

"What happened?"

"Nothing. I—nothing."

"Solaire." He steps closer, voice low. "What happened?"

I should tell him. We don't keep secrets. Haven't in fifteen years. But something stops me—the look in his eyes, already wary, already afraid. Like he knows whatever I'm about to say will change everything.

"Can we talk? Somewhere private?"

He nods once. We collect our purchases, load them onto the cart Aldric sent with us, and walk to the edge of town where the old well stands. No one comes here—the water went bad years ago, and they built a new well closer in. It's quiet. Safe.

I tell him everything.

The merchant. The description. The symbol. The message. The possibility—impossible, insane, but there nonetheless—that my father survived.

Garicus listens without interrupting. His face goes through a series of expressions I can't read. When I finish, silence stretches between us like a chasm.

"It's not real," he says finally.

"The merchant saw him—"

"The merchant saw someone. Not your father."

"He knew my name. He wore the symbol."

"Scavengers pick through ruins all the time. Could have taken the medallion from a body. Could be a con artist who heard about the raid, figured he'd—"

"Why would a con artist wait fifteen years? Why would he—"

"I don't know!" Garicus's voice cracks like a whip. "But your father is dead, Solaire. They're all dead. We were there. We saw."

"We saw ruins. We saw bodies. We didn't see everyone."

"Who survives a massacre and doesn't come back for their child?"

The question lands like a blow. I don't have an answer. Can't imagine a reason good enough. But the merchant's words echo: _I thought you were dead. I looked for you._

"Maybe he looked in the wrong places," I say quietly. "Maybe he thought I died. Maybe—"

"Maybe he abandoned you." Garicus's eyes are hard. "Maybe he ran when the raiders came and left you to burn. Maybe finding out he's alive is worse than thinking he's dead."

"You don't know that."

"Neither do you." He grabs my shoulders, and there's something desperate in his grip. "Listen to me. Whoever that man is, whatever he wants—it's not worth it. You have a life here. You have the Order. You have—"

"You." I finish for him. "I know. But Garicus, what if it's true? What if he's alive and I do nothing?"

"What if it's true and you go looking and he's exactly who I think he is—someone who left you to die?" His voice drops to almost a whisper. "What if you find him and it breaks you?"

I want to argue. Want to insist I'm stronger than that. But the fear in Garicus's eyes isn't for me finding the truth. It's for what happens after.

He's afraid of losing me.

The realization sits heavy in my chest.

"I have to know," I say. "I'm sorry. I know you think it's a mistake. But I have to."

"Then I'm coming with you."

"Garicus—"

"No." His grip tightens. "Fifteen years ago we made a promise. Whatever comes. However long it takes. You're not doing this alone."

I search his face. Find only stubborn loyalty and bone-deep dread.

"Okay," I say. "Together."

He nods. Doesn't let go.

We stand there as the sun sinks lower, two brothers bound by a promise made in ashes, facing the possibility that everything we thought we knew was a lie. The Order will want us back soon. Aldric will ask where we've been. We'll have to decide whether to tell him.

But for now, it's just us and the silence and the terrible, fragile hope that maybe—maybe—the dead aren't as dead as we thought.

And maybe that's the worst possibility of all.

---

Later, after we've returned to the Keep and unloaded supplies and endured Aldric's questions about why we took so long, I lie in my bed and stare at the ceiling.

Garicus is in his own bed across the room. We stopped sharing years ago, once we grew too big to fit. But on nights when the nightmares get bad, I still hear him cross the space between us. Still feel the weight of his hand on my shoulder, anchoring me to the waking world.

Tonight he doesn't move. Just lies there, breathing carefully, pretending to sleep.

I pull the medallion over my head. Study it in the moonlight. The compass rose. The inscription I can't read. All this time I wore it as a memory of the dead.

What if it was a beacon for the living?

_I thought you were dead. I looked for you._

"Solaire." Garicus's voice cuts through the dark.


"Yeah?"

"Promise me something."

"Anything."

"When we find him—if we find him—promise me you won't believe him immediately. Promise me you'll question, you'll doubt, you'll make him prove he is who he says he is."

I close my eyes. "I promise."

"And promise me that no matter what he says, no matter what truth he offers—you'll remember who was there when you woke up in the ruins. Who stayed."

The words hang in the dark like an accusation. Or a plea.

"I remember," I say. "I'll always remember."

"Good." His breathing evens out. Real sleep this time, or close enough.

I hold the medallion tight and wonder if finding the truth will feel like salvation or betrayal.

And I wonder if there's any difference between the two.

---




# Chapter 6: Ashes and Truth

The investigations started small.

A question here, a bribe there. Merchants who traveled the northern routes, innkeepers who remembered faces, scribes who kept records of travelers passing through. Weeks slipped by in fragments—mornings spent training with Garicus, afternoons running errands for the Order, evenings walking dusty roads to taverns where strangers might know something I didn't.

I told myself it was curiosity. Just wanting to know if the merchant's words held truth—if somewhere, impossibly, my father still drew breath.

I didn't tell Garicus.

That was the first betrayal. Small, but it counted. We'd shared everything for fifteen years. Every scar, every nightmare, every secret thought. But this... this was mine. A fragile, desperate thing I couldn't bear to expose to his pragmatic gaze.

He'd ask why it mattered. Why I needed to know. And I didn't have an answer that wouldn't sound like I was abandoning what we'd built together.

So I kept quiet and kept searching.

Three weeks into my investigation, I found a priest who remembered a man matching the description. Tall, hollow-eyed, wearing robes that marked him as clergy from the Ashen Reach. The priest had seen him at a monastery months ago, praying for hours in the ruins.

"What monastery?" I asked.

"Blackwood. Burned twenty years past. Nothing there now but ghosts and ash."

I paid him and left before he could ask why I cared.

That night I lay awake in the room I shared with Garicus, listening to his steady breathing, memorizing the location on mental maps. Blackwood Monastery. Two days' ride northwest. I could make an excuse—scouting mission, personal errand, anything. Just go and see. Confirm it was nothing.

Confirm my father was still dead, like he'd been for fifteen years.

Before I could plan further, the letter arrived.

---

It came tucked into a supply shipment, wedged between sacks of grain like it belonged there. No seal. No marking except my name on the outside in careful, slanted script.

I opened it in the stables where no one would see.

*Solaire,*

*I know you've been searching. I know what you've heard. It's true.*

*Meet me where the old walls still stand, where we used to sing before the fires came. Three days hence, at sunset. Come alone.*

*I will explain everything.*

*—Theron*

My hands shook. The paper crumpled in my grip before I smoothed it out, read it again. And again.

Theron. My father's name. A name I barely remembered, pulled from fragments of lullabies and half-dreams. The merchant had been right. He was alive.

And he knew I was looking for him.

The implications crashed over me in waves. How long had he known I survived? Why hadn't he come for me? What truth could possibly justify fifteen years of silence?

I needed to go. Had to go.

But first, I had to tell Garicus.

---

I found him in the armory, sharpening his blade with methodical precision. The rasp of whetstone on steel filled the small room. He didn't look up when I entered.

"You've been distracted," he said. Statement, not question.

"I know."

"Going to tell me why?"

I closed the door. Sat on the bench opposite him. The words stuck in my throat.

"Solaire." He set the blade aside, met my eyes. "Whatever it is, just say it."

"My father is alive."

The silence stretched like a drawn bowstring. Garicus stared at me, expression unreadable.

"How do you know?"

"A merchant recognized my medallion. Said a man was asking about survivors from Thornhaven. I've been... I've been looking into it. Asking around." The words came faster now, spilling out. "I found people who'd seen him. A priest who talked to him. And today I got a letter. He wants to meet me. Three days from now."

"Where?"

"Blackwood Monastery."

Garicus stood abruptly, paced to the window. His jaw worked like he was chewing words too bitter to speak.

"You're not going," he said finally.

"Garicus—"

"You're not going." He turned, and the fury in his eyes made me flinch. "You think this is real? You think after fifteen years, your father just decides to show up and explain himself? It's a trap, Solaire. Or worse, it's him trying to ease his guilt before he dies."

"You don't know that."

"I know that anyone who abandoned their child to burn doesn't deserve a second chance."

The venom in his voice cut deeper than any blade. "He might have had a reason—"

"There is no reason!" Garicus slammed his fist against the wall. The impact echoed. "We made a promise, Solaire. To each other. Not to dead fathers or convenient letters. To each other."

"I remember the promise."

"Do you? Because it feels like you're about to break it."

"I'm not breaking anything." My own anger rose to match his. "I'm going to meet him. That's all. One conversation. Then I'll know."

"Know what? That he's a coward? That he let you think you were alone for fifteen years while he was out there somewhere, alive and free?" Garicus shook his head. "You already know that, Solaire. You just don't want to admit it."

"He's my father."

"I'm your brother."

The words hung between us like a blade waiting to fall.

Garicus took a breath. When he spoke again, his voice was quieter. Harder to hear because it wasn't anger anymore—it was pain.

"Don't go. Please. Whatever he has to say, it won't change what happened. It won't give you back what you lost. It'll just hurt more."

I wanted to agree. Wanted to tear up the letter and pretend it never came. Stay here with Garicus and let the past stay buried.

But I couldn't.

"I have to know," I said. "I'm sorry. I have to."

Garicus looked at me for a long moment. Then he turned away, picked up his sword, and went back to sharpening it.

"Then go," he said. "But don't expect me to follow you into it."

I left him there, the sound of whetstone on steel following me down the corridor like an accusation.

---

The ride to Blackwood took two days, just as I'd calculated. I went alone despite Garicus's warning, despite Aldric's suspicious questions when I requested leave, despite the voice in my head that sounded disturbingly like common sense.

The monastery revealed itself gradually through the trees—blackened stone walls overgrown with ivy, a collapsed bell tower, windows that gaped like empty eye sockets. The fire that destroyed it had been thorough. Only the bones remained.

I dismounted at the gate, tied my horse to a tree, and walked into the ruins.

Sunset painted everything gold and red. Promises in ash, I thought, and almost laughed at the bitter poetry of it.

"Solaire."

The voice came from behind me. Deep. Unfamiliar and yet somehow known, like a song half-remembered from childhood.

I turned.

A man stood in the shadow of the ruined chapel. Tall, just as the merchant described. Priest's robes, gray and travel-worn. But it was his face that stopped my breath—older than I'd imagined, lined with grief and sleepless nights, but unmistakably the face from my fragments of memory.

My father.

Theron.

He stepped into the light. His hands were empty, held palm-up like he was showing me he bore no weapons. The gesture was so vulnerable it hurt to look at.

"You came," he said.

"You asked me to."

"I wasn't sure you would. Wasn't sure you should." He took another step closer. Stopped when I tensed. "You look like your mother."

The words were a knife between my ribs. "Don't."

"I'm sorry. I—" He stopped. Started again. "I don't know how to do this."

"Then why did you ask me here?"

"Because you deserve the truth. Because I've been a coward for fifteen years and I can't—I can't keep living with the silence." His voice cracked. "I need you to know what happened. Even if you hate me for it. Especially if you hate me for it."

I wanted to tell him I didn't care. That whatever truth he'd carried all these years didn't matter anymore. But the lie wouldn't come.

"Tell me," I said.

He nodded. Walked to a fallen pillar and sat heavily, like the weight of what he was about to say had already broken something in him.

"Thornhaven was going to die," he began. "One way or another. The question was how many would die with it."

---

He spoke for an hour. Maybe more. I lost track of time as the words poured out of him—confession, justification, grief all tangled together.

The cult. The village elders, desperate after two years of failed harvests and plague. The forbidden texts they'd found promising power in exchange for sacrifice. Thirteen children under ten years old. Dragon summoning, ancient and catastrophic.

"They'd already taken three," Theron said. His hands shook. "Three children. Killed them in secret, claimed illness or accident. I found the altar. The symbols. The blood. I confronted the elders and they—" He stopped. Swallowed. "They told me I'd be next if I spoke. That my family would burn if I didn't help them finish the ritual."

"So you ran," I said. My voice sounded distant, not mine.

"I ran to the Order of the Crimson Vigil. Told them everything. Begged them to stop it." He looked at me, and I saw the desperation there—still fresh, still raw after fifteen years. "They said they'd handle it. I thought that meant arrest the elders, stop the ritual, save the children who were left."

"But they burned the village instead."

"They burned the village instead," he echoed. "Everyone. The guilty and the innocent. The elders who planned it and the families who knew nothing. Your mother." His voice broke completely. "You."

"Why?"

"Containment. That's what they called it. Can't let knowledge of dragon summoning spread. Can't risk cult members escaping to try again elsewhere. Burn it all and let history forget." He looked at his hands like they were covered in blood. "I led them there, Solaire. I gave them the location, the layout, the best time to strike. I thought I was saving people. I killed them instead."

The world tilted. I sat down hard on the rubble, my legs suddenly unable to hold me.

"You didn't know," I said. Testing the words, seeing if they could be true.

"I should have. I trusted the Order to be better than monsters. I was wrong." He raised his head, met my eyes. "I've spent fifteen years trying to atone. Hunting down cult remnants. Saving villages before they turn to darkness. It doesn't matter. Nothing I do will ever balance the scales."

"You thought I was dead."

"For thirteen years, yes. Then I heard rumors of survivors. Two boys, taken as wards. I started asking questions. Found your name on Order records." He smiled, broken and terrible. "You were alive. My son was alive. And I was the reason you were orphaned."

I couldn't speak. Couldn't think past the roaring in my head.

"I'm telling you this because you deserve to know," Theron continued. "Not because I expect forgiveness. Not because I think it makes anything right. Just because the truth is the only thing I have left to give you."

He stood. Took a step back, giving me space.

"I'll go now. You won't see me again unless you want to. I just..." He stopped. His voice dropped to a whisper. "I'm sorry, Solaire. For all of it. I'm so sorry."

He turned to leave.

"Wait."

He stopped.

I stood on shaking legs, faced this man who was my father and a stranger and the architect of everything that shaped me.

"The children," I said. "The ones they were going to sacrifice. Did the Order save them?"

Theron's expression crumpled. "There were no children left to save. The cult had already completed the ritual the night before I reached the Vigil. The dragon came. It destroyed half the village before the elders lost control and it turned on them. Everyone was already dying when the Order arrived."

The ground dropped out from under me.

"So they burned corpses," I said. "Murdered the survivors to cover up their failure."

"Yes."

"And you helped them."

"Yes."

I wanted to scream. To hit him. To demand he explain how any of that was justifiable. But I couldn't find the air.

Theron didn't move. Didn't defend himself. Just stood there, waiting for whatever judgment I'd deliver.

I had none to give.

"Go," I said finally.

He nodded. Turned. Walked away into the gathering dark.

I stood in the ruins of Blackwood Monastery until the stars came out, trying to understand what I'd learned. Trying to reconcile the man who raised me with the man who doomed me. Trying to feel anything other than the hollow ache spreading through my chest.

My father was alive.

My father led the Order to our village.

My father tried to save us and damned us instead.

The truth wasn't freedom. It was another kind of cage.

---

I rode back to Greystone Keep through the night, arriving just before dawn. Garicus was awake, sitting on the wall where we'd made our promise years ago. He saw me coming and stood.

I dismounted. Walked to him. He searched my face, reading what he found there.

"He was there," Garicus said.

"Yes."

"Did he tell you why?"

"Yes."

Garicus waited. When I didn't elaborate, he asked, "Do you want to talk about it?"

"No."

He nodded. Didn't push. Just stood beside me as the sun rose, two brothers watching morning break over a world that made less sense than it did yesterday.

"I'm sorry," I said finally.

"For what?"

"For going. For not listening. For—" My voice cracked. "For thinking the truth would make anything better."

Garicus put his hand on my shoulder. Squeezed once.

"Truth doesn't make things better," he said quietly. "It just makes them true."

We stood there until the training bells rang, calling us to another day of discipline and violence and purpose. Then we walked down together, shoulder to shoulder, and didn't speak of it again.

But something had shifted. Some certainty I'd carried for fifteen years had cracked and would never fully heal.

My father was alive.

And I wished he wasn't.

---




# Chapter 7: The Weight of Silence

I waited three days before seeking Aldric out.

Three days of going through the motions—training with Garicus in careful silence, eating meals I couldn't taste, sleeping in fragments between nightmares that now had my father's face. Three days of carrying Theron's confession like a stone in my chest, growing heavier with each passing hour.

The dragon cult. The ritual. The Order's raid.

My father's words circled endlessly: *I led them there. I thought I was saving people. I killed them instead.*

But there was another question beneath that one, sharper and more urgent: Did Aldric know?

The man who found us in the ashes, who took us in when he could have left us to die, who raised us for fifteen years—did he know what the Order had done? Did he know what Theron had told them?

I needed to hear him say it. Needed to look him in the eye and ask the question I'd been too afraid to voice for a decade and a half.

On the third day, I found him in the chapel.

---

The crimson candles were burning, filling the small stone room with their sweet-sick smell. Aldric knelt at the altar, his head bowed, lips moving in silent prayer. He looked older in the flickering light—the gray in his beard more pronounced, the lines around his eyes deeper.

For a moment, I considered leaving. Coming back later. Never asking at all.

Then I thought of my mother's face, half-forgotten. Of Garicus in the cellar, listening to screams. Of Theron's broken expression when he said *everyone was already dying when the Order arrived*.

I stepped into the chapel. The door closed behind me with a soft click.

Aldric's prayer stopped. He didn't turn.

"Solaire," he said quietly. "I wondered when you'd come."

"You knew I'd been gone."

"You requested three days' leave and came back looking like you'd seen a ghost." He rose slowly, joints creaking, and faced me. His eyes were calm. Knowing. "Of course I knew."

"Did you follow me?"

"No. But I can guess where you went." He studied my face. "You found your father."

Not a question. I answered anyway. "Yes."

"And he told you about Thornhaven."

My breath caught. "You knew. All this time, you knew he was alive."

"I suspected." Aldric moved to one of the benches, sat with the careful movements of a man conscious of old wounds. "The records showed a priest fled the village before the raid. Theron of Thornhaven. Your family name. It wasn't difficult to connect."

"Why didn't you tell me?"

"What would I have said?" His voice was gentle, which somehow made it worse. "Your father survived by betraying your village to the Order? He's spent fifteen years hunting cult remnants because he can't live with what he helped us do? How does that conversation go, Solaire?"

I wanted to rage at him. To demand why he thought keeping secrets was acceptable, why he'd let me grow up thinking I knew the shape of my own past when it was all lies.

Instead, I asked the question that mattered.

"Did you know about the cult? Was the raid justified?"

Silence fell like ash.

Aldric looked at me for a long moment. I watched him make the calculation—saw the exact instant he decided to stop protecting me from the truth.

"Yes," he said. "I knew."

The word landed like a blade.

"Theron came to the Order with evidence," Aldric continued. His voice was steady, factual. "Ritual markings. Testimonies about missing children. Names of the elders involved. He begged us to stop them before they could complete the summoning."

"But they'd already completed it."

"We didn't know that when we rode out." He met my gaze without flinching. "We thought we were preventing a dragon summoning. By the time we arrived, the ritual had been performed the night before. The dragon had already destroyed half the village. The cult lost control. It killed most of them before it was banished."

"So you found a village already dying, and you burned it anyway."

"We found a village where forbidden knowledge had been practiced. Where children had been sacrificed. Where survivors could spread the ritual to others." His hands tightened into fists. "We did what the Order exists to do. We contained it."

"By murdering everyone who was left."

"By preventing the knowledge from spreading!" For the first time, anger sharpened Aldric's voice. "Do you know what happens if dragon summoning becomes common practice? If desperate villages across the realm decide child sacrifice is acceptable for power? We'd have a dozen Thornhavens. A hundred."

"So you killed mine to prevent theoretical ones."

"Yes."

The admission sat between us like a corpse.

I stood there, shaking, trying to reconcile this with the man who taught me to fight, who bandaged my training wounds, who sat with me through nightmares and never once complained. Trying to make sense of how the same hands that raised me had helped destroy everything I came from.

"You were there," I said. The realization came slowly, terrible in its certainty. "In the village. During the raid."

Aldric's face went very still.

"You weren't just investigating after. You were part of it. You were one of the knights who—" My voice cracked. I tried again. "Who burned my home. Who killed my mother."

"Yes."

The world tilted. I grabbed the back of a pew to steady myself.

"I was part of the raid," Aldric said quietly. "I argued against the wholesale slaughter. Fought with the commander, insisted we could arrest the cult leaders and spare the innocent. I was overruled." He looked at his hands. "So I followed orders. And when we found you and Garicus in the ruins the next morning, I took you in."

"Out of guilt."

"Out of guilt," he agreed. "And love. And the desperate hope that I could make something right out of what we'd done wrong."

I laughed, bitter and broken. "You raised us on their graves. You took us to the place that trained the knights who killed our families, and you made us just like you."

"I made you strong enough to survive," Aldric said. "Strong enough to face the truth when it came."

"The truth that you're a murderer? That everything I've been taught, everything I've sworn to uphold, is built on the same lie that killed my village?" I was shouting now, couldn't stop myself. "What truth am I supposed to face, Aldric? That you're no better than the cult you claim to fight?"

"We are better." His voice was steel. "The cult murdered children for power. We stopped them."

"After they'd already succeeded! You didn't stop anything! You just killed the witnesses!"

"And saved every other village that might have heard the story and tried to replicate it!" Aldric stood, met me eye to eye. "Yes, we killed innocents. Yes, it was brutal and ugly and wrong. But the alternative was worse. You want to know what dragon summoning unleashed looks like? I've seen it, Solaire. Cities gone in a night. Thousands dead. That ritual in your village was one of twelve we stopped that year. Twelve villages that thought they could control what they summoned. Only yours succeeded in the casting before we arrived."

"Succeeded in killing themselves, you mean."

"And if even one person had survived with the knowledge? If they'd written it down, taught it to others, spread it like plague?" He shook his head. "We couldn't take that risk."

"So you took my childhood instead."

The words fell like a sentence.

Aldric's anger drained away. He sat back down, suddenly looking every one of his fifty-two years.

"Yes," he said quietly. "I did. And I've lived with that choice every day since."

---

I should have left then. Should have walked away before I said something irreversible. But the questions kept pouring out, toxic and necessary.

"When did you decide?" I asked. "When did you look at us—two terrified children in the ruins—and think, yes, I'll take them in. I'll raise them to serve the Order that destroyed their lives."

"It wasn't like that."

"Then what was it like?"

Aldric was quiet for a long moment. When he spoke, his voice was rough with something that might have been grief.

"I saw you holding Garicus's hand. Both of you covered in ash, eyes hollow, waiting for someone—anyone—to come. And I thought: these boys survived what we did. The least I can do is make sure they don't die of exposure or starvation or the dozen other things that kill orphans." He looked up at me. "I didn't plan to keep you. Didn't plan to train you. I was going to take you to a monastery, or find families to take you in."

"What changed?"

"You did. Both of you." A ghost of a smile crossed his face. "You followed me everywhere. Asked endless questions. Garicus was so determined to be useful he'd practice sword drills until he collapsed. You wouldn't let me out of your sight for weeks. You needed me." His voice dropped. "I needed to be needed. To make something good out of what I'd done."

"We weren't your redemption," I said. "We were people. Children. We deserved the truth."

"When?" Aldric's voice sharpened. "When you were eight? Ten? Should I have sat you down after training and said, 'By the way, I helped kill your parents'? Would that have made it better?"

"It would have let us choose! We could have left, could have—"

"Gone where?" He stood again, and now there was real anger in his face. "To what life? You were orphans of a burned village. No family, no coin, no prospects. I gave you a home. Training. Purpose. A brotherhood with each other that most people never find. Yes, I did it out of guilt. But I also did it out of love, Solaire. I raised you. I taught you. I was proud of you."

"Was?" The word came out smaller than I intended.

Aldric's expression softened. "Am proud of you. Even now, when you're looking at me like I'm a monster. I'm proud that you have the strength to ask these questions. That you refuse to accept easy answers."

"There are no easy answers," I said bitterly. "There's just you, and what you did, and the fact that I can't—" My voice broke. "I can't reconcile it. The man who raised me and the man who destroyed my village. They can't both be you."

"But they are." He spread his hands. "I'm both, Solaire. The knight who follows orders and the man who regrets them. The teacher who loves his wards and the killer who gave them cause to become orphans. I can't separate those parts. I've tried."

I stood there, shaking, staring at this man I'd called father in all but name. Trying to find anger strong enough to burn away the tangled mess of love and betrayal.

I couldn't.

"I need to go," I said.

"Solaire—"

"I need to go." I walked toward the door, each step feeling like wading through deep water. "I can't... I can't be here right now."

Aldric didn't try to stop me. Just stood there in the candlelight, looking old and tired and broken.

At the door, I stopped. Didn't turn around.

"Did you love us?" I asked. "Truly? Or was it always guilt?"

The silence stretched so long I thought he wouldn't answer.

Then: "Both. Always both. I'm sorry, Solaire. I wish I could give you a better answer. But it's the only truth I have."

I left him there.

---

The Keep was quiet as I walked its corridors. Training had ended for the day. Most of the knights were at dinner or in their quarters. I moved through the familiar passages like a ghost, seeing them differently now.

This place raised me. Shaped me. Made me into a weapon.

The same place that created the knights who burned Thornhaven.

I found myself at the outer wall without consciously choosing the destination—the spot where Garicus and I had reaffirmed our oath years ago. Where we'd promised that no matter what the Order demanded, we belonged to each other first.

Someone was already there.

Garicus stood with his back to me, looking out over the forest. He didn't turn when I approached, but his shoulders shifted—acknowledgment without words.

"You talked to Aldric," he said.

"How did you know?"

"You have that look. Like someone told you the sky is actually the ground and you can't figure out which way is up." He glanced at me. "What did he say?"

I wanted to tell him everything. Wanted to pour it all out—the cult, the dragon, the raid, Aldric's guilt and justifications and impossible choices. The words crowded in my throat.

"He knew," I managed. "About my father. About the cult. About everything."

"And?"

"He was there, Garicus. During the raid. He helped burn the village. Then he found us and decided to..." I couldn't finish.

"To take us in," Garicus finished quietly. "Out of guilt."

"You already knew."

He shook his head. "Suspected. Little things over the years. The way he'd change the subject when we asked about the raid. How he always had answers about Order protocol but never about that specific mission." He turned to face me fully. "I didn't want to know for certain. Easier to just... accept what we had."

"How can you accept this?" I demanded. "He destroyed our lives and then raised us to serve the people who did it!"

"He also kept us alive." Garicus's voice was calm. Too calm. "Fed us. Trained us. Gave us each other. Yes, it was built on blood and guilt. But it's still what we have."

"That's not enough."

"Isn't it?" He studied my face. "What's the alternative, Solaire? Leave the Order? Hunt down every knight who participated? Kill Aldric for his honesty?"

"I don't know!" The words ripped out of me. "I don't know what I'm supposed to do with this. I don't know how to keep serving an Order that murdered innocents. I don't know how to trust Aldric when every kindness was paid for with my mother's life. I don't know—"

My voice broke. The trembling I'd been suppressing for days finally overwhelmed me. I grabbed the stone wall for support, breathing hard, trying not to fall apart completely.

Garicus's hand landed on my shoulder. Steady. Anchoring.

"I know," he said quietly. "I don't have answers either. But I know we're still here. Still together. That still means something."

"Does it? When everything else is a lie?"

"It's not a lie." His grip tightened. "Our oath. Our brotherhood. That was never about Aldric or the Order. It was about us. That's still real, Solaire. Maybe it's the only thing that is."

I wanted to believe him. Wanted to hold onto that certainty the way I had when we were eight years old and making promises in the ash. But the foundation had cracked, and I couldn't see how to rebuild it.

"What do we do?" I whispered.

Garicus was quiet for a long time. When he spoke, his voice was heavy with something I couldn't name.

"We survive," he said. "Same as always. We figure out what we believe, what we can live with. And we do it together."

"And Aldric?"

"I don't know." He let his hand fall. "Maybe we forgive him eventually. Maybe we don't. But right now, we just... survive knowing what we know."

It wasn't enough. Wasn't close to enough. But it was all we had.

We stood there as the sun set, two brothers whose entire world had shifted beneath them, trying to find solid ground. Below us, the Keep continued its rhythms—dinner bells, changing guard, the everyday machinery of the Order we'd sworn to serve.

The Order that killed our families.

The Order that gave us each other.

I didn't know which truth mattered more. Didn't know if there was a right answer, or if moral clarity was just another comfortable lie we told ourselves.

I only knew that Aldric's admission had broken something in me that would never fully heal.

And that Garicus was still standing beside me, solid and real and here, even when everything else had turned to ash and questions.

Maybe that would be enough.

Maybe it would have to be.

---

That night, I couldn't sleep.

I lay in my bed, staring at the ceiling, listening to Garicus's breathing across the room. Even and deep. He'd always been able to sleep through anything—a skill learned in the cellar, waiting for morning.

I envied him.

My mind kept circling back to Aldric's face in the chapel. The resignation there. The guilt he'd carried for fifteen years, thinking he could atone by raising us right. By teaching us to be better than the order that destroyed our lives.

By making us into the very thing we should have feared.

The worst part was understanding it. Seeing the logic, the impossible choice he'd faced. If dragon summoning spread, how many villages would burn? How many children would die?

Preventive justice. The greater good. All the justifications that made murder sound like mercy.

I'd heard them all in Aldric's voice when we executed Elara.

And now I knew where they'd learned to speak that way.

A sound pulled me from my thoughts. Soft footsteps in the corridor outside. They paused at our door, and I knew without seeing that it was Aldric—checking on us the way he had when we first arrived, terrified children who woke screaming in the night.

The footsteps moved on.

I closed my eyes and tried to find sleep. Tried not to think about the fact that I still loved him, despite everything. That I hated him, despite everything.

That both truths could exist at once, and neither one made the other less real.

Dawn was hours away, but I waited for it anyway.

Waited for morning, and the choices it would bring.

# Chapter 8: Blood and Duty

I count the days since Solaire came back from Blackwood.

Seven.

Seven days of watching him move through training like a ghost piloting a corpse. Seven days of stilted conversations and careful silences. Seven days of pretending I don't know exactly what broke inside him when he met his father.

He won't tell me. Not the details. Just that Theron confirmed everything—the cult, the ritual, the Order's response. That Aldric knew. Has always known.

And yesterday, Solaire confronted him.

I wasn't there. Solaire went alone, found Aldric in his quarters after evening meal, and they spoke for two hours behind a closed door. When Solaire emerged, his face was ash-pale and his hands were shaking. He walked past me without a word, went to our room, and hasn't spoken since.

Aldric, for his part, looks ten years older. I saw him this morning in the training yard, watching the younger initiates with the expression of a man attending his own funeral.

He knows I'm coming. Has to know. If Solaire confronted him, I will too. That's how it works—we're a matched pair, two halves of the same oath. What one does, the other follows.

Except this time, I'm not following to support. I'm following to understand.

Because unlike Solaire, I don't need to know if the raid was justified. I need to know why Aldric lied about it for fifteen years.

---

I find him where I knew I would: the chapel. Same place we swore our oaths eight years ago. The crimson candles are burning—fat rendered from executed mages, Aldric told us once when we asked. A reminder that every flame casts shadows.

He doesn't turn when I enter. Just stands before the altar, hands clasped behind his back, staring at the candles like they hold answers.

"Garicus." His voice is quiet. Resigned. "I wondered when you'd come."

"Did you." Not a question. I close the door behind me. The chapel suddenly feels very small.

"You and Solaire—you've never been far apart. Not in any way that matters." He finally turns to face me. The candlelight carves deep shadows under his eyes. "He came to me with questions. I assume you have your own."

"Different questions," I say.

"I'm listening."

I walk closer. Stop three paces from him. Close enough to see the lines grief has cut into his face, far enough to maintain the distance this conversation requires.

"How long have you known?" I ask. "About Theron. About why Thornhaven burned."

"Since the beginning." No hesitation. No attempt to soften it. "I was part of the raiding party. Twenty knights, sent to contain a dragon summoning ritual. We arrived too late to stop it, too early to save anyone."

My hands curl into fists. I force them open.

"Theron led you there."

"Yes. He'd been the village priest. Found evidence of the cult, came to us for help. We promised intervention. We delivered annihilation." Aldric's mouth twists. "Standard procedure for containment."

"And the survivors? Me and Solaire?"

"Unintentional. We swept the village twice, found no one. You were hidden well. By the time we left, you'd already emerged." He pauses. "I found you in the square, holding each other. Two boys in the ashes, making promises they were too young to understand."

The memory surfaces before I can stop it: Solaire's hand in mine, cold and small. The oath. _Never let each other die alone._

"Why did you take us?" The question comes out harder than I intended.

"Because I couldn't leave you." Simple. Final. "The others wanted to. Said orphans complicate missions, that the Order isn't a charity. I overruled them."

"Out of guilt."

"Out of guilt," he confirms. "We'd taken everything from you. The least I could do was offer you something in return."

"You offered us service to the organization that murdered our families." I hear the rage building in my voice, don't bother controlling it. "You trained us to become the very thing that destroyed us."

"Yes."

The admission lands like a blow. No justification. No defense. Just acknowledgment.

"You raised us on their graves," I say. The words taste like ash. "Did you think we'd never ask why?"

Aldric closes his eyes. When he opens them again, they're red-rimmed but dry. "I hoped you wouldn't. That you'd be strong enough to survive without ever learning the truth. That the life I gave you would be enough to balance what was taken." He laughs, bitter and brief. "I was a fool."

"You were a coward."

The word hangs between us. Aldric doesn't flinch.

"Yes," he says again. "I was. I am. I took you in to ease my conscience and told myself it was mercy. I trained you to fight the darkness and never told you I was part of it. I made you swear oaths to an Order that deals in preventive murder and called it justice." He meets my eyes. "I am everything you think I am, Garicus. And I'm sorry."

I want the apology to feel hollow. Want to dismiss it as too little, too late. But there's something in his voice—not just regret, but genuine anguish—that makes it impossible.

"Were they guilty?" I ask. "The villagers. The cult. Were they actually going to summon a dragon?"

"They'd already summoned one." Aldric's voice drops. "By the time we arrived, the ritual was complete. The dragon had manifested, killed thirteen children and half the village, then turned on the cultists when they lost control. We found corpses and survivors in roughly equal measure."

"So you killed the survivors."

"We executed the cult members and silenced the witnesses. Yes."

"My mother—was she—"

"I don't know." And that might be the worst answer. "We didn't ask questions. Didn't check innocence or guilt. Everyone left alive became a threat to contain. So we contained them."

The chapel walls seem to close in. I force myself to breathe.

"Why?" The question breaks on the way out. "If the dragon was already dead, if the ritual failed, why burn everything? Why not just arrest the cultists? Why murder everyone?"

"Because knowledge is contagion." Aldric speaks like he's reciting doctrine. "The texts they used, the ritual components, the witnesses who saw dragon summoning work—all of it had to be destroyed. Can't risk it spreading. Can't let desperate villages in other regions think forbidden magic might save them." He gestures at the candles. "We burn it all and let the ashes scatter. That's what the Order does."

"That's what you did to us."

"Yes."

I want to hit him. Want to draw my sword and make him defend himself, make him fight back, make him be something other than this broken man accepting blame like penance.

I don't move.

"Did you know?" I ask. "When you took us in. When you taught us to fight. When you made us swear oaths. Did you know we'd find out eventually?"

"I suspected." He runs a hand over his face. "Secrets like that don't stay buried. Not when the people carrying them get close. I trained you hoping you'd be strong enough to survive the truth when it came."

"Strong enough to forgive you, you mean."

"No." The word is sharp. Certain. "I never expected forgiveness. I trained you to survive losing me. Because I knew when the truth came out, I'd lose both of you."

The admission sits heavy in the air.

"Solaire already hates you," I say.

"I know."

"He thinks the Order is evil. Thinks what you did was murder."

"It was."

"He's questioning everything. His oaths. His service. Whether any of it means anything."

Aldric nods slowly. "That's the difference between you and him. Always has been."

I frown. "What difference?"

"Solaire needs to believe in the righteousness of what we do. Needs the work to be just, the cause to be pure." Aldric looks at me with something that might be understanding. "You don't. You never have. You believe in pragmatism. In doing what's necessary to survive."

"You don't know what I believe."

"I've watched you for fifteen years, Garicus. I know exactly what you believe." He steps closer. "You believe in Solaire. In protecting him. In keeping your promise. Everything else is secondary. The Order, justice, even truth—none of it matters as much as that oath you made in the ashes."

I want to deny it. Can't.

"You're right," I say. "About that part."

"Then let me ask you something." Aldric's voice is careful. Probing. "Knowing what you know now—would you change it? Would you trade the life I gave you, the training, the purpose, to go back and die in Thornhaven with the others?"

The question is a knife between the ribs.

"No," I admit.

"Would you trade it to have been found by someone else? Raised differently?"

I think about Solaire. About the promise. About fifteen years of fighting side by side, sleeping in the same room, knowing someone has my back absolutely and without question.

"No," I say again.

"Then what are you angry about?"

The question stops me cold.

What am I angry about?

Not the raid. I understand why it happened—can see the logic, however brutal. A village practicing dragon summoning is a threat. Containment makes sense even if the method was monstrous.

Not being taken in. However guilty Aldric's motives, he gave us a life when we had nothing. Trained us. Fed us. Made us into something other than victims.

Not even the Order's methods. I've seen enough in five years to know that mercy is a luxury and preventive justice, while ugly, prevents worse outcomes.

So what's left?

The lie.

"You never told us," I say quietly. "Fifteen years, and you never gave us the chance to choose. You decided we were better off not knowing. You made that choice for us."

"Yes."

"Like we were children."

"You were children."

"We're not anymore." My voice rises. "We haven't been for years. But you kept lying. Kept pretending you were just a knight who found orphans, not the man who created them."

Aldric's expression cracks. Just for a moment, but I see it—the guilt he's been carrying, the weight of fifteen years of deception.

"You're right," he says. "I should have told you. Years ago. Should have trusted you with the truth."

"Why didn't you?"

"Because I'm a coward." Simple. Devastating. "Because telling you meant watching you look at me the way Solaire looked at me yesterday. The way you're looking at me now. And I—" His voice breaks. "I couldn't bear it."

The admission is so raw, so honest, that I have to look away.

We stand in silence. The candles gutter. Somewhere in the keep, a bell rings—change of watch, or evening meal, or some other marker of time passing while we stand here among the ruins of trust.

"What happens now?" I ask finally.

"That's up to you." Aldric straightens. "If you want to leave the Order, I'll help you. Both of you. Falsify records, provide coin, whatever you need to start over somewhere the Crimson Vigil won't find you."

"You'd do that?"

"I owe you that much. More than that."

I consider it. Leaving. Taking Solaire and walking away from Greystone, from the oaths, from the man who raised us on lies and blood. Starting fresh somewhere no one knows our names.

It's tempting.

But.

"Solaire's changing," I say. "Meeting his father, learning the truth—it's breaking something in him. Something I don't know how to fix."

Aldric nods. "Solaire wants the world to make sense. Wants good and evil to be clear, choices to have right answers. Learning that his father tried to do good and caused horror anyway—that the Order is both savior and destroyer—it conflicts with how he sees the world."

"And me?"

"You've always known the world doesn't make sense. That's your strength." Aldric's eyes are sharp now. Assessing. "But it's also your weakness. Because you're so focused on survival, on keeping your promise to Solaire, that you don't see what's happening."

"Which is?"

"He's pulling away. Not from you—never from you. But from everything else. The Order. The oaths. The life we built." Aldric pauses. "If you're not careful, you'll lose him to his own disillusionment."

The fear that's been gnawing at my gut for seven days crystallizes into certainty.

He's right.

Solaire is changing. Has been since he got that letter. Since he met his father. Since he learned that the people who saved us also damned us.

And I don't know how to pull him back.

"What do I do?" The question comes out smaller than I intended.

"Be there." Aldric's voice softens. "That's all you've ever been able to do. Be there when he needs you. Remind him what matters. Don't let him drown in the questions."

"What if the questions are bigger than the answers?"

"Then you hold him while he drowns and make sure he knows he's not alone." Aldric meets my eyes. "That's what you promised him, isn't it? Never alone. No matter what."

The oath. Always the oath.

"I hate you," I say. Not loud. Not angry. Just true. "For lying. For taking us. For making us into this."

"I know."

"But I understand why you did it."

"I know that too."

"That doesn't mean I forgive you."

Aldric nods. "I don't expect you to."

I turn to leave. Stop at the door.

"Aldric."

"Yes?"

"If Solaire wants to leave—if he can't stay in the Order after this—I'm going with him."

"I know."

"And if that means walking away from you, from everything you taught us—"

"I know," Aldric says again. His voice is quiet. Resigned. "I've always known that, Garicus. You made your choice fifteen years ago in the ashes. I was just borrowing you. Both of you. Until you decided you didn't need me anymore."

I should feel vindicated. Should feel like I've won something.

I just feel tired.

"Solaire needs time," I say. "Don't push him. Don't ask him to make decisions yet."

"I won't."

"And if you lie to us again—"

"I won't." Aldric's voice is steel. "You have my word. No more secrets. No more lies. Whatever you ask me, I'll answer truthfully."

"Even if the truth destroys us?"

"Especially then." He smiles, sad and exhausted. "I've learned my lesson, Garicus. Lies only delay the destruction. Truth at least gives you a chance to build something after."

I nod once. Leave.

The corridor outside the chapel is cold. Empty. I walk through it without seeing, my mind caught on Aldric's words.

_He's pulling away._

Seven days of silence. Seven days of Solaire moving through the world like he's not quite present. Seven days of watching my brother disappear into questions I can't answer.

I find him on the wall. Our wall. The place where we reaffirmed our oath years ago. He's sitting with his legs dangling over the edge, staring at nothing.

"Hey," I say.

He doesn't turn. "Hey."

I sit beside him. For a long time, neither of us speaks.

"I talked to Aldric," I finally say.

"I figured."

"He told me everything. The raid. Why he took us. Why he lied."

Solaire nods. His face is unreadable.

"Are you angry?" he asks.

"Yes."

"At him?"

"At all of it." I lean back against the stone. "At Aldric for lying. At your father for leading the Order there. At the cult for starting this whole mess. At the universe for burning our village and thinking that was fair."

"But you understand why Aldric did it."

It's not a question. Solaire knows me too well.

"Yeah," I admit. "I understand."

"I don't." His voice is small. Lost. "I keep trying. Keep thinking about what he said—containment, prevention, the greater good. And logically, I see it. But emotionally—" He stops. Swallows hard. "I can't reconcile the man who raised me with the man who killed my mother."

"They're the same man," I say. "People are complicated. They can be both."

"That's what terrifies me." Solaire finally looks at me. His eyes are red. "If Aldric can be both savior and murderer, if my father can be both victim and accomplice—how do I know who I am? How do I know if the oath I swore to the Order means anything? How do I know if I'm doing good or just becoming another person who justifies atrocities?"

The questions hang in the air, huge and unanswerable.

"I don't know," I say.

"That's not helpful."

"I know." I reach over, grab his hand. "But I know one thing. The oath we made to each other—that one's real. That one matters. Whatever else is complicated, that's simple."

Solaire's fingers tighten on mine. "What if I want to leave? Walk away from the Order, from Greystone, from all of this?"

My heart stutters. But I keep my voice steady.

"Then we leave together."

"Garicus—"

"No." I turn to face him fully. "Fifteen years ago we promised never to let each other die alone. That promise doesn't have conditions. Doesn't depend on where we are or who we serve. You leave, I follow. That's how it works."

"That's not fair to you."

"I don't care about fair." I squeeze his hand. "I care about keeping you alive. Keeping us together. That's all I've ever cared about."

Solaire's face crumples. He pulls his hand free, presses it to his eyes.

"I don't know what to do," he whispers. "I'm so angry. At Aldric. At my father. At the Order. At myself for not seeing it sooner. I don't know how to make it make sense."

"Then don't." I put my arm around his shoulders. "Don't try to make sense of it. Just... survive it. One day at a time. And when you figure out what you want, we'll do it. Together."

He leans against me. Small. Broken. Nothing like the confident knight who left for Blackwood seven days ago.

"I'm scared," he admits.

"Of what?"

"Of losing you. Of this changing us. Of not being able to go back to how things were."

I close my eyes. Feel the weight of him against my side, solid and real and still here.

"Things have already changed," I say quietly. "We can't go back. But we can go forward. And wherever that is, I'll be there."

"Promise?"

"I already did. Fifteen years ago." I pull him closer. "Never alone, Sol. No matter what comes next."

We sit like that as the sun sets, two brothers who survived fire and lies and the slow death of certainty. Below us, the keep continues its rhythms. Training. Eating. Sleeping. The Order's heartbeat, steady and unaware.

But up here, on this wall where we once swore to serve, we hold each other and know that oath means nothing compared to the one we made in the ashes.

Aldric was right about one thing: I've always known what I believe.

I believe in Solaire.

Everything else is just noise.

# Chapter 9: The Fracture

## Garicus

I find the letter by accident.

It's tucked inside Solaire's kit bag, the leather satchel he carries on missions. I'm not looking for it—just trying to find the whetstone he borrowed three days ago and hasn't returned. My blade needs sharpening, and I know he has the good one, the Mirean stone he bargained for at Crossroads.

The paper falls out when I lift the bag. Unfolds on the floor between my boots.

I shouldn't read it. Know I shouldn't even as I'm bending to pick it up.

But I do anyway.

*Solaire,*

*Meet me at the oak grove, two miles east of the Keep. Tomorrow at midday. There's more you need to know about the ritual, about what the elders were planning before I intervened.*

*Come alone. The Order has eyes everywhere.*

*—T*

T. Theron.

The paper crumples in my fist before I can stop myself.

He's been meeting him. After everything—after the confession, after the ride back in silence, after I thought he'd realized the truth was poison—he's been meeting him.

How many times? How long has this been going on?

I smooth the letter out. Check the date. Three days ago. The same day Solaire volunteered for a solo patrol. Said he needed space to think. I believed him.

I was a fool.

The door opens. Solaire walks in, still wearing his riding cloak, face flushed from cold or exertion. He sees me. Sees the letter in my hands.

Goes very still.

"Garicus—"

"How long?" My voice sounds strange. Flat. Like all the anger has burned down to ash and this is what's left.

"It's not what you think."

"How. Long."

He closes the door. Doesn't meet my eyes. "Two weeks. Maybe three."

"Three weeks." I hold up the letter. "You've been sneaking off to meet your father for three weeks and you didn't tell me."

"I was going to—"

"When? When were you going to tell me you were choosing him over everything we built?" The anger cracks through now, hot and jagged. "Over the oath? Over us?"

"I'm not choosing him!" Solaire's hands fist at his sides. "I'm trying to understand! There's more to this than what he told me that first night. The cult, the ritual, what the Order did—it's all connected and I need to know how deep it goes!"

"Why?"

The question stops him.

"Why do you need to know?" I step closer. "Will it bring back the dead? Will it change what happened? We survived, Solaire. We made something from the ashes. Why isn't that enough?"

"Because it's built on lies!" His voice rises to match mine. "Everything—the Order, Aldric's training, the oaths we swore—all of it stands on the graves of people who died for no reason! Don't you want to know why?"

"I know why." I throw the letter at his feet. "Evil men made evil choices. The Order tried to stop them and failed. Aldric took us in because he felt guilty. There's your truth. There's your understanding. Does it help?"

Solaire stares at the crumpled paper. "You talked to Aldric."

"Of course I talked to Aldric! You came back from Blackwood looking like the world had ended and wouldn't tell me what happened! So yes, I confronted him. I made him tell me everything—about the cult, about the dragon, about why they burned the village instead of just arresting the guilty." My throat is raw. When did I start shouting? "He told me. All of it. And you know what I realized?"

"What?"

"That it doesn't matter." The words come out hard as stone. "The Order made a choice. A bad choice, maybe. But the cult had already won. The dragon had already come and gone. Everyone was dying anyway. What difference does it make if they burned fast or slow?"

"The difference is intent!" Solaire is shouting now too. "The difference is that the Order chose murder over mercy! Chose covering up their failure over admitting they were too late! Don't you see? We've been serving the same people who killed our families!"

"I see someone desperate for his father's approval." The words are vicious. I know it even as I'm saying them. Can't stop. "I see someone who'd rather believe in conspiracy and injustice than accept that sometimes the world is just cruel and random. Your father failed you, Solaire. He ran instead of fighting. He trusted the wrong people. And now he's trying to make you complicit in that failure by—"

"He's trying to make amends!"

"By poisoning you against the only family you have left!" I grab his shoulders, force him to look at me. "I'm your family! Me! Not some man who abandoned you and spent fifteen years hiding in shame! I was there when you woke up in the ruins! I was there every nightmare, every training session, every mission! Where was he?"

Solaire wrenches free. "You think I don't know that? You think I don't remember?" His eyes are bright with unshed tears. "But he's my father, Garicus. My blood. And he's offering me answers to questions I've had my whole life!"

"And I'm offering you the same thing I've always offered—to stand beside you no matter what. Why isn't that enough anymore?"

The question hangs between us like a sword suspended by thread.

Solaire's face does something complicated. Pain and frustration and something that looks horribly like pity. "Because you're asking me to stay ignorant. To stop questioning. To accept that the people who raised us are the same ones who orphaned us and just... live with that."

"Yes." I don't flinch. "I'm asking you to survive. Because that's what we do. We survive."

"By forgetting?"

"By not letting the past destroy the present!" My hands are shaking. I clench them into fists. "Every moment you spend with Theron is a moment you're not here. Not with me. Not with the life we built. You're choosing ghosts over—"

"Over what? Over you?" Solaire's laugh is bitter. "You keep saying that like I have to choose. Like I can't have both."

"You can't." The certainty in my voice surprises even me. "Because one of us is asking you to move forward and one is dragging you backward into ash and questions that have no good answers. And you're so focused on understanding the why that you can't see you're losing the what."

"What am I losing?"

"Us."

The word falls like an executioner's blade.

Solaire stares at me. "That's not fair."

"It's true." I turn away. Can't look at him anymore. "We swore an oath, Solaire. Never alone. Never apart. But you've been alone for weeks now. Meeting him in secret. Keeping things from me. Breaking the promise a little more each day."

"I was protecting you—"

"From what? The truth? I don't need protecting!" I spin back. "I need my brother. The one who swore we'd face everything together. Where is he? Because all I see now is someone who's already chosen which side of the fracture he's standing on."

Silence. Just our breathing, harsh and uneven.

"I need to understand," Solaire says finally. Quiet. Almost broken. "I need to know if there's something more than just tragedy and bad timing. If there's meaning in what happened. Purpose."

"There's no purpose in massacre, Sol."

"Then what was the point?" His voice cracks. "What was the point of surviving if all we do is serve the people who made us orphans? If we enforce the same ruthless logic that killed our families?"

And there it is. The real question. The one underneath all the others.

I don't have an answer that won't hurt us both.

"The point," I say slowly, "is that we're alive. We have each other. We made something from nothing. That's enough for me. If it's not enough for you..." I stop. Start again. "If you need more than that, then go. Keep meeting your father. Keep searching for meaning in ash. But don't expect me to wait here while you do."

"What are you saying?"

"I'm saying make a choice." The words taste like blood. "Him or me. The past or the present. Because I can't—" My voice breaks. I force it steady. "I can't watch you die slowly from the inside while you chase a truth that will only hurt more."

Solaire's face is white. "You're asking me to abandon my father."

"I'm asking you to remember who was there when he wasn't."

"That's not fair."

"None of this is fair!" I'm shouting again. "You think I want to say this? You think I want to watch everything we built fall apart because some man you barely remember decided his guilt was more important than your peace? But here we are, Sol. You with your letters and secret meetings, me with the certainty that this ends badly no matter what we do. So yes, I'm asking you to choose. Because we can't go on like this."

The silence that follows is so complete I can hear my own heartbeat.

Solaire reaches down. Picks up the crumpled letter. Smooths it out with careful fingers.

"I'm meeting him tomorrow," he says. Doesn't look at me. "At the oak grove. There's something about the ritual he needs to tell me. Something about why the Order really burned the village."

"Solaire—"

"I have to go, Garicus. I have to know."

"And if I ask you not to? If I ask you to stay here with me instead?"

He finally meets my eyes. And what I see there breaks something in my chest I didn't know could break.

"I'm sorry," he whispers. "I'm so sorry. But I have to."

I nod once. Turn away so he won't see my face.

"Then go," I say. "But don't come back expecting things to be the same."

I hear him move toward the door. Hear it open.

"Garicus—"

"Go, Solaire."

The door closes. His footsteps fade down the corridor.

And for the first time in fifteen years, I'm alone in a room that used to be ours.

---

## Solaire

The oak grove is silent when I arrive. Too silent. No birdsong, no wind through the branches—just the oppressive quiet that comes before storms or violence.

Theron is already there, standing beneath the largest oak. He looks older than he did two weeks ago. More worn. But his eyes are clear when they find mine.

"You came," he says.

"You said there was more I needed to know."

He nods. Gestures to a fallen log. We sit like conspirators, which I suppose we are. The Order wouldn't approve of these meetings. Aldric certainly wouldn't. And Garicus—

I push the thought away. Focus on why I'm here.

"Tell me," I say.

Theron takes a breath. "The ritual the elders performed. The dragon summoning. It wasn't random desperation."

"You said they were starving. That the harvests failed."

"They did. But the cult's texts—the forbidden knowledge they used—those didn't appear by accident. Someone gave them to the elders. Someone with deep knowledge of dragon lore and the power to move through villages undetected."

My stomach drops. "The Order."

"A faction within it, yes." His voice is careful. Clinical, like he's reporting rather than confessing. "There are those in the Crimson Vigil who believe that small evils prevent greater ones. That allowing cults to form, to attempt rituals, to fail—it justifies the Order's existence. Proves they're necessary."

"That's insane."

"That's pragmatism." He meets my eyes. "How do you maintain power when the threats you fight become rare? You create new ones. Not directly—nothing so crude. But you leave breadcrumbs. Plant seeds. And when the harvest comes in blood and fire, you're there to clean it up."

I can't breathe. "You're saying the Order deliberately—"

"I'm saying I found evidence that the texts in Thornhaven came from a Vigil archive. That the timing of my report and the arrival of the knights was too precise. That when I told them the ritual had already happened, they didn't seem surprised." His hands clench. "And I'm saying that when I asked Aldric about it years later, he didn't deny it. He just said some choices are too complicated for men like me to understand."

The world tilts.

"Aldric knew."

"Aldric participated." Theron's voice is gentle. Horrible. "He was part of the raid, Solaire. He helped burn the village. And then he took you and Garicus as wards, raised you to serve the same system that orphaned you. That's not guilt. That's grooming."

No. No, that can't be—

But it fits. All of it fits. Aldric's reluctance to talk about the raid. His careful lessons about necessary evils and preventive justice. The way he watched us with that look—not love, but assessment. Like we were experiments.

"Why are you telling me this?" I can barely form words.

"Because you're asking the right questions. Because you deserve to know what you're serving. And because—" He stops. Looks away. "Because I failed you once. I won't fail you again by letting you stay blind."

"What do you want me to do with this?"

"That's your choice." He stands. "I've told you the truth as I understand it. What you do with it—whether you confront Aldric, whether you leave the Order, whether you dismiss it all as the ravings of a guilty father—that's yours to decide."

He starts to walk away.

"Wait." I stand too. "If this is true—if the Order really did this—then there must be others. Other villages. Other setups."

"There are." He turns back. "I've been tracking them for years. Trying to stop the next Thornhaven before it happens. Sometimes I succeed. Sometimes I'm too late. But I keep trying because it's the only thing I can do."

"You're fighting the entire Crimson Vigil."

"I'm fighting a corrupt faction within it." His smile is grim. "Not all knights are complicit. Some genuinely believe in protecting the realm. But the leadership, the inner circle—they're rotten. And they've been rotten for a long time."

I think about Garicus. About the argument this morning. About the choice he forced on me.

"My brother doesn't believe you," I say quietly. "He thinks you're manipulating me. Using my grief to turn me against the Order."

"Is he wrong?"

The question surprises me. "You're telling me the truth."

"I'm telling you what I believe is true. There's a difference." Theron's expression is complicated. "I'm also your father trying to atone for abandoning you. Those motivations can coexist. You should question me, Solaire. Question everything. Especially the people who tell you questioning is dangerous."

Like Garicus. Like Aldric.

"What if questioning breaks everything?" I ask. "What if the truth destroys the only stability I have?"

"Then you'll build something new." He steps closer. "Your brother loves you. Whatever happens between you two, that won't change. But love isn't the same as truth. Sometimes you have to choose which one you can live without."

He leaves then, walking into the trees until the shadows swallow him whole.

I stand in the oak grove until the sun sinks low, turning the sky to ash and ember. Promises in ash. That's what my whole life has been—promises made on burning ground, meant to hold against impossible weight.

The promise to Garicus: never alone, never apart.

The promise to the Order: service until death.

The promise to myself, unspoken but present since Blackwood: to understand what made me, no matter the cost.

They can't all be kept.

---

## Garicus

I don't sleep that night.

I lie in the dark of our room—my room now, maybe, I don't know anymore—and stare at the ceiling. The other bed is empty. Solaire didn't come back after the oak grove. Didn't come back for evening meal. Didn't come back at all.

Maybe he won't.

The thought sits in my chest like a stone. Heavy. Cold. Inevitable.

At some point I must sleep, because I wake to gray dawn light and the sound of the door opening.

Solaire steps inside. He looks haggard. Didn't sleep either, from the shadows under his eyes.

We stare at each other across the space that used to feel small and now seems vast.

"I'm not staying," he says.

The words don't surprise me. I think I knew, on some level, from the moment I found that letter. Knew that whatever Theron was offering him—truth, purpose, vindication—it was stronger than anything I could give.

"Where will you go?"

"I don't know yet. North, maybe. Theron mentioned other villages. Other places the Order might be... operating." He can't quite say the words. Can't quite admit what he's choosing.

"You're leaving the Order."

"I'm leaving Greystone." His voice is careful. "I haven't decided about the Order yet."

"That's a lie." I sit up. "You've already decided. You're just afraid to say it."

"Maybe." He looks at his hands. "I need time, Garicus. Time to think. To figure out what I believe without Aldric in my ear telling me how to think. Without you asking me to choose between—"

"Between us and him. I'm asking you to choose between us and him." I stand. Face him fully. "Don't pretend this is about needing space. You're choosing your father over the oath. Just be honest about it."

"I'm not—" He stops. Tries again. "It's not that simple."

"It's exactly that simple!" My hands fist. "Fifteen years, Solaire. Fifteen years we've had each other's backs. Fifteen years of that promise being the only thing that mattered. And you're throwing it away for a man who wasn't there! For conspiracy theories and maybe-truths and the chance—the chance—that Aldric is some kind of monster!"

"What if he is?"

The question stops me cold.

"What if Aldric really is complicit?" Solaire presses. "What if the Order really did engineer what happened to us? Don't you want to know? Don't you want justice?"

"I want you!" The confession rips out of me before I can stop it. "I don't care about justice or truth or any of it! I care that you're here, that you're safe, that we're together! That's all I've ever cared about!"

Solaire's face crumples. "I know. I know you do. But Garicus, I can't—I can't just ignore this. I can't serve the people who might have murdered our families for political gain. I can't pretend that's acceptable just because they fed us and trained us and gave us purpose!"

"So you'll destroy the only real family you have left chasing ghosts!"

"You'll always be my family." His voice breaks. "But I need to do this. I need to know. And I need you to—I need you to understand that I'm not abandoning you. I'm just... I'm taking a different path for a while."

"How long is 'a while'?"

He doesn't answer. Can't.

Because we both know the truth: once he leaves, there's no coming back. Not really. The fracture has already happened. We're just pretending it hasn't.

"I'm sorry," Solaire whispers. "I'm so sorry."

I want to tell him it's fine. That I understand. That I'll wait however long it takes for him to come back.

But the lies won't come.

"The promise," I say instead. My voice sounds distant. Not mine. "We swore never to let each other die alone. Do you remember?"

"Of course I remember."

"Then you should know you're already breaking it." I meet his eyes. "You're not dying by sword or fire. You're dying from the inside, letting Theron's truth poison everything we built. And I'm here, watching it happen, and I can't stop it. So tell me, Solaire—who's breaking the oath? You for leaving? Or me for not following?"

The question hangs unanswered.

Solaire picks up his pack. The one he must have prepared before he even came back here. He knew he was leaving. Came to say goodbye, not to be convinced.

"I'll send word," he says. "Let you know where I am. What I find."

"Don't." The word is harsh. Final. "If you're leaving, leave. Don't send letters like we're friends keeping in touch. We're brothers, Solaire. We don't get to do this halfway."

His hand tightens on the pack strap. "So this is it? I leave and we're just... done?"

"No." I turn away. Can't watch him go. "You left the moment you chose to believe Theron over me. This is just you admitting it."

I hear him move toward the door. Hear it open.

"Garicus—"

"Go."

"I love you. You know that, right? I love you and this isn't—"

"Go, Solaire. Please."

The door closes.

His footsteps fade.

And for the first time in fifteen years, I'm truly alone.

---

## Solaire

I make it three corridors before I have to stop.

My back hits the cold stone wall and I slide down, knees to chest, breath coming in gasps that might be sobs. Might be the sound of something essential breaking.

I'm doing this. I'm really doing this.

Leaving Garicus. Leaving Greystone. Leaving the only certainty I've had since I was seven years old and woke up in ash.

A figure appears at the end of the corridor. Aldric. Of course. Nothing happens in this Keep without him knowing.

He approaches slowly. Stops a careful distance away.

"You're leaving," he says. Not a question.

"Yes."

"May I ask where you're going?"

"North. Maybe east. I don't know yet."

Aldric nods. Like he expected this. Like he's been waiting for it.

"Your father has been telling you things," he says. "About the Order. About Thornhaven."

I look up sharply. "How did you—"

"I pay attention, Solaire. It's my job." He sits on the floor across from me, grunting with the effort. When did he get old? "He's not entirely wrong."

The admission knocks the breath from me. "What?"

"The Order's methods are... complex. Morally ambiguous at best. I've done things I'm not proud of. Participated in operations that might look, from certain angles, like the very evil we claim to fight." His eyes are tired. Ancient. "Your father sees conspiracy. I see pragmatism. The truth, as usual, lives somewhere in between."

"You knew the texts came from Vigil archives."

"I suspected. Never confirmed."

"You helped burn the village anyway."

"I followed orders. Then I spent fifteen years trying to atone by raising two boys to be better than I was." He leans his head back against the wall. "Whether that's grooming or genuine affection is a question I ask myself every day."

The honesty stuns me. "Why are you telling me this?"

"Because you're leaving regardless. Because Garicus is upstairs breaking apart and someone should understand why." He looks at me. "And because you remind me of myself at your age—convinced that truth mattered more than anything. That if I just understood enough, questioned enough, I could find the answer that made everything make sense."

"Did you?"

"No." The word is flat. Final. "I found complexity. Impossible choices. Good people doing terrible things for understandable reasons. Bad people doing good things for selfish ones. I found the world, Solaire. And it didn't care about my need for moral clarity."

"So I should just accept it? Stop questioning?"

"I'm saying you should count the cost." He stands, offers his hand. I take it. He pulls me up. "Garicus is your brother. Your true brother, forged in the same fire. Whatever truth you find out there, whatever answers you're searching for—they won't keep you warm at night. They won't fight beside you. They won't remember your mother's smile or the way Thornhaven smelled in autumn."

"He will," I finish.

"He will." Aldric squeezes my shoulder once. "Truth is cold comfort, son. Don't trade warmth for it unless you're certain you can survive the freeze."

He leaves then. Walks down the corridor and doesn't look back.

I stand in the empty hallway for a long time. Then I shoulder my pack and walk toward the Keep's gates.

I don't look back either.

## Garicus

Three days pass.

I train. Eat. Sleep. Repeat. The routine holds me together when nothing else does.

Aldric doesn't ask questions. Just adjusts the training roster so I'm working solo instead of partnered. The other knights give me space. They know. Everyone knows.

The one who swore to never be apart is gone.

On the fourth day, I walk to the wall. Our wall. The place where we reaffirmed the oath, where we sat and talked and pretended the world made sense.

I sit alone now. Watch the sun set. The sky turns red and gold—promises in ash, Solaire called it once. Beautiful and already burning.

A raven lands on the wall beside me. Looks at me with one black eye, tilts its head.

"He's not coming back," I tell it.

The raven caws once. Takes flight.

I watch it disappear into the distance and wonder if Solaire is watching the same sunset somewhere. Wonder if he's thinking of me. If he regrets leaving or if the truth he's chasing feels worth the cost.

I wonder if I'll ever stop waiting for him to come home.

The sun sinks below the horizon. Darkness settles over Greystone Keep.

And I sit in it, alone for the first time in fifteen years, learning what it means to survive when the only person you swore to protect chooses to walk away.

The oath is broken.

We're broken.

And there's nothing left to do but learn to live in the fracture.

# Chapter 10: The Choice

Six days.

Six days since Solaire walked out of Greystone Keep and I let him go.

Six days of training alone, eating alone, sleeping in a room that echoes with absence. Six days of telling myself I made the right choice, that forcing him to decide was necessary, that clean breaks heal better than slow tears.

Six days of knowing I was lying to myself.

The messenger arrives on the seventh day.

---

He's young, maybe fifteen. Roadworn and nervous, clutching a sealed letter like it might bite him. He finds me in the armory, where I've been sharpening my blade to a mirror edge for the third time this week.

"Ser Garicus?" His voice cracks. "I was told to deliver this to you personally."

I take the letter. No seal. No marking. Just my name in handwriting I'd recognize anywhere.

The messenger hovers. I flip him a copper without looking at him. "Go."

He goes.

I don't open the letter immediately. Just hold it, feeling the weight of paper and ink and whatever words Solaire thought important enough to send after telling me not to write.

Finally, I break the fold.

*Garicus,*

*I know what I said. I know I shouldn't be writing. But you need to know this.*

*Theron has been tracking another village. Riverton, three days northeast of the Keep. He has evidence of cult activity—symbols, missing children, the same pattern as Thornhaven. He's planning to stop it.*

*He's planning to burn it.*

*I've tried to talk him out of it. Tried to convince him to bring evidence to the Order, to do this properly. But he doesn't trust them. He thinks they're complicit. He thinks the only way to stop it is to do what they did to us.*

*I'm going with him tomorrow. To Riverton. I don't know if I can stop him, but I have to try.*

*I thought you should know. In case I'm wrong. In case he's right. In case this is the last time you hear from me.*

*I'm sorry. For everything. I wish we could have found a different way.*

*—S*

My hands are shaking.

Theron is going to burn another village. And Solaire is going to be there when he does it.

I don't think. Don't hesitate. Just grab my kit, saddle a horse, and ride.

---

Three days northeast at killing pace becomes two and a half. My horse is half-dead by the time I spot smoke on the horizon.

Not the thick black smoke of a village burning. Just campfire smoke, thin and gray. I follow it to a small clearing in the woods, hidden from the main road by dense trees.

Theron's camp.

Three tents, a fire, supplies laid out with military precision. And in the center, a map weighted down with stones, covered in markings and notes.

Solaire sits beside the fire. He looks up when I dismount, and for a moment neither of us speaks.

He's thinner. The six days have carved shadows under his eyes, hollows in his cheeks. He looks like he did at sixteen, right after the first mission. Right after Elara.

"You came," he says finally.

"You asked me to."

"I didn't—" He stops. "The letter was just information. I didn't think you'd—"

"You're about to watch your father burn a village and you thought I wouldn't come?" I tie my horse to a tree. It's shaking, lathered with sweat. I should walk it. Water it. I don't move. "Where is he?"

"Scouting Riverton. Confirming locations." Solaire's hands twist together. "Garicus, I tried to stop him. I told him this was wrong, that he was becoming the thing he hated, but he won't listen."

"Then why are you still here?"

The question lands like a blow. Solaire flinches.

"Because he's my father. Because if I'm not here, there's no one to stop him from—" He gestures helplessly at the map. "From doing this."

I walk to the map. Look down at the careful notations. Riverton's layout. Guard positions. Escape routes. Barrels marked "oil."

It's a raid plan. Methodical. Professional. Exactly how the Order would do it.

"He's going to murder them," I say. Not a question.

"He says he's going to stop the cult."

"By burning everyone who might have knowledge of it. By silencing witnesses. By doing exactly what the Order did to us." I look at Solaire. "Do you hear yourself? This is Thornhaven all over again."

"I know!" His voice breaks. "I know, Garicus. Why do you think I wrote to you? I can't—I can't stop him alone. He's convinced this is necessary. That if he doesn't act, more children will die."

"And you believe him?"

"I don't know what I believe anymore." He stands, faces me fully. "He showed me evidence. Names of children who've gone missing. Symbols carved in the woods around the village. The same texts that were in Thornhaven, showing up in Riverton's church."

"How convenient."

Solaire's eyes flash. "You think he's lying?"

"I think he's seeing what he wants to see. What he needs to see to justify this." I step closer. "Thornhaven destroyed him, Sol. You can see that, right? He's spent fifteen years trying to atone by hunting cults that might not exist, by preventing disasters that are already over. This isn't justice. It's obsession."

"What if you're wrong?" His voice is desperate now. "What if there really is a cult? What if children are dying and we do nothing?"

"Then we investigate. Properly. We bring it to the Order—"

"The Order that might have caused it in the first place?"

"The Order that has resources and authority! Not one broken man with oil and a torch!"

We're shouting now. Old argument, new stakes.

"He's not broken," Solaire says. But his voice wavers. "He's trying to make amends."

"He's trying to make himself feel better. And he's using you to do it."

Footsteps crunch through the underbrush. We both turn.

Theron emerges from the trees. He's dressed for violence—dark clothes, boots good for running, a sword at his hip I didn't see him carrying before. When he sees me, he stops.

"Garicus." His voice is careful. Measuring. "I didn't expect you."

"Clearly." I gesture at the map. "Planning something?"

He doesn't deny it. Just walks to the fire, sits. The movement is casual, but his hand stays near his sword.

"Your brother told you," he says.

"He did."

"And you've come to stop me."

"I've come to stop this." I point at the map. "Whatever you think you're doing, it ends now."

Theron's laugh is bitter. "I'm doing what the Order won't. What they can't, because they're compromised."

"You're about to murder innocents because you think you see a pattern."

"I know I see a pattern." He pulls a leather journal from his coat, tosses it to me. "Three children missing in the last month. All under ten years old. All from families on the outskirts. The village elders claim runaways or animal attacks, but the bodies were never found."

I catch the journal. Don't open it.

"Symbols in the forest," Theron continues. "The same ones I saw in Thornhaven. Dragon summoning circles. Fresh, Garicus. Drawn within the last week."

"Anyone could draw symbols."

"The texts," he says. "I found them in the church. Hidden under the altar. Exact copies of the ones used in Thornhaven." His eyes are fevered. Certain. "This is happening again. Right now. And if we don't stop it—"

"Then we investigate," I cut in. "We confirm. We find actual proof, not paranoid suspicion."

"There's no time!" Theron stands, and now there's real anger in his voice. "The ritual requires thirteen children. They have three. If we wait for your proof, how many more will die?"

"How many will die if you're wrong?" I step closer. "How many innocents will you burn trying to stop a cult that exists only in your head?"

"I am not wrong." Each word is clipped. Final. "I have seen this before. I know how it starts. I know how it ends. And I will not stand by and watch another village damn itself."

"So you'll damn it yourself instead."

The accusation hangs in the air.

Theron's face goes cold. "I will do what's necessary. What I should have done fifteen years ago instead of running to the Order. I will act."

"By becoming exactly what you fought against."

"By preventing what I failed to prevent before!" His composure cracks. "You don't understand. You weren't there. You didn't see what the dragon did, didn't hear the children screaming, didn't find the bodies—" His voice breaks. Starts again. "I have spent fifteen years living with that failure. I will not fail again."

And there it is. The truth underneath the justifications.

This isn't about Riverton. It's about Thornhaven. About guilt he can never cleanse. About trying to save children who are already dead.

I look at Solaire. He's staring at his father with horror and pity and recognition. Seeing, maybe for the first time, what I've seen all along.

"He's broken," I say quietly.

"I'm standing right here," Theron snaps.

"I know." I don't look away from Solaire. "That's why I'm talking to him, not you. Because you've already made your choice. But he hasn't."

"Garicus—" Solaire's voice is small.

"Six days ago you left because you needed to find truth. To understand what happened to us." I step toward him. "Look at him, Sol. Really look. Is this truth? Or is this just another man who believes he's righteous while villages burn?"

Solaire's face crumples.

"You're manipulating him," Theron says.

"I'm showing him what you are." I finally turn to face him. "You're a good man who did something terrible and can't forgive himself. So you keep doing terrible things, calling them prevention, calling them necessary, because if you stop you'll have to face the fact that Thornhaven was your fault."

"It was the Order's fault!"

"It was everyone's fault!" My voice rises. "The cult. The elders. The Order. You. Everyone made choices that seemed right and led to horror. That's how it works. That's how it always works. And you standing here planning to burn another village won't change that!"

Silence falls. Just the fire crackling and three people breathing hard.

"The evidence—" Theron starts.

"Show me." I hold up the journal. "Show us. Right now. We'll go to Riverton. We'll investigate together. And if there's really a cult, we'll stop it. Properly. Not with fire."

"There's no time—"

"There's always time for truth." I look at Solaire. "Isn't there? Isn't that what you've been searching for?"

Solaire's throat works. "Yes."

"Then come with me. We'll check his evidence. Verify it. Make sure this is real before anyone burns."

I can see the war in Solaire's face. Loyalty to his father. The oath to me. The desperate need to believe there's a right answer.

"Solaire," Theron says. His voice is gentler now. Pleading. "I am trying to protect you. To stop this before it starts. Don't you trust me?"

"I—" Solaire looks between us. "I don't know."

"You trusted me enough to leave Greystone," Theron presses. "Trusted me enough to question the Order. Trust me now. Let me do this."

"This isn't trust," I say. "This is asking him to be complicit in massacre. To stand by while you murder people based on suspicion."

"Based on evidence!"

"Then show us the evidence!" I throw the journal back at him. "Take us to Riverton. Show us the symbols, the texts, the proof. If it's real, we'll help you stop it. The right way."

Theron catches the journal. His jaw works.

"And if we investigate and you're wrong?" Solaire asks quietly. "What happens then?"

The question seems to break something in Theron. His shoulders slump.

"Then I've lost everything," he whispers. "Again."

---

We ride to Riverton as the sun climbs. Three people on horseback, none of us speaking.

The village appears through the trees around midday. Small. Maybe a hundred people. Wooden buildings, thatched roofs, smoke rising from chimneys. Children playing in the square.

It looks exactly like Thornhaven probably looked before it burned.

"The symbols are in the forest," Theron says. His voice is hollow. Mechanical. "North side. I can show you."

We dismount. Tie the horses. Walk into the woods.

The symbols are there. Carved into tree trunks, scratched in the dirt. Dragon summoning circles, or something close to them. I'm not an expert. Neither is Solaire.

But Theron is. And he's staring at them like they're written in his own blood.

"See?" His voice shakes. "I wasn't lying. It's real."

"Or someone wants us to think it's real," I say.

"Why would—"

"I don't know. But these could be drawn by anyone. They don't prove there's a cult."

"The children—"

"Could have run away. Could have been taken by bandits. Could be anything." I kneel beside one of the symbols. It's fresh. Too fresh. "When did you find these?"

"Two days ago."

"And they're already this clear? No weathering? No leaf cover?"

Theron's face goes pale.

"Someone made these recently," I say. "Very recently. Maybe even after you started watching the village."

"No. That's—" He stops. Thinks. "The texts. In the church. I can show you those."

We walk back to the village. Riverton's church is small, simple stone with a wooden cross. The door is unlocked. Theron leads us to the altar, moves it aside with shaking hands.

Nothing.

No hidden compartment. No texts. Just stone and dust and the smell of old incense.

"They were here," Theron whispers. "I saw them. I held them."

"When?"

"A week ago. Maybe... maybe longer. I've been..." He presses his hands to his face. "I've been so tired. The nights blend together. But I saw them, Garicus. I swear I saw them."

Solaire and I exchange a look.

"The missing children," I say gently. "Can you show us records? Families we can talk to?"

Theron pulls the journal out again. Flips through it with trembling fingers. The pages are covered in cramped writing, notes layered over notes, diagrams and names and dates that don't quite connect.

It's the journal of a man seeing patterns that aren't there.

"I—" He stops. Stares at the page. "I wrote this. I know I wrote this. But I can't..." He looks up at us. "I can't remember. Can't remember which family. Which children. It's all—"

His voice breaks.

And I watch Solaire's face as he understands what I've known since we arrived: there is no cult. There are no missing children. There's just a broken man seeing the ghosts of Thornhaven everywhere he looks.

"Father," Solaire says softly. "When did you last sleep?"

Theron doesn't answer. Can't.

"When did you last eat? Really eat, not just field rations?"

"I don't—" He sways. Catches himself against the altar. "I have to stop them. Have to prevent it. Can't fail again. Can't let the children—"

"There are no children," I say. As gently as I can. "Not here. Not like Thornhaven. You've been hunting a ghost."

"No." But there's no conviction in his voice anymore. Just desperation. "No, I saw the signs. I know the pattern. I can't be wrong again. I can't—"

He collapses to his knees.

Solaire moves before I can. Drops beside him, catches him before he falls completely. "Father. Father, look at me."

Theron looks up. His face is ash-pale, his eyes red-rimmed and lost.

"There's no cult," Solaire says. His voice cracks but stays steady. "You've been seeing shadows. Trying so hard to prevent Thornhaven from happening again that you've been creating it in your mind. Do you understand?"

A sound escapes Theron. Not quite a sob. Not quite a scream.

"I was so sure," he whispers. "I was so certain. The symbols, the texts, the pattern—it was all there. I saw it."

"You saw what you needed to see," Solaire says. "Because you can't forgive yourself for Thornhaven. Because you've been carrying that guilt for fifteen years and it broke you."

"I failed them." Tears cut through the dirt on Theron's face. "I failed them all. Your mother. You. Every child who died because I was too slow, too trusting, too—"

"You failed by trying to save them," Solaire says. "That's the truth, Father. You did everything you could and it wasn't enough. And now you're trying to make it enough by seeing cults everywhere. By preventing disasters that don't exist."

Theron's hands clutch at Solaire's arms. "What do I do? How do I— How do I live with this?"

Solaire looks at me. There's so much pain in his eyes. So much understanding.

"You stop," he says quietly. "You stop hunting. Stop trying to atone. You just... you live. And you let yourself grieve what you couldn't save."

"I can't." Theron's voice is a child's. Small. Afraid. "If I stop, I have to face what I did. Have to accept that I led them to you. That I'm the reason you—"

"You're the reason I'm alive." Solaire's voice breaks completely now. "If you hadn't reported the cult, the ritual would have spread. More villages would have tried it. More children would have died. You did what you thought was right. The Order chose massacre. That's not your fault."

"I led them there—"

"And then you spent fifteen years trying to make it right. That has to be enough. It has to be, Father. Because if you keep this up, you won't prevent another Thornhaven. You'll cause one."

The words settle over us like ash.

Theron stares at his son. At the boy he abandoned and found and is losing again, this time by his own doing.

"I'm sorry," he whispers. "Solaire, I'm so sorry."

"I know." Solaire pulls him close. "I know you are."

And my brother—my stubborn, questioning, impossible brother—holds his father while they both cry.

---

We bind Theron's wrists.

He doesn't resist. Just sits in the church while I tie the knots, gentle but secure. He's broken completely now. Trembling. Seeing clearly for the first time in years what he's become.

"What will you do with me?" he asks.

I look at Solaire.

"Take him to the Order," Solaire says. His voice is steady now. Decided. "Let them deal with him. He needs help we can't give."

"They'll execute me," Theron says. Not afraid. Just stating fact. "Attempting to massacre a village. That's death by Order law."

"They'll imprison you," I say. "Maybe. If we explain that you're broken, not malicious."

"Is there a difference?"

Solaire closes his eyes. "I don't know. But I'm not letting you burn to death in some cell because you made mistakes. You're still my father. That still matters."

Theron's face crumples again. "I don't deserve you."

"No," Solaire agrees. "You don't. But you have me anyway."

We lead him out of the church. The villagers watch us—three strangers with a bound man. They'll talk. Rumors will spread. Maybe the Order will investigate and find nothing, or maybe they'll decide Theron's paranoia was justified enough to scare them.

I don't care.

I care that Solaire is walking beside me. That when Theron stumbles, we both catch him. That the fracture between us is closing, slowly, with every step.

We make camp that night a mile from Riverton. Theron sleeps under watch. Solaire and I sit by the fire, not speaking.

Finally, he breaks the silence.

"I'm sorry."

I don't pretend not to understand. "For what?"

"For leaving. For choosing him over you. For—" His voice catches. "For almost letting him burn innocents because I wanted so badly for him to be right."

"He was your father. You wanted to believe him."

"He was a broken man I barely knew. You were my brother. I should have—"

"Chosen me?" I look at him. "You did, Sol. In the end. You chose right."

"After you came. After you showed me what he really was." His hands fist. "I couldn't see it on my own."

"Yes, you could. You wrote to me. You brought me here." I shift closer. "You wanted someone to stop you from making his mistake. That's still choosing right."

Solaire's eyes are wet. "I broke the oath."

"For six days."

"Six days where I—"

"Where you tried to understand your father and learned he couldn't be saved." I grab his hand. Squeeze hard. "The oath was never about being perfect. It was about not dying alone. And you didn't. I was here."

"Because I asked you to be."

"Because I always am." I meet his eyes. "Fifteen years, Sol. One fight, six days apart, one village that didn't burn. We're still here. Still together. The oath isn't broken."

"Are you sure?"

"I'm sure."

He leans against me. I put my arm around him.

"What happens now?" he asks.

"We take your father to the Order. We face whatever judgment comes." I pause. "And then we decide if we stay or go."

"Together?"

"Together."

He's quiet for a long moment. Then: "I don't know if I can serve them anymore. Knowing what they are."

"I know."

"But I don't know where else to go. What else to do."

"We'll figure it out." I tighten my grip on him. "That's what we've always done."

The fire crackles. Stars emerge overhead. And two brothers sit in the dark, holding each other through the aftermath of choices that nearly destroyed them.

The oath survived.

Barely.

But it survived.

And maybe that's enough.

# Chapter 11: Somewhere No One's Burning

The chains on my father's wrists are too heavy for what he is now.

He shuffles between Garicus and me as we walk the long road back to Greystone Keep, his priest's robes torn and stained with mud from the scuffle last night. We stopped him. Garicus and I, together, stopped him from burning another village on nothing but paranoid conviction and fifteen years of guilt transformed into crusade.

The village was innocent. We found the proof—no cult, no ritual texts, just farmers frightened by their own shadows and Theron's accusations. My father, broken by what he did to Thornhaven, had been seeing evil everywhere. Creating it where it didn't exist, trying to prevent horrors that lived only in his mind.

He doesn't resist. Hasn't spoken since Garicus restrained him while I searched the evidence. Since I looked him in the eye and said, "No more."

The worst part is understanding. I understand how guilt can calcify into paranoia. How trying to atone for one massacre might drive you to justify another. How the man who tried to save Thornhaven by betraying it could become the man who'd burn innocents again because the first betrayal never stopped hurting.

I understand.

And I'm still bringing him to the Order for judgment.

Garicus hasn't said much either. We're not fighting anymore—the argument that split us dissolved last night when he tracked me to the village Theron was targeting. I was already there, already stopping my father, already making the choice Garicus feared I wouldn't make.

He showed up ready to drag me home. Found me binding Theron's hands instead.

"You came back," was all he said.

"I never left," I replied. "Not really."

It was a lie. We both knew it. But it was the lie we needed to move forward.

Now we walk in careful silence, three men carrying different weights toward the same gray stones.

---

The gates of Greystone Keep rise before us like a judgment. I've spent weeks away, following Theron's trail, trying to verify his conspiracy theories about the Order's corruption. I found some truth—evidence of morally ambiguous operations, preventive strikes that cost innocent lives, the kind of pragmatic brutality Aldric admitted to.

But I didn't find the grand conspiracy Theron promised. No evidence of deliberately planted cult texts. No proof the Order engineered Thornhaven's fall.

Just men making impossible choices and living with the consequences.

Just my father, driven mad by guilt, seeing patterns in chaos.

Just me, caught between truth and family, finally understanding they're not always compatible.

The guards at the gate recognize us. Their eyes widen at the sight of Theron in chains. Word will spread fast—the wayward ward returning with a prisoner. Aldric will hear before we reach his quarters.

"Solaire." Theron's voice is hoarse. He hasn't used it in hours. "You don't have to do this."

"Yes, I do."

"They'll execute me. You know they will."

"I know."

Garicus glances at me. Checking. Making sure I'm certain.

I nod once.

We walk through the gates.

---

They take Theron to the cells first. Knights I've trained with, men I've called brothers, lead my father away with professional detachment. He doesn't fight. Just looks back once, and the expression on his face is almost relief.

He's been running from this for fifteen years. Maybe being caught is its own kind of peace.

Garicus and I stand in the courtyard after he's gone. The familiar sounds of the Keep wash over us—steel on steel from the training yard, shouted orders, the rhythms of an institution that doesn't pause for individual tragedy.

"You should clean up," Garicus says. "Before you see Aldric."

"I should see him now."

"Solaire—"

"Now, Garicus." I meet his eyes. "Before I lose my nerve."

He studies my face. Whatever he sees there makes him nod. "I'll be in our room. When you're ready."

Our room. Not my room. Not his room. Ours again.

It's a small thing. It means everything.

---

I find Aldric in the chapel. Of course. Where else would he be when one of his wards returns dragging in the father whose guilt has haunted them both?

He's not praying. Just sitting on the front pew, hands clasped loosely between his knees, staring at the crimson candles like they might offer answers they've never contained.

"Ser Aldric," I say from the doorway.

He doesn't turn. "Solaire. Welcome home."

"I'm not sure this is home anymore."

"No. I suppose it isn't." He gestures to the pew beside him. "Sit. Tell me what happened."

I sit. The stone is cold even through my travel-worn clothes. I smell like horse and road dust and failure.

"I found him," I say. "My father. Found what he's been doing since Thornhaven—hunting cult remnants. Trying to prevent more dragon summonings. He's been traveling from village to village, investigating rumors, stopping rituals before they start."

"Noble work," Aldric says carefully.

"It was. Until it wasn't." I lean forward, elbows on knees. "He broke, Aldric. The guilt of what he did—leading you to Thornhaven, watching it burn—it broke something in him. Started seeing cults everywhere. Last night he tried to burn a village based on nothing but suspicion and fear. We stopped him. Garicus and me."

"We?"

"Garicus tracked me. Thought I was in trouble. Found me choosing." I look at my hands. They're still shaking slightly. "Choosing to stop my father instead of believing him. Choosing you and Garicus and this place over the man who gave me life and destroyed it."

Aldric is quiet for a long moment. "That's not an easy choice."

"None of them are." I finally meet his eyes. "You were right. About everything. Truth didn't set me free. It just complicated things further. Made me understand how my father could do what he did. How you could do what you did. How all of you—cult elders, Order knights, desperate priests—could make terrible choices thinking they were right."

"And what did you learn?"

"That moral clarity is a luxury." The words taste bitter. "That sometimes the people who hurt us didn't mean to. That good intentions pave roads to places like Thornhaven." I pause. "That I've been serving an imperfect institution run by flawed men trying their best and often failing. Just like everywhere else."

"Does that mean you're staying?"

The question hangs in the air like smoke.

"No," I say.

Aldric nods. Doesn't look surprised. "Where will you go?"

"I don't know yet. Somewhere that isn't defined by Thornhaven. By ashes and oaths sworn in trauma. Somewhere Garicus and I can figure out who we are when we're not someone's wards or someone's revenge or someone's guilt."

"Garicus is going with you."

"Yes."

"Good." Aldric's voice is rough. "He shouldn't be alone. Neither should you."

"We made that promise a long time ago."

"I know. I was there." He stands, moves to the candles. Touches one gently, watching the flame dance. "I've made a lot of mistakes with you two. Took you in for the wrong reasons. Lied by omission. Trained you to serve a system that destroyed your families. But keeping you together—that might be the one thing I did right."

"You could have told us the truth sooner."

"Would it have helped? Would knowing at eight years old that I participated in the raid have made you stronger? Safer? Happier?" He turns to face me. "Or would it just have hurt you sooner?"

"I don't know." The honesty feels important. "Maybe both. Maybe neither. But it would have been our pain to carry on our terms, not yours to manage."

Aldric absorbs that. Nods slowly. "You're right. I robbed you of agency by choosing what truth you could handle. I'm sorry, Solaire. For that and... for all of it."

"I know." And I do. I've seen his guilt, worn into every line of his face. "I forgive you."

The words surprise us both.

Aldric's expression cracks. "You shouldn't. I don't deserve—"

"I forgive you anyway." I stand, face him fully. "Not because what you did was acceptable. It wasn't. But because carrying hatred alongside everything else will kill me. So I forgive you for Thornhaven. For the lies. For raising us in the shadow of your guilt." I pause. "And I thank you. For not leaving us in the ruins. For giving us each other. For teaching us to survive."

"Solaire—"

"I'm not done." My voice hardens slightly. "I forgive you. But I can't stay here. Can't serve the Order knowing what I know. Can't look at these walls and not see my mother's death in every stone. Can't be your ward or your redemption project or proof that guilt can be atoned for."

"What will you be, then?"

"I don't know yet." The admission feels like freedom. "But I'll figure it out. With Garicus. Somewhere far from here."

Aldric studies me for a long moment. Then he reaches into his cloak, pulls out a leather pouch. Coin, from the weight of it. He holds it out.

"Take this. It's not much, but it will get you started."

"Aldric—"

"Take it." His voice is gentle but firm. "Let me do this one thing without complicating it. Let me help you leave."

I take the pouch. It's heavier than expected. More than "not much."

"Thank you," I say.

"I'll handle your father's trial," Aldric continues. "He'll be judged by the Order. Given what he's done—both the good and the attempted burning—the sentence could be execution or lifetime imprisonment. Do you want to be present?"

I think about Theron's face when the guards took him away. The relief there.

"No," I say quietly. "I said goodbye last night. Whatever happens to him now... that's between him and the Order."

"And you can live with that?"

"I'll have to." I turn to leave. Stop at the door. "Aldric?"

"Yes?"

"The conspiracy Theron talked about. The idea that the Order deliberately seeds cults to justify its existence. Was he right?"

Silence. Heavy and telling.

"There are factions within the Vigil that believe in preventive intervention," Aldric says finally. "Operations that might look, from certain angles, like what your father described. Whether that constitutes conspiracy or pragmatic strategy depends on your perspective."

"That's not an answer."

"It's the only one I have." He meets my eyes. "The world is more complicated than good and evil, Solaire. The Order is made of men. Some noble, some corrupt, most somewhere in between. If you're looking for pure righteousness or clear villainy, you'll be disappointed everywhere you look."

"I'm already disappointed."

"Then you're learning." He almost smiles. "Go. Pack. Say your goodbyes. And Solaire?"

"Yes?"

"When you find wherever you're going—wherever no one's burning—send word. Just so I know you're alive."

"I will."

It's the last lie I tell him. The kind one. The one that lets both of us pretend this isn't goodbye forever.

I leave him in the chapel among the candles made from executed mages, a knight growing old with his guilt, and I don't look back.

---

Our room feels smaller than it did six weeks ago.

Garicus is there, already packing. His efficiency is almost mechanical—bedroll, spare clothes, weapons, supplies. Everything we'll need and nothing we won't.

"You saw Aldric," he says without turning.

"Yes."

"And?"

"And we're leaving." I pull my own pack from under the bed. Start filling it. "Tomorrow at dawn. Before I can change my mind."

"You won't change your mind."

"How do you know?"

He finally looks at me. "Because I know you. You've made your choice. You don't unmake those."

He's right. The choice happened last night when I chose to stop Theron instead of help him. When I chose Garicus's trust over my father's truth. When I chose the present over the past.

We pack in companionable silence. It feels like the old days—before Theron, before the fracture, when we moved around each other like dancers who'd memorized every step.

"Where are we going?" Garicus asks eventually.

"I don't know. Somewhere without the Order. Without the past."

"That's not a plan."

"It's the beginning of one." I fold a shirt, tuck it into the pack. "We'll figure it out as we go. Together."

The word hangs between us. Together. The core of our oath. The promise that survived everything else.

"I'm sorry," I say. "For leaving. For choosing Theron. For making you think our oath meant less than it did."

"You chose right in the end."

"I almost didn't." My throat tightens. "I almost believed him. Almost let the conspiracy and the guilt and the need for meaning pull me away from the only thing that's ever been real. You were right to be angry."

Garicus stops packing. Sits on his bed, facing me.

"I was angry because I was scared," he says. "Scared of losing you. Of the oath breaking. Of being alone again." He looks at his hands. "But you weren't wrong to want answers. To need truth. I just... I couldn't watch you drown in it."

"You did watch me drown. Then you pulled me out."

"That's what we do." He stands. Crosses the space between our beds. Pulls me into a hard embrace. "We save each other. However many times it takes."

I hold onto him like he's the only solid thing in a world made of smoke and ash. Maybe he is.

"Never alone," I whisper.

"Never alone," he echoes.

We stand like that until the weight in my chest eases. Until breathing doesn't hurt quite so much.

Then we finish packing. And when the last of our belongings is secured, when there's nothing left of our fifteen years in this room but empty beds and old memories, we walk out together.

---

We leave at dawn, as planned.

The courtyard is quiet. Most of the Keep still sleeps. A few guards on duty nod as we pass, asking no questions. Aldric must have spread word—let them go without incident.

I see him once, watching from the tower window. He doesn't wave. Doesn't call out. Just stands witness as we walk through the gates and into the wide world beyond.

Garicus and I ride in silence for the first hour. The sun rises slowly, painting the frost-covered landscape in shades of gold and pink. Beautiful, despite everything.

We reach a crossroads as midmorning light slants through the trees. Three roads diverge—north toward the mountains, east toward the coast, south back the way we came.

We stop. Dismount. Stand at the convergence of paths, neither of us quite ready to choose.

"Where do we go?" Garicus asks. The question I've been waiting for.

I think about Thornhaven, burning in my memory. About Greystone Keep, gray and cold and never quite home. About every village we rode through in service to the Order, enforcing a justice we no longer believe in.

I think about ash and smoke and promises made by children who didn't know what they were swearing to.

And then I think about the future. Undefined. Terrifying. Full of possibility.

"Somewhere no one's burning," I say.

Garicus considers this. A smile tugs at his mouth—small, genuine, the first I've seen in weeks.

"That's a good place," he says.

"Think we can find it?"

"We can try." He looks at the three roads, then at me. "East?"

"East sounds good."

We mount again. Turn our horses toward the rising sun. And as Greystone Keep disappears behind us, as the road stretches forward into unknown territory, I feel something loosen in my chest.

Not happiness. Not yet. But the possibility of it. The understanding that we're no longer defined by what destroyed us, but by what we choose next.

We ride side by side, two brothers who survived fire and truth and the slow death of certainty. We have no masters now. No oaths except to each other. No purpose except the one we'll create.

It should terrify me. Should feel like falling.

It feels like flying.

"Garicus?" I say after another mile.

"Yeah?"

"Thank you. For not giving up on me. For coming after me when I was lost."

"You'd do the same for me."

"I would. I will. However long it takes."

"However long it takes," he agrees.

The oath has changed. Evolved. No longer born from trauma and desperation, but from genuine choice. From knowing each other completely and choosing to stay anyway.

Maybe that's what it always should have been.

Ahead, the road curves into forest. New territory. Unmapped. Ours to explore.

We ride toward it without hesitation, leaving smoke and ash and everything we thought we had to be behind us.

Somewhere ahead is a place no one's burning. A place we can build instead of destroy. A place where the promises we make matter because we choose them freely, not because eight-year-olds swore them in desperation.

We don't know where it is.

But we'll find it together.

And that's enough.

## Epilogue Fragment

*Five years later*

The village of Ashford is small, comfortable, unremarkable. A place where nothing terrible has ever happened and likely never will. The kind of place people come to forget.

We came to remember.

Garicus runs the blacksmith shop on the east edge of town. He's good at it—patient with the work, methodical, finding peace in the rhythm of hammer on metal. The villagers like him. Trust him. Don't ask about the scars or why he moves like a fighter.

I run the school. Six students so far, children whose families can't afford private tutors. I teach them letters and numbers and stories that don't involve burning villages or impossible choices. They call me Teacher Solaire. They smile when they see me.

It's a quiet life. Simple. Nothing like the one we left behind.

Some nights I dream of Thornhaven. Of my mother's face, fading. Of Aldric in the chapel, watching us leave. Of Theron in chains, relief and resignation warring in his eyes.

I wake to Garicus in the next room, steady and solid and here. And the dreams fade.

We don't talk about the Order much. Don't talk about what happened to Theron—whether he was executed or imprisoned, whether our testimony at his trial mattered. We sent one letter to Aldric, two years ago. Told him we were alive and well. Asked him not to respond.

He didn't.

But sometimes I think I see crimson cloaks in the distance. Wonder if he's checking on us. Making sure his wards survived their freedom.

I hope he did. I hope he found peace too.

Garicus appears in the doorway of my classroom after the last student leaves. He's soot-stained, smiling, holding two apples.

"Good day?" he asks.

"Good day," I confirm.

We walk home together as the sun sets, painting the sky in shades of red and gold. Not promises in ash anymore. Just beauty. Just light.

"Think we found it?" I ask.

"Found what?"

"The place where no one's burning."

Garicus looks at me. At the quiet village around us. At the life we've built from nothing but choice and stubborn loyalty to each other.

"Yeah," he says. "I think we did."

We walk the rest of the way in comfortable silence.

Brothers. Not by blood or trauma.

By choice.

Always by choice.

And that makes all the difference.
