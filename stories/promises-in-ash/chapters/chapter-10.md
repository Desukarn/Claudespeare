# Chapter 10: The Choice

Six days.

Six days since Solaire walked out of Greystone Keep and I let him go.

Six days of training alone, eating alone, sleeping in a room that echoes with absence. Six days of telling myself I made the right choice, that forcing him to decide was necessary, that clean breaks heal better than slow tears.

Six days of knowing I was lying to myself.

The messenger arrives on the seventh day.

---

He's young, maybe fifteen. Roadworn and nervous, clutching a sealed letter like it might bite him. He finds me in the armory, where I've been sharpening my blade to a mirror edge for the third time this week.

"Ser Garicus?" His voice cracks. "I was told to deliver this to you personally."

I take the letter. No seal. No marking. Just my name in handwriting I'd recognize anywhere.

The messenger hovers. I flip him a copper without looking at him. "Go."

He goes.

I don't open the letter immediately. Just hold it, feeling the weight of paper and ink and whatever words Solaire thought important enough to send after telling me not to write.

Finally, I break the fold.

*Garicus,*

*I know what I said. I know I shouldn't be writing. But you need to know this.*

*Theron has been tracking another village. Riverton, three days northeast of the Keep. He has evidence of cult activity—symbols, missing children, the same pattern as Thornhaven. He's planning to stop it.*

*He's planning to burn it.*

*I've tried to talk him out of it. Tried to convince him to bring evidence to the Order, to do this properly. But he doesn't trust them. He thinks they're complicit. He thinks the only way to stop it is to do what they did to us.*

*I'm going with him tomorrow. To Riverton. I don't know if I can stop him, but I have to try.*

*I thought you should know. In case I'm wrong. In case he's right. In case this is the last time you hear from me.*

*I'm sorry. For everything. I wish we could have found a different way.*

*—S*

My hands are shaking.

Theron is going to burn another village. And Solaire is going to be there when he does it.

I don't think. Don't hesitate. Just grab my kit, saddle a horse, and ride.

---

Three days northeast at killing pace becomes two and a half. My horse is half-dead by the time I spot smoke on the horizon.

Not the thick black smoke of a village burning. Just campfire smoke, thin and gray. I follow it to a small clearing in the woods, hidden from the main road by dense trees.

Theron's camp.

Three tents, a fire, supplies laid out with military precision. And in the center, a map weighted down with stones, covered in markings and notes.

Solaire sits beside the fire. He looks up when I dismount, and for a moment neither of us speaks.

He's thinner. The six days have carved shadows under his eyes, hollows in his cheeks. He looks like he did at sixteen, right after the first mission. Right after Elara.

"You came," he says finally.

"You asked me to."

"I didn't—" He stops. "The letter was just information. I didn't think you'd—"

"You're about to watch your father burn a village and you thought I wouldn't come?" I tie my horse to a tree. It's shaking, lathered with sweat. I should walk it. Water it. I don't move. "Where is he?"

"Scouting Riverton. Confirming locations." Solaire's hands twist together. "Garicus, I tried to stop him. I told him this was wrong, that he was becoming the thing he hated, but he won't listen."

"Then why are you still here?"

The question lands like a blow. Solaire flinches.

"Because he's my father. Because if I'm not here, there's no one to stop him from—" He gestures helplessly at the map. "From doing this."

I walk to the map. Look down at the careful notations. Riverton's layout. Guard positions. Escape routes. Barrels marked "oil."

It's a raid plan. Methodical. Professional. Exactly how the Order would do it.

"He's going to murder them," I say. Not a question.

"He says he's going to stop the cult."

"By burning everyone who might have knowledge of it. By silencing witnesses. By doing exactly what the Order did to us." I look at Solaire. "Do you hear yourself? This is Thornhaven all over again."

"I know!" His voice breaks. "I know, Garicus. Why do you think I wrote to you? I can't—I can't stop him alone. He's convinced this is necessary. That if he doesn't act, more children will die."

"And you believe him?"

"I don't know what I believe anymore." He stands, faces me fully. "He showed me evidence. Names of children who've gone missing. Symbols carved in the woods around the village. The same texts that were in Thornhaven, showing up in Riverton's church."

"How convenient."

Solaire's eyes flash. "You think he's lying?"

"I think he's seeing what he wants to see. What he needs to see to justify this." I step closer. "Thornhaven destroyed him, Sol. You can see that, right? He's spent fifteen years trying to atone by hunting cults that might not exist, by preventing disasters that are already over. This isn't justice. It's obsession."

"What if you're wrong?" His voice is desperate now. "What if there really is a cult? What if children are dying and we do nothing?"

"Then we investigate. Properly. We bring it to the Order—"

"The Order that might have caused it in the first place?"

"The Order that has resources and authority! Not one broken man with oil and a torch!"

We're shouting now. Old argument, new stakes.

"He's not broken," Solaire says. But his voice wavers. "He's trying to make amends."

"He's trying to make himself feel better. And he's using you to do it."

Footsteps crunch through the underbrush. We both turn.

Theron emerges from the trees. He's dressed for violence—dark clothes, boots good for running, a sword at his hip I didn't see him carrying before. When he sees me, he stops.

"Garicus." His voice is careful. Measuring. "I didn't expect you."

"Clearly." I gesture at the map. "Planning something?"

He doesn't deny it. Just walks to the fire, sits. The movement is casual, but his hand stays near his sword.

"Your brother told you," he says.

"He did."

"And you've come to stop me."

"I've come to stop this." I point at the map. "Whatever you think you're doing, it ends now."

Theron's laugh is bitter. "I'm doing what the Order won't. What they can't, because they're compromised."

"You're about to murder innocents because you think you see a pattern."

"I know I see a pattern." He pulls a leather journal from his coat, tosses it to me. "Three children missing in the last month. All under ten years old. All from families on the outskirts. The village elders claim runaways or animal attacks, but the bodies were never found."

I catch the journal. Don't open it.

"Symbols in the forest," Theron continues. "The same ones I saw in Thornhaven. Dragon summoning circles. Fresh, Garicus. Drawn within the last week."

"Anyone could draw symbols."

"The texts," he says. "I found them in the church. Hidden under the altar. Exact copies of the ones used in Thornhaven." His eyes are fevered. Certain. "This is happening again. Right now. And if we don't stop it—"

"Then we investigate," I cut in. "We confirm. We find actual proof, not paranoid suspicion."

"There's no time!" Theron stands, and now there's real anger in his voice. "The ritual requires thirteen children. They have three. If we wait for your proof, how many more will die?"

"How many will die if you're wrong?" I step closer. "How many innocents will you burn trying to stop a cult that exists only in your head?"

"I am not wrong." Each word is clipped. Final. "I have seen this before. I know how it starts. I know how it ends. And I will not stand by and watch another village damn itself."

"So you'll damn it yourself instead."

The accusation hangs in the air.

Theron's face goes cold. "I will do what's necessary. What I should have done fifteen years ago instead of running to the Order. I will act."

"By becoming exactly what you fought against."

"By preventing what I failed to prevent before!" His composure cracks. "You don't understand. You weren't there. You didn't see what the dragon did, didn't hear the children screaming, didn't find the bodies—" His voice breaks. Starts again. "I have spent fifteen years living with that failure. I will not fail again."

And there it is. The truth underneath the justifications.

This isn't about Riverton. It's about Thornhaven. About guilt he can never cleanse. About trying to save children who are already dead.

I look at Solaire. He's staring at his father with horror and pity and recognition. Seeing, maybe for the first time, what I've seen all along.

"He's broken," I say quietly.

"I'm standing right here," Theron snaps.

"I know." I don't look away from Solaire. "That's why I'm talking to him, not you. Because you've already made your choice. But he hasn't."

"Garicus—" Solaire's voice is small.

"Six days ago you left because you needed to find truth. To understand what happened to us." I step toward him. "Look at him, Sol. Really look. Is this truth? Or is this just another man who believes he's righteous while villages burn?"

Solaire's face crumples.

"You're manipulating him," Theron says.

"I'm showing him what you are." I finally turn to face him. "You're a good man who did something terrible and can't forgive himself. So you keep doing terrible things, calling them prevention, calling them necessary, because if you stop you'll have to face the fact that Thornhaven was your fault."

"It was the Order's fault!"

"It was everyone's fault!" My voice rises. "The cult. The elders. The Order. You. Everyone made choices that seemed right and led to horror. That's how it works. That's how it always works. And you standing here planning to burn another village won't change that!"

Silence falls. Just the fire crackling and three people breathing hard.

"The evidence—" Theron starts.

"Show me." I hold up the journal. "Show us. Right now. We'll go to Riverton. We'll investigate together. And if there's really a cult, we'll stop it. Properly. Not with fire."

"There's no time—"

"There's always time for truth." I look at Solaire. "Isn't there? Isn't that what you've been searching for?"

Solaire's throat works. "Yes."

"Then come with me. We'll check his evidence. Verify it. Make sure this is real before anyone burns."

I can see the war in Solaire's face. Loyalty to his father. The oath to me. The desperate need to believe there's a right answer.

"Solaire," Theron says. His voice is gentler now. Pleading. "I am trying to protect you. To stop this before it starts. Don't you trust me?"

"I—" Solaire looks between us. "I don't know."

"You trusted me enough to leave Greystone," Theron presses. "Trusted me enough to question the Order. Trust me now. Let me do this."

"This isn't trust," I say. "This is asking him to be complicit in massacre. To stand by while you murder people based on suspicion."

"Based on evidence!"

"Then show us the evidence!" I throw the journal back at him. "Take us to Riverton. Show us the symbols, the texts, the proof. If it's real, we'll help you stop it. The right way."

Theron catches the journal. His jaw works.

"And if we investigate and you're wrong?" Solaire asks quietly. "What happens then?"

The question seems to break something in Theron. His shoulders slump.

"Then I've lost everything," he whispers. "Again."

---

We ride to Riverton as the sun climbs. Three people on horseback, none of us speaking.

The village appears through the trees around midday. Small. Maybe a hundred people. Wooden buildings, thatched roofs, smoke rising from chimneys. Children playing in the square.

It looks exactly like Thornhaven probably looked before it burned.

"The symbols are in the forest," Theron says. His voice is hollow. Mechanical. "North side. I can show you."

We dismount. Tie the horses. Walk into the woods.

The symbols are there. Carved into tree trunks, scratched in the dirt. Dragon summoning circles, or something close to them. I'm not an expert. Neither is Solaire.

But Theron is. And he's staring at them like they're written in his own blood.

"See?" His voice shakes. "I wasn't lying. It's real."

"Or someone wants us to think it's real," I say.

"Why would—"

"I don't know. But these could be drawn by anyone. They don't prove there's a cult."

"The children—"

"Could have run away. Could have been taken by bandits. Could be anything." I kneel beside one of the symbols. It's fresh. Too fresh. "When did you find these?"

"Two days ago."

"And they're already this clear? No weathering? No leaf cover?"

Theron's face goes pale.

"Someone made these recently," I say. "Very recently. Maybe even after you started watching the village."

"No. That's—" He stops. Thinks. "The texts. In the church. I can show you those."

We walk back to the village. Riverton's church is small, simple stone with a wooden cross. The door is unlocked. Theron leads us to the altar, moves it aside with shaking hands.

Nothing.

No hidden compartment. No texts. Just stone and dust and the smell of old incense.

"They were here," Theron whispers. "I saw them. I held them."

"When?"

"A week ago. Maybe... maybe longer. I've been..." He presses his hands to his face. "I've been so tired. The nights blend together. But I saw them, Garicus. I swear I saw them."

Solaire and I exchange a look.

"The missing children," I say gently. "Can you show us records? Families we can talk to?"

Theron pulls the journal out again. Flips through it with trembling fingers. The pages are covered in cramped writing, notes layered over notes, diagrams and names and dates that don't quite connect.

It's the journal of a man seeing patterns that aren't there.

"I—" He stops. Stares at the page. "I wrote this. I know I wrote this. But I can't..." He looks up at us. "I can't remember. Can't remember which family. Which children. It's all—"

His voice breaks.

And I watch Solaire's face as he understands what I've known since we arrived: there is no cult. There are no missing children. There's just a broken man seeing the ghosts of Thornhaven everywhere he looks.

"Father," Solaire says softly. "When did you last sleep?"

Theron doesn't answer. Can't.

"When did you last eat? Really eat, not just field rations?"

"I don't—" He sways. Catches himself against the altar. "I have to stop them. Have to prevent it. Can't fail again. Can't let the children—"

"There are no children," I say. As gently as I can. "Not here. Not like Thornhaven. You've been hunting a ghost."

"No." But there's no conviction in his voice anymore. Just desperation. "No, I saw the signs. I know the pattern. I can't be wrong again. I can't—"

He collapses to his knees.

Solaire moves before I can. Drops beside him, catches him before he falls completely. "Father. Father, look at me."

Theron looks up. His face is ash-pale, his eyes red-rimmed and lost.

"There's no cult," Solaire says. His voice cracks but stays steady. "You've been seeing shadows. Trying so hard to prevent Thornhaven from happening again that you've been creating it in your mind. Do you understand?"

A sound escapes Theron. Not quite a sob. Not quite a scream.

"I was so sure," he whispers. "I was so certain. The symbols, the texts, the pattern—it was all there. I saw it."

"You saw what you needed to see," Solaire says. "Because you can't forgive yourself for Thornhaven. Because you've been carrying that guilt for fifteen years and it broke you."

"I failed them." Tears cut through the dirt on Theron's face. "I failed them all. Your mother. You. Every child who died because I was too slow, too trusting, too—"

"You failed by trying to save them," Solaire says. "That's the truth, Father. You did everything you could and it wasn't enough. And now you're trying to make it enough by seeing cults everywhere. By preventing disasters that don't exist."

Theron's hands clutch at Solaire's arms. "What do I do? How do I— How do I live with this?"

Solaire looks at me. There's so much pain in his eyes. So much understanding.

"You stop," he says quietly. "You stop hunting. Stop trying to atone. You just... you live. And you let yourself grieve what you couldn't save."

"I can't." Theron's voice is a child's. Small. Afraid. "If I stop, I have to face what I did. Have to accept that I led them to you. That I'm the reason you—"

"You're the reason I'm alive." Solaire's voice breaks completely now. "If you hadn't reported the cult, the ritual would have spread. More villages would have tried it. More children would have died. You did what you thought was right. The Order chose massacre. That's not your fault."

"I led them there—"

"And then you spent fifteen years trying to make it right. That has to be enough. It has to be, Father. Because if you keep this up, you won't prevent another Thornhaven. You'll cause one."

The words settle over us like ash.

Theron stares at his son. At the boy he abandoned and found and is losing again, this time by his own doing.

"I'm sorry," he whispers. "Solaire, I'm so sorry."

"I know." Solaire pulls him close. "I know you are."

And my brother—my stubborn, questioning, impossible brother—holds his father while they both cry.

---

We bind Theron's wrists.

He doesn't resist. Just sits in the church while I tie the knots, gentle but secure. He's broken completely now. Trembling. Seeing clearly for the first time in years what he's become.

"What will you do with me?" he asks.

I look at Solaire.

"Take him to the Order," Solaire says. His voice is steady now. Decided. "Let them deal with him. He needs help we can't give."

"They'll execute me," Theron says. Not afraid. Just stating fact. "Attempting to massacre a village. That's death by Order law."

"They'll imprison you," I say. "Maybe. If we explain that you're broken, not malicious."

"Is there a difference?"

Solaire closes his eyes. "I don't know. But I'm not letting you burn to death in some cell because you made mistakes. You're still my father. That still matters."

Theron's face crumples again. "I don't deserve you."

"No," Solaire agrees. "You don't. But you have me anyway."

We lead him out of the church. The villagers watch us—three strangers with a bound man. They'll talk. Rumors will spread. Maybe the Order will investigate and find nothing, or maybe they'll decide Theron's paranoia was justified enough to scare them.

I don't care.

I care that Solaire is walking beside me. That when Theron stumbles, we both catch him. That the fracture between us is closing, slowly, with every step.

We make camp that night a mile from Riverton. Theron sleeps under watch. Solaire and I sit by the fire, not speaking.

Finally, he breaks the silence.

"I'm sorry."

I don't pretend not to understand. "For what?"

"For leaving. For choosing him over you. For—" His voice catches. "For almost letting him burn innocents because I wanted so badly for him to be right."

"He was your father. You wanted to believe him."

"He was a broken man I barely knew. You were my brother. I should have—"

"Chosen me?" I look at him. "You did, Sol. In the end. You chose right."

"After you came. After you showed me what he really was." His hands fist. "I couldn't see it on my own."

"Yes, you could. You wrote to me. You brought me here." I shift closer. "You wanted someone to stop you from making his mistake. That's still choosing right."

Solaire's eyes are wet. "I broke the oath."

"For six days."

"Six days where I—"

"Where you tried to understand your father and learned he couldn't be saved." I grab his hand. Squeeze hard. "The oath was never about being perfect. It was about not dying alone. And you didn't. I was here."

"Because I asked you to be."

"Because I always am." I meet his eyes. "Fifteen years, Sol. One fight, six days apart, one village that didn't burn. We're still here. Still together. The oath isn't broken."

"Are you sure?"

"I'm sure."

He leans against me. I put my arm around him.

"What happens now?" he asks.

"We take your father to the Order. We face whatever judgment comes." I pause. "And then we decide if we stay or go."

"Together?"

"Together."

He's quiet for a long moment. Then: "I don't know if I can serve them anymore. Knowing what they are."

"I know."

"But I don't know where else to go. What else to do."

"We'll figure it out." I tighten my grip on him. "That's what we've always done."

The fire crackles. Stars emerge overhead. And two brothers sit in the dark, holding each other through the aftermath of choices that nearly destroyed them.

The oath survived.

Barely.

But it survived.

And maybe that's enough.
