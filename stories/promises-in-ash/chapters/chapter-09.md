# Chapter 9: The Fracture

## Garicus

I find the letter by accident.

It's tucked inside Solaire's kit bag, the leather satchel he carries on missions. I'm not looking for it—just trying to find the whetstone he borrowed three days ago and hasn't returned. My blade needs sharpening, and I know he has the good one, the Mirean stone he bargained for at Crossroads.

The paper falls out when I lift the bag. Unfolds on the floor between my boots.

I shouldn't read it. Know I shouldn't even as I'm bending to pick it up.

But I do anyway.

*Solaire,*

*Meet me at the oak grove, two miles east of the Keep. Tomorrow at midday. There's more you need to know about the ritual, about what the elders were planning before I intervened.*

*Come alone. The Order has eyes everywhere.*

*—T*

T. Theron.

The paper crumples in my fist before I can stop myself.

He's been meeting him. After everything—after the confession, after the ride back in silence, after I thought he'd realized the truth was poison—he's been meeting him.

How many times? How long has this been going on?

I smooth the letter out. Check the date. Three days ago. The same day Solaire volunteered for a solo patrol. Said he needed space to think. I believed him.

I was a fool.

The door opens. Solaire walks in, still wearing his riding cloak, face flushed from cold or exertion. He sees me. Sees the letter in my hands.

Goes very still.

"Garicus—"

"How long?" My voice sounds strange. Flat. Like all the anger has burned down to ash and this is what's left.

"It's not what you think."

"How. Long."

He closes the door. Doesn't meet my eyes. "Two weeks. Maybe three."

"Three weeks." I hold up the letter. "You've been sneaking off to meet your father for three weeks and you didn't tell me."

"I was going to—"

"When? When were you going to tell me you were choosing him over everything we built?" The anger cracks through now, hot and jagged. "Over the oath? Over us?"

"I'm not choosing him!" Solaire's hands fist at his sides. "I'm trying to understand! There's more to this than what he told me that first night. The cult, the ritual, what the Order did—it's all connected and I need to know how deep it goes!"

"Why?"

The question stops him.

"Why do you need to know?" I step closer. "Will it bring back the dead? Will it change what happened? We survived, Solaire. We made something from the ashes. Why isn't that enough?"

"Because it's built on lies!" His voice rises to match mine. "Everything—the Order, Aldric's training, the oaths we swore—all of it stands on the graves of people who died for no reason! Don't you want to know why?"

"I know why." I throw the letter at his feet. "Evil men made evil choices. The Order tried to stop them and failed. Aldric took us in because he felt guilty. There's your truth. There's your understanding. Does it help?"

Solaire stares at the crumpled paper. "You talked to Aldric."

"Of course I talked to Aldric! You came back from Blackwood looking like the world had ended and wouldn't tell me what happened! So yes, I confronted him. I made him tell me everything—about the cult, about the dragon, about why they burned the village instead of just arresting the guilty." My throat is raw. When did I start shouting? "He told me. All of it. And you know what I realized?"

"What?"

"That it doesn't matter." The words come out hard as stone. "The Order made a choice. A bad choice, maybe. But the cult had already won. The dragon had already come and gone. Everyone was dying anyway. What difference does it make if they burned fast or slow?"

"The difference is intent!" Solaire is shouting now too. "The difference is that the Order chose murder over mercy! Chose covering up their failure over admitting they were too late! Don't you see? We've been serving the same people who killed our families!"

"I see someone desperate for his father's approval." The words are vicious. I know it even as I'm saying them. Can't stop. "I see someone who'd rather believe in conspiracy and injustice than accept that sometimes the world is just cruel and random. Your father failed you, Solaire. He ran instead of fighting. He trusted the wrong people. And now he's trying to make you complicit in that failure by—"

"He's trying to make amends!"

"By poisoning you against the only family you have left!" I grab his shoulders, force him to look at me. "I'm your family! Me! Not some man who abandoned you and spent fifteen years hiding in shame! I was there when you woke up in the ruins! I was there every nightmare, every training session, every mission! Where was he?"

Solaire wrenches free. "You think I don't know that? You think I don't remember?" His eyes are bright with unshed tears. "But he's my father, Garicus. My blood. And he's offering me answers to questions I've had my whole life!"

"And I'm offering you the same thing I've always offered—to stand beside you no matter what. Why isn't that enough anymore?"

The question hangs between us like a sword suspended by thread.

Solaire's face does something complicated. Pain and frustration and something that looks horribly like pity. "Because you're asking me to stay ignorant. To stop questioning. To accept that the people who raised us are the same ones who orphaned us and just... live with that."

"Yes." I don't flinch. "I'm asking you to survive. Because that's what we do. We survive."

"By forgetting?"

"By not letting the past destroy the present!" My hands are shaking. I clench them into fists. "Every moment you spend with Theron is a moment you're not here. Not with me. Not with the life we built. You're choosing ghosts over—"

"Over what? Over you?" Solaire's laugh is bitter. "You keep saying that like I have to choose. Like I can't have both."

"You can't." The certainty in my voice surprises even me. "Because one of us is asking you to move forward and one is dragging you backward into ash and questions that have no good answers. And you're so focused on understanding the why that you can't see you're losing the what."

"What am I losing?"

"Us."

The word falls like an executioner's blade.

Solaire stares at me. "That's not fair."

"It's true." I turn away. Can't look at him anymore. "We swore an oath, Solaire. Never alone. Never apart. But you've been alone for weeks now. Meeting him in secret. Keeping things from me. Breaking the promise a little more each day."

"I was protecting you—"

"From what? The truth? I don't need protecting!" I spin back. "I need my brother. The one who swore we'd face everything together. Where is he? Because all I see now is someone who's already chosen which side of the fracture he's standing on."

Silence. Just our breathing, harsh and uneven.

"I need to understand," Solaire says finally. Quiet. Almost broken. "I need to know if there's something more than just tragedy and bad timing. If there's meaning in what happened. Purpose."

"There's no purpose in massacre, Sol."

"Then what was the point?" His voice cracks. "What was the point of surviving if all we do is serve the people who made us orphans? If we enforce the same ruthless logic that killed our families?"

And there it is. The real question. The one underneath all the others.

I don't have an answer that won't hurt us both.

"The point," I say slowly, "is that we're alive. We have each other. We made something from nothing. That's enough for me. If it's not enough for you..." I stop. Start again. "If you need more than that, then go. Keep meeting your father. Keep searching for meaning in ash. But don't expect me to wait here while you do."

"What are you saying?"

"I'm saying make a choice." The words taste like blood. "Him or me. The past or the present. Because I can't—" My voice breaks. I force it steady. "I can't watch you die slowly from the inside while you chase a truth that will only hurt more."

Solaire's face is white. "You're asking me to abandon my father."

"I'm asking you to remember who was there when he wasn't."

"That's not fair."

"None of this is fair!" I'm shouting again. "You think I want to say this? You think I want to watch everything we built fall apart because some man you barely remember decided his guilt was more important than your peace? But here we are, Sol. You with your letters and secret meetings, me with the certainty that this ends badly no matter what we do. So yes, I'm asking you to choose. Because we can't go on like this."

The silence that follows is so complete I can hear my own heartbeat.

Solaire reaches down. Picks up the crumpled letter. Smooths it out with careful fingers.

"I'm meeting him tomorrow," he says. Doesn't look at me. "At the oak grove. There's something about the ritual he needs to tell me. Something about why the Order really burned the village."

"Solaire—"

"I have to go, Garicus. I have to know."

"And if I ask you not to? If I ask you to stay here with me instead?"

He finally meets my eyes. And what I see there breaks something in my chest I didn't know could break.

"I'm sorry," he whispers. "I'm so sorry. But I have to."

I nod once. Turn away so he won't see my face.

"Then go," I say. "But don't come back expecting things to be the same."

I hear him move toward the door. Hear it open.

"Garicus—"

"Go, Solaire."

The door closes. His footsteps fade down the corridor.

And for the first time in fifteen years, I'm alone in a room that used to be ours.

---

## Solaire

The oak grove is silent when I arrive. Too silent. No birdsong, no wind through the branches—just the oppressive quiet that comes before storms or violence.

Theron is already there, standing beneath the largest oak. He looks older than he did two weeks ago. More worn. But his eyes are clear when they find mine.

"You came," he says.

"You said there was more I needed to know."

He nods. Gestures to a fallen log. We sit like conspirators, which I suppose we are. The Order wouldn't approve of these meetings. Aldric certainly wouldn't. And Garicus—

I push the thought away. Focus on why I'm here.

"Tell me," I say.

Theron takes a breath. "The ritual the elders performed. The dragon summoning. It wasn't random desperation."

"You said they were starving. That the harvests failed."

"They did. But the cult's texts—the forbidden knowledge they used—those didn't appear by accident. Someone gave them to the elders. Someone with deep knowledge of dragon lore and the power to move through villages undetected."

My stomach drops. "The Order."

"A faction within it, yes." His voice is careful. Clinical, like he's reporting rather than confessing. "There are those in the Crimson Vigil who believe that small evils prevent greater ones. That allowing cults to form, to attempt rituals, to fail—it justifies the Order's existence. Proves they're necessary."

"That's insane."

"That's pragmatism." He meets my eyes. "How do you maintain power when the threats you fight become rare? You create new ones. Not directly—nothing so crude. But you leave breadcrumbs. Plant seeds. And when the harvest comes in blood and fire, you're there to clean it up."

I can't breathe. "You're saying the Order deliberately—"

"I'm saying I found evidence that the texts in Thornhaven came from a Vigil archive. That the timing of my report and the arrival of the knights was too precise. That when I told them the ritual had already happened, they didn't seem surprised." His hands clench. "And I'm saying that when I asked Aldric about it years later, he didn't deny it. He just said some choices are too complicated for men like me to understand."

The world tilts.

"Aldric knew."

"Aldric participated." Theron's voice is gentle. Horrible. "He was part of the raid, Solaire. He helped burn the village. And then he took you and Garicus as wards, raised you to serve the same system that orphaned you. That's not guilt. That's grooming."

No. No, that can't be—

But it fits. All of it fits. Aldric's reluctance to talk about the raid. His careful lessons about necessary evils and preventive justice. The way he watched us with that look—not love, but assessment. Like we were experiments.

"Why are you telling me this?" I can barely form words.

"Because you're asking the right questions. Because you deserve to know what you're serving. And because—" He stops. Looks away. "Because I failed you once. I won't fail you again by letting you stay blind."

"What do you want me to do with this?"

"That's your choice." He stands. "I've told you the truth as I understand it. What you do with it—whether you confront Aldric, whether you leave the Order, whether you dismiss it all as the ravings of a guilty father—that's yours to decide."

He starts to walk away.

"Wait." I stand too. "If this is true—if the Order really did this—then there must be others. Other villages. Other setups."

"There are." He turns back. "I've been tracking them for years. Trying to stop the next Thornhaven before it happens. Sometimes I succeed. Sometimes I'm too late. But I keep trying because it's the only thing I can do."

"You're fighting the entire Crimson Vigil."

"I'm fighting a corrupt faction within it." His smile is grim. "Not all knights are complicit. Some genuinely believe in protecting the realm. But the leadership, the inner circle—they're rotten. And they've been rotten for a long time."

I think about Garicus. About the argument this morning. About the choice he forced on me.

"My brother doesn't believe you," I say quietly. "He thinks you're manipulating me. Using my grief to turn me against the Order."

"Is he wrong?"

The question surprises me. "You're telling me the truth."

"I'm telling you what I believe is true. There's a difference." Theron's expression is complicated. "I'm also your father trying to atone for abandoning you. Those motivations can coexist. You should question me, Solaire. Question everything. Especially the people who tell you questioning is dangerous."

Like Garicus. Like Aldric.

"What if questioning breaks everything?" I ask. "What if the truth destroys the only stability I have?"

"Then you'll build something new." He steps closer. "Your brother loves you. Whatever happens between you two, that won't change. But love isn't the same as truth. Sometimes you have to choose which one you can live without."

He leaves then, walking into the trees until the shadows swallow him whole.

I stand in the oak grove until the sun sinks low, turning the sky to ash and ember. Promises in ash. That's what my whole life has been—promises made on burning ground, meant to hold against impossible weight.

The promise to Garicus: never alone, never apart.

The promise to the Order: service until death.

The promise to myself, unspoken but present since Blackwood: to understand what made me, no matter the cost.

They can't all be kept.

## Garicus

I don't sleep that night.

I lie in the dark of our room—my room now, maybe, I don't know anymore—and stare at the ceiling. The other bed is empty. Solaire didn't come back after the oak grove. Didn't come back for evening meal. Didn't come back at all.

Maybe he won't.

The thought sits in my chest like a stone. Heavy. Cold. Inevitable.

At some point I must sleep, because I wake to gray dawn light and the sound of the door opening.

Solaire steps inside. He looks haggard. Didn't sleep either, from the shadows under his eyes.

We stare at each other across the space that used to feel small and now seems vast.

"I'm not staying," he says.

The words don't surprise me. I think I knew, on some level, from the moment I found that letter. Knew that whatever Theron was offering him—truth, purpose, vindication—it was stronger than anything I could give.

"Where will you go?"

"I don't know yet. North, maybe. Theron mentioned other villages. Other places the Order might be... operating." He can't quite say the words. Can't quite admit what he's choosing.

"You're leaving the Order."

"I'm leaving Greystone." His voice is careful. "I haven't decided about the Order yet."

"That's a lie." I sit up. "You've already decided. You're just afraid to say it."

"Maybe." He looks at his hands. "I need time, Garicus. Time to think. To figure out what I believe without Aldric in my ear telling me how to think. Without you asking me to choose between—"

"Between us and him. I'm asking you to choose between us and him." I stand. Face him fully. "Don't pretend this is about needing space. You're choosing your father over the oath. Just be honest about it."

"I'm not—" He stops. Tries again. "It's not that simple."

"It's exactly that simple!" My hands fist. "Fifteen years, Solaire. Fifteen years we've had each other's backs. Fifteen years of that promise being the only thing that mattered. And you're throwing it away for a man who wasn't there! For conspiracy theories and maybe-truths and the chance—the chance—that Aldric is some kind of monster!"

"What if he is?"

The question stops me cold.

"What if Aldric really is complicit?" Solaire presses. "What if the Order really did engineer what happened to us? Don't you want to know? Don't you want justice?"

"I want you!" The confession rips out of me before I can stop it. "I don't care about justice or truth or any of it! I care that you're here, that you're safe, that we're together! That's all I've ever cared about!"

Solaire's face crumples. "I know. I know you do. But Garicus, I can't—I can't just ignore this. I can't serve the people who might have murdered our families for political gain. I can't pretend that's acceptable just because they fed us and trained us and gave us purpose!"

"So you'll destroy the only real family you have left chasing ghosts!"

"You'll always be my family." His voice breaks. "But I need to do this. I need to know. And I need you to—I need you to understand that I'm not abandoning you. I'm just... I'm taking a different path for a while."

"How long is 'a while'?"

He doesn't answer. Can't.

Because we both know the truth: once he leaves, there's no coming back. Not really. The fracture has already happened. We're just pretending it hasn't.

"I'm sorry," Solaire whispers. "I'm so sorry."

I want to tell him it's fine. That I understand. That I'll wait however long it takes for him to come back.

But the lies won't come.

"The promise," I say instead. My voice sounds distant. Not mine. "We swore never to let each other die alone. Do you remember?"

"Of course I remember."

"Then you should know you're already breaking it." I meet his eyes. "You're not dying by sword or fire. You're dying from the inside, letting Theron's truth poison everything we built. And I'm here, watching it happen, and I can't stop it. So tell me, Solaire—who's breaking the oath? You for leaving? Or me for not following?"

The question hangs unanswered.

Solaire picks up his pack. The one he must have prepared before he even came back here. He knew he was leaving. Came to say goodbye, not to be convinced.

"I'll send word," he says. "Let you know where I am. What I find."

"Don't." The word is harsh. Final. "If you're leaving, leave. Don't send letters like we're friends keeping in touch. We're brothers, Solaire. We don't get to do this halfway."

His hand tightens on the pack strap. "So this is it? I leave and we're just... done?"

"No." I turn away. Can't watch him go. "You left the moment you chose to believe Theron over me. This is just you admitting it."

I hear him move toward the door. Hear it open.

"Garicus—"

"Go."

"I love you. You know that, right? I love you and this isn't—"

"Go, Solaire. Please."

The door closes.

His footsteps fade.

And for the first time in fifteen years, I'm truly alone.

## Solaire

I make it three corridors before I have to stop.

My back hits the cold stone wall and I slide down, knees to chest, breath coming in gasps that might be sobs. Might be the sound of something essential breaking.

I'm doing this. I'm really doing this.

Leaving Garicus. Leaving Greystone. Leaving the only certainty I've had since I was seven years old and woke up in ash.

A figure appears at the end of the corridor. Aldric. Of course. Nothing happens in this Keep without him knowing.

He approaches slowly. Stops a careful distance away.

"You're leaving," he says. Not a question.

"Yes."

"May I ask where you're going?"

"North. Maybe east. I don't know yet."

Aldric nods. Like he expected this. Like he's been waiting for it.

"Your father has been telling you things," he says. "About the Order. About Thornhaven."

I look up sharply. "How did you—"

"I pay attention, Solaire. It's my job." He sits on the floor across from me, grunting with the effort. When did he get old? "He's not entirely wrong."

The admission knocks the breath from me. "What?"

"The Order's methods are... complex. Morally ambiguous at best. I've done things I'm not proud of. Participated in operations that might look, from certain angles, like the very evil we claim to fight." His eyes are tired. Ancient. "Your father sees conspiracy. I see pragmatism. The truth, as usual, lives somewhere in between."

"You knew the texts came from Vigil archives."

"I suspected. Never confirmed."

"You helped burn the village anyway."

"I followed orders. Then I spent fifteen years trying to atone by raising two boys to be better than I was." He leans his head back against the wall. "Whether that's grooming or genuine affection is a question I ask myself every day."

The honesty stuns me. "Why are you telling me this?"

"Because you're leaving regardless. Because Garicus is upstairs breaking apart and someone should understand why." He looks at me. "And because you remind me of myself at your age—convinced that truth mattered more than anything. That if I just understood enough, questioned enough, I could find the answer that made everything make sense."

"Did you?"

"No." The word is flat. Final. "I found complexity. Impossible choices. Good people doing terrible things for understandable reasons. Bad people doing good things for selfish ones. I found the world, Solaire. And it didn't care about my need for moral clarity."

"So I should just accept it? Stop questioning?"

"I'm saying you should count the cost." He stands, offers his hand. I take it. He pulls me up. "Garicus is your brother. Your true brother, forged in the same fire. Whatever truth you find out there, whatever answers you're searching for—they won't keep you warm at night. They won't fight beside you. They won't remember your mother's smile or the way Thornhaven smelled in autumn."

"He will," I finish.

"He will." Aldric squeezes my shoulder once. "Truth is cold comfort, son. Don't trade warmth for it unless you're certain you can survive the freeze."

He leaves then. Walks down the corridor and doesn't look back.

I stand in the empty hallway for a long time. Then I shoulder my pack and walk toward the Keep's gates.

I don't look back either.

## Garicus

Three days pass.

I train. Eat. Sleep. Repeat. The routine holds me together when nothing else does.

Aldric doesn't ask questions. Just adjusts the training roster so I'm working solo instead of partnered. The other knights give me space. They know. Everyone knows.

The one who swore to never be apart is gone.

On the fourth day, I walk to the wall. Our wall. The place where we reaffirmed the oath, where we sat and talked and pretended the world made sense.

I sit alone now. Watch the sun set. The sky turns red and gold—promises in ash, Solaire called it once. Beautiful and already burning.

A raven lands on the wall beside me. Looks at me with one black eye, tilts its head.

"He's not coming back," I tell it.

The raven caws once. Takes flight.

I watch it disappear into the distance and wonder if Solaire is watching the same sunset somewhere. Wonder if he's thinking of me. If he regrets leaving or if the truth he's chasing feels worth the cost.

I wonder if I'll ever stop waiting for him to come home.

The sun sinks below the horizon. Darkness settles over Greystone Keep.

And I sit in it, alone for the first time in fifteen years, learning what it means to survive when the only person you swore to protect chooses to walk away.

The oath is broken.

We're broken.

And there's nothing left to do but learn to live in the fracture.
