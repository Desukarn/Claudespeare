# Chapter 8: Blood and Duty

I count the days since Solaire came back from Blackwood.

Seven.

Seven days of watching him move through training like a ghost piloting a corpse. Seven days of stilted conversations and careful silences. Seven days of pretending I don't know exactly what broke inside him when he met his father.

He won't tell me. Not the details. Just that Theron confirmed everything—the cult, the ritual, the Order's response. That Aldric knew. Has always known.

And yesterday, Solaire confronted him.

I wasn't there. Solaire went alone, found Aldric in his quarters after evening meal, and they spoke for two hours behind a closed door. When Solaire emerged, his face was ash-pale and his hands were shaking. He walked past me without a word, went to our room, and hasn't spoken since.

Aldric, for his part, looks ten years older. I saw him this morning in the training yard, watching the younger initiates with the expression of a man attending his own funeral.

He knows I'm coming. Has to know. If Solaire confronted him, I will too. That's how it works—we're a matched pair, two halves of the same oath. What one does, the other follows.

Except this time, I'm not following to support. I'm following to understand.

Because unlike Solaire, I don't need to know if the raid was justified. I need to know why Aldric lied about it for fifteen years.

---

I find him where I knew I would: the chapel. Same place we swore our oaths eight years ago. The crimson candles are burning—fat rendered from executed mages, Aldric told us once when we asked. A reminder that every flame casts shadows.

He doesn't turn when I enter. Just stands before the altar, hands clasped behind his back, staring at the candles like they hold answers.

"Garicus." His voice is quiet. Resigned. "I wondered when you'd come."

"Did you." Not a question. I close the door behind me. The chapel suddenly feels very small.

"You and Solaire—you've never been far apart. Not in any way that matters." He finally turns to face me. The candlelight carves deep shadows under his eyes. "He came to me with questions. I assume you have your own."

"Different questions," I say.

"I'm listening."

I walk closer. Stop three paces from him. Close enough to see the lines grief has cut into his face, far enough to maintain the distance this conversation requires.

"How long have you known?" I ask. "About Theron. About why Thornhaven burned."

"Since the beginning." No hesitation. No attempt to soften it. "I was part of the raiding party. Twenty knights, sent to contain a dragon summoning ritual. We arrived too late to stop it, too early to save anyone."

My hands curl into fists. I force them open.

"Theron led you there."

"Yes. He'd been the village priest. Found evidence of the cult, came to us for help. We promised intervention. We delivered annihilation." Aldric's mouth twists. "Standard procedure for containment."

"And the survivors? Me and Solaire?"

"Unintentional. We swept the village twice, found no one. You were hidden well. By the time we left, you'd already emerged." He pauses. "I found you in the square, holding each other. Two boys in the ashes, making promises they were too young to understand."

The memory surfaces before I can stop it: Solaire's hand in mine, cold and small. The oath. _Never let each other die alone._

"Why did you take us?" The question comes out harder than I intended.

"Because I couldn't leave you." Simple. Final. "The others wanted to. Said orphans complicate missions, that the Order isn't a charity. I overruled them."

"Out of guilt."

"Out of guilt," he confirms. "We'd taken everything from you. The least I could do was offer you something in return."

"You offered us service to the organization that murdered our families." I hear the rage building in my voice, don't bother controlling it. "You trained us to become the very thing that destroyed us."

"Yes."

The admission lands like a blow. No justification. No defense. Just acknowledgment.

"You raised us on their graves," I say. The words taste like ash. "Did you think we'd never ask why?"

Aldric closes his eyes. When he opens them again, they're red-rimmed but dry. "I hoped you wouldn't. That you'd be strong enough to survive without ever learning the truth. That the life I gave you would be enough to balance what was taken." He laughs, bitter and brief. "I was a fool."

"You were a coward."

The word hangs between us. Aldric doesn't flinch.

"Yes," he says again. "I was. I am. I took you in to ease my conscience and told myself it was mercy. I trained you to fight the darkness and never told you I was part of it. I made you swear oaths to an Order that deals in preventive murder and called it justice." He meets my eyes. "I am everything you think I am, Garicus. And I'm sorry."

I want the apology to feel hollow. Want to dismiss it as too little, too late. But there's something in his voice—not just regret, but genuine anguish—that makes it impossible.

"Were they guilty?" I ask. "The villagers. The cult. Were they actually going to summon a dragon?"

"They'd already summoned one." Aldric's voice drops. "By the time we arrived, the ritual was complete. The dragon had manifested, killed thirteen children and half the village, then turned on the cultists when they lost control. We found corpses and survivors in roughly equal measure."

"So you killed the survivors."

"We executed the cult members and silenced the witnesses. Yes."

"My mother—was she—"

"I don't know." And that might be the worst answer. "We didn't ask questions. Didn't check innocence or guilt. Everyone left alive became a threat to contain. So we contained them."

The chapel walls seem to close in. I force myself to breathe.

"Why?" The question breaks on the way out. "If the dragon was already dead, if the ritual failed, why burn everything? Why not just arrest the cultists? Why murder everyone?"

"Because knowledge is contagion." Aldric speaks like he's reciting doctrine. "The texts they used, the ritual components, the witnesses who saw dragon summoning work—all of it had to be destroyed. Can't risk it spreading. Can't let desperate villages in other regions think forbidden magic might save them." He gestures at the candles. "We burn it all and let the ashes scatter. That's what the Order does."

"That's what you did to us."

"Yes."

I want to hit him. Want to draw my sword and make him defend himself, make him fight back, make him be something other than this broken man accepting blame like penance.

I don't move.

"Did you know?" I ask. "When you took us in. When you taught us to fight. When you made us swear oaths. Did you know we'd find out eventually?"

"I suspected." He runs a hand over his face. "Secrets like that don't stay buried. Not when the people carrying them get close. I trained you hoping you'd be strong enough to survive the truth when it came."

"Strong enough to forgive you, you mean."

"No." The word is sharp. Certain. "I never expected forgiveness. I trained you to survive losing me. Because I knew when the truth came out, I'd lose both of you."

The admission sits heavy in the air.

"Solaire already hates you," I say.

"I know."

"He thinks the Order is evil. Thinks what you did was murder."

"It was."

"He's questioning everything. His oaths. His service. Whether any of it means anything."

Aldric nods slowly. "That's the difference between you and him. Always has been."

I frown. "What difference?"

"Solaire needs to believe in the righteousness of what we do. Needs the work to be just, the cause to be pure." Aldric looks at me with something that might be understanding. "You don't. You never have. You believe in pragmatism. In doing what's necessary to survive."

"You don't know what I believe."

"I've watched you for fifteen years, Garicus. I know exactly what you believe." He steps closer. "You believe in Solaire. In protecting him. In keeping your promise. Everything else is secondary. The Order, justice, even truth—none of it matters as much as that oath you made in the ashes."

I want to deny it. Can't.

"You're right," I say. "About that part."

"Then let me ask you something." Aldric's voice is careful. Probing. "Knowing what you know now—would you change it? Would you trade the life I gave you, the training, the purpose, to go back and die in Thornhaven with the others?"

The question is a knife between the ribs.

"No," I admit.

"Would you trade it to have been found by someone else? Raised differently?"

I think about Solaire. About the promise. About fifteen years of fighting side by side, sleeping in the same room, knowing someone has my back absolutely and without question.

"No," I say again.

"Then what are you angry about?"

The question stops me cold.

What am I angry about?

Not the raid. I understand why it happened—can see the logic, however brutal. A village practicing dragon summoning is a threat. Containment makes sense even if the method was monstrous.

Not being taken in. However guilty Aldric's motives, he gave us a life when we had nothing. Trained us. Fed us. Made us into something other than victims.

Not even the Order's methods. I've seen enough in five years to know that mercy is a luxury and preventive justice, while ugly, prevents worse outcomes.

So what's left?

The lie.

"You never told us," I say quietly. "Fifteen years, and you never gave us the chance to choose. You decided we were better off not knowing. You made that choice for us."

"Yes."

"Like we were children."

"You were children."

"We're not anymore." My voice rises. "We haven't been for years. But you kept lying. Kept pretending you were just a knight who found orphans, not the man who created them."

Aldric's expression cracks. Just for a moment, but I see it—the guilt he's been carrying, the weight of fifteen years of deception.

"You're right," he says. "I should have told you. Years ago. Should have trusted you with the truth."

"Why didn't you?"

"Because I'm a coward." Simple. Devastating. "Because telling you meant watching you look at me the way Solaire looked at me yesterday. The way you're looking at me now. And I—" His voice breaks. "I couldn't bear it."

The admission is so raw, so honest, that I have to look away.

We stand in silence. The candles gutter. Somewhere in the keep, a bell rings—change of watch, or evening meal, or some other marker of time passing while we stand here among the ruins of trust.

"What happens now?" I ask finally.

"That's up to you." Aldric straightens. "If you want to leave the Order, I'll help you. Both of you. Falsify records, provide coin, whatever you need to start over somewhere the Crimson Vigil won't find you."

"You'd do that?"

"I owe you that much. More than that."

I consider it. Leaving. Taking Solaire and walking away from Greystone, from the oaths, from the man who raised us on lies and blood. Starting fresh somewhere no one knows our names.

It's tempting.

But.

"Solaire's changing," I say. "Meeting his father, learning the truth—it's breaking something in him. Something I don't know how to fix."

Aldric nods. "Solaire wants the world to make sense. Wants good and evil to be clear, choices to have right answers. Learning that his father tried to do good and caused horror anyway—that the Order is both savior and destroyer—it conflicts with how he sees the world."

"And me?"

"You've always known the world doesn't make sense. That's your strength." Aldric's eyes are sharp now. Assessing. "But it's also your weakness. Because you're so focused on survival, on keeping your promise to Solaire, that you don't see what's happening."

"Which is?"

"He's pulling away. Not from you—never from you. But from everything else. The Order. The oaths. The life we built." Aldric pauses. "If you're not careful, you'll lose him to his own disillusionment."

The fear that's been gnawing at my gut for seven days crystallizes into certainty.

He's right.

Solaire is changing. Has been since he got that letter. Since he met his father. Since he learned that the people who saved us also damned us.

And I don't know how to pull him back.

"What do I do?" The question comes out smaller than I intended.

"Be there." Aldric's voice softens. "That's all you've ever been able to do. Be there when he needs you. Remind him what matters. Don't let him drown in the questions."

"What if the questions are bigger than the answers?"

"Then you hold him while he drowns and make sure he knows he's not alone." Aldric meets my eyes. "That's what you promised him, isn't it? Never alone. No matter what."

The oath. Always the oath.

"I hate you," I say. Not loud. Not angry. Just true. "For lying. For taking us. For making us into this."

"I know."

"But I understand why you did it."

"I know that too."

"That doesn't mean I forgive you."

Aldric nods. "I don't expect you to."

I turn to leave. Stop at the door.

"Aldric."

"Yes?"

"If Solaire wants to leave—if he can't stay in the Order after this—I'm going with him."

"I know."

"And if that means walking away from you, from everything you taught us—"

"I know," Aldric says again. His voice is quiet. Resigned. "I've always known that, Garicus. You made your choice fifteen years ago in the ashes. I was just borrowing you. Both of you. Until you decided you didn't need me anymore."

I should feel vindicated. Should feel like I've won something.

I just feel tired.

"Solaire needs time," I say. "Don't push him. Don't ask him to make decisions yet."

"I won't."

"And if you lie to us again—"

"I won't." Aldric's voice is steel. "You have my word. No more secrets. No more lies. Whatever you ask me, I'll answer truthfully."

"Even if the truth destroys us?"

"Especially then." He smiles, sad and exhausted. "I've learned my lesson, Garicus. Lies only delay the destruction. Truth at least gives you a chance to build something after."

I nod once. Leave.

The corridor outside the chapel is cold. Empty. I walk through it without seeing, my mind caught on Aldric's words.

_He's pulling away._

Seven days of silence. Seven days of Solaire moving through the world like he's not quite present. Seven days of watching my brother disappear into questions I can't answer.

I find him on the wall. Our wall. The place where we reaffirmed our oath years ago. He's sitting with his legs dangling over the edge, staring at nothing.

"Hey," I say.

He doesn't turn. "Hey."

I sit beside him. For a long time, neither of us speaks.

"I talked to Aldric," I finally say.

"I figured."

"He told me everything. The raid. Why he took us. Why he lied."

Solaire nods. His face is unreadable.

"Are you angry?" he asks.

"Yes."

"At him?"

"At all of it." I lean back against the stone. "At Aldric for lying. At your father for leading the Order there. At the cult for starting this whole mess. At the universe for burning our village and thinking that was fair."

"But you understand why Aldric did it."

It's not a question. Solaire knows me too well.

"Yeah," I admit. "I understand."

"I don't." His voice is small. Lost. "I keep trying. Keep thinking about what he said—containment, prevention, the greater good. And logically, I see it. But emotionally—" He stops. Swallows hard. "I can't reconcile the man who raised me with the man who killed my mother."

"They're the same man," I say. "People are complicated. They can be both."

"That's what terrifies me." Solaire finally looks at me. His eyes are red. "If Aldric can be both savior and murderer, if my father can be both victim and accomplice—how do I know who I am? How do I know if the oath I swore to the Order means anything? How do I know if I'm doing good or just becoming another person who justifies atrocities?"

The questions hang in the air, huge and unanswerable.

"I don't know," I say.

"That's not helpful."

"I know." I reach over, grab his hand. "But I know one thing. The oath we made to each other—that one's real. That one matters. Whatever else is complicated, that's simple."

Solaire's fingers tighten on mine. "What if I want to leave? Walk away from the Order, from Greystone, from all of this?"

My heart stutters. But I keep my voice steady.

"Then we leave together."

"Garicus—"

"No." I turn to face him fully. "Fifteen years ago we promised never to let each other die alone. That promise doesn't have conditions. Doesn't depend on where we are or who we serve. You leave, I follow. That's how it works."

"That's not fair to you."

"I don't care about fair." I squeeze his hand. "I care about keeping you alive. Keeping us together. That's all I've ever cared about."

Solaire's face crumples. He pulls his hand free, presses it to his eyes.

"I don't know what to do," he whispers. "I'm so angry. At Aldric. At my father. At the Order. At myself for not seeing it sooner. I don't know how to make it make sense."

"Then don't." I put my arm around his shoulders. "Don't try to make sense of it. Just... survive it. One day at a time. And when you figure out what you want, we'll do it. Together."

He leans against me. Small. Broken. Nothing like the confident knight who left for Blackwood seven days ago.

"I'm scared," he admits.

"Of what?"

"Of losing you. Of this changing us. Of not being able to go back to how things were."

I close my eyes. Feel the weight of him against my side, solid and real and still here.

"Things have already changed," I say quietly. "We can't go back. But we can go forward. And wherever that is, I'll be there."

"Promise?"

"I already did. Fifteen years ago." I pull him closer. "Never alone, Sol. No matter what comes next."

We sit like that as the sun sets, two brothers who survived fire and lies and the slow death of certainty. Below us, the keep continues its rhythms. Training. Eating. Sleeping. The Order's heartbeat, steady and unaware.

But up here, on this wall where we once swore to serve, we hold each other and know that oath means nothing compared to the one we made in the ashes.

Aldric was right about one thing: I've always known what I believe.

I believe in Solaire.

Everything else is just noise.
