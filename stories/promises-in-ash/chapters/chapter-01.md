# Chapter 1: The Raid

The smell came first.

Garicus was helping Mother sort the autumn turnips when the smoke slipped through the cracks in the shutters. Not the good smoke from cooking fires or the forge. Wrong smoke. Bitter.

"Inside," Mother said. Not loud. Quiet, the way she spoke when Father came home angry.

He dropped the turnip. It rolled under the table.

Mother crossed to the window, one hand on the shutter. She went very still. Garicus knew that stillness. Rabbits did it when hawks circled.

"Garicus." Her voice came out strange. Too calm. "The cellar. Now."

"But—"

"Now."

She grabbed his wrist and pulled him across the room. The cellar door was beside the hearth, a heavy wooden square set into the floor. Mother yanked it open. The ladder disappeared into darkness.

"Down."

"Mother, what's—"

A scream cut through the air outside. High and sharp and suddenly silent.

Mother shoved him toward the ladder. Garicus caught the top rung, descended three steps, looked up. She knelt at the opening, her face backlit by firelight from the hearth.

"Listen to me." Her hands shook. She gripped the edge of the door to steady them. "You stay down here. You stay quiet. You don't come up until the sun comes through the cracks. Do you understand?"

"I don't—"

"Promise me, Garicus."

He'd never heard her voice break like that.

"I promise."

She pulled something from her apron. The small iron key Father used for the lockbox. She pressed it into his palm, closed his fingers around it.

"Keep this. Don't lose it. Your father will—" She stopped. Started again. "Just keep it safe."

More screams now. Closer. Men shouting in a language Garicus didn't know.

Mother kissed his forehead. Her lips were cold.

Then she slammed the cellar door shut.

Darkness swallowed him whole.

Garicus clung to the ladder, heart hammering against his ribs. Above, Mother's footsteps crossed the floor. Something heavy scraped—the table, dragged over the cellar door. Hiding it.

Then her footsteps ran toward the front of the house.

The door crashed open.

"Please," Mother said. "We have nothing, we're not—"

A man's voice, rough and indifferent. Words Garicus couldn't understand.

Mother screamed.

Garicus pressed his forehead against the ladder rung. The wood bit into his skin. He wanted to climb up, to push through the door, to—

To what? He was eight years old. Small for his age. He had no weapon, no strength, no—

Metal on metal. A wet sound. Mother's scream cut short.

Something heavy hit the floor above him.

Garicus bit his lip until he tasted blood. Don't make a sound. Don't make a sound. She said stay quiet. She made him promise.

Footsteps crossed overhead. Floorboards creaked. Someone kicked over a chair. The sound of drawers yanked open, contents spilling out.

Then the footsteps moved away. The door slammed. Voices outside, growing distant.

But not silent.

Garicus descended the rest of the ladder on shaking legs. The cellar floor was hard-packed dirt, damp and cold. He couldn't see anything. The darkness was absolute, thick as water.

He found the wall with his hands and pressed his back against it. Drew his knees up to his chest. Wrapped his arms around them.

And waited.

Screams echoed through Thornhaven. Men and women both. Children crying for their mothers. The crackle of fire grew louder, closer. Heat seeped through the floorboards above. The cellar filled with smoke.

Garicus pulled his shirt up over his nose and mouth. His eyes burned. He squeezed them shut, but tears came anyway.

Father was at the fields today, checking the late harvest. He wouldn't be back until evening. Would he run when he saw the smoke? Would he try to fight?

Would he come for Garicus?

No. Don't think about it. Mother said wait until morning. Mother said—

Another scream, this one so close Garicus felt it in his bones. He recognized the voice.

Mara. The baker's daughter. She was nine. She'd given him a honey cake last market day, laughed when he got it all over his face.

The scream ended.

Garicus shoved his fist into his mouth to keep from making a sound.

Hours crawled past. Or maybe minutes. Time stopped making sense in the dark. The screaming gradually faded. The fire sounds changed—less crackling, more the deep roar of buildings fully ablaze. The smoke thickened. Garicus could barely breathe.

He thought about climbing the ladder. Just to see. Just to check if Mother—

No. He promised.

So he stayed.

Eventually, the sounds changed again. Hoofbeats, leaving. The thunder of many horses riding away at speed. Shouts in the distance, orders given. Then, slowly, quiet.

The fire kept burning. Garicus could hear it eating through Thornhaven, timber by timber. Sometimes something collapsed with a crash. But no more screams.

Silence was worse than screaming.

Because silence meant no one was left to scream.

Garicus pressed his face against his knees and tried not to think. Tried not to imagine what was up there. Tried not to remember the wet sound Mother made when she stopped screaming.

He must have slept. When he opened his eyes, thin gray light filtered through the cracks around the cellar door. Dawn.

Mother said wait for morning.

It was morning.

Garicus climbed the ladder on numb legs. At the top, he pressed his shoulder against the door and pushed. It didn't budge. The table. Mother had moved the table.

He pushed harder. The door lifted an inch. He got his fingers into the gap, braced his feet on the ladder rungs, and shoved with everything he had.

The table scraped aside. The cellar door fell open.

Smoke poured down. Garicus choked, scrambled up and out, fell to his hands and knees on the floor.

The house was gone.

Not collapsed. Not burned to the foundation. Just—hollowed. The walls still stood, blackened and skeletal. The roof had caved in. Gray morning light filtered through the gaps, illuminating ash and char.

The hearth still stood. The table was overturned beside the cellar door, scorched but intact.

And there, near the front door—

Garicus looked away.

He knew he should check. Should see if maybe, somehow—

But he knew. The stillness told him. The silence.

He stumbled to the door, or where the door had been. The frame remained. The door itself lay in the yard, kicked off its hinges.

Thornhaven was ash.

The buildings that hadn't burned to the ground were burning still, sluggish flames eating through what remained. Bodies lay in the street. Too many to count. Garicus recognized some: Master Torven the blacksmith, face-down outside his forge. Old Willem who told stories at the tavern, sprawled in the mud.

Others he couldn't identify. Didn't want to.

Garicus walked into the street. His feet moved without thought. The morning air was thick with smoke and the smell of burned meat. He should feel something—horror, grief, rage. But there was only a strange, distant numbness.

Everyone was dead.

Father wouldn't come home. Mother was—

He didn't finish the thought.

A sound broke through the silence. Quiet. Almost a whimper.

Garicus stopped. Listened.

There. To the right, near the baker's shop—what was left of it.

He picked his way through the rubble. The bakery had collapsed completely, just a pile of blackened beams and shattered stone. The oven chimney still stood, impossibly intact amid the destruction.

The sound came again. Weak. Barely human.

Garicus climbed over a fallen beam. His hands came away black with soot.

A boy lay half-buried in the rubble. Smaller than Garicus, maybe seven years old. Blond hair matted with blood and ash. Eyes closed. Breathing in shallow gasps.

Solaire. The baker's son. Garicus knew him by sight—they'd never spoken. Solaire was always trailing after his mother, asking questions, laughing at everything.

He wasn't laughing now.

Garicus knelt beside him. "Can you hear me?"

No response.

Garicus grabbed Solaire's shoulders and pulled. The boy came free of the rubble with a scrape of stone. He was light, too light. Garicus dragged him away from the collapsed building, into the street.

Solaire's eyes flickered open. Blue, pale as winter sky. They stared at nothing for a moment, unfocused. Then found Garicus.

"Mother?" Solaire's voice came out cracked, barely a whisper.

Garicus looked back at the bakery ruins. At the bodies in the street. At Thornhaven, burning and silent and dead.

"No," he said.

Solaire's face crumpled. He tried to sit up, fell back. "Father? Mara?"

"No."

"Anyone?"

Garicus met his eyes. "Just us."

Solaire stared at him for a long moment. Then he started to cry. Not loud, hysterical sobs. Quiet, broken sounds that barely made it past his lips.

Garicus sat beside him in the ash and said nothing. What was there to say?

They were eight and seven years old. Alone in a graveyard that used to be home.

Eventually, Solaire's crying faded to hiccups, then silence. He sat up slowly, wincing. Blood crusted the side of his head. More on his arm. But he could move. That was something.

"Why?" Solaire asked. His voice was small. Lost. "Why did they do this?"

Garicus didn't answer. How could he? He was a child. He didn't understand why men killed, why they burned villages, why Mother had to die.

He only understood one thing, sitting there in the ruins: he was alive, and he shouldn't be.

Everyone else was dead. Mother. Father. All of Thornhaven. Dead because Garicus hid in a cellar while they screamed.

The numbness cracked. Guilt poured in like water into a drowning man's lungs.

"I hid," Garicus said. His voice sounded strange. Flat. "Mother told me to hide, and I did."

Solaire looked at him. "You're alive."

"Everyone else is dead."

"We're alive," Solaire said. Like it mattered. Like being alive meant something when everyone you loved was ash.

But then Solaire reached out and grabbed Garicus's hand. His fingers were small and cold and covered in soot.

"Don't leave me," Solaire whispered. "Please. Don't leave me alone."

Garicus looked at their joined hands. Black with ash. Shaking.

He thought about the cellar. The darkness. The screams. The hours waiting alone, not knowing if anyone else survived, if anyone would ever come.

He thought about Solaire, waking up buried in rubble, calling for his family, finding only silence and bodies.

Alone was worse than death.

"I won't," Garicus said.

It wasn't enough. Not for what they'd lost. Not for the emptiness that used to be home.

So he said it again, and this time he meant it like Mother meant her final words. Like a promise that mattered more than fear or sense.

"I won't let you die alone," Garicus said. "Ever. I promise."

Solaire's hand tightened on his. Tears cut clean lines through the ash on his face.

"I promise too," Solaire said. "We stay together. No matter what. We don't—" His voice broke. He tried again. "We don't let each other die alone."

Garicus nodded. Found Solaire's other hand and gripped both tight.

"We stay together," Garicus repeated.

They sat like that as the sun rose higher, two boys in the ash and ruin of everything they'd known, holding on to the only thing left: each other.

The fires burned low. Smoke drifted across Thornhaven's corpse. Somewhere in the distance, a raven called. The sound echoed through empty streets.

Garicus didn't let go.

Neither did Solaire.

They would wait for someone to come. A survivor, a traveler, anyone. And when they came—if they came—Garicus and Solaire would still be together.

Because that was the promise.

The only promise that mattered anymore.

Dawn light filtered through the smoke, pale and cold. The ashes settled. The fires died.

And two boys sat vigil over the grave of their world, bound by an oath they didn't yet understand, holding each other in the silence that followed the screaming.

Together.

Always together.

No matter what came next.

## Chapter Notes

**Plot advancement:**
- Establishes the inciting incident: the raid that destroys Thornhaven and orphans both boys
- Sets up the core relationship between Garicus and Solaire through their oath
- Creates the foundational trauma that will drive both characters throughout the story
- Ends with them waiting for rescue (sets up Aldric's arrival in Chapter 2)

**Character development:**
- Garicus's pragmatic survival instinct established (follows Mother's orders exactly, stays hidden despite horror)
- His survivor's guilt begins here (alive while everyone else died)
- His protective nature emerges (finds Solaire, makes the promise)
- Solaire's need for connection and fear of isolation introduced (don't leave me alone)
- Both characters' core dynamic: Solaire reaches out, Garicus responds with loyalty

**World reveals:**
- The violence and brutality of raids in this world
- Thornhaven as a small, isolated village (everyone knows everyone)
- The raiders speak a foreign language (suggests organized force, not bandits)
- No apparent survivors except the two boys (sets up questions about why the village was targeted)
- Cultural importance of promises (both boys treating the oath as sacred immediately)

**Writing notes:**
- Kept sentences short and direct during action/horror sequences
- Used longer, more contemplative sentences during quiet moments in cellar
- Emphasized sensory details (smoke smell, darkness, sounds) to ground reader in Garicus's POV
- Child's perspective maintained: doesn't understand why, only experiences what
- Oath scene kept simple but weighted with emotion—no flowery language, just desperate children
