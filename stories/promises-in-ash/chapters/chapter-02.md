# Chapter 2: The Knight

The first thing Solaire knew was pain.

It bloomed behind his eyes like fire, sharp and immediate. He tried to move and couldn't—his body felt distant, disconnected. Heavy. Something pressed against his face, rough and gritty. Stone? No. Ash.

He opened his eyes to gray.

Gray sky above, choked with smoke. Gray dust coating everything—his hands, his clothes, the rubble surrounding him. And sitting beside him, knees drawn up, face streaked with soot and tears, was Garicus.

"You're awake." Garicus's voice was small. Broken.

Solaire tried to sit up. The world tilted violently, and Garicus's hands were suddenly on his shoulders, steadying him. "Slow. Don't... don't move too fast."

"What—" Solaire's throat burned. He coughed, tasted ash and blood. "What happened?"

Garicus didn't answer. His eyes slid away, focusing on something past Solaire's shoulder. Following his gaze, Solaire saw it: the ruin of his village. Collapsed houses. Charred timber. Bodies—

He looked away, bile rising.

"They came at night," Garicus whispered. "Men in armor. They were looking for... I don't know. Something. Someone. They burned everything."

Fragments surfaced in Solaire's memory. His mother's voice, urgent and afraid. The sound of boots on cobblestones. A shout. Then impact—something striking his head—and darkness.

"My mother," Solaire said. The words scraped out of him. "Where is—"

"Gone." Garicus's voice was flat. Final. "Everyone's gone. It's just us."

The words didn't make sense. Couldn't make sense. Solaire looked around wildly, searching for movement, for anyone else alive in the ruins. There was nothing. Only ash and silence and the two of them, small and fragile amid the devastation.

"We made a promise," Garicus said suddenly. His hands gripped Solaire's shoulders tighter. "Last night. Do you remember? We promised we'd never let each other die alone."

Solaire didn't remember. But he nodded anyway, because Garicus looked so desperate, so terrifyingly alone, and Solaire understood with sudden clarity that he felt exactly the same.

"I remember," he lied.

Garicus's shoulders sagged with relief. "Good. Because we're all we have now."

They sat together in the ash, two boys who had barely known each other before the world ended, clinging to a promise neither of them fully understood. The smoke drifted. The sun climbed higher, revealing more of the destruction. And still, no one came.

Until the hoofbeats.

---

Garicus heard them first. He went rigid, eyes wide with sudden fear. "Hide," he hissed, pulling Solaire behind a collapsed wall.

"Maybe they're here to help—"

"Maybe they're the ones who did this." Garicus's voice was sharp, brooking no argument. They pressed against the rubble, breathing shallow, as the sound grew closer.

Three riders emerged from the smoke. Knights, armored in steel plate, crimson cloaks billowing behind them. The lead rider sat tall in his saddle, gray-bearded and grim-faced, his gaze sweeping the ruins with practiced efficiency. He raised a hand, and the others stopped.

"Spread out," he commanded. His voice carried despite its quietness—a voice used to being obeyed. "Search for survivors. And search for the altar."

The altar? Solaire glanced at Garicus, confused, but Garicus was staring at the knights with something like hatred.

The knights dismounted, moving methodically through the rubble. One poked at a collapsed doorway with his sword. Another bent to examine something on the ground—a scrap of fabric, or perhaps bone. The leader stood apart, scanning the horizon as if expecting something.

"Please," Solaire breathed, so quietly only Garicus could hear. "They might help us."

"They might kill us."

"Why would they—"

"I don't know!" Garicus's whisper cracked. "I don't know anything anymore. But I'm not taking chances."

Solaire wanted to argue. Wanted to believe that armored men with noble bearing had come to save them, to make sense of the senseless. But Garicus's fear was infectious, and beneath it, Solaire felt his own terror—what if these were the same men who had come in the night? What if they had unfinished business?

One of the knights called out. "Ser Aldric! Bodies in the square. Signs of ritual."

The gray-bearded man—Aldric—moved toward the voice, his expression darkening. Solaire watched him go, this stranger who might be salvation or doom, and made a choice.

He stood up.

"Solaire—" Garicus grabbed for him, but Solaire was already stumbling forward, legs unsteady, voice cracking as he called out.

"Please! Help us!"

All three knights turned. Hands went to sword hilts. Aldric's eyes locked onto Solaire, sharp and assessing, and for a moment Solaire was certain he'd made a fatal mistake.

Then Aldric's expression shifted—just slightly, a crack in the stern facade. "Lower your weapons," he said quietly. "He's a child."

Garicus appeared beside Solaire, moving to place himself between his friend and the knights. Protective even in terror. Aldric noticed. His gaze moved between the two boys, lingering on their faces, their ash-covered clothes, the way they stood together like the last two stones in a collapsing wall.

"What are your names?" Aldric asked.

"Solaire." His voice was barely a whisper.

"Garicus." Defiant, despite everything.

Aldric dismounted slowly, deliberately non-threatening. He approached them with the careful movements of someone gentling a frightened animal. When he was close enough, he knelt—bringing himself to their eye level. Solaire saw his face clearly then: weathered, scarred, but not cruel. Just tired. So very tired.

"How many others?" Aldric asked.

"None," Garicus said. "Just us."

"Your families?"

"Dead." The word fell like a stone.

Aldric closed his eyes briefly. When he opened them again, something had changed in his expression—grief, perhaps, or guilt. "I am sorry," he said, and it sounded like he meant it. "No child should see what you have seen."

One of the other knights approached, younger, eager. "Ser, we found the altar. Bloodstains. Ritual markings consistent with—"

"Not now, Loras." Aldric's voice was steel.

The young knight blinked, confused, but stepped back.

Aldric turned his attention back to the boys. "Can you ride?"

Solaire nodded hesitantly. Garicus said nothing.

"The village is gone," Aldric said. "There is nothing left for you here. The Order will send someone to investigate, to document, to..." He trailed off, as if realizing how hollow it all sounded. "You cannot stay here. Do you understand?"

"Where would we go?" Solaire asked.

Aldric was quiet for a long moment. His fellow knights were watching now, waiting. Clearly, this was not standard procedure. Clearly, whatever came next would break protocol.

"With me," Aldric said finally. "I will take you to Greystone Keep. You will be wards of the Order."

"Ser Aldric," the older knight began, "that's not—"

"I know what it is and isn't, Marcus." Aldric rose to his feet, his hand resting on the pommel of his sword—not a threat, just a statement. "These boys are survivors of a massacre we failed to prevent. I will not leave them to die of exposure or be taken by slavers. If the commander takes issue, he may address it with me."

Marcus looked like he wanted to argue. Instead, he sighed. "Your conscience, Ser. Your burden."

"Yes," Aldric said quietly. "It is."

---

They left within the hour.

Garicus refused to ride with any of the knights, so Aldric fashioned a makeshift seat behind his own saddle. Solaire rode with Marcus, who said nothing but whose grip on the reins was steady and sure. The third knight, Loras, ranged ahead, scouting.

Solaire looked back once as they rode away. The village—his village, though it felt like someone else's life now—was already fading into the smoke. He tried to remember his mother's face and found the memory slipping, replaced by ash and ruin.

He turned forward and didn't look back again.

They rode in silence. The countryside was barren, winter-dead fields stretching in all directions. Occasionally they passed other travelers—merchants, farmers—who gave the crimson-cloaked knights a wide berth. No one asked about the two ash-covered children.

As the sun began to set, Aldric called a halt. They made camp in a clearing beside a frozen stream, the knights moving with practiced efficiency to set up tents and start a fire. Solaire and Garicus sat together on a fallen log, watching the flames. Neither of them spoke. There were no words for what they'd lost.

Aldric approached with two bowls of stew. He handed them over without comment, then settled himself across the fire. The silence stretched.

"Why did you take us?" Garicus asked finally. His voice was hard, suspicious.

Aldric was quiet for a moment. "Because you needed help."

"That's not an answer."

"It is the only answer I can give you."

Garicus glared at him. Aldric met his gaze steadily, without anger or defensiveness. Just acceptance.

Solaire, emboldened by the warmth of the fire and the food in his belly, asked the question that had been gnawing at him. "The knight—Loras—he mentioned an altar. What did he mean?"

Aldric's jaw tightened. "Something that no longer concerns you."

"Our village burned and you say it doesn't concern us?"

"Your village burned because evil men made evil choices." Aldric's voice was measured, careful. "That evil is being dealt with. You need only concern yourselves with surviving."

"How do we do that?" Solaire pressed. "We have nothing. No family. No home. You say we're wards, but what does that even mean?"

Aldric regarded him for a long moment. Then he said, "It means you will be fed. Clothed. Trained, if you have the stomach for it. It means you will learn to protect yourselves and others. And it means—" He paused, seeming to choose his words with care. "—it means you will have a place in this world, when so many would cast you aside."

"At what cost?" Garicus asked.

"Discipline. Obedience. Service." Aldric's gaze was unflinching. "The Order does not take without expecting something in return. But neither does it abandon those under its protection."

It wasn't a reassuring answer. But it was honest, and somehow that mattered more.

They finished their meal in silence. The other knights settled into their own routines—Marcus sharpening his blade, Loras checking the perimeter. Aldric sat by the fire, staring into the flames with an expression that might have been regret.

Solaire leaned against Garicus, exhausted beyond words. Garicus's arm came around him automatically, protective even in sleep.

"We'll be okay," Solaire whispered. Trying to convince himself as much as Garicus.

Garicus said nothing. But his arm tightened, and that was answer enough.

---

They reached Greystone Keep three days later.

It rose from the barren landscape like a fist of stone—gray walls, gray towers, gray battlements under a gray sky. No banners flew. No color broke the monotony. It looked, Solaire thought, like a place where hope went to die.

"Welcome home," Marcus said dryly as they rode through the gates.

The courtyard was filled with activity—knights training, stablehands leading horses, servants carrying supplies. Everyone wore variations of the same crimson and gray. Everyone moved with purpose. It was overwhelming after the silence of the ruins.

Aldric dismounted and helped the boys down. He gestured to a severe-looking woman in gray robes. "Matron Elys will see to your immediate needs. Food, baths, beds. Tomorrow we'll discuss your training."

"Training for what?" Solaire asked.

Aldric's expression was unreadable. "For survival. For justice. For whatever path the world sets before you." He paused. "The boy you were died in that village. The man you will become is forged here."

It should have sounded inspirational. Instead, it sounded like a sentence.

Matron Elys led them away, through cold stone corridors that echoed with their footsteps. She showed them to a small room with two narrow beds, a washbasin, and a single window overlooking the training yard. Austere. Functional. Safe.

"Rest," she commanded. "Tomorrow begins your new life."

She left, closing the door behind her with a soft click.

Solaire and Garicus stood in the center of the room, two boys in a strange place, everything familiar stripped away. Then, moving in silent agreement, they pushed the beds together. They climbed onto the makeshift pallet and lay side by side, staring at the ceiling.

"Do you think we'll ever go back?" Solaire whispered.

"There's nothing to go back to," Garicus said.

"I know. But... do you think we'll want to? Someday?"

Garicus turned his head to look at Solaire. His eyes were old beyond his years, haunted by things no child should remember. "I think we'll spend the rest of our lives trying not to."

Solaire wanted to argue. Wanted to believe they could heal, could move forward, could become something other than victims of a massacre. But exhaustion dragged him down, and the words wouldn't come.

He closed his eyes. Beside him, Garicus's breathing slowly evened out.

Outside, the sounds of the Keep continued—metal on metal, shouted commands, the rhythms of a place built on discipline and violence. This was their world now. These gray stones and crimson cloaks. This man Aldric, with his careful words and haunted eyes.

Solaire's last thought before sleep took him was a question: What had they been saved for?

Only time would tell.

And in the morning, the answer would begin.
