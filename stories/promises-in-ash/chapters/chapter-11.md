# Chapter 11: Somewhere No One's Burning

The chains on my father's wrists are too heavy for what he is now.

He shuffles between Garicus and me as we walk the long road back to Greystone Keep, his priest's robes torn and stained with mud from the scuffle last night. We stopped him. Garicus and I, together, stopped him from burning another village on nothing but paranoid conviction and fifteen years of guilt transformed into crusade.

The village was innocent. We found the proof—no cult, no ritual texts, just farmers frightened by their own shadows and Theron's accusations. My father, broken by what he did to Thornhaven, had been seeing evil everywhere. Creating it where it didn't exist, trying to prevent horrors that lived only in his mind.

He doesn't resist. Hasn't spoken since Garicus restrained him while I searched the evidence. Since I looked him in the eye and said, "No more."

The worst part is understanding. I understand how guilt can calcify into paranoia. How trying to atone for one massacre might drive you to justify another. How the man who tried to save Thornhaven by betraying it could become the man who'd burn innocents again because the first betrayal never stopped hurting.

I understand.

And I'm still bringing him to the Order for judgment.

Garicus hasn't said much either. We're not fighting anymore—the argument that split us dissolved last night when he tracked me to the village Theron was targeting. I was already there, already stopping my father, already making the choice Garicus feared I wouldn't make.

He showed up ready to drag me home. Found me binding Theron's hands instead.

"You came back," was all he said.

"I never left," I replied. "Not really."

It was a lie. We both knew it. But it was the lie we needed to move forward.

Now we walk in careful silence, three men carrying different weights toward the same gray stones.

---

The gates of Greystone Keep rise before us like a judgment. I've spent weeks away, following Theron's trail, trying to verify his conspiracy theories about the Order's corruption. I found some truth—evidence of morally ambiguous operations, preventive strikes that cost innocent lives, the kind of pragmatic brutality Aldric admitted to.

But I didn't find the grand conspiracy Theron promised. No evidence of deliberately planted cult texts. No proof the Order engineered Thornhaven's fall.

Just men making impossible choices and living with the consequences.

Just my father, driven mad by guilt, seeing patterns in chaos.

Just me, caught between truth and family, finally understanding they're not always compatible.

The guards at the gate recognize us. Their eyes widen at the sight of Theron in chains. Word will spread fast—the wayward ward returning with a prisoner. Aldric will hear before we reach his quarters.

"Solaire." Theron's voice is hoarse. He hasn't used it in hours. "You don't have to do this."

"Yes, I do."

"They'll execute me. You know they will."

"I know."

Garicus glances at me. Checking. Making sure I'm certain.

I nod once.

We walk through the gates.

---

They take Theron to the cells first. Knights I've trained with, men I've called brothers, lead my father away with professional detachment. He doesn't fight. Just looks back once, and the expression on his face is almost relief.

He's been running from this for fifteen years. Maybe being caught is its own kind of peace.

Garicus and I stand in the courtyard after he's gone. The familiar sounds of the Keep wash over us—steel on steel from the training yard, shouted orders, the rhythms of an institution that doesn't pause for individual tragedy.

"You should clean up," Garicus says. "Before you see Aldric."

"I should see him now."

"Solaire—"

"Now, Garicus." I meet his eyes. "Before I lose my nerve."

He studies my face. Whatever he sees there makes him nod. "I'll be in our room. When you're ready."

Our room. Not my room. Not his room. Ours again.

It's a small thing. It means everything.

---

I find Aldric in the chapel. Of course. Where else would he be when one of his wards returns dragging in the father whose guilt has haunted them both?

He's not praying. Just sitting on the front pew, hands clasped loosely between his knees, staring at the crimson candles like they might offer answers they've never contained.

"Ser Aldric," I say from the doorway.

He doesn't turn. "Solaire. Welcome home."

"I'm not sure this is home anymore."

"No. I suppose it isn't." He gestures to the pew beside him. "Sit. Tell me what happened."

I sit. The stone is cold even through my travel-worn clothes. I smell like horse and road dust and failure.

"I found him," I say. "My father. Found what he's been doing since Thornhaven—hunting cult remnants. Trying to prevent more dragon summonings. He's been traveling from village to village, investigating rumors, stopping rituals before they start."

"Noble work," Aldric says carefully.

"It was. Until it wasn't." I lean forward, elbows on knees. "He broke, Aldric. The guilt of what he did—leading you to Thornhaven, watching it burn—it broke something in him. Started seeing cults everywhere. Last night he tried to burn a village based on nothing but suspicion and fear. We stopped him. Garicus and me."

"We?"

"Garicus tracked me. Thought I was in trouble. Found me choosing." I look at my hands. They're still shaking slightly. "Choosing to stop my father instead of believing him. Choosing you and Garicus and this place over the man who gave me life and destroyed it."

Aldric is quiet for a long moment. "That's not an easy choice."

"None of them are." I finally meet his eyes. "You were right. About everything. Truth didn't set me free. It just complicated things further. Made me understand how my father could do what he did. How you could do what you did. How all of you—cult elders, Order knights, desperate priests—could make terrible choices thinking they were right."

"And what did you learn?"

"That moral clarity is a luxury." The words taste bitter. "That sometimes the people who hurt us didn't mean to. That good intentions pave roads to places like Thornhaven." I pause. "That I've been serving an imperfect institution run by flawed men trying their best and often failing. Just like everywhere else."

"Does that mean you're staying?"

The question hangs in the air like smoke.

"No," I say.

Aldric nods. Doesn't look surprised. "Where will you go?"

"I don't know yet. Somewhere that isn't defined by Thornhaven. By ashes and oaths sworn in trauma. Somewhere Garicus and I can figure out who we are when we're not someone's wards or someone's revenge or someone's guilt."

"Garicus is going with you."

"Yes."

"Good." Aldric's voice is rough. "He shouldn't be alone. Neither should you."

"We made that promise a long time ago."

"I know. I was there." He stands, moves to the candles. Touches one gently, watching the flame dance. "I've made a lot of mistakes with you two. Took you in for the wrong reasons. Lied by omission. Trained you to serve a system that destroyed your families. But keeping you together—that might be the one thing I did right."

"You could have told us the truth sooner."

"Would it have helped? Would knowing at eight years old that I participated in the raid have made you stronger? Safer? Happier?" He turns to face me. "Or would it just have hurt you sooner?"

"I don't know." The honesty feels important. "Maybe both. Maybe neither. But it would have been our pain to carry on our terms, not yours to manage."

Aldric absorbs that. Nods slowly. "You're right. I robbed you of agency by choosing what truth you could handle. I'm sorry, Solaire. For that and... for all of it."

"I know." And I do. I've seen his guilt, worn into every line of his face. "I forgive you."

The words surprise us both.

Aldric's expression cracks. "You shouldn't. I don't deserve—"

"I forgive you anyway." I stand, face him fully. "Not because what you did was acceptable. It wasn't. But because carrying hatred alongside everything else will kill me. So I forgive you for Thornhaven. For the lies. For raising us in the shadow of your guilt." I pause. "And I thank you. For not leaving us in the ruins. For giving us each other. For teaching us to survive."

"Solaire—"

"I'm not done." My voice hardens slightly. "I forgive you. But I can't stay here. Can't serve the Order knowing what I know. Can't look at these walls and not see my mother's death in every stone. Can't be your ward or your redemption project or proof that guilt can be atoned for."

"What will you be, then?"

"I don't know yet." The admission feels like freedom. "But I'll figure it out. With Garicus. Somewhere far from here."

Aldric studies me for a long moment. Then he reaches into his cloak, pulls out a leather pouch. Coin, from the weight of it. He holds it out.

"Take this. It's not much, but it will get you started."

"Aldric—"

"Take it." His voice is gentle but firm. "Let me do this one thing without complicating it. Let me help you leave."

I take the pouch. It's heavier than expected. More than "not much."

"Thank you," I say.

"I'll handle your father's trial," Aldric continues. "He'll be judged by the Order. Given what he's done—both the good and the attempted burning—the sentence could be execution or lifetime imprisonment. Do you want to be present?"

I think about Theron's face when the guards took him away. The relief there.

"No," I say quietly. "I said goodbye last night. Whatever happens to him now... that's between him and the Order."

"And you can live with that?"

"I'll have to." I turn to leave. Stop at the door. "Aldric?"

"Yes?"

"The conspiracy Theron talked about. The idea that the Order deliberately seeds cults to justify its existence. Was he right?"

Silence. Heavy and telling.

"There are factions within the Vigil that believe in preventive intervention," Aldric says finally. "Operations that might look, from certain angles, like what your father described. Whether that constitutes conspiracy or pragmatic strategy depends on your perspective."

"That's not an answer."

"It's the only one I have." He meets my eyes. "The world is more complicated than good and evil, Solaire. The Order is made of men. Some noble, some corrupt, most somewhere in between. If you're looking for pure righteousness or clear villainy, you'll be disappointed everywhere you look."

"I'm already disappointed."

"Then you're learning." He almost smiles. "Go. Pack. Say your goodbyes. And Solaire?"

"Yes?"

"When you find wherever you're going—wherever no one's burning—send word. Just so I know you're alive."

"I will."

It's the last lie I tell him. The kind one. The one that lets both of us pretend this isn't goodbye forever.

I leave him in the chapel among the candles made from executed mages, a knight growing old with his guilt, and I don't look back.

---

Our room feels smaller than it did six weeks ago.

Garicus is there, already packing. His efficiency is almost mechanical—bedroll, spare clothes, weapons, supplies. Everything we'll need and nothing we won't.

"You saw Aldric," he says without turning.

"Yes."

"And?"

"And we're leaving." I pull my own pack from under the bed. Start filling it. "Tomorrow at dawn. Before I can change my mind."

"You won't change your mind."

"How do you know?"

He finally looks at me. "Because I know you. You've made your choice. You don't unmake those."

He's right. The choice happened last night when I chose to stop Theron instead of help him. When I chose Garicus's trust over my father's truth. When I chose the present over the past.

We pack in companionable silence. It feels like the old days—before Theron, before the fracture, when we moved around each other like dancers who'd memorized every step.

"Where are we going?" Garicus asks eventually.

"I don't know. Somewhere without the Order. Without the past."

"That's not a plan."

"It's the beginning of one." I fold a shirt, tuck it into the pack. "We'll figure it out as we go. Together."

The word hangs between us. Together. The core of our oath. The promise that survived everything else.

"I'm sorry," I say. "For leaving. For choosing Theron. For making you think our oath meant less than it did."

"You chose right in the end."

"I almost didn't." My throat tightens. "I almost believed him. Almost let the conspiracy and the guilt and the need for meaning pull me away from the only thing that's ever been real. You were right to be angry."

Garicus stops packing. Sits on his bed, facing me.

"I was angry because I was scared," he says. "Scared of losing you. Of the oath breaking. Of being alone again." He looks at his hands. "But you weren't wrong to want answers. To need truth. I just... I couldn't watch you drown in it."

"You did watch me drown. Then you pulled me out."

"That's what we do." He stands. Crosses the space between our beds. Pulls me into a hard embrace. "We save each other. However many times it takes."

I hold onto him like he's the only solid thing in a world made of smoke and ash. Maybe he is.

"Never alone," I whisper.

"Never alone," he echoes.

We stand like that until the weight in my chest eases. Until breathing doesn't hurt quite so much.

Then we finish packing. And when the last of our belongings is secured, when there's nothing left of our fifteen years in this room but empty beds and old memories, we walk out together.

---

We leave at dawn, as planned.

The courtyard is quiet. Most of the Keep still sleeps. A few guards on duty nod as we pass, asking no questions. Aldric must have spread word—let them go without incident.

I see him once, watching from the tower window. He doesn't wave. Doesn't call out. Just stands witness as we walk through the gates and into the wide world beyond.

Garicus and I ride in silence for the first hour. The sun rises slowly, painting the frost-covered landscape in shades of gold and pink. Beautiful, despite everything.

We reach a crossroads as midmorning light slants through the trees. Three roads diverge—north toward the mountains, east toward the coast, south back the way we came.

We stop. Dismount. Stand at the convergence of paths, neither of us quite ready to choose.

"Where do we go?" Garicus asks. The question I've been waiting for.

I think about Thornhaven, burning in my memory. About Greystone Keep, gray and cold and never quite home. About every village we rode through in service to the Order, enforcing a justice we no longer believe in.

I think about ash and smoke and promises made by children who didn't know what they were swearing to.

And then I think about the future. Undefined. Terrifying. Full of possibility.

"Somewhere no one's burning," I say.

Garicus considers this. A smile tugs at his mouth—small, genuine, the first I've seen in weeks.

"That's a good place," he says.

"Think we can find it?"

"We can try." He looks at the three roads, then at me. "East?"

"East sounds good."

We mount again. Turn our horses toward the rising sun. And as Greystone Keep disappears behind us, as the road stretches forward into unknown territory, I feel something loosen in my chest.

Not happiness. Not yet. But the possibility of it. The understanding that we're no longer defined by what destroyed us, but by what we choose next.

We ride side by side, two brothers who survived fire and truth and the slow death of certainty. We have no masters now. No oaths except to each other. No purpose except the one we'll create.

It should terrify me. Should feel like falling.

It feels like flying.

"Garicus?" I say after another mile.

"Yeah?"

"Thank you. For not giving up on me. For coming after me when I was lost."

"You'd do the same for me."

"I would. I will. However long it takes."

"However long it takes," he agrees.

The oath has changed. Evolved. No longer born from trauma and desperation, but from genuine choice. From knowing each other completely and choosing to stay anyway.

Maybe that's what it always should have been.

Ahead, the road curves into forest. New territory. Unmapped. Ours to explore.

We ride toward it without hesitation, leaving smoke and ash and everything we thought we had to be behind us.

Somewhere ahead is a place no one's burning. A place we can build instead of destroy. A place where the promises we make matter because we choose them freely, not because eight-year-olds swore them in desperation.

We don't know where it is.

But we'll find it together.

And that's enough.

## Epilogue Fragment

*Five years later*

The village of Ashford is small, comfortable, unremarkable. A place where nothing terrible has ever happened and likely never will. The kind of place people come to forget.

We came to remember.

Garicus runs the blacksmith shop on the east edge of town. He's good at it—patient with the work, methodical, finding peace in the rhythm of hammer on metal. The villagers like him. Trust him. Don't ask about the scars or why he moves like a fighter.

I run the school. Six students so far, children whose families can't afford private tutors. I teach them letters and numbers and stories that don't involve burning villages or impossible choices. They call me Teacher Solaire. They smile when they see me.

It's a quiet life. Simple. Nothing like the one we left behind.

Some nights I dream of Thornhaven. Of my mother's face, fading. Of Aldric in the chapel, watching us leave. Of Theron in chains, relief and resignation warring in his eyes.

I wake to Garicus in the next room, steady and solid and here. And the dreams fade.

We don't talk about the Order much. Don't talk about what happened to Theron—whether he was executed or imprisoned, whether our testimony at his trial mattered. We sent one letter to Aldric, two years ago. Told him we were alive and well. Asked him not to respond.

He didn't.

But sometimes I think I see crimson cloaks in the distance. Wonder if he's checking on us. Making sure his wards survived their freedom.

I hope he did. I hope he found peace too.

Garicus appears in the doorway of my classroom after the last student leaves. He's soot-stained, smiling, holding two apples.

"Good day?" he asks.

"Good day," I confirm.

We walk home together as the sun sets, painting the sky in shades of red and gold. Not promises in ash anymore. Just beauty. Just light.

"Think we found it?" I ask.

"Found what?"

"The place where no one's burning."

Garicus looks at me. At the quiet village around us. At the life we've built from nothing but choice and stubborn loyalty to each other.

"Yeah," he says. "I think we did."

We walk the rest of the way in comfortable silence.

Brothers. Not by blood or trauma.

By choice.

Always by choice.

And that makes all the difference.
