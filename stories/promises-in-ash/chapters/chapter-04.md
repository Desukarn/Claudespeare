# Chapter 4: First Blood

Two years changes everything and nothing.

I'm eighteen now, tall enough that Aldric doesn't have to look down when we speak. The scar on my thigh from the initiation trial has faded to a thin white line. My sword feels like breathing—natural, necessary, mine. I've trained for this my whole life, or at least the part of my life that matters. Ten years of drills and sparring and lessons in how to read danger in a stranger's eyes.

Ten years to prepare for my first real mission.

And I'm not ready.

The village is called Millbrook. Three days' ride from Greystone Keep, nestled in a river valley where the current runs too fast for ice even in winter. Population maybe two hundred. Aldric gave us the briefing last night: reports of blood magic, livestock found drained and marked with symbols, a traveler who swore he heard chanting from the forest at midnight.

"Probably nothing," Aldric said, reading the parchment without looking at us. "Provincial paranoia. But we check anyway."

Garicus asked the question I didn't dare: "And if it's not nothing?"

Aldric finally met our eyes. "Then we do what the Order does. We stop it."

Now we're riding through Millbrook's main street—if you can call it that—while shutters crack open and faces peer out at our crimson cloaks. Fear. That's what I see in those faces. Not relief. Not hope. Fear.

I've never been on this side of it before.

Aldric dismounts first, smooth despite his age. Fifty-two this winter, though he moves like a man ten years younger. Garicus and I follow his lead, tying our horses to a post outside what looks like the village elder's house. It's larger than the others, with glass windows instead of oiled parchment. Wealth is relative.

The door opens before Aldric can knock. A woman stands there—maybe forty, gray threading through her dark hair, eyes sharp and wary. "Ser Aldric," she says. Not a question.

"Elder Marten?" Aldric inclines his head, the bare minimum of courtesy.

"Yes." She doesn't invite us in. Doesn't step aside. Just stands there like a wall between us and whatever she's protecting. "We weren't expecting the Order."

"You sent a report."

"I sent a question. I didn't ask for an inquisition."

"Good thing we're not here for one, then." Aldric's voice is mild, but there's steel underneath. "We're here to investigate. If there's no problem, we'll leave. If there is..." He lets the sentence hang.

Elder Marten's jaw tightens. Then she steps aside. "Come in."

---

The inside of her house smells like woodsmoke and old herbs. We sit at a rough-hewn table while she pours water from a clay pitcher. No one touches the cups.

"Tell me about the livestock," Aldric says.

"Three goats, two chickens. Found them over the course of a week. Drained of blood, symbols carved into the dirt around them."

"What kind of symbols?"

"I don't know. I'm not a scholar of forbidden arts." Her tone is sharp. "That's why we asked for guidance, not an armed party."

Aldric ignores the jab. "The traveler who heard chanting. Is he still in the village?"

"Left two days ago. Didn't want trouble."

"Smart man." Aldric leans back in his chair. "Is there anyone in Millbrook who might practice blood magic? Anyone new to the village, anyone with... unusual knowledge?"

Elder Marten's hesitation is brief but telling. "There's a woman. Elara. She came here five months ago, fleeing the war in the north. She has some skill with healing herbs, helps with births. Some of the older folk say she has a gift."

"A gift," Aldric repeats. The word sounds like an accusation.

"People talk. Doesn't mean anything."

"Where can we find her?"

Elder Marten stands. "I'll take you."

---

Elara lives on the edge of the village, in a cottage so small it barely qualifies as a structure. Smoke rises from the chimney. Herbs hang in bunches from the eaves, drying in the cold air. A garden plot beside the door, winter-dead but carefully tended.

It looks... ordinary. Harmless.

Garicus sees it differently. I can tell by how his hand drifts to his sword hilt, by the way his eyes scan the tree line. He's looking for threats. Finding them everywhere.

Elder Marten knocks. "Elara? It's Marten. I've brought... visitors."

The door opens. The woman who emerges is younger than I expected—maybe twenty-five, with auburn hair and eyes the color of forest moss. She looks at Elder Marten, then at us, and something in her expression closes off.

"The Order," she says flatly.

"Knights of the Crimson Vigil," Aldric confirms. "We're investigating reports of blood magic in the area."

"And you thought to start with the stranger." She doesn't sound surprised. Doesn't even sound angry. Just tired. "Of course."

"We're simply gathering information," Aldric says. "May we come in?"

"Do I have a choice?"

"Yes."

Elara studies him for a long moment. Then she steps aside. "Fine. But I have work to do, so make it quick."

The cottage interior is even smaller than it looks from outside. A pallet in one corner, a table with two chairs, shelves lined with clay jars and dried plants. A pot simmers over the fire, filling the air with the scent of something medicinal. Clean. Poor but proud.

Aldric doesn't sit. Neither do we. "You practice healing," he states.

"I help where I can. Midwifery. Poultices for fever. Nothing more."

"Nothing magical?"

"Magic?" Elara laughs, bitter and short. "I know plants, Ser Knight. Which roots ease pain, which leaves stop bleeding. My mother taught me. Her mother taught her. If that's magic, then every grandmother in the realm is a witch."

Garicus speaks for the first time. "The animals. The symbols. You know anything about that?"

"Nothing." She meets his gaze without flinching. "I'm a healer, not a butcher."

"Blood magic often starts with animals," Aldric says quietly. "Small sacrifices. Practice. Then it escalates."

"Then find someone who butchers animals." Elara's voice is sharp now, fear bleeding through. "I've done nothing wrong."

Aldric is silent for a moment. Thinking. Weighing. I can see the calculation in his eyes.

"We'll need to search your home," he says finally.

"What?"

"Standard procedure. If you've done nothing wrong, you have nothing to hide."

"I have nothing to hide because I've done nothing wrong." Elara's hands are shaking now. "You can't just—"

"We can." Aldric's voice is gentle, which somehow makes it worse. "We are the Order. This is what we do."

I want to say something. Want to point out that she's just a woman trying to survive, that healing isn't the same as corruption, that fear of strangers isn't evidence of guilt.

I don't say anything.

Garicus moves first, systematically searching the shelves. I follow because that's what we do—we follow orders. We check jars and bundles, looking for... what? Blood? Ritual components? I don't even know what I'm looking for.

I find herbs. Willow bark. Chamomile. Things my mother might have used if she'd lived long enough to teach me their names.

Aldric searches the pallet, the small chest beside it. Elara stands frozen by the fire, watching us violate her space, and I can see in her face the exact moment she stops being a person and becomes a suspect.

I've seen that look before. In the mirror, when I remember the raiders.

Garicus finishes first. "Nothing."

I shake my head. "Nothing here either."

Aldric straightens. "Thank you for your cooperation," he says, as if Elara had a choice. "We may have more questions later."

"Am I free to go about my work?" Her voice is ice over rage.

"For now."

We leave. Elder Marten is waiting outside, her expression unreadable. "Satisfied?" she asks.

"We're rarely satisfied," Aldric replies. "But we're thorough."

---

That night we make camp outside the village. Aldric doesn't trust their hospitality, and I don't blame him—we're about as welcome as plague rats. The fire crackles between us, shadows dancing across Aldric's weathered face.

"She's hiding something," Garicus says.

I look up, startled. "What makes you say that?"

"She was too defensive. Too quick to explain." He pokes at the fire with a stick. "Innocent people don't react like that."

"Innocent people react exactly like that when knights show up and start accusing them of blood magic." The words come out sharper than I intended.

Garicus frowns. "You think she's clean?"

"I think we found nothing. No evidence. No ritual components. Just herbs."

"The symbols on the ground—"

"Could be anything. Could be some kid playing with sticks. Could be completely unrelated to her."

"Could be carefully hidden," Garicus counters. "We searched for an hour. She's lived there for months."

Aldric has been silent, staring into the flames. Now he speaks. "It doesn't matter."

We both turn to him.

"What do you mean it doesn't matter?" I ask.

"Evidence would make it cleaner. Easier. But the absence of evidence is not evidence of absence." He meets my eyes. "She's a stranger in a village where strange things are happening. She admits to knowledge of herbs and healing—which is how blood magic often begins. And she's alone, with no family to vouch for her character."

My stomach drops. "You can't be serious."

"I'm always serious, Solaire."

"We found nothing!" I'm on my feet now, though I don't remember standing. "You searched her home. There was nothing there!"

"We found nothing today," Aldric corrects. "How long until we find something tomorrow? Next week? How many more animals need to die? How long until it's not animals anymore?"

"You don't know that will happen—"

"I know what happens when we do nothing." His voice is quiet, final. "I've seen it. You haven't."

"So what, we just execute her on suspicion? On fear?"

"On preventive justice." Aldric stands, meeting me eye-to-eye now. "That's what the Order does, Solaire. We don't wait for the body count to justify action. We prevent it."

"That's murder."

The word hangs between us like drawn steel.

Garicus stands too, placing himself slightly between us. Not taking sides. Just present. "Solaire—"

"Don't." I step back. "Don't tell me this is necessary. Don't tell me this is what we do. We're supposed to hunt the guilty, not—not terrorize healers because they're convenient."

"Convenient?" Aldric's voice hardens. "You think I find this convenient? You think I enjoy condemning people on insufficient evidence? This is the burden of the vigil, Solaire. We make the hard choices so others don't have to. We carry the guilt so villages can sleep at night."

"Villages like ours?" The question escapes before I can stop it.

Aldric goes very still.

"Villages like Thornhaven?" I press. "Is that what happened? Did knights show up and decide preventive justice was necessary? Did they carry the guilt while everyone burned?"

"That was different," Aldric says quietly.

"How?"

He doesn't answer. Can't answer. I can see it in his face—the confirmation I've never wanted, the truth I've always suspected.

Garicus's hand is on my arm now. "Solaire. Not here. Not now."

I shake him off. "When, then? When do we talk about this? When do we ask why we're about to do the exact same thing that was done to us?"

"It's not the same," Garicus says, but his voice lacks conviction.

"Isn't it? Strange people in a village. Reports. Suspicion. And then—" I make a gesture encompassing the camp, the village, everything. "Then knights in crimson cloaks decide who lives and who doesn't."

Aldric's face is stone. "Go to your tent, Solaire."

"I'm not a child—"

"Go. To. Your. Tent." Each word falls like an axe blow. "We'll discuss this in the morning when you've had time to think clearly."

"I'm thinking clearly now!"

"No, you're thinking emotionally. There's a difference." He turns away from me, addressing Garicus instead. "Make sure he stays in camp tonight."

"Aldric—" Garicus starts.

"That's an order."

Silence. The fire pops. Somewhere in the village, a dog barks.

I walk to my tent because the alternative is saying something I can't take back. Garicus follows, hovering just outside while I collapse onto my bedroll.

"He's wrong," I say to the canvas roof.

Garicus sits at the tent's entrance. His outline blocks the firelight. "Maybe."

"Maybe?"

"I don't know, Sol. I've never done this before either."

"But you think he might be right."

He's quiet for a long moment. "I think... I think if we're wrong and we do nothing, people die. And if we're wrong and we act, one person dies. The math isn't complicated."

"One innocent person."

"We don't know she's innocent."

"We don't know she's guilty!"

Garicus turns to look at me. In the dim light, he looks older than eighteen. Looks like Aldric. "Would you rather wait to find out? Risk it? Bet the village that she's exactly what she claims?"

I want to say yes. Want to believe in proof and justice and waiting for certainty. But I remember Thornhaven. Remember the bodies. Remember waking up to ash and silence and Garicus's hand in mine, the only thing tethering me to life.

Would I have traded Elara's life for that not happening? For my mother to still be alive?

The thought makes me sick.

"I don't know," I whisper.

Garicus nods. "Yeah. Me neither."

He stays there until I pretend to sleep, then quietly returns to the fire. I lie awake listening to him and Aldric talking in low voices, making plans I don't want to hear.

And I think about a woman in a small cottage, brewing medicine over her fire, completely unaware that tomorrow three men in crimson cloaks will decide whether she deserves to see another sunrise.

---

Dawn comes too soon.

Aldric wakes us with his usual efficiency. We eat in silence—dried meat, hard bread, water from our skins. Then we pack up camp and ride back into Millbrook.

Elara is in her garden when we arrive. She looks up at the sound of hooves, and I watch understanding dawn in her eyes. She knows. Somehow, she knows.

She doesn't run. Just stands there, dirt on her hands, watching us approach.

Aldric dismounts. "Elara of Millbrook, by the authority of the Order of the Crimson Vigil, you are accused of practicing blood magic within the borders of—"

"I'm innocent," she says. Not pleading. Just stating fact.

"The evidence suggests otherwise."

"What evidence?" Her voice rises. "You found nothing! I've done nothing!"

"The pattern suggests—"

"The pattern suggests you needed someone to blame and I'm convenient!" She's backing away now, toward her cottage. "I'm a stranger. I'm alone. I'm easy."

"Garicus," Aldric says quietly.

Garicus dismounts. Draws his sword.

My breath stops.

"Please," Elara says. Looking at me now. Why is she looking at me? "Please, I've done nothing. I just want to help people. I just want to—"

"I'm sorry," Aldric says. And the terrible thing is, he sounds like he means it. "This is preventive justice. For the safety of the realm."

Elara runs. Not toward the forest, where she might have a chance. Toward her cottage. Toward home. Like sanctuary still means something.

Garicus catches her before she reaches the door. His hand closes on her arm and she screams—not words, just pure terror—and I'm moving before I can think, my hand on my own sword.

"Solaire." Aldric's voice cuts through the chaos. "Stand down."

"She's unarmed!"

"She's guilty."

"You don't know that!"

"I know enough!" For the first time in ten years, Aldric raises his voice at me. "I know what happens when we hesitate! I know what indecision costs! And I will not—I will NOT—let another village burn because we were too afraid to act!"

He's not talking about Millbrook anymore.

Garicus has forced Elara to her knees. She's stopped struggling. Just crying now, quiet and broken. Like she's already a ghost.

"Do it," Aldric orders.

Garicus raises his sword.

I should stop him. Should draw my own blade, get between them, refuse the order. Should do something other than stand here frozen, watching my brother become an executioner.

But I don't move.

Because Aldric might be right. Because Thornhaven burned. Because I don't know enough about magic or patterns or preventive justice to be certain. Because I'm eighteen years old and I'm scared and I don't want to be responsible for the next massacre.

So I watch.

Garicus's blade falls.

It's quick. Clean. One stroke. Elara makes a sound—small, surprised—and then nothing.

Silence.

Garicus steps back. His sword is red. His face is blank.

Aldric closes Elara's eyes with gentle fingers. "May your soul find peace in the next world," he murmurs. The ritual words for the executed. As if prayers change anything.

I can't breathe. Can't look away. Can't reconcile the body on the ground with the woman who brewed medicine yesterday, who told us she just wanted to help people.

Aldric turns to me. "This is the work, Solaire. This is what we swore to do."

"This is murder," I hear myself say.

"This is war." His eyes are haunted but certain. "And in war, we don't wait for perfect intelligence. We act on the best information we have, and we live with the consequences."

"What if you're wrong?"

"Then I'll answer for it when I stand before whatever god judges knights." He wipes blood from his hands with a cloth. "But I won't answer for inaction. Not again."

Again.

The word confirms everything I've suspected and feared. Thornhaven wasn't random. Wasn't bandits or bad luck. It was this—preventive justice, hard choices, the greater good measured in corpses.

Garicus sheathes his sword. Looks at me. Waiting for... what? Condemnation? Absolution?

I can't give him either.

We bury Elara in her own garden. Aldric says it's procedure—can't leave bodies in villages, causes talk. I think it's guilt, trying to dress itself up as mercy.

Elder Marten watches from her doorway as we work. When we're done, she approaches. "I hope you're satisfied, Ser Knight."

"I'm never satisfied," Aldric replies. "But the village is safe."

"Is it?" She looks at the fresh-turned earth. "Or did you just teach us that strangers aren't welcome, that being alone is dangerous, that the Order cares more about suspicion than truth?"

Aldric has no answer.

We ride out before noon. Millbrook's gates close behind us with finality.

---

We camp that night in silence. I sit apart from the fire, unable to stomach food or company. Garicus tries to approach once, but I shake my head and he retreats.

Aldric lets me be.

I think about Elara. About her cottage and her herbs and her desperate claim of innocence. I think about the villagers who will remember her execution, who will whisper about the Order's justice. I think about the next stranger who arrives in Millbrook, and how welcome they'll be.

I think about Garicus's face when he raised that sword—blank, dutiful, already halfway to not-feeling. And I wonder if that's what I'll look like in a year, in five, in ten. If this is how you survive the vigil: by turning off the part of you that questions, that doubts, that sees people instead of threats.

Garicus sits down beside me. Doesn't speak. Just sits.

After a while, I find words. "Do you think she was guilty?"

He's quiet for a long time. "I think Aldric believes she was. And I think he's seen more than we have."

"That's not an answer."

"I don't have one." He looks at his hands. They're clean now, but I know he can still see the blood. "I did what I was trained to do. What we both were trained to do. And I'll probably have to do it again."

"How can you be okay with that?"

"I'm not okay with it." His voice is raw. "I'm just... accepting it. Because the alternative is what? Abandoning the Order? Going rogue? We made an oath, Sol."

"We made two oaths," I remind him. "One to the Order. One to each other. Which one matters more?"

He meets my eyes. "You know which one."

"Do I?" I gesture toward the village behind us. "Because back there, you chose the Order. You did what Aldric said without question."

"I trust Aldric."

"Even when he might be wrong?"

Garicus is quiet. Then: "Especially then. Because if I don't trust someone who's done this longer than we've been alive, how am I supposed to trust myself?"

It's not a bad point. But it doesn't make the weight in my chest lighter.

"I saw her face," I whisper. "Right before. She wasn't guilty. I know she wasn't."

"Maybe." Garicus leans back, looking at the stars. "Or maybe you wanted her to be innocent so badly that you convinced yourself. Either way, it's done now."

"That's supposed to make me feel better?"

"No. Nothing's supposed to make you feel better." He turns his head toward me. "This is the work, Sol. Aldric's right about that. It's ugly and uncertain and sometimes we'll get it wrong. But we do it anyway because the alternative is worse."

"What if the alternative isn't worse?" The question feels like betrayal just asking it. "What if we're the ones making things worse?"

Garicus doesn't have an answer for that.

We sit together under stars that don't care about justice or innocence, two brothers bound by oaths they're starting to question, and I feel the first crack form in the foundation of everything I thought I knew.

Aldric was right about one thing: this is the work.

I just don't know if I can do it.

## Chapter Notes

**Plot advancement:** Solaire's first mission plants seeds of doubt about the Order's methods. Execution of potentially innocent person based on suspicion rather than proof. Reveals that Thornhaven was likely targeted by the Order (Aldric's "not again" comment). Creates first real tension between Solaire and Garicus—Garicus accepts the Order's logic, Solaire questions it. Sets up the eventual fracture in their relationship and trust in Aldric.

**Character development:**
- Solaire begins questioning authority, sees the gray areas in "preventive justice"
- Garicus reveals his pragmatic nature—trusts Aldric completely, believes in "better safe than sorry"
- Aldric shown as genuinely conflicted but certain, his guilt over Thornhaven driving his ruthless efficiency
- The execution scene deliberately uncomfortable, violence not glorified
- Solaire's horror juxtaposed with his inability to act—fear of being wrong paralyzes him

**World reveals:**
- How the Order operates in practice: investigation, suspicion, execution without trial
- "Preventive justice" as doctrine—act on insufficient evidence to prevent worse outcomes
- Villages fear the Order rather than trust them
- Implication that Thornhaven was an Order operation, not random raid
- The moral cost of the Order's methods on both victims and perpetrators

**Writing notes:**
- Maintained Solaire's POV voice: questioning, emotionally aware, prone to second-guessing
- Built tension slowly from investigation through confrontation to execution
- Used Elara as sympathetic victim—healer, refugee, just trying to survive
- Garicus and Solaire's argument shows their differing responses to moral ambiguity
- Aldric's "not again" moment reveals backstory without explaining everything
- Ending emphasizes the weight of the choice and Solaire's crisis of faith
