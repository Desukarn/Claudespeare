# Chapter 7: The Weight of Silence

I waited three days before seeking Aldric out.

Three days of going through the motions—training with Garicus in careful silence, eating meals I couldn't taste, sleeping in fragments between nightmares that now had my father's face. Three days of carrying Theron's confession like a stone in my chest, growing heavier with each passing hour.

The dragon cult. The ritual. The Order's raid.

My father's words circled endlessly: *I led them there. I thought I was saving people. I killed them instead.*

But there was another question beneath that one, sharper and more urgent: Did Aldric know?

The man who found us in the ashes, who took us in when he could have left us to die, who raised us for fifteen years—did he know what the Order had done? Did he know what Theron had told them?

I needed to hear him say it. Needed to look him in the eye and ask the question I'd been too afraid to voice for a decade and a half.

On the third day, I found him in the chapel.

---

The crimson candles were burning, filling the small stone room with their sweet-sick smell. Aldric knelt at the altar, his head bowed, lips moving in silent prayer. He looked older in the flickering light—the gray in his beard more pronounced, the lines around his eyes deeper.

For a moment, I considered leaving. Coming back later. Never asking at all.

Then I thought of my mother's face, half-forgotten. Of Garicus in the cellar, listening to screams. Of Theron's broken expression when he said *everyone was already dying when the Order arrived*.

I stepped into the chapel. The door closed behind me with a soft click.

Aldric's prayer stopped. He didn't turn.

"Solaire," he said quietly. "I wondered when you'd come."

"You knew I'd been gone."

"You requested three days' leave and came back looking like you'd seen a ghost." He rose slowly, joints creaking, and faced me. His eyes were calm. Knowing. "Of course I knew."

"Did you follow me?"

"No. But I can guess where you went." He studied my face. "You found your father."

Not a question. I answered anyway. "Yes."

"And he told you about Thornhaven."

My breath caught. "You knew. All this time, you knew he was alive."

"I suspected." Aldric moved to one of the benches, sat with the careful movements of a man conscious of old wounds. "The records showed a priest fled the village before the raid. Theron of Thornhaven. Your family name. It wasn't difficult to connect."

"Why didn't you tell me?"

"What would I have said?" His voice was gentle, which somehow made it worse. "Your father survived by betraying your village to the Order? He's spent fifteen years hunting cult remnants because he can't live with what he helped us do? How does that conversation go, Solaire?"

I wanted to rage at him. To demand why he thought keeping secrets was acceptable, why he'd let me grow up thinking I knew the shape of my own past when it was all lies.

Instead, I asked the question that mattered.

"Did you know about the cult? Was the raid justified?"

Silence fell like ash.

Aldric looked at me for a long moment. I watched him make the calculation—saw the exact instant he decided to stop protecting me from the truth.

"Yes," he said. "I knew."

The word landed like a blade.

"Theron came to the Order with evidence," Aldric continued. His voice was steady, factual. "Ritual markings. Testimonies about missing children. Names of the elders involved. He begged us to stop them before they could complete the summoning."

"But they'd already completed it."

"We didn't know that when we rode out." He met my gaze without flinching. "We thought we were preventing a dragon summoning. By the time we arrived, the ritual had been performed the night before. The dragon had already destroyed half the village. The cult lost control. It killed most of them before it was banished."

"So you found a village already dying, and you burned it anyway."

"We found a village where forbidden knowledge had been practiced. Where children had been sacrificed. Where survivors could spread the ritual to others." His hands tightened into fists. "We did what the Order exists to do. We contained it."

"By murdering everyone who was left."

"By preventing the knowledge from spreading!" For the first time, anger sharpened Aldric's voice. "Do you know what happens if dragon summoning becomes common practice? If desperate villages across the realm decide child sacrifice is acceptable for power? We'd have a dozen Thornhavens. A hundred."

"So you killed mine to prevent theoretical ones."

"Yes."

The admission sat between us like a corpse.

I stood there, shaking, trying to reconcile this with the man who taught me to fight, who bandaged my training wounds, who sat with me through nightmares and never once complained. Trying to make sense of how the same hands that raised me had helped destroy everything I came from.

"You were there," I said. The realization came slowly, terrible in its certainty. "In the village. During the raid."

Aldric's face went very still.

"You weren't just investigating after. You were part of it. You were one of the knights who—" My voice cracked. I tried again. "Who burned my home. Who killed my mother."

"Yes."

The world tilted. I grabbed the back of a pew to steady myself.

"I was part of the raid," Aldric said quietly. "I argued against the wholesale slaughter. Fought with the commander, insisted we could arrest the cult leaders and spare the innocent. I was overruled." He looked at his hands. "So I followed orders. And when we found you and Garicus in the ruins the next morning, I took you in."

"Out of guilt."

"Out of guilt," he agreed. "And love. And the desperate hope that I could make something right out of what we'd done wrong."

I laughed, bitter and broken. "You raised us on their graves. You took us to the place that trained the knights who killed our families, and you made us just like you."

"I made you strong enough to survive," Aldric said. "Strong enough to face the truth when it came."

"The truth that you're a murderer? That everything I've been taught, everything I've sworn to uphold, is built on the same lie that killed my village?" I was shouting now, couldn't stop myself. "What truth am I supposed to face, Aldric? That you're no better than the cult you claim to fight?"

"We are better." His voice was steel. "The cult murdered children for power. We stopped them."

"After they'd already succeeded! You didn't stop anything! You just killed the witnesses!"

"And saved every other village that might have heard the story and tried to replicate it!" Aldric stood, met me eye to eye. "Yes, we killed innocents. Yes, it was brutal and ugly and wrong. But the alternative was worse. You want to know what dragon summoning unleashed looks like? I've seen it, Solaire. Cities gone in a night. Thousands dead. That ritual in your village was one of twelve we stopped that year. Twelve villages that thought they could control what they summoned. Only yours succeeded in the casting before we arrived."

"Succeeded in killing themselves, you mean."

"And if even one person had survived with the knowledge? If they'd written it down, taught it to others, spread it like plague?" He shook his head. "We couldn't take that risk."

"So you took my childhood instead."

The words fell like a sentence.

Aldric's anger drained away. He sat back down, suddenly looking every one of his fifty-two years.

"Yes," he said quietly. "I did. And I've lived with that choice every day since."

---

I should have left then. Should have walked away before I said something irreversible. But the questions kept pouring out, toxic and necessary.

"When did you decide?" I asked. "When did you look at us—two terrified children in the ruins—and think, yes, I'll take them in. I'll raise them to serve the Order that destroyed their lives."

"It wasn't like that."

"Then what was it like?"

Aldric was quiet for a long moment. When he spoke, his voice was rough with something that might have been grief.

"I saw you holding Garicus's hand. Both of you covered in ash, eyes hollow, waiting for someone—anyone—to come. And I thought: these boys survived what we did. The least I can do is make sure they don't die of exposure or starvation or the dozen other things that kill orphans." He looked up at me. "I didn't plan to keep you. Didn't plan to train you. I was going to take you to a monastery, or find families to take you in."

"What changed?"

"You did. Both of you." A ghost of a smile crossed his face. "You followed me everywhere. Asked endless questions. Garicus was so determined to be useful he'd practice sword drills until he collapsed. You wouldn't let me out of your sight for weeks. You needed me." His voice dropped. "I needed to be needed. To make something good out of what I'd done."

"We weren't your redemption," I said. "We were people. Children. We deserved the truth."

"When?" Aldric's voice sharpened. "When you were eight? Ten? Should I have sat you down after training and said, 'By the way, I helped kill your parents'? Would that have made it better?"

"It would have let us choose! We could have left, could have—"

"Gone where?" He stood again, and now there was real anger in his face. "To what life? You were orphans of a burned village. No family, no coin, no prospects. I gave you a home. Training. Purpose. A brotherhood with each other that most people never find. Yes, I did it out of guilt. But I also did it out of love, Solaire. I raised you. I taught you. I was proud of you."

"Was?" The word came out smaller than I intended.

Aldric's expression softened. "Am proud of you. Even now, when you're looking at me like I'm a monster. I'm proud that you have the strength to ask these questions. That you refuse to accept easy answers."

"There are no easy answers," I said bitterly. "There's just you, and what you did, and the fact that I can't—" My voice broke. "I can't reconcile it. The man who raised me and the man who destroyed my village. They can't both be you."

"But they are." He spread his hands. "I'm both, Solaire. The knight who follows orders and the man who regrets them. The teacher who loves his wards and the killer who gave them cause to become orphans. I can't separate those parts. I've tried."

I stood there, shaking, staring at this man I'd called father in all but name. Trying to find anger strong enough to burn away the tangled mess of love and betrayal.

I couldn't.

"I need to go," I said.

"Solaire—"

"I need to go." I walked toward the door, each step feeling like wading through deep water. "I can't... I can't be here right now."

Aldric didn't try to stop me. Just stood there in the candlelight, looking old and tired and broken.

At the door, I stopped. Didn't turn around.

"Did you love us?" I asked. "Truly? Or was it always guilt?"

The silence stretched so long I thought he wouldn't answer.

Then: "Both. Always both. I'm sorry, Solaire. I wish I could give you a better answer. But it's the only truth I have."

I left him there.

---

The Keep was quiet as I walked its corridors. Training had ended for the day. Most of the knights were at dinner or in their quarters. I moved through the familiar passages like a ghost, seeing them differently now.

This place raised me. Shaped me. Made me into a weapon.

The same place that created the knights who burned Thornhaven.

I found myself at the outer wall without consciously choosing the destination—the spot where Garicus and I had reaffirmed our oath years ago. Where we'd promised that no matter what the Order demanded, we belonged to each other first.

Someone was already there.

Garicus stood with his back to me, looking out over the forest. He didn't turn when I approached, but his shoulders shifted—acknowledgment without words.

"You talked to Aldric," he said.

"How did you know?"

"You have that look. Like someone told you the sky is actually the ground and you can't figure out which way is up." He glanced at me. "What did he say?"

I wanted to tell him everything. Wanted to pour it all out—the cult, the dragon, the raid, Aldric's guilt and justifications and impossible choices. The words crowded in my throat.

"He knew," I managed. "About my father. About the cult. About everything."

"And?"

"He was there, Garicus. During the raid. He helped burn the village. Then he found us and decided to..." I couldn't finish.

"To take us in," Garicus finished quietly. "Out of guilt."

"You already knew."

He shook his head. "Suspected. Little things over the years. The way he'd change the subject when we asked about the raid. How he always had answers about Order protocol but never about that specific mission." He turned to face me fully. "I didn't want to know for certain. Easier to just... accept what we had."

"How can you accept this?" I demanded. "He destroyed our lives and then raised us to serve the people who did it!"

"He also kept us alive." Garicus's voice was calm. Too calm. "Fed us. Trained us. Gave us each other. Yes, it was built on blood and guilt. But it's still what we have."

"That's not enough."

"Isn't it?" He studied my face. "What's the alternative, Solaire? Leave the Order? Hunt down every knight who participated? Kill Aldric for his honesty?"

"I don't know!" The words ripped out of me. "I don't know what I'm supposed to do with this. I don't know how to keep serving an Order that murdered innocents. I don't know how to trust Aldric when every kindness was paid for with my mother's life. I don't know—"

My voice broke. The trembling I'd been suppressing for days finally overwhelmed me. I grabbed the stone wall for support, breathing hard, trying not to fall apart completely.

Garicus's hand landed on my shoulder. Steady. Anchoring.

"I know," he said quietly. "I don't have answers either. But I know we're still here. Still together. That still means something."

"Does it? When everything else is a lie?"

"It's not a lie." His grip tightened. "Our oath. Our brotherhood. That was never about Aldric or the Order. It was about us. That's still real, Solaire. Maybe it's the only thing that is."

I wanted to believe him. Wanted to hold onto that certainty the way I had when we were eight years old and making promises in the ash. But the foundation had cracked, and I couldn't see how to rebuild it.

"What do we do?" I whispered.

Garicus was quiet for a long time. When he spoke, his voice was heavy with something I couldn't name.

"We survive," he said. "Same as always. We figure out what we believe, what we can live with. And we do it together."

"And Aldric?"

"I don't know." He let his hand fall. "Maybe we forgive him eventually. Maybe we don't. But right now, we just... survive knowing what we know."

It wasn't enough. Wasn't close to enough. But it was all we had.

We stood there as the sun set, two brothers whose entire world had shifted beneath them, trying to find solid ground. Below us, the Keep continued its rhythms—dinner bells, changing guard, the everyday machinery of the Order we'd sworn to serve.

The Order that killed our families.

The Order that gave us each other.

I didn't know which truth mattered more. Didn't know if there was a right answer, or if moral clarity was just another comfortable lie we told ourselves.

I only knew that Aldric's admission had broken something in me that would never fully heal.

And that Garicus was still standing beside me, solid and real and here, even when everything else had turned to ash and questions.

Maybe that would be enough.

Maybe it would have to be.

---

That night, I couldn't sleep.

I lay in my bed, staring at the ceiling, listening to Garicus's breathing across the room. Even and deep. He'd always been able to sleep through anything—a skill learned in the cellar, waiting for morning.

I envied him.

My mind kept circling back to Aldric's face in the chapel. The resignation there. The guilt he'd carried for fifteen years, thinking he could atone by raising us right. By teaching us to be better than the order that destroyed our lives.

By making us into the very thing we should have feared.

The worst part was understanding it. Seeing the logic, the impossible choice he'd faced. If dragon summoning spread, how many villages would burn? How many children would die?

Preventive justice. The greater good. All the justifications that made murder sound like mercy.

I'd heard them all in Aldric's voice when we executed Elara.

And now I knew where they'd learned to speak that way.

A sound pulled me from my thoughts. Soft footsteps in the corridor outside. They paused at our door, and I knew without seeing that it was Aldric—checking on us the way he had when we first arrived, terrified children who woke screaming in the night.

The footsteps moved on.

I closed my eyes and tried to find sleep. Tried not to think about the fact that I still loved him, despite everything. That I hated him, despite everything.

That both truths could exist at once, and neither one made the other less real.

Dawn was hours away, but I waited for it anyway.

Waited for morning, and the choices it would bring.
