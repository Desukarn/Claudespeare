# Chapter 6: Ashes and Truth

The investigations started small.

A question here, a bribe there. Merchants who traveled the northern routes, innkeepers who remembered faces, scribes who kept records of travelers passing through. Weeks slipped by in fragments—mornings spent training with Garicus, afternoons running errands for the Order, evenings walking dusty roads to taverns where strangers might know something I didn't.

I told myself it was curiosity. Just wanting to know if the merchant's words held truth—if somewhere, impossibly, my father still drew breath.

I didn't tell Garicus.

That was the first betrayal. Small, but it counted. We'd shared everything for fifteen years. Every scar, every nightmare, every secret thought. But this... this was mine. A fragile, desperate thing I couldn't bear to expose to his pragmatic gaze.

He'd ask why it mattered. Why I needed to know. And I didn't have an answer that wouldn't sound like I was abandoning what we'd built together.

So I kept quiet and kept searching.

Three weeks into my investigation, I found a priest who remembered a man matching the description. Tall, hollow-eyed, wearing robes that marked him as clergy from the Ashen Reach. The priest had seen him at a monastery months ago, praying for hours in the ruins.

"What monastery?" I asked.

"Blackwood. Burned twenty years past. Nothing there now but ghosts and ash."

I paid him and left before he could ask why I cared.

That night I lay awake in the room I shared with Garicus, listening to his steady breathing, memorizing the location on mental maps. Blackwood Monastery. Two days' ride northwest. I could make an excuse—scouting mission, personal errand, anything. Just go and see. Confirm it was nothing.

Confirm my father was still dead, like he'd been for fifteen years.

Before I could plan further, the letter arrived.

---

It came tucked into a supply shipment, wedged between sacks of grain like it belonged there. No seal. No marking except my name on the outside in careful, slanted script.

I opened it in the stables where no one would see.

*Solaire,*

*I know you've been searching. I know what you've heard. It's true.*

*Meet me where the old walls still stand, where we used to sing before the fires came. Three days hence, at sunset. Come alone.*

*I will explain everything.*

*—Theron*

My hands shook. The paper crumpled in my grip before I smoothed it out, read it again. And again.

Theron. My father's name. A name I barely remembered, pulled from fragments of lullabies and half-dreams. The merchant had been right. He was alive.

And he knew I was looking for him.

The implications crashed over me in waves. How long had he known I survived? Why hadn't he come for me? What truth could possibly justify fifteen years of silence?

I needed to go. Had to go.

But first, I had to tell Garicus.

---

I found him in the armory, sharpening his blade with methodical precision. The rasp of whetstone on steel filled the small room. He didn't look up when I entered.

"You've been distracted," he said. Statement, not question.

"I know."

"Going to tell me why?"

I closed the door. Sat on the bench opposite him. The words stuck in my throat.

"Solaire." He set the blade aside, met my eyes. "Whatever it is, just say it."

"My father is alive."

The silence stretched like a drawn bowstring. Garicus stared at me, expression unreadable.

"How do you know?"

"A merchant recognized my medallion. Said a man was asking about survivors from Thornhaven. I've been... I've been looking into it. Asking around." The words came faster now, spilling out. "I found people who'd seen him. A priest who talked to him. And today I got a letter. He wants to meet me. Three days from now."

"Where?"

"Blackwood Monastery."

Garicus stood abruptly, paced to the window. His jaw worked like he was chewing words too bitter to speak.

"You're not going," he said finally.

"Garicus—"

"You're not going." He turned, and the fury in his eyes made me flinch. "You think this is real? You think after fifteen years, your father just decides to show up and explain himself? It's a trap, Solaire. Or worse, it's him trying to ease his guilt before he dies."

"You don't know that."

"I know that anyone who abandoned their child to burn doesn't deserve a second chance."

The venom in his voice cut deeper than any blade. "He might have had a reason—"

"There is no reason!" Garicus slammed his fist against the wall. The impact echoed. "We made a promise, Solaire. To each other. Not to dead fathers or convenient letters. To each other."

"I remember the promise."

"Do you? Because it feels like you're about to break it."

"I'm not breaking anything." My own anger rose to match his. "I'm going to meet him. That's all. One conversation. Then I'll know."

"Know what? That he's a coward? That he let you think you were alone for fifteen years while he was out there somewhere, alive and free?" Garicus shook his head. "You already know that, Solaire. You just don't want to admit it."

"He's my father."

"I'm your brother."

The words hung between us like a blade waiting to fall.

Garicus took a breath. When he spoke again, his voice was quieter. Harder to hear because it wasn't anger anymore—it was pain.

"Don't go. Please. Whatever he has to say, it won't change what happened. It won't give you back what you lost. It'll just hurt more."

I wanted to agree. Wanted to tear up the letter and pretend it never came. Stay here with Garicus and let the past stay buried.

But I couldn't.

"I have to know," I said. "I'm sorry. I have to."

Garicus looked at me for a long moment. Then he turned away, picked up his sword, and went back to sharpening it.

"Then go," he said. "But don't expect me to follow you into it."

I left him there, the sound of whetstone on steel following me down the corridor like an accusation.

---

The ride to Blackwood took two days, just as I'd calculated. I went alone despite Garicus's warning, despite Aldric's suspicious questions when I requested leave, despite the voice in my head that sounded disturbingly like common sense.

The monastery revealed itself gradually through the trees—blackened stone walls overgrown with ivy, a collapsed bell tower, windows that gaped like empty eye sockets. The fire that destroyed it had been thorough. Only the bones remained.

I dismounted at the gate, tied my horse to a tree, and walked into the ruins.

Sunset painted everything gold and red. Promises in ash, I thought, and almost laughed at the bitter poetry of it.

"Solaire."

The voice came from behind me. Deep. Unfamiliar and yet somehow known, like a song half-remembered from childhood.

I turned.

A man stood in the shadow of the ruined chapel. Tall, just as the merchant described. Priest's robes, gray and travel-worn. But it was his face that stopped my breath—older than I'd imagined, lined with grief and sleepless nights, but unmistakably the face from my fragments of memory.

My father.

Theron.

He stepped into the light. His hands were empty, held palm-up like he was showing me he bore no weapons. The gesture was so vulnerable it hurt to look at.

"You came," he said.

"You asked me to."

"I wasn't sure you would. Wasn't sure you should." He took another step closer. Stopped when I tensed. "You look like your mother."

The words were a knife between my ribs. "Don't."

"I'm sorry. I—" He stopped. Started again. "I don't know how to do this."

"Then why did you ask me here?"

"Because you deserve the truth. Because I've been a coward for fifteen years and I can't—I can't keep living with the silence." His voice cracked. "I need you to know what happened. Even if you hate me for it. Especially if you hate me for it."

I wanted to tell him I didn't care. That whatever truth he'd carried all these years didn't matter anymore. But the lie wouldn't come.

"Tell me," I said.

He nodded. Walked to a fallen pillar and sat heavily, like the weight of what he was about to say had already broken something in him.

"Thornhaven was going to die," he began. "One way or another. The question was how many would die with it."

---

He spoke for an hour. Maybe more. I lost track of time as the words poured out of him—confession, justification, grief all tangled together.

The cult. The village elders, desperate after two years of failed harvests and plague. The forbidden texts they'd found promising power in exchange for sacrifice. Thirteen children under ten years old. Dragon summoning, ancient and catastrophic.

"They'd already taken three," Theron said. His hands shook. "Three children. Killed them in secret, claimed illness or accident. I found the altar. The symbols. The blood. I confronted the elders and they—" He stopped. Swallowed. "They told me I'd be next if I spoke. That my family would burn if I didn't help them finish the ritual."

"So you ran," I said. My voice sounded distant, not mine.

"I ran to the Order of the Crimson Vigil. Told them everything. Begged them to stop it." He looked at me, and I saw the desperation there—still fresh, still raw after fifteen years. "They said they'd handle it. I thought that meant arrest the elders, stop the ritual, save the children who were left."

"But they burned the village instead."

"They burned the village instead," he echoed. "Everyone. The guilty and the innocent. The elders who planned it and the families who knew nothing. Your mother." His voice broke completely. "You."

"Why?"

"Containment. That's what they called it. Can't let knowledge of dragon summoning spread. Can't risk cult members escaping to try again elsewhere. Burn it all and let history forget." He looked at his hands like they were covered in blood. "I led them there, Solaire. I gave them the location, the layout, the best time to strike. I thought I was saving people. I killed them instead."

The world tilted. I sat down hard on the rubble, my legs suddenly unable to hold me.

"You didn't know," I said. Testing the words, seeing if they could be true.

"I should have. I trusted the Order to be better than monsters. I was wrong." He raised his head, met my eyes. "I've spent fifteen years trying to atone. Hunting down cult remnants. Saving villages before they turn to darkness. It doesn't matter. Nothing I do will ever balance the scales."

"You thought I was dead."

"For thirteen years, yes. Then I heard rumors of survivors. Two boys, taken as wards. I started asking questions. Found your name on Order records." He smiled, broken and terrible. "You were alive. My son was alive. And I was the reason you were orphaned."

I couldn't speak. Couldn't think past the roaring in my head.

"I'm telling you this because you deserve to know," Theron continued. "Not because I expect forgiveness. Not because I think it makes anything right. Just because the truth is the only thing I have left to give you."

He stood. Took a step back, giving me space.

"I'll go now. You won't see me again unless you want to. I just..." He stopped. His voice dropped to a whisper. "I'm sorry, Solaire. For all of it. I'm so sorry."

He turned to leave.

"Wait."

He stopped.

I stood on shaking legs, faced this man who was my father and a stranger and the architect of everything that shaped me.

"The children," I said. "The ones they were going to sacrifice. Did the Order save them?"

Theron's expression crumpled. "There were no children left to save. The cult had already completed the ritual the night before I reached the Vigil. The dragon came. It destroyed half the village before the elders lost control and it turned on them. Everyone was already dying when the Order arrived."

The ground dropped out from under me.

"So they burned corpses," I said. "Murdered the survivors to cover up their failure."

"Yes."

"And you helped them."

"Yes."

I wanted to scream. To hit him. To demand he explain how any of that was justifiable. But I couldn't find the air.

Theron didn't move. Didn't defend himself. Just stood there, waiting for whatever judgment I'd deliver.

I had none to give.

"Go," I said finally.

He nodded. Turned. Walked away into the gathering dark.

I stood in the ruins of Blackwood Monastery until the stars came out, trying to understand what I'd learned. Trying to reconcile the man who raised me with the man who doomed me. Trying to feel anything other than the hollow ache spreading through my chest.

My father was alive.

My father led the Order to our village.

My father tried to save us and damned us instead.

The truth wasn't freedom. It was another kind of cage.

---

I rode back to Greystone Keep through the night, arriving just before dawn. Garicus was awake, sitting on the wall where we'd made our promise years ago. He saw me coming and stood.

I dismounted. Walked to him. He searched my face, reading what he found there.

"He was there," Garicus said.

"Yes."

"Did he tell you why?"

"Yes."

Garicus waited. When I didn't elaborate, he asked, "Do you want to talk about it?"

"No."

He nodded. Didn't push. Just stood beside me as the sun rose, two brothers watching morning break over a world that made less sense than it did yesterday.

"I'm sorry," I said finally.

"For what?"

"For going. For not listening. For—" My voice cracked. "For thinking the truth would make anything better."

Garicus put his hand on my shoulder. Squeezed once.

"Truth doesn't make things better," he said quietly. "It just makes them true."

We stood there until the training bells rang, calling us to another day of discipline and violence and purpose. Then we walked down together, shoulder to shoulder, and didn't speak of it again.

But something had shifted. Some certainty I'd carried for fifteen years had cracked and would never fully heal.

My father was alive.

And I wished he wasn't.
