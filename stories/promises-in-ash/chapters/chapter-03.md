# Chapter 3: Forged in Fire

Eight years.

I count them sometimes, when the nightmares wake me—eight winters since the cellar, eight springs since the promise. Eight years of Aldric's voice barking corrections, of wooden swords becoming steel, of calluses layering over softer skin until my hands barely remembered what it meant to hold anything but a weapon.

The training yard at Greystone Keep smells like iron and sweat. Always has. I stand in its center now, sixteen years old and harder than I was at eight, waiting for the trial that will make us wards of the Order. The morning sun cuts through the mist, catching on the practice dummies that line the yard's edge—straw men we've beaten to pieces and sewn back together a hundred times.

Solaire shifts beside me. He's taller than me now, has been for two years. I stopped growing at fourteen. He didn't.

"Nervous?" he asks.

"No."

"Liar." He grins, but it doesn't reach his eyes. His fingers tap against his thigh—one, two, three, pause, repeat. Old habit. He does it when he's trying not to think about something.

I don't ask what. I already know. Same thing I'm trying not to think about: what comes after we pass. The missions. The villages. The things Aldric doesn't talk about when he thinks we're sleeping.

The keep's gates grind open. Three knights emerge, crimson cloaks heavy despite the summer heat. Master Edran leads them—older than Aldric, with a scar that splits his face from temple to jaw. The other two I don't recognize. Don't need to. They're here to watch us bleed.

"Garicus." Edran's voice carries like a blade scraping stone. "Solaire. You stand before the Order of the Crimson Vigil as supplicants. For eight years you have trained. Today you prove yourselves worthy."

The ritual words. We knew they were coming. Doesn't make them feel less like a noose tightening.

"The trial is simple," Edran continues. "Defeat your opponent with live steel. First blood, or yield. The Order does not suffer the weak."

A fourth knight steps from the gate. Younger than Edran, older than us. Maybe twenty. His sword is already drawn.

"Garicus," Edran says. "You first."

I step forward. The yard goes quiet except for the wind and Solaire's breathing behind me. I draw my sword—the one Aldric gave me on my fourteenth birthday, plain crossguard and leather-wrapped hilt, edge sharp enough to split hair. It feels lighter than it did two years ago. Feels like part of my arm.

The knight doesn't introduce himself. Doesn't need to. This isn't about names.

He comes at me fast. Not testing, not teaching—trying to end it. His blade sweeps low, aiming for my knee. I pivot, let it hiss past, bring my sword down where his neck will be in half a second.

He's not there. He's already moving, blade rising to catch mine with a sound like a bell cracking. The impact shudders up my arm but I don't let go. Never let go. That was the first lesson.

We circle. He's better than me. I can see it in how he moves—effortless, like he's done this a thousand times and will do it a thousand more. I've done it eight years. It's not the same.

But I've spent eight years learning how to win when I shouldn't.

He lunges. I don't dodge. I step in, inside his guard, too close for his blade to bite. My shoulder drives into his chest and I feel his breath leave in a rush. His sword arm swings wide, trying to recover, and I'm already dropping low, sweeping his legs.

He goes down hard. My blade finds his throat before he can roll away.

"Yield," I say.

He looks at Edran. Edran nods.

"Yield," the knight repeats.

I step back. Offer him a hand. He doesn't take it. Just stands, sheathes his blade, walks back to the others without a word.

My hand is shaking. I don't let them see it.

"Solaire," Edran calls.

Solaire steps forward. His fingers have stopped tapping. That's how I know he's terrified—when he goes still, when he stops filling silence with movement. I want to tell him it's fine, that he's ready, that I've watched him beat me in practice more times than I can count.

I don't say anything. He wouldn't believe me anyway.

The knight facing him is different—smaller, faster. She doesn't wait for Edran to finish speaking. She just attacks.

Solaire barely gets his blade up in time. Steel screams against steel and he stumbles back, off-balance. She presses the advantage, raining blows like she's trying to break him through sheer force. He blocks, deflects, retreats step by step across the yard.

He's losing.

I know the moment he realizes it too. I see it in how his shoulders tighten, how his grip shifts on his sword. He's thinking too much. Always does when it matters.

The knight feints high, cuts low. Solaire jumps back but not fast enough—her blade kisses his thigh, drawing a thin line of red through his trousers.

First blood.

"Yield," the knight says.

Solaire doesn't move. His eyes find mine across the yard, wide and desperate. I shake my head once.

He doesn't yield.

Instead he surges forward, sword cutting in a wild arc that makes my teeth ache. It's too much, too reckless—the kind of move that gets you killed in a real fight. But this isn't a real fight. This is a trial. And sometimes trials aren't about winning clean.

The knight blocks, but she has to give ground. Solaire keeps coming, pressing her back toward the fence, every strike a little wilder than the last. She's better than him. But she's not expecting him to fight like someone with nothing to lose.

Her foot catches on uneven ground. She stumbles.

Solaire's blade stops an inch from her ribs.

"Yield," he says. His voice doesn't shake. Doesn't gloat. Just states fact.

The knight looks at him for a long moment. Then she smiles—brief, sharp—and sheathes her sword.

"Yield."

Solaire steps back, breathing hard. The cut on his thigh is bleeding through his trousers but he doesn't seem to notice. When our eyes meet again I nod. Just once.

He grins. This time it reaches his eyes.

---

The oath ceremony happens at sunset.

They lead us to the keep's chapel, a stone room with no windows and crimson candles burning in niches along the walls. The candles smell wrong—sweet and rotten at once. Aldric told us once what they're made from. We didn't ask again.

Edran stands at the altar, the other knights forming a half-circle behind him. Aldric is there too, off to the side, his face unreadable in the flickering light.

"Kneel," Edran commands.

We kneel. The stone is cold even through our trousers. My knee finds a crack and settles into it like it was made for this.

"Garicus. Solaire. You have proven your strength. Now you will prove your commitment. Repeat the words I speak."

He begins the oath. We repeat it.

"I pledge my sword to the Order of the Crimson Vigil."

The words taste like ash.

"I will stand against the darkness, no matter the cost."

Solaire's voice wavers on 'cost.' Mine doesn't. I stopped wavering years ago.

"I will hunt those who summon forbidden powers, who threaten the innocent, who would bring ruin upon the realm."

My mother's screams, distant and half-remembered, echo under the words. I push them down.

"I will be the blade in the night, the vigil that never sleeps, the fire that purges corruption."

Fire. Always fire. The Order loves its fire.

"This I swear, on blood and honor, until death claims me."

Edran draws a knife from his belt—small, ceremonial, sharp. He cuts his palm, then holds it out to me. I take the knife. Cut my own palm. The pain is bright and clean. I clasp his hand, our blood mixing.

He does the same with Solaire.

"Rise, wards of the Order."

We stand. My hand throbs in time with my pulse. Beside me, Solaire is still gripping his bleeding palm, staring at it like he's trying to memorize the shape of the wound.

"You are dismissed," Edran says. "Training begins at dawn."

The other knights file out. Aldric lingers, watching us, but when I meet his gaze he turns and follows the others.

We're alone in the chapel. Just us and the candles and the oath still hanging in the air like smoke.

"Come on," I say quietly.

We walk out together, through the keep's corridors, past the training yard, to the place we always go when we need to be away from everything else: the wall overlooking the forest. The sun is below the horizon now, the sky streaked with red and gold. Promises in ash, I think. That's what this looks like.

Solaire sits on the wall's edge, legs dangling over empty air. I sit beside him. We don't talk for a long time.

Finally, he speaks.

"Do you remember what we promised? That night?"

"Yes."

"Say it."

I close my eyes. I can still smell the smoke, still hear the silence after the screaming stopped.

"Never let each other die alone."

"Never let each other die alone," he echoes. His hand finds mine—the one that's still bleeding from the oath. He squeezes it. I squeeze back.

"I don't care what oath they made us swear in there," Solaire says. His voice is quiet but steady. "That one doesn't count. Not really. The only oath that matters is the one we made in the ashes."

"I know."

"To each other. Not to them."

"I know."

He turns to look at me, and in the dying light he looks so much like the boy from eight years ago—small and scared and desperate for something to hold onto—that my chest aches.

"Promise me again," he says. "Right now. No ceremony, no blood, no knights watching. Just us."

I turn to face him fully. The forest below us is darkening, the keep behind us is lighting its torches, and somewhere in the chapel Edran is probably cleaning blood off the altar. None of it matters.

"I promise," I say. "Never alone. Whatever comes. However long it takes."

"However long it takes," Solaire repeats.

We sit there as night falls, two boys who survived fire once and swore they'd survive it again. The Order has our blood now. Our swords. Our mornings and our evenings and the years stretching ahead.

But they don't have this. They'll never have this.

---

Aldric stands in the chapel long after the candles should have been extinguished.

He tells himself he's checking that the altar is clean, that the ceremonial knife has been returned to its proper place, that everything is ready for tomorrow's training. He tells himself a lot of things these days.

The truth is simpler: he can't bear to go back to his quarters yet. Can't bear to lie down and pretend to sleep when all he'll see is Garicus's face during the oath, blank and hard as stone. Solaire's face, trying so hard to believe the words mattered.

They're good boys. He raised them to be good. Taught them to fight, to survive, to question nothing. And they learned. God help him, they learned so well.

He should feel proud.

Instead he feels like a man lighting a pyre and calling it warmth.

The knife is still on the altar. He picks it up, turns it over in his hands. Eight years ago he stood in their burning village and made a choice: take them, raise them, teach them to be weapons. He thought it was mercy. Thought if he trained them hard enough, loved them carefully enough, they'd be strong enough to survive when the truth finally came.

Now he's not sure there's any strength in the world that will be enough.

"I'm sorry," he says to the empty chapel. To the boys who aren't there. To the man he was before he learned that righteousness and ruin wear the same cloak.

The candles gutter. He doesn't relight them.

He leaves the knife on the altar and walks out into the dark.
