## Synopsis

Walter's sister Beatrice is dying. In desperation, he steals what he believes is a wish-granting fairy trapped in a bottle—an ancient prize from a wealthy collector's forbidden room.

But the creature he's unleashed is something far older and more terrible than any children's tale. It speaks in gentle tones and appears delicate as flowers, but it operates on incomprehensible rules that have nothing to do with human concepts like death, love, or self.

The fairy grants his wish to "make her well." But well means something very different to a being that doesn't understand what it means to be alive.

Set in a beautifully rendered world of lamplight, tea, and worn quilts, *The Bottled Dark* is a tragedy about love that destroys, patterns that cannot be stopped, and the horrifying realization that some things are worse than death.

**A Ghibli-style horror**: bright, beautiful, whimsical on the surface—and deeply, viscerally terrifying underneath.

# Chapter 1: The Dying Room

The coughing started again just after sunset.

Walt set down the tin cup he'd been heating over the lamp and turned toward the narrow bed pushed against the far wall. Bea's shoulders shook with each spasm, her thin frame curling inward like paper near a flame. He crossed the small attic room in three steps and knelt beside her, one hand on her back.

"Easy," he said. "Just breathe."

She couldn't, not yet. The coughs tore through her, wet and rattling. Walt waited, his hand making small circles between her shoulder blades, feeling each ridge of her spine through the worn cotton of her nightgown. When the fit finally passed, she sagged back against the pillows, face pale as the moth-eaten quilt pooled around her waist.

"Better?" he asked.

Bea managed a smile. Her lips had a faint blue tinge that hadn't been there last week. "Better than what?"

"Better than yesterday."

"That's a low bar, Walt."

He returned to the lamp and poured the now-warm water into a chipped teacup painted with faded roses. The apothecary had left them a powder—something for the pain, he'd said, though Walt suspected it did nothing at all. He stirred it in anyway and brought the cup to her.

She took it in both hands. Her fingers looked like bird bones wrapped in skin.

"You don't have to hover," she said, but there was no heat in it. She sipped the tea, grimaced at the bitter taste, sipped again.

Walt moved to the small window and looked out. Rain slicked the narrow street below, turning the cobblestones black and making halos around the gas lamps. Their attic room sat above a candle-maker's shop, and most evenings the smell of tallow crept up through the floorboards. Tonight it mixed with the scent of Bea's medicinal tea and the damp wool of his coat hanging by the door.

"Did you eat?" he asked.

"Some broth. Mrs. Hadley brought it up this afternoon."

"That was kind of her."

"She thinks I'm dying."

Walt's jaw tightened. He didn't turn from the window. "You're not."

"Walt—"

"You're not."

Silence, except for the rain and the occasional creak of someone moving in the room below. Bea set the teacup down on the floor beside the bed, her movements slow and deliberate.

"Come sit with me," she said.

He did, pulling the room's only chair—a rickety thing with an uneven leg—close to her bedside. In the lamplight, her face looked softer, younger. She'd turned twenty-one last month. Walt had stolen a honey cake from a baker's window sill to mark the occasion, and they'd split it, laughing at their own ridiculousness.

She wasn't laughing now.

"The apothecary came this morning," she said.

Walt nodded. He'd passed the man on the stairs, had seen the look in his eyes. The kind of look you'd give a horse with a broken leg.

"He said there's nothing more he can do." Bea's voice was quiet, matter-of-fact. "The tinctures aren't working. The powders aren't working. He said I should... prepare myself."

"He's wrong."

"He's not, though." She reached out and took Walt's hand. Her palm was cold. "I'm tired, Walt. All the time. I can barely walk from the bed to the window without needing to rest. And the coughing—"

"Will get better."

"It won't." She squeezed his hand. "It's alright. I've made my peace with it."

"Well, I haven't."

Bea sighed, but it was a fond sound. "You're stubborn."

"Runs in the family."

"Does it?" Her mouth quirked. "I seem to remember being the reasonable one."

Despite everything, Walt almost smiled. "You're the one who tried to climb the cathedral roof when you were nine."

"I wanted to see the gargoyles up close."

"You fell and broke your arm."

"And I saw the gargoyles." She leaned back against the pillows, eyes drifting closed. "Worth it."

Walt studied her face—the dark circles under her eyes, the hollow of her cheeks, the way her chest rose and fell with each labored breath. Six months ago, she'd been well enough to work. She'd done mending for a seamstress, careful, patient work that brought in just enough to keep them fed. Now she could barely hold a needle.

Six months. That's all it had taken for the illness to hollow her out.

"I'm going to fix this," he said.

Bea opened her eyes. "How?"

"I don't know yet."

"Walt—"

"There are physicians in the city. Real ones, not just apothecaries. People who study at universities. They'd know what to do."

"We can't afford them."

"I'll find a way."

She looked at him for a long moment, something sad and knowing in her expression. "Don't do anything stupid."

"Would I ever?"

"Yes. Frequently."

He stood, needing to move, and busied himself tidying things that didn't need tidying—straightening the blanket at the foot of her bed, adjusting the lamp's wick, moving the teacup to the small table they used for meals. Bea watched him, but she didn't speak.

When there was nothing left to rearrange, Walt picked up his coat from its peg by the door.

"Where are you going?" Bea asked.

"Out for a bit. Need some air."

"It's raining."

"I'll manage."

She didn't argue, but her eyes followed him as he shrugged into the coat. It was still damp from earlier, cold against his shirt.

"Walt," she said.

He paused at the door.

"Don't do anything stupid," she said again, softer this time. "Please."

He looked back at her—small and pale in the lamplight, already half-shadow. "Get some rest. I won't be long."

The narrow stairs creaked under his weight. He passed through the candle-maker's shop, nodded to the owner's wife who was sweeping the floor, and stepped out into the rain.

The streets were quiet, slick and dark. Walt pulled his coat tighter and walked.

He had no plan, not really. Just a bone-deep certainty that he couldn't sit in that room and watch Bea fade. Wouldn't. He'd done enough watching in his life—watched their parents sicken and die when he was sixteen and Bea was eleven, watched their small inheritance disappear to creditors and landlords, watched opportunities close like doors in a hallway until theft was the only one left open.

He'd kept them alive through lean times and leaner. Kept a roof over their heads, food in their bellies. It hadn't been a good life, but it had been a life.

He wouldn't watch it end. Not like this.

The tavern on Millgate Street was warm and loud, packed with bodies seeking shelter from the weather. Walt slipped inside and found a spot near the back where he could watch the room. He had a few coins in his pocket—enough for one drink, maybe two if he nursed them.

He ordered ale and listened.

Taverns were good for listening. People talked freely when they thought no one important was paying attention. Walt had learned years ago that being unremarkable was a useful skill. You could hear a lot if you looked like the kind of person no one would bother remembering.

Tonight he heard about a shipment delayed by the rain, a merchant's daughter who'd run off with a soldier, the price of grain going up again. Nothing useful.

Then a voice cut through the others—cultivated, expensive. Out of place in this room.

"...absolutely extraordinary. The detail on the etchings alone must have taken months. And the contents! My God, the contents."

Walt shifted slightly, angling himself to see the speaker. A man in his fifties, well-dressed in dark wool and brass buttons, holding court at a corner table. A collector, by the look of him. The type who bought and sold curiosities to other wealthy eccentrics.

"What is it?" someone asked.

The collector waved a hand. "A bottle. But not just any bottle—it's covered in symbols, protective wards I think, though I haven't been able to translate them all. And inside..." He leaned forward, voice dropping to a dramatic whisper. "There's something alive in there."

Murmurs around the table. Walt took a sip of his ale and tried to look bored.

"Alive?" a woman said. "What, like a insect?"

"I don't know what it is. Something small, luminous. It moves. Sometimes I think I can hear it singing, though that's probably my imagination."

"Where'd you get it?"

"Estate sale. Previous owner died rather suddenly." The collector's smile was thin. "His notes were... incomplete. But from what I could piece together, the bottle has been passed down through several collections. Always changing hands. No one keeps it long."

"Why not?"

The collector shrugged. "Bad luck, perhaps. Or bad nerves. It is rather unsettling to look at."

"But valuable?"

"Immensely. I've already had three inquiries from potential buyers. I'm thinking of auctioning it next month."

Walt set his ale down carefully, mind working. A valuable curiosity in the hands of a collector. The type of thing that might fetch enough to pay for a real physician. Maybe several physicians.

He finished his drink and left quietly, slipping back out into the rain.

The collector's house wouldn't be hard to find. Men like that always lived in the nicer parts of the city, places where the streets had names instead of numbers and the gas lamps actually worked. Walt had done enough second-story work to know how to get in and out of a wealthy man's home.

The walk back to the attic room was longer than the walk out, or maybe it just felt that way. By the time Walt climbed the stairs, his coat was soaked through and rain dripped from his hair.

Bea was asleep. Her breathing sounded rough even in rest, but her face was peaceful.

Walt hung his coat to dry and sat down in the chair beside her bed. Through the window, the rain kept falling, turning the world outside into a blur of dark water and dim light.

Tomorrow, he thought. Tomorrow he'd find the collector's house. Tomorrow he'd take that bottle and sell it to one of those inquiring buyers. Tomorrow he'd get enough money to save her.

Bea stirred in her sleep, a small sound escaping her lips. Walt reached out and pulled the quilt up to her shoulders.

"Tomorrow," he whispered to the room, to the rain, to whatever was listening. "I'll fix this tomorrow."

In the morning, he'd be a thief again. But tonight, he was just her brother, keeping watch in the lamplight while she slept.

Tomorrow would come soon enough.

# Chapter 2: The Collector's House

The house stood three stories tall on a street where the cobblestones actually fit together properly.

Walt approached from the south, keeping to the shadows between the gas lamps. It was well past midnight, and the rain had finally stopped, leaving everything slick and clean-smelling. The collector's home was exactly the type he'd expected—old money, old stone, the kind of place that had probably housed the same family for generations until someone died and their things got sold off piece by piece.

He'd spent the day watching it. Learning the rhythms of the household. The cook left at six. The housekeeper at seven. The collector himself had gone out around nine and returned an hour later, alone. By eleven, all the lights were out except for one on the third floor, which went dark around midnight.

Now Walt stood in the narrow alley that ran alongside the house, looking up at a window on the second floor. It was latched, but that wouldn't matter. Latches never did.

He'd brought the tools of his trade—a thin knife, a length of rope, a small dark lantern. The rope went around a drainpipe, tested for weight. It held. Walt climbed quickly, boots finding purchase on the stone, hands sure despite the damp.

The window gave without much protest. He slid it up, slipped through, and closed it behind him.

Inside was dark and still. Walt waited, counting his breaths, letting his eyes adjust. Gradually, shapes emerged from the blackness—furniture, doorways, the bulk of something large and draped in cloth.

He was in a sitting room of some kind. The air smelled of dust and old wood and something else, something faintly sweet and wrong that he couldn't quite place. Walt opened his lantern just enough to let out a sliver of light and moved deeper into the house.

The collector had mentioned his acquisitions. They'd be somewhere he could show them off, somewhere with space and security. Walt found it on the second floor, at the end of a long hallway—a room with a heavy door that wasn't locked. Rich people never thought they needed to lock doors inside their own homes.

The cabinet of curiosities was exactly what the name suggested.

Glass-fronted cases lined the walls, filled with objects arranged for display. Taxidermy birds with jeweled eyes. Crystals that caught the light from Walt's lantern and threw it back in sharp rainbows. A human skull covered in intricate carvings. Mechanical insects made of brass and copper, frozen mid-step. Books bound in leather that looked uncomfortably like skin.

Walt moved past them all, looking for the bottle.

He found it on a central table, elevated on a small wooden stand.

The bottle itself was beautiful. Heavy glass with a greenish tint, covered in symbols etched so deeply they caught the light. The cork was sealed with wax and wrapped in silver wire, as if someone had been very serious about keeping it closed.

But it was what was inside that made Walt stop and stare.

Light. Soft and golden-green, moving like it had a source. Like something alive was producing it.

Walt set his lantern down and leaned closer. The light pulsed gently, almost like breathing. As he watched, it brightened for a moment, and he saw—

A shape. Small. Delicate.

It moved.

Walt's breath caught. The collector hadn't been exaggerating. There was something in there. Something that turned in the thick liquid (was it liquid? it didn't move quite like water) and pressed what might have been a hand against the inside of the glass.

For just a moment, Walt could have sworn it looked at him.

He reached out slowly, fingers brushing the bottle's surface. It was cold. No—warm. No—both, in a way that didn't make sense. The glass felt smooth and wrong against his palm, like touching something from a dream.

This was what the collector had talked about. The thing with the etchings and the protective wards and the strange contents that no one kept for long.

Bad luck, the collector had said. Or bad nerves.

Walt picked it up.

The bottle was heavier than it looked. The weight seemed to shift in his hand, as if whatever was inside was moving to compensate for the change in position. Walt held it up to his lantern light and looked closer.

The symbols covered every inch of the glass—spirals and angles and shapes that looked almost like writing but in no alphabet Walt recognized. Protective wards, the collector had guessed. Warnings, maybe. Or instructions.

Whatever they said, someone had been very careful about it.

Walt's original plan had been simple: take the bottle, sell it to one of those interested buyers the collector had mentioned, use the money for Bea. But looking at it now, holding it in his hands while the thing inside pulsed with gentle light...

He should leave it. Every instinct he'd honed over years of thieving told him to put it back and find something else. This was the kind of object that came with complications.

But Bea was dying. And this was valuable.

Walt wrapped the bottle in a cloth from his pocket and tucked it carefully into his coat. The weight of it settled against his ribs, wrong-temperature and heavy.

He was halfway back to the window when he heard footsteps in the hallway.

Walt froze. The lantern was still open on the table, its light a tiny beacon in the dark. He moved quickly, closing it, plunging the room back into blackness. The footsteps came closer.

The door opened.

Lamplight spilled into the room, and with it came the collector—the same man from the tavern, now dressed in a nightshirt and robe, holding a lamp in one hand and a fireplace poker in the other.

Their eyes met.

The collector's gaze dropped to Walt's coat, where the wrapped bottle made a tell-tale bulge. Understanding crossed his face. And then—

Relief.

It was quick, gone almost as soon as it appeared, replaced by appropriate shock and alarm. But Walt had seen it. The flash of something that looked almost like gratitude.

"So," the collector said. His voice was steady. "You've taken it."

Walt said nothing. His hand moved to the knife in his belt.

The collector noticed, but he didn't raise the poker. Didn't call for help. Didn't do any of the things a wealthy man should do when confronted with a thief in his home.

Instead, he smiled thinly. "Good."

Walt's fingers tightened on the knife hilt. "You're not going to stop me?"

"No." The collector set his lamp down on a nearby table, movements careful and deliberate. "In fact, I'm going to let you leave. Through the front door, if you'd like. Less risk of falling."

"Why?"

The collector looked at him for a long moment. In the lamplight, his face was lined and tired, older than he'd seemed in the tavern. "Because I've been trying to rid myself of that cursed thing for six months, and no one will buy it. Oh, they inquire. They come to look. But the moment they touch it, they change their minds." He glanced at the spot where the bottle had stood. "You're the first person who's actually taken it."

Walt didn't move. This had to be a trick. Some way of getting him to lower his guard so the collector could call for the constables.

But the man just stood there, looking almost... sad.

"You'll sell it," the collector said. It wasn't a question. "Or use it. I don't know which, and I don't want to know. But let me give you one piece of advice." He met Walt's eyes. "It will give you exactly what you ask for. No more, no less. And that will be the problem."

"I don't understand."

"You will." The collector stepped aside, clearing a path to the door. "Now go. Please. Before I change my mind."

Walt moved past him carefully, watching for any sign of attack. None came. The collector just stood there in his nightshirt, looking at the empty space where the bottle had been, expression unreadable.

At the door, Walt paused. "The protective wards on the glass. What do they say?"

"I don't know. I never translated them all." The collector's smile was bitter. "But the ones I did manage said 'Do not open.' In seven different dead languages."

Walt's hand moved unconsciously to his coat, feeling the weight of the bottle. "And you kept it anyway?"

"I'm a collector. We can't help ourselves." He looked at Walt. "You should leave. Now. While you still can."

Walt didn't need to be told twice.

He left through the front door, as suggested, and walked quickly through the empty streets. The bottle seemed to pulse against his ribs in time with his heartbeat. Or maybe that was his imagination.

He didn't look back.

By the time Walt reached the familiar streets of his own neighborhood, the sky was beginning to lighten in the east. The candle-maker's shop was dark and quiet. He climbed the stairs as softly as he could and let himself into the attic room.

Bea was asleep, curled on her side, one hand tucked under her pillow. Her breathing sounded terrible—wet and rattling—but at least she was breathing.

# Chapter 2: The Collector's House

The house stood three stories tall on a street where the cobblestones actually fit together properly.

He'd brought the tools of his trade—a thin knife, a length of rope, a small dark lantern. The rope went around a drainpipe, tested for weight. It held. Walt climbed quickly, boots finding purchase on the stone, hands sure despite the damp.

The window gave without much protest. He slid it up, slipped through, and closed it behind him.

Inside was dark and still. Walt waited, counting his breaths, letting his eyes adjust. Gradually, shapes emerged from the blackness—furniture, doorways, the bulk of something large and draped in cloth.

He was in a sitting room of some kind. The air smelled of dust and old wood and something else, something faintly sweet and wrong that he couldn't place. Walt opened his lantern enough to let out a sliver of light and moved deeper into the house.

The collector had mentioned his acquisitions. They'd be somewhere he could show them off, somewhere with space and security. Walt found it on the second floor, at the end of a long hallway—a room with a heavy door that wasn't locked. Rich people never thought they needed to lock doors inside their own homes.

The cabinet of curiosities was exactly what the name suggested.

Glass-fronted cases lined the walls, filled with objects arranged for display. Taxidermy birds with jeweled eyes. Crystals that caught the light from Walt's lantern and threw it back in sharp rainbows. A human skull covered in intricate carvings. Mechanical insects made of brass and copper, frozen mid-step. Books bound in leather that looked uncomfortably like skin.

Walt moved past them all, looking for the bottle.

He found it on a central table, elevated on a small wooden stand.

The bottle itself was beautiful. Heavy glass with a greenish tint, covered in symbols etched so deeply they caught the light. The cork was sealed with wax and wrapped in silver wire, as if someone had been serious about keeping it closed.

But it was what was inside that made Walt stop and stare.

Light. Soft and golden-green, moving like it had a source. Like something alive was producing it.

Walt set his lantern down and leaned closer. The light pulsed gently, almost like breathing. As he watched, it brightened for a moment, and he saw—

A shape. Small. Delicate.

It moved.

Walt's breath caught. The collector hadn't been exaggerating. There was something in there. Something that turned in the thick liquid (was it liquid? it didn't move like water) and pressed what might have been a hand against the inside of the glass.

For a moment, Walt could have sworn it looked at him.

He reached out slowly, fingers brushing the bottle's surface. It was cold. No—warm. No—both, in a way that didn't make sense. The glass felt smooth and wrong against his palm, like touching something from a dream.

This was what the collector had talked about. The thing with the etchings and the protective wards and the strange contents that no one kept for long.

Bad luck, the collector had said. Or bad nerves.

Whatever they said, someone had been careful about it.

Relief.

# Chapter 3: First Contact

Bea woke to find Walt asleep in the chair beside her bed, his head tipped forward, chin nearly touching his chest.

The morning light coming through the window was thin and gray. Rain had started again sometime during the night, a soft patter against the glass. Bea shifted carefully, trying not to wake him, but the movement triggered a cough that she couldn't quite suppress.

Walt's eyes opened immediately. He straightened, blinking sleep away, one hand going to the small of his back.

"You should've slept in the bed," Bea said, voice hoarse.

"You were in it."

"There's room."

"You need the rest more than I do." He stood, stretching, and moved to the table where the kettle usually sat. That's when Bea saw it.

The bottle.

It sat on the table, catching what little light filtered through the window, and it was the most beautiful thing she'd ever seen.

"What is that?" she asked.

Walt turned, following her gaze. His expression shifted into something careful and neutral. "Something I found."

"You stole it."

"Yes."

Bea tried to sit up. The effort cost her, but she managed it, propping herself against the thin pillows. "Walt—"

"It's valuable. I can sell it. Get enough money to pay for proper doctors, not just old Brennan and his useless powders."

"It's glowing."

"I know."

"There's something inside it."

Walt was quiet for a moment. Then he picked up the bottle—carefully, like it might break or bite—and brought it to her. "I don't know what it is," he admitted. "The man I took it from said it was alive. I thought he was exaggerating."

Bea took the bottle from him, feeling its weight settle into her palms. The glass was warm on one side and cold on the other, temperature shifting against her skin in a way that made no sense. The symbols etched into the surface were beautiful and precise, spiraling around the bottle in patterns that seemed to move when she wasn't looking directly at them.

And inside—

"Oh," Bea breathed.

The light pulsed. Brightened. And in its glow, she saw the thing that made it.

It was small, no larger than her hand. Delicate, like something made of flower petals and spider silk. It had wings—translucent, veined with gold—and a shape that was almost human but not quite. Too many joints in the fingers. Eyes that reflected the light in colors that didn't exist.

It was looking at her.

Bea's hands trembled. Not from weakness, not this time. From something else. An instinct that went deeper than thought, older than words. Every part of her that still remembered how to survive was screaming that this was *wrong*.

"We should get rid of it," she said.

"What?"

"Whatever this is, Walt, it's not—" She stopped, trying to find words for the feeling coiling in her chest. "It's not right. Look at it. Really look at it."

Walt frowned, but he looked. Bea watched his face as he studied the creature in the bottle, watched the moment his expression shifted from curiosity to something more uncertain.

"It's strange," he said finally. "But that doesn't mean it's dangerous."

"Doesn't it?"

Before Walt could answer, the thing in the bottle moved. It pressed against the glass, fingers (too many fingers) spreading wide, and opened its mouth.

The sound that came out was like wind chimes. Beautiful and pure and wrong in a way that made Bea's teeth ache.

"We can cure the dying-thing," it said.

Bea almost dropped the bottle. Only Walt's quick hands kept it from falling, taking it from her and setting it back on the table. They both stared at it.

The creature tilted its head—too far, at an angle human necks couldn't manage—and waited.

"Did it just—" Bea started.

"Yes," Walt said.

The creature blinked, eyes reflecting the lamplight in shades of gold and green and colors Bea couldn't name. "We can help," it said in that wind-chime voice that seemed to come from everywhere and nowhere at once. "The sick-thing, the dying-one. We can unknit the sickness."

Walt's hand was on Bea's shoulder, grip tight. "Can you understand us?" he asked.

"Yessss." The word had too many sounds in it, harmonics that hurt to hear. "We understand. We can help. You want the dying-thing to live. We want—" It paused, head tilting the other direction. "—to complete the pattern."

Bea's instincts screamed louder. "No. Walt, no. Whatever it is, whatever it's offering—"

"It says it can cure you."

"It's in a bottle covered in warning symbols. In seven languages, you said the collector told you." She grabbed Walt's wrist, felt how cold her own fingers were against his skin. "This isn't medicine. This isn't—it's something else."

"The pattern must complete," the creature said, voice soft and soothing as honey. "The dying-thing fits. The right shape. The right time. We will unknit the sickness. She will be well."

"See?" Walt pulled away from Bea's grip. "It can help."

"At what cost?"

Walt didn't answer. Bea could see the war playing out on his face—hope against caution, desperation against sense. She knew which one would win. She'd known since the moment she saw the bottle.

"Walt," she tried one more time. "Please. I'm ready. I've made my peace with—"

"Well, I haven't." He turned to the bottle, to the creature inside. "You can cure her? Make her well?"

"Yesss. We will unknit the sickness. Unweave the dying. She will be... more."

"More what?"

The creature just smiled. Its mouth had too many teeth, all of them small and perfect and white as new snow.

"Don't—" Bea started.

But Walt was already speaking. "Make my sister well," he said, voice firm despite the tremor in his hands. "Cure her. Please."

The creature's eyes brightened. It pressed both hands against the glass, and the light inside the bottle grew. "Yessss," it said. "We will help. We will complete. We will unknot the dying-thing and make her well well well—"

Heat bloomed in Bea's chest.

It wasn't painful, not exactly. More like warmth spreading through cold water, ice melting under a sudden sun. It radiated from her lungs outward, following her veins, settling into her bones.

The constant ache she'd been living with for months—the tightness in her chest, the weight that made every breath a conscious effort—eased.

Not gone. But... less.

Bea drew in a breath. Then another. No cough. No rattle. Just air, clean and full, filling her lungs the way it used to.

"Bea?" Walt's voice was tight with hope. "Are you—"

"I don't know." She put a hand to her chest, feeling her heartbeat. Strong. Steady. When was the last time it had felt steady? "I feel... different."

"Good different?"

"I think so." But even as she said it, something in her—that same instinct that had screamed danger—whispered that this wasn't right. The warmth in her chest felt wrong. Not painful, but *other*. Like it didn't quite belong to her.

The creature in the bottle hummed, a sound that made Bea's vision blur at the edges. "Good," it said. "Good good good. We are unknitting. She will be well. She will be more-than-well. She will be *perfect*."

Walt knelt beside the bed, taking Bea's hands in his. "How do you feel? Really?"

"Warm," Bea said. "And... tired. But not the same kind of tired. Not sick-tired. Just tired."

"You should rest." Walt was grinning now, relief and joy bright in his face. "Sleep. Get your strength back."

Bea wanted to argue, wanted to say that this felt wrong, that they should be careful, that creatures in bottles didn't grant wishes without prices.

But she was tired. And for the first time in months, her chest didn't hurt.

She lay back against the pillows. Walt pulled the quilt up around her shoulders, tucking it in the way he had when she was little and woke from nightmares.

"Thank you," he said to the creature. "I don't know what you are or what you want, but—thank you."

The creature tilted its head. "We want to complete the pattern," it said simply. "That is all. That is everything. You have helped. We have helped. Now the pattern continues."

Walt nodded as if this made sense. He set the bottle back on the table, within sight but not within reach.

Bea watched it through half-closed eyes. The light pulsed, soft and steady. The creature moved inside its glass prison, wings fluttering, fingers tracing patterns on the inside of the bottle that left glowing trails in their wake.

It was still looking at her.

Bea closed her eyes, but she could feel its gaze. Could feel the warmth in her chest, spreading, settling, changing things in ways she didn't understand.

Sleep came quickly, faster than it should have. As she drifted off, she thought she heard singing. A voice like wind chimes, humming a melody that had no beginning and no end, just spiraling patterns of sound that wound through her dreams and made her think of things she had no names for.

When Bea woke, the light outside had changed. Afternoon, maybe. She sat up—and realized it didn't hurt.

No shortness of breath. No tightness in her chest. No need to move slowly and carefully to avoid triggering a coughing fit.

She stood up. Actually stood, without needing to brace herself on the bedframe.

Walt wasn't in the room. The bottle sat on the table, glowing softly. The creature inside was very still, watching her.

Bea walked to the window—walked, not shuffled—and looked out at the rain-darkened street below. Her reflection in the glass looked almost like she remembered. Thin, still. Pale. But standing upright. Breathing.

She brought her hand up to the window and froze.

Her reflection moved a fraction of a second after she did.

Bea lowered her hand slowly. Tried again. The reflection followed, perfectly synchronized this time. She must have imagined it. Trick of the light. She'd just woken up, after all.

Behind her, the creature hummed softly.

Bea turned to look at it. In the gray afternoon light, it seemed even more beautiful than before. Delicate. Perfect.

And so, so wrong.

"What did you do to me?" she asked.

The creature's head tilted. "We are unknitting. The sickness is being unwoven. You will be well."

"And then what?"

"Then you will be perfect." Its smile was gentle. "Do not fear, little chrysalis. We are helping. We are helping. The pattern will be so beautiful when it completes."

Bea wrapped her arms around herself. The warmth in her chest pulsed in time with the light in the bottle, and she realized she couldn't tell anymore which was her heartbeat and which was something else.

"What pattern?" she whispered.

But the creature just hummed that spiral melody and smiled its too-many-teethed smile and said nothing at all.

When Walt came back an hour later, arms full of bread and cheese and apples—things he must have spent the last of their money on—Bea was sitting at the table. She'd brushed her hair and put on a dress that hadn't fit properly in weeks.

"You're up," Walt said, and his smile could have lit the whole room. "You're really up. How do you feel?"

Bea looked at him. At her brother, who'd stolen a cursed thing from a collector's house because he couldn't let her go. Who'd traded something unknown for her life because desperation didn't leave room for caution.

She could tell him the truth. Could say that something felt deeply wrong, that her reflection moved strangely and her heartbeat had harmonics it shouldn't have and the warmth in her chest felt like it was changing her from the inside out.

But Walt looked so happy. So relieved.

And she was standing. Breathing without pain.

"Better," Bea said. "I feel better."

Walt set down his purchases and pulled her into a hug. Bea leaned into it, eyes on the bottle over his shoulder.

The creature watched them with eyes like shifting colors.

And hummed.

Bea wrapped her arms around herself. The warmth in her chest pulsed in time with the light in the bottle, and she realized she couldn't tell anymore which was her heartbeat and which was something else.

"What pattern?" she whispered.

"You're up," Walt said, and his smile could have lit the whole room. "You're up. How do you feel?"

Bea looked at him. At her brother, who'd stolen a cursed thing from a collector's house because he couldn't let her go. Who'd traded something unknown for her life because desperation didn't leave room for caution.

She could tell him the truth. Could say that something felt deeply wrong, that her reflection moved strangely and her heartbeat had harmonics it shouldn't have and the warmth in her chest felt like it was changing her from the inside out.

But Walt looked so happy. So relieved.

And she was standing. Breathing without pain.

"Better," Bea said. "I feel better."

Walt set down his purchases and pulled her into a hug. Bea leaned into it, eyes on the bottle over his shoulder.

The creature watched them with eyes like shifting colors.

And hummed.

# Chapter 5: Questions and Alien Answers

The library smelled of old paper and lamp oil.

# Chapter 6: Point of No Return

The full moon turned the attic room silver.

Bea stood at the window, face pressed to the glass, arms spread wide as if to embrace the light. She'd been there for hours, unmoving, barely breathing. "Am I?" she asked. Not cruelly. Just curious, as if the answer was genuinely uncertain.

"Yes."

Bea studied him for a long moment. Then something shifted in her expression—pain breaking through the alien serenity—and she was Bea again, really Bea, looking at him with eyes full of fear.

"Walt," she said, voice cracking. "I'm losing myself. I can feel it. There are moments when I don't remember who I am, when human things don't make sense anymore. I looked at my hands this morning and couldn't remember what they were for."

Walt crossed the room and took those hands in his. They were too warm, fever-hot, and when he looked closely he could see her skin had become too smooth, too perfect, like porcelain rather than flesh.

"We'll fix this," he said, knowing it was a lie.

"How?" Bea laughed, and the sound had harmonics that hurt to hear. "You read the books. You talked to the collector. There's no way to stop it."

"There's always a way."

"Is there?" She pulled her hands free. "Name one. Please. Because I'm running out of time to be afraid, and I want—I need to be afraid. Fear means I'm still human."

Walt looked at the bottle. The creature inside was pressed against the glass, watching them with bright eyes. It had been more active recently, moving constantly, humming louder. As if it could sense the pattern approaching completion.

"Iron," Walt said. "The books said iron disrupts Fair Folk magic."

"We're not magic," the creature said, voice like wind chimes. "We're pattern. Iron is just iron. It changes nothing."

"Then I'll try it anyway." Walt pulled a knife from his belt—iron blade, old and cheap. He set it on the table beside the bottle.

Nothing happened.

The creature looked at it with what might have been amusement. "You see? These are not our rules. Your protections mean nothing."

"Salt," Walt tried next, pulling a cloth pouch from his pocket. He poured a circle of salt around the bottle, the white grains bright in the moonlight. "Salt and iron. The old wards."

The creature just hummed.

Bea made a sound—half laugh, half sob. "It's not working."

"Rowan, then." Walt had brought branches of rowan wood, tied with red thread the way the books said. He placed them around the bottle, completing the traditional protective barrier.

The creature's glow brightened slightly. "Pretty," it said. "But useless. We are already inside the dying-thing. She breathes us now. Her blood carries our pattern. You cannot ward against what is already within."

Walt swept the rowan branches aside with one arm. Salt scattered across the table. The knife clattered to the floor.

"Then what?" He was shouting now, not caring who heard. "What the hell can I do?"

"Nothing," the creature said gently. "Accept. That is all. Let the pattern complete. It will be beautiful."

"I don't want beautiful!" Walt grabbed the bottle, fingers tight on the glass. "I want my sister back. I want Bea, not whatever you're making her into."

The bottle was warm-cold under his hands, temperature shifting in ways that made no sense. The creature pressed its too-many-fingered hands against his palms, separated only by glass.

"We are making her perfect," it said. "More than she was. Better than dying. Why do you grieve?"

"Because you're taking her away from me!"

"We are saving her. That is what you asked."

Walt's grip tightened. It would be so easy to throw the bottle. To smash it against the wall and end this, consequences be damned. Maybe the collector was wrong. Maybe breaking it would destroy the creature and stop the transformation.

Maybe it would just make everything worse.

"Don't," Bea said quietly.

Walt looked at her. She'd moved closer, moonlight painting her in shades of silver and gold. Her eyes were full of that alien serenity again, but underneath it he could see fear.

"The collector said breaking it would release everything at once," she continued. "Everything it's been doing slowly would happen immediately. To me. To you. To everyone nearby. We'd all change."

Walt felt something break in his chest. "Bea—"

"Bea?"

"The moonlight—I need—" She turned back to the window, pressing against the glass. When the moon emerged from the clouds, she sagged with relief. "I'm sorry. I can't help it. The moon feeds me now. Or the pattern does, through the moon. I don't know. I don't understand what I'm becoming."

"What happens when it completes?" he asked. Not to Bea. To the creature. "What happens when your pattern is finished?"

The creature's eyes brightened. "She will be like us. Part of the pattern. She will sing the songs that shape the world. She will see as we see, be as we are."

"Will she remember me?"

"Yessss. Memory is part of the pattern. But understanding will change. Love will change. She will love you as we love—vast and incomprehensible and terrible in its perfection."

"That's not love."

"It is the only love that lasts forever." The creature pressed against the glass. "We do not abandon. We do not forget. We carry all things in the pattern eternally. Is that not what you wanted? For her to live? To not fade? To be?"

"I wanted her to be *human*."

"Human is dying. Human is brief. Human is dust." The creature's voice was gentle, patient. "We offer eternity. We offer perfection. We offer—"

"I don't care what you offer!" Walt's hands balled into fists. "I want my sister back."

"She is here. She will always be here. But she will be more."

Bea made a sound—low and broken. She'd slid down the wall beneath the window, arms wrapped around her knees, face hidden. Moonlight pooled around her like water.

"I'm scared," she whispered. "I'm so scared, and I can feel the fear fading. Like it's being smoothed away. Soon I won't remember how to be afraid. I won't remember how to be sad. I'll just be... perfect."

Walt knelt beside her. "Tell me what to do. Please, Bea. Tell me what you need."

She looked up at him, tears on her face that caught the moonlight and glowed. "Hold me. While I still remember what that means."

He did. He wrapped his arms around her and held on while she cried, while the moon climbed higher, while the creature hummed its endless melody.

Bea's sobs gradually faded. Her breathing evened out—or stopped, Walt couldn't tell anymore. When she pulled back, her face was calm. Too calm. The fear was gone from her eyes, replaced by that alien serenity.

"It's happening," she said, and her voice was steady. "I can feel it. The transformation is accelerating. Soon I'll..." She paused, tilting her head in a gesture that mirrored the creature's. "Soon I'll complete."

"How long?"

"Hours, maybe. The next moonrise at most." She stood, movements fluid and strange. "You should leave, Walt. Go somewhere far from here before it finishes. You don't need to watch this."

"I'm not leaving you."

"You already have." She looked at him, and her eyes were full of something vast. "I'm already gone, brother. You're just watching the afterimage fade."

She turned back to the window. The moonlight embraced her, and Walt watched as her shadow stretched across the floor—too many angles, too many limbs, too much that wasn't Bea.

The creature hummed louder, and Bea's voice joined it.

Perfect harmony. Perfect horror.

Walt buried his face in his hands and tried not to hear the moment when Bea's voice stopped sounding human.

When he looked up again, his sister was standing in moonlight, wings spreading from her shoulders—translucent and veined with gold—while she sang in languages older than words.

The pattern was nearly complete.

And Walt could do nothing but watch.

The creature hummed.

Bea made a sound—half laugh, half sob. "It's not working."

"Rowan, then." Walt had brought branches of rowan wood, tied with red thread the way the books said. He placed them around the bottle, completing the traditional protective barrier.

The creature's glow brightened slightly. "Pretty," it said. "But useless. We are already inside the dying-thing. She breathes us now. Her blood carries our pattern. You cannot ward against what is already within."

Walt swept the rowan branches aside with one arm. Salt scattered across the table. The knife clattered to the floor.

"Then what?" He was shouting now, not caring who heard. "What the hell can I do?"

"Nothing," the creature said gently. "Accept. That is all. Let the pattern complete. It will be beautiful."

"I don't want beautiful!" Walt grabbed the bottle, fingers tight on the glass. "I want my sister back. I want Bea, not whatever you're making her into."

"We are making her perfect," it said. "More than she was. Better than dying. Why do you grieve?"

"Because you're taking her away from me!"

"We are saving her. That is what you asked."

Walt's grip tightened. It would be so easy to throw the bottle. To smash it against the wall and end this, consequences be damned. Maybe the collector was wrong. Maybe breaking it would destroy the creature and stop the transformation.

"Don't," Bea said quietly.

Walt looked at her. She'd moved closer, moonlight painting her in shades of silver and gold. Her eyes were full of that alien serenity again, but underneath it he could see fear.

"The collector said breaking it would release everything at once," she continued. "Everything it's been doing slowly would happen immediately. To me. To you. To everyone nearby. We'd all change."

Walt felt something break in his chest. "Bea—"

"I'm not going to die," she said. Her voice was soft, almost resigned. "That's what you wanted, right? For me to live? Well, I'm going to live forever. I'm going to be eternal and perfect and completely, utterly OTHER. And there's nothing either of us can do about it."

"I could—" Walt stopped, the words sticking in his throat.

Bea knew anyway. "Kill me?" She smiled, sad and knowing. "Could you? Really? Before the transformation completes?"

"If you asked me to."

"I did ask. Days ago. And you said no." She reached out and touched his face, fingers fever-warm. "You can't let me go, Walt. That's always been your problem. You hold on to things so tightly they break."

"I'm sorry."

"I know." She pulled her hand back. "I'm sorry too. I should have fought harder when you first used the bottle. Should have made you get rid of it, should have—" She stopped, shaking her head. "Bea?"

"The moonlight—I need—" She turned back to the window, pressing against the glass. When the moon emerged from the clouds, she sagged with relief. "I'm sorry. I can't help it. The moon feeds me now. Or the pattern does, through the moon. I don't know. I don't understand what I'm becoming."

"What happens when it completes?" he asked. Not to Bea. To the creature. "What happens when your pattern is finished?"

"Will she remember me?"

"Yessss. Memory is part of the pattern. But understanding will change. Love will change. She will love you as we love—vast and incomprehensible and terrible in its perfection."

"That's not love."

"It is the only love that lasts forever." The creature pressed against the glass. "We do not abandon. We do not forget. We carry all things in the pattern eternally. Is that not what you wanted? For her to live? To not fade? To be?"

"I wanted her to be *human*."

"Human is dying. Human is brief. Human is dust." The creature's voice was gentle, patient. "We offer eternity. We offer perfection. We offer—"

"I don't care what you offer!" Walt's hands balled into fists. "I want my sister back."

"She is here. She will always be here. But she will be more."

Bea made a sound—low and broken. She'd slid down the wall beneath the window, arms wrapped around her knees, face hidden. Moonlight pooled around her like water.

"I'm scared," she whispered. "I'm so scared, and I can feel the fear fading. Like it's being smoothed away. Soon I won't remember how to be afraid. I won't remember how to be sad. I'll be... perfect."

Walt knelt beside her. "Tell me what to do. Please, Bea. Tell me what you need."

She looked up at him, tears on her face that caught the moonlight and glowed. "Hold me. While I still remember what that means."

He did. He wrapped his arms around her and held on while she cried, while the moon climbed higher, while the creature hummed its endless melody.

Bea's sobs gradually faded. Her breathing evened out—or stopped, Walt couldn't tell anymore. When she pulled back, her face was calm. Too calm. The fear was gone from her eyes, replaced by that alien serenity.

"It's happening," she said, and her voice was steady. "I can feel it. The transformation is accelerating. Soon I'll..." She paused, tilting her head in a gesture that mirrored the creature's. "Soon I'll complete."

"How long?"

"Hours, maybe. The next moonrise at most." She stood, movements fluid and strange. "You should leave, Walt. Go somewhere far from here before it finishes. You don't need to watch this."

"I'm not leaving you."

"You already have." She looked at him, and her eyes were full of something vast. "I'm already gone, brother. You're watching the afterimage fade."

She turned back to the window. The moonlight embraced her, and Walt watched as her shadow stretched across the floor—too many angles, too many limbs, too much that wasn't Bea.

The creature hummed louder, and Bea's voice joined it.

Perfect harmony. Perfect horror.

Walt buried his face in his hands and tried not to hear the moment when Bea's voice stopped sounding human.

When he looked up again, his sister was standing in moonlight, wings spreading from her shoulders—translucent and veined with gold—while she sang in languages older than words.

The pattern was nearly complete.

And Walt could do nothing but watch.

# Chapter 9: The Pattern Complete

The first month was the hardest.

Walt tried to leave three times. Made it as far as the street before turning back. He told himself he was staying to watch over Bea, to keep her safe, to be there if some fragment of her humanity returned.

But really, he stayed because leaving would mean accepting that she was truly lost. And he wasn't ready to accept that.

Not yet.

Maybe never.

Bea stood at the window most hours, face turned to the sky. She didn't sleep. Didn't eat. Didn't need anything Walt could provide. The wings spread from her shoulders like permanent shadows, catching light in ways that made them impossible to look at directly.

She hummed constantly—that spiral melody the creature had sung, now joined by harmonies Walt couldn't quite hear. Sometimes he'd wake in the dark hours to find her standing over him, eyes glowing softly, just watching.

"Why are you looking at me?" he asked once.

"I am trying to remember what it meant to love you," she said. Her voice was gentle but empty. "I know I did once. I have the memory. But the feeling is... gone. I am trying to understand how human-me felt it."

"And?"

"I cannot." She turned back to the window. "It is like trying to remember how to be afraid. The concept exists, but the experience does not. I look at you and I see beauty in your pattern, perfection in your place in the design. But love?" Her head tilted. "That is a human thing. I am not human anymore."

Walt wanted to scream. To rage. To break something.

Instead, he just nodded and went back to sleep.

Or tried to.

Mrs. Hadley from the candle shop came up to check on them after the first week. She took one look at Bea—at the wings, the glowing eyes, the too-perfect face—and fled down the stairs, making sounds of terror.

After that, no one visited.

Walt heard the whispers in the street. Heard people cross themselves when they passed below the window. Heard mothers warning children away from the haunted attic where something beautiful and wrong lived.

He didn't correct them.

What would he say? "Don't worry, it's just my sister. She made a deal with a fairy and now she's eternally perfect and completely OTHER"?

They'd lock him up. Or worse, they'd lock Bea up. Put her in some curiosity show, charge admission to see the girl with wings who spoke in harmonics.

So Walt said nothing. Kept to himself. Bought supplies when necessary and avoided eye contact. Let the neighborhood think what it wanted.

Two months in, he came home to find Bea creating another pattern.

She'd arranged every object in the room—furniture, dishes, his spare clothes, the empty bottle, everything—into a massive spiral that filled the floor from wall to wall. She stood in the center of it, wings spread, humming.

"What are you doing?" Walt asked from the doorway.

"Practicing," Bea said without looking at him. "The creature showed me how to see patterns. Now I am learning to create them. Look—do you see the beauty in it?"

Walt looked. The arrangement made his eyes hurt, made his stomach turn. Objects he'd lived with for years suddenly seemed wrong, alien, part of something vast and terrible.

"No," he said.

"Pity." Bea turned to him, and her smile was the creature's smile—gentle and patient and completely inhuman. "The pattern is perfect. Every piece in its place. Every angle aligned. The mathematics of it are sublime."

"It's wrong."

"Wrong by human measure. But human measure is flawed." She gestured at the spiral. "This is *right*. This is how things should be arranged. If you could see it as I see it, you would weep with joy."

Walt carefully stepped over the pattern's edges and made his way to his chair. "Can you put everything back when you're done?"

"Done?" Bea laughed—a sound like breaking crystal. "I will never be done. The pattern is eternal. It grows and shifts and changes, but it never completes. That is its beauty."

Walt closed his eyes. "Please, Bea. Just put the furniture back so I can sleep."

Silence. Then, slowly: "You called me Bea."

"That's your name."

"Is it?" She moved closer, wings rustling. "I remember being Bea. Remember her thoughts, her fears, her loves. But I am not her anymore. I am... more. Other. Different." She knelt in front of Walt's chair, head tilting. "Perhaps you should call me something else. Something that reflects what I have become."

"No." Walt opened his eyes and looked at her. "You're Bea. You'll always be Bea. I don't care what you've become."

Something flickered in her expression—too fast to read, gone before Walt could name it. Then her face smoothed back into that terrible serenity.

"As you wish," she said. She stood and, with a gesture, the furniture rearranged itself. Not back to its original places—Bea didn't remember or care about those—but into functional positions. Chair by the window. Bed against the wall. Table in the center.

Close enough.

"Thank you," Walt said.

Bea didn't respond. She was already back at the window, face turned to the afternoon sun, humming.

Walt aged. Lines appeared at the corners of his eyes. Gray crept into his hair. His hands developed the tremor that came with too many years of picking locks in the cold.

Bea didn't age at all.

He brought her things sometimes. Offerings, almost. The honey cakes she used to love. Flowers from the market. Once, a book of poetry she'd read to him when they were younger.

She accepted them with that gentle smile and arranged them in her patterns. The cakes turned to dust. The flowers dried and crumbled. The book's pages became part of a spiral made from torn paper and moonlight.

"Is there?" She tilted her head. "From where I stand, existence is perfection. Life was brief and painful and full of fear. I do not miss it."

"I live alone," he lied. "In a small room above a candle shop."

He didn't see Anna again.

Perfect. Eternal. Lost.

"You say that often," she observed. "Why?"

"Because I did this to you. Because I couldn't let you go."

Walt thought about it. Really thought. The honest answer surprised him.

"I don't know," he admitted. "I'd want to choose differently. To let you die peacefully instead of this. But I don't know if I'd actually do it. Loving someone means you can't always make the right choice. Sometimes you just make the one you can live with."

"Can you live with this?"

Walt looked at her—at the stranger wearing his sister's face, the pattern wearing his sister's name, the eternity he'd bought with her humanity.

"I'm trying," he said.

Bea nodded slowly. Then she did something she hadn't done in years. She crossed the room and placed one hand on Walt's shoulder. Her touch was fever-hot, inhuman, but the gesture itself was almost familiar.

"Thank you," she said. Her voice still had no warmth, but there was something in it—an echo, maybe, of who she'd been. "For staying. For trying. For being the brother-one even when I am no longer the sister-one. That is..." She paused, searching for words. "...kind."

Then she went back to the window and resumed her humming, and Walt sat in his chair and wept silently while the moon rose and the patterns shifted and the world continued, indifferent and beautiful and exactly as terrible as it had always been.

Years later—how many, Walt had stopped counting—he would die.

Peacefully, in his sleep, old and worn and finally letting go.

And Bea would remain.

Standing at the window. Wings spread. Humming that endless melody.

Perfect and terrible and utterly, eternally alone.

The pattern the creature had promised was complete. Beautiful in its way. Tragic in every way that mattered.

In the end, the fairy had been right about one thing: Bea would never die.

She would never again be alive either.

But the fairy had never promised that.

"I wanted you to remember how you *felt* about them."

"Feelings are human attachments. I am not attached anymore. I simply *am*." She turned back to the window. "Does that distress you?"

"Yes."

"Why? You saved me. I will exist forever. I will never die, never age, never fade. Is that not what you wanted?"

Walt looked at her—beautiful and terrible and so far removed from the sister he'd save. "I wanted you to live, Bea. This isn't living. This is existing. There's a difference."

"Is there?" She tilted her head. "From where I stand, existence is perfection. Life was brief and painful and full of fear. I do not miss it."

"I miss you."

Bea said nothing. After a long moment, she went back to humming.

Eighteen months after the transformation, Walt met someone.

Her name was Anna. She ran a bookshop three streets over. She had kind eyes and careful hands and absolutely no idea what waited in Walt's attic room.

He didn't tell her. How could he?

"I live alone," he lied. "In a small room above a candle shop."

She didn't pry. They took walks together. Talked about books and weather and the small happinesses of ordinary life. Once, she kissed him in the rain, and Walt felt something in his chest that wasn't grief.

Hope, maybe. Or the memory of it.

He thought about leaving then. Thought about packing his few possessions and walking away from the attic room and the sister who wasn't his sister anymore. Thought about building a life with Anna, somewhere far from here, somewhere the patterns didn't reach.

But when he came home that night, Bea was standing in the doorway.

"You smell different," she said.

Walt froze. "What?"

"Different. Like someone else's soap. Someone else's warmth." She tilted her head. "You were with a human. A female human. You touched her."

"How do you know that?"

"I can see the threads. The connections between things. You have a new thread now, running from you to her. It glows." Bea stepped closer. "Are you leaving?"

"I don't know."

"You should." Her voice was empty of emotion. "There is nothing for you here. I do not need you. I will exist with or without your presence. You should seek your own pattern."

Walt looked at her. "Do you want me to go?"

"Want is a human concept. I do not want. I simply observe what is and what could be. You staying serves no purpose. Your grief changes nothing. Your love—" She paused. "—changes nothing."

"Then I'll stay."

"Why?"

Because leaving would be admitting defeat. Because some stubborn part of him still hoped she might come back. Because he'd destroyed her trying to save her, and walking away would make that destruction meaningless.

"Because you're my sister," Walt said.

Bea looked at him for a long moment. Then, quietly: "I was your sister. That girl is gone. I am what remains."

She turned and walked to the window, wings spreading to catch the moonlight.

Walt stood in the doorway and felt the last piece of hope die.

He didn't see Anna again.

Two years. Three. Five.

Walt's hair went fully gray. His hands shook more. The stairs became harder to climb. The city changed around him—new buildings, new people, new lives unfolding in the streets below.

Bea remained unchanged.

Perfect. Eternal. Lost.

He still brought her things sometimes. Not offerings anymore. Just... habits. Bread she wouldn't eat. Tea she wouldn't drink. Words she wouldn't hear.

"I'm sorry," he said one evening. The same words he'd said a thousand times, hoping they might matter.

Bea turned from the window. In the lamplight, her wings cast impossible shadows on the walls—too many angles, too much that shouldn't exist.

"You say that often," she observed. "Why?"

"Because I did this to you. Because I couldn't let you go."

"And if you could go back? If you could choose again?"

Walt thought about it. thought. The honest answer surprised him.

"I don't know," he admitted. "I'd want to choose differently. To let you die peacefully instead of this. But I don't know if I'd actually do it. Loving someone means you can't always make the right choice. Sometimes you make the one you can live with."

"Can you live with this?"

Walt looked at her—at the stranger wearing his sister's face, the pattern wearing his sister's name, the eternity he'd bought with her humanity.

"I'm trying," he said.

Bea nodded slowly. Then she did something she hadn't done in years. She crossed the room and placed one hand on Walt's shoulder. Her touch was fever-hot, inhuman, but the gesture itself was almost familiar.

"Thank you," she said. Her voice still had no warmth, but there was something in it—an echo, maybe, of who she'd been. "For staying. For trying. For being the brother-one even when I am no longer the sister-one. That is..." She paused, searching for words. "...kind."

Then she went back to the window and resumed her humming, and Walt sat in his chair and wept silently while the moon rose and the patterns shifted and the world continued, indifferent and beautiful and exactly as terrible as it had always been.

Years later—how many, Walt had stopped counting—he would die.

Peacefully, in his sleep, old and worn and finally letting go.

And Bea would remain.

Standing at the window. Wings spread. Humming that endless melody.

Perfect and terrible and utterly, eternally alone.

The pattern the creature had promised was complete. Beautiful in its way. Tragic in every way that mattered.

In the end, the fairy had been right about one thing: Bea would never die.

She would never again be alive either.

But the fairy had never promised that.
