# Chapter 5: Questions and Alien Answers

The library smelled of old paper and lamp oil.

Walt sat at a scarred wooden table in the back corner, surrounded by books he'd pulled from the shelves. Most were worthless for his purposes—treatises on agriculture, histories of minor noble families, religious texts. But he'd found three that mentioned the Fair Folk, and he read them with the desperate focus of a drowning man reaching for driftwood.

*They do not lie, but their truths will not serve you,* one text read. *They are bound by rules older than speech, and those rules are not for human understanding.*

Another: *The Aos Sí grant what you ask with precision and without mercy. Language is a cage they slip through with ease.*

The third was more direct: *Do not bargain with them. Do not accept their gifts. The price is always more than you know how to pay.*

Walt closed the last book and pressed his palms against his eyes. The words blurred together, a litany of warnings that all said the same thing: he'd made a terrible mistake.

Outside the library window, afternoon was sliding into evening. Bea would be alone with the bottle. He'd left her that morning curled on the bed, humming that spiral melody in her sleep, and he didn't want to think about what she might be doing now.

"Interesting reading?"

Walt's head snapped up. A man stood at the end of the table—the collector, dressed in dark wool and carrying a walking stick. His expression was unreadable.

"You," Walt said.

"Me." The collector sat down without invitation, setting his stick aside. "I wondered if I'd see you again. When the stories started circulating about a sick girl who'd miraculously recovered, I suspected." He gestured at the books. "Now I'm certain. You used it."

Walt's hand moved to his belt, where a knife wasn't. He'd left it at home. "If you're here to take it back—"

"I wouldn't touch that cursed thing again if you paid me." The collector's smile was thin and bitter. "No, I'm here because I feel some responsibility. I let you take it. The least I can do is help you understand what you're dealing with."

"Then tell me how to stop it. How to reverse what it's doing to my sister."

The collector was quiet for a long moment. When he spoke, his voice was gentle in a way that made Walt's stomach drop. "You can't."

"There has to be a way."

"Mr. Moore—is it Moore? Yes, I made inquiries." The collector leaned forward. "That thing in the bottle is not a spirit or a demon or anything our theology has names for. It's older than such concepts. It exists outside our rules, our morality, our understanding of how the world works."

"The books said it's bound by rules."

"Its own rules. Not ours." The collector pulled one of the books toward him, flipping to a marked page. "The Fair Folk—if that's even an adequate name for what it is—operate according to patterns we can't perceive. They see the world differently than we do. Time, space, life, death—these are just... components to them. Elements in a pattern."

"Bea isn't a component," Walt said through clenched teeth. "She's a person."

"To you. To it?" The collector shook his head. "She's the right shape. The right color in its grand design. It doesn't hate her or love her. Those are human concepts. It simply needs her to complete what it's started."

Walt wanted to overturn the table, to shout, to break something. Instead, he forced himself to stay seated, to breathe. "You trapped it once. Bottled it. There must be a way to stop it."

"I didn't trap it." The collector's face went distant. "My mentor did, forty years ago. It took him three years and most of his sanity. He studied every text on the Fair Folk he could find, learned dead languages to read protective wards, sacrificed things I don't like to think about. And in the end, all he managed was containment, not banishment. The bottle slows it down but doesn't stop it."

"Then breaking the bottle—"

"Would be the worst thing you could do." The collector's voice sharpened. "Everything it's done slowly over the past week would happen at once. Not to your sister. To everything in the pattern's range. This room, this building, maybe more. Do you understand? Everything would transform."

Walt's throat went dry. "How do you know?"

"Because my mentor tried. Before he died." The collector's eyes were haunted. "He broke an earlier bottle, thinking it would destroy the creature. It didn't. It just... released everything at once. The estate where he worked, everyone in it—they all changed. Became like it. Or part of it. I don't know. I was young, I ran." He looked at his hands. "I can still hear them singing sometimes."

The silence that followed was broken only by the sound of someone shelving books in another part of the library.

"What does it want?" Walt asked finally. "It keeps talking about a pattern. What pattern?"

"I don't know. My mentor spent years trying to understand, and he never figured it out." The collector straightened, pulling his stick close. "But I can tell you what I observed in the months I kept it. It collects things—small objects, moments, essences. It arranges them according to some aesthetic principle we can't grasp. Your sister fits into that arrangement somehow."

"And when the pattern completes?"

The collector stood. "My mentor believed she would become like it. Not dead, not alive. Other. Part of the pattern itself." He met Walt's eyes. "I'm sorry. Truly. But once the pattern begins, it must complete. Fighting it only makes things worse."

"There has to be something—"

"There isn't." The collector picked up his stick. "The kindest thing you can do now is make peace with what's coming. Let the transformation finish quickly than drawing it out. And for God's sake, don't break the bottle."

He left, footsteps echoing in the library's quiet.

Walt sat alone among the books, staring at nothing.

When he finally returned home, the sun had set. The attic room was dark except for the glow from the bottle and a single lamp burning low.

Bea sat at the table, creating another of her disturbing patterns. This one was made from household objects and bits of things he didn't recognize—flowers that hadn't been there this morning, small stones arranged in spirals, something that might have been shed snakeskin.

"Where did you get these?" Walt asked, gesturing at the flowers.

Bea looked up, eyes distant. "I don't remember. They were here." She touched one of the petals. It was black, shot through with veins of silver. "Aren't they beautiful?"

"No."

She flinched. Then, slowly, she swept the arrangement aside, objects clattering to the floor. "You're right. I don't know why I keep doing this."

But Walt could see her fingers twitching, wanting to arrange, to create, to complete the patterns that sang in her head.

He crossed to the bottle. The creature was still, watching him with those too-bright eyes.

"I need answers," Walt said.

The creature tilted its head. "Ask."

"Are you hurting her?"

"No. We are completing her. Hurt is not part of the pattern."

"Will she be herself when you're done?"

"She becomes more-than-herself. She shall be perfect."

"That's not what I asked." Walt's hands balled into fists. "Will she still be Bea? Will she still know me, still remember who she is?"

The creature considered this, head tilting back and forth. "She will remember. Memory is part of the pattern. But understanding of memory will change. She will know the brother-one but through different eyes. See you as we see you—as a shape in the pattern, a note in the song."

"So no. She won't be herself."

"She grows more. Better. Perfect." The creature pressed against the glass. "Why do you grieve? We are saving her. Death will not touch her. Time will not diminish her. She will be eternal."

"She'll be like you."

"Yessss." The word was full of harmonics. "Like us. Part of us. Part of the pattern. She will sing, and the world will listen."

Walt turned away, bile rising in his throat. Behind him, he heard Bea stand, felt her hand on his shoulder.

"I heard," she said quietly. Her voice was shaking. "Walt, I don't want to be perfect. I don't want to be eternal. I wanted to not die."

"I know."

"Can you... can you end it? Before it finishes?"

Walt turned to her. Bea's face was pale but composed, the same expression she'd worn when telling him she'd made peace with dying. "What are you asking?"

"I'm asking if you can kill me before I stop being me."

The words hit like a physical blow. "Bea—"

"Because I think I'd die than become whatever that thing is trying to make me. And I think..." Her voice cracked. "I think I'm running out of time to choose."

Walt pulled her into his arms, holding tight. She felt wrong—too warm, too still, her heartbeat offbeat in a way that made his chest hurt.

"I can't," he whispered into her hair. "Don't ask me to do that."

"Then what do we do?"

Walt had no answer. They stood together in the lamplight while the creature hummed its spiral melody and the pattern continued its inexorable completion.

Later, after Bea had fallen into whatever passed for sleep these days, Walt sat in his chair and stared at the bottle.

"Can I stop this?" he asked. "Is there any way?"

The creature looked at him with something that might have been pity if it understood the concept. "You can choose to stop asking us to help. But the pattern is already in motion. Stopping would leave her trapped between. Neither dying nor living nor complete. Forever incomplete. We would not advise."

"What would you advise?"

"Let the pattern complete. It will be beautiful. She becomes perfect. You will understand when it finishes."

"I don't want to understand."

"But you will." The creature's smile was gentle. "The pattern touches all things. You asked us to help. We helped. Now the pattern includes you both. You can fight or you can accept, but the ending is the same."

Walt closed his eyes. "How much time does she have?"

"Time is a human measure. The pattern has its own rhythm." The creature paused. "But in your counting... days. Perhaps a week. Perhaps less. The transformation accelerates as completion approaches."

"And I can't stop it."

"No."

"Can't slow it."

"No."

"Can't save her."

The creature was quiet for a long moment. Then, softly: "We are saving her. That is what you asked. That is what we are doing. If the result is not what you wanted, that is the failure of your asking, not our answering."

Walt opened his eyes and looked at the thing in the bottle—beautiful and terrible and utterly, incomprehensibly alien.

"I hate you," he said.

"Hate is a human concept," the creature replied. "We do not understand it. But we understand patterns. And yours is made of love that destroys. That is beautiful too, in its way."

Walt turned his back on it and watched Bea sleep, her chest barely moving, her fingers twitching in dreams full of spirals and songs and things she was forgetting how to be afraid of.

Outside, the moon rose, and Bea's eyes opened—glowing faintly in the dark, reflecting light that had no source.

She sat up, walked to the window, and pressed her face against the glass.

"It's singing again," she whispered. "Can you hear it, Walt? The moon is singing, and I know all the words."

Walt said nothing.

There was nothing left to say.
