# Chapter 2: The Collector's House

The house stood three stories tall on a street where the cobblestones actually fit together properly.

Walt approached from the south, keeping to the shadows between the gas lamps. It was well past midnight, and the rain had finally stopped, leaving everything slick and clean-smelling. The collector's home was exactly the type he'd expected—old money, old stone, the kind of place that had probably housed the same family for generations until someone died and their things got sold off piece by piece.

He'd spent the day watching it. Learning the rhythms of the household. The cook left at six. The housekeeper at seven. The collector himself had gone out around nine and returned an hour later, alone. By eleven, all the lights were out except for one on the third floor, which went dark around midnight.

Now Walt stood in the narrow alley that ran alongside the house, looking up at a window on the second floor. It was latched, but that wouldn't matter. Latches never did.

He'd brought the tools of his trade—a thin knife, a length of rope, a small dark lantern. The rope went around a drainpipe, tested for weight. It held. Walt climbed quickly, boots finding purchase on the stone, hands sure despite the damp.

The window gave without much protest. He slid it up, slipped through, and closed it behind him.

Inside was dark and still. Walt waited, counting his breaths, letting his eyes adjust. Gradually, shapes emerged from the blackness—furniture, doorways, the bulk of something large and draped in cloth.

He was in a sitting room of some kind. The air smelled of dust and old wood and something else, something faintly sweet and wrong that he couldn't quite place. Walt opened his lantern just enough to let out a sliver of light and moved deeper into the house.

The collector had mentioned his acquisitions. They'd be somewhere he could show them off, somewhere with space and security. Walt found it on the second floor, at the end of a long hallway—a room with a heavy door that wasn't locked. Rich people never thought they needed to lock doors inside their own homes.

The cabinet of curiosities was exactly what the name suggested.

Glass-fronted cases lined the walls, filled with objects arranged for display. Taxidermy birds with jeweled eyes. Crystals that caught the light from Walt's lantern and threw it back in sharp rainbows. A human skull covered in intricate carvings. Mechanical insects made of brass and copper, frozen mid-step. Books bound in leather that looked uncomfortably like skin.

Walt moved past them all, looking for the bottle.

He found it on a central table, elevated on a small wooden stand.

The bottle itself was beautiful. Heavy glass with a greenish tint, covered in symbols etched so deeply they caught the light. The cork was sealed with wax and wrapped in silver wire, as if someone had been very serious about keeping it closed.

But it was what was inside that made Walt stop and stare.

Light. Soft and golden-green, moving like it had a source. Like something alive was producing it.

Walt set his lantern down and leaned closer. The light pulsed gently, almost like breathing. As he watched, it brightened for a moment, and he saw—

A shape. Small. Delicate.

It moved.

Walt's breath caught. The collector hadn't been exaggerating. There was something in there. Something that turned in the thick liquid (was it liquid? it didn't move quite like water) and pressed what might have been a hand against the inside of the glass.

For just a moment, Walt could have sworn it looked at him.

He reached out slowly, fingers brushing the bottle's surface. It was cold. No—warm. No—both, in a way that didn't make sense. The glass felt smooth and wrong against his palm, like touching something from a dream.

This was what the collector had talked about. The thing with the etchings and the protective wards and the strange contents that no one kept for long.

Bad luck, the collector had said. Or bad nerves.

Walt picked it up.

The bottle was heavier than it looked. The weight seemed to shift in his hand, as if whatever was inside was moving to compensate for the change in position. Walt held it up to his lantern light and looked closer.

The symbols covered every inch of the glass—spirals and angles and shapes that looked almost like writing but in no alphabet Walt recognized. Protective wards, the collector had guessed. Warnings, maybe. Or instructions.

Whatever they said, someone had been very careful about it.

Walt's original plan had been simple: take the bottle, sell it to one of those interested buyers the collector had mentioned, use the money for Bea. But looking at it now, holding it in his hands while the thing inside pulsed with gentle light...

He should leave it. Every instinct he'd honed over years of thieving told him to put it back and find something else. This was the kind of object that came with complications.

But Bea was dying. And this was valuable.

Walt wrapped the bottle in a cloth from his pocket and tucked it carefully into his coat. The weight of it settled against his ribs, wrong-temperature and heavy.

He was halfway back to the window when he heard footsteps in the hallway.

Walt froze. The lantern was still open on the table, its light a tiny beacon in the dark. He moved quickly, closing it, plunging the room back into blackness. The footsteps came closer.

The door opened.

Lamplight spilled into the room, and with it came the collector—the same man from the tavern, now dressed in a nightshirt and robe, holding a lamp in one hand and a fireplace poker in the other.

Their eyes met.

The collector's gaze dropped to Walt's coat, where the wrapped bottle made a tell-tale bulge. Understanding crossed his face. And then—

Relief.

It was quick, gone almost as soon as it appeared, replaced by appropriate shock and alarm. But Walt had seen it. The flash of something that looked almost like gratitude.

"So," the collector said. His voice was steady. "You've taken it."

Walt said nothing. His hand moved to the knife in his belt.

The collector noticed, but he didn't raise the poker. Didn't call for help. Didn't do any of the things a wealthy man should do when confronted with a thief in his home.

Instead, he smiled thinly. "Good."

Walt's fingers tightened on the knife hilt. "You're not going to stop me?"

"No." The collector set his lamp down on a nearby table, movements careful and deliberate. "In fact, I'm going to let you leave. Through the front door, if you'd like. Less risk of falling."

"Why?"

The collector looked at him for a long moment. In the lamplight, his face was lined and tired, older than he'd seemed in the tavern. "Because I've been trying to rid myself of that cursed thing for six months, and no one will buy it. Oh, they inquire. They come to look. But the moment they touch it, they change their minds." He glanced at the spot where the bottle had stood. "You're the first person who's actually taken it."

Walt didn't move. This had to be a trick. Some way of getting him to lower his guard so the collector could call for the constables.

But the man just stood there, looking almost... sad.

"You'll sell it," the collector said. It wasn't a question. "Or use it. I don't know which, and I don't want to know. But let me give you one piece of advice." He met Walt's eyes. "It will give you exactly what you ask for. No more, no less. And that will be the problem."

"I don't understand."

"You will." The collector stepped aside, clearing a path to the door. "Now go. Please. Before I change my mind."

Walt moved past him carefully, watching for any sign of attack. None came. The collector just stood there in his nightshirt, looking at the empty space where the bottle had been, expression unreadable.

At the door, Walt paused. "The protective wards on the glass. What do they say?"

"I don't know. I never translated them all." The collector's smile was bitter. "But the ones I did manage said 'Do not open.' In seven different dead languages."

Walt's hand moved unconsciously to his coat, feeling the weight of the bottle. "And you kept it anyway?"

"I'm a collector. We can't help ourselves." He looked at Walt. "You should leave. Now. While you still can."

Walt didn't need to be told twice.

He left through the front door, as suggested, and walked quickly through the empty streets. The bottle seemed to pulse against his ribs in time with his heartbeat. Or maybe that was his imagination.

He didn't look back.

By the time Walt reached the familiar streets of his own neighborhood, the sky was beginning to lighten in the east. The candle-maker's shop was dark and quiet. He climbed the stairs as softly as he could and let himself into the attic room.

Bea was asleep, curled on her side, one hand tucked under her pillow. Her breathing sounded terrible—wet and rattling—but at least she was breathing.

Walt set the bottle down on the small table, unwrapping it carefully. In the pre-dawn dimness, it still glowed, soft and steady.

The thing inside moved. Turned. Pressed against the glass as if looking out.

At him.

At Bea.

Walt sat down in his chair and stared at it. The collector's words echoed in his mind: *It will give you exactly what you ask for. No more, no less. And that will be the problem.*

He should sell it. That was the plan. Take it to one of those interested buyers, get the money, pay for a real physician.

But looking at it now, at the way the light moved and the shape inside shifted—at the way it seemed to be *aware*—a different thought crept into his mind.

What if it could help directly?

The collector had called it cursed. Had said people wouldn't buy it once they touched it. Had been *relieved* to have it stolen.

But the collector also said it would give you exactly what you asked for.

Walt reached out and touched the glass. Warm-and-cold. Wrong and right.

Beside him, Bea coughed in her sleep, a wet, tearing sound that didn't stop for nearly a minute. When it finally did, there were flecks of blood on her lips.

Walt looked at the bottle. At the light. At the thing inside that might have been looking back at him.

Tomorrow, he thought. Tomorrow he'd decide what to do with it.

But tomorrow was already here, creeping in through the window with the first gray light of dawn.

And Bea was still dying.

Walt pulled his chair closer to her bed and sat watch, the bottle glowing softly on the table behind him, while the city woke up outside and the world stayed stubbornly, inevitably the same.
