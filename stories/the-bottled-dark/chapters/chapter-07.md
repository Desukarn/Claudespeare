# Chapter 7: What Remains

Three days after the wings appeared, Bea stopped pretending to eat.

She sat at the table across from Walt, a bowl of soup growing cold in front of her, and watched him with eyes that shifted between a dozen colors. The wings folded against her back, barely visible unless the light caught them right—then they shimmered like oil on water, beautiful and wrong.

"You should eat something," Walt said, knowing it was pointless.

Bea looked down at the soup as if seeing it for the first time. "Why?"

"Because that's what people do."

"I'm not people anymore." She said it without inflection, a simple statement of fact. "I sustain on moonlight now. Food is just... matter. It means nothing."

Walt set down his spoon, appetite gone. "You used to love mushroom soup."

"Did I?" Bea tilted her head. "I remember that I loved it. I remember the taste, the warmth. But the feeling attached to the memory is... distant. Like it belonged to someone else." She reached out and touched the bowl's rim. "Tell me about her. The girl who loved soup."

"That's you. That's Bea."

"Is it?" Her smile was gentle and terrible. "I wear her face. I carry her memories. But I don't feel what she felt. When I look at you, I see my brother. I know I love you. But that love is..." She paused, searching for words. "Vast. Incomprehensible. Not warm. Not soft. Just... eternal."

Walt's throat tightened. "Do you want me to go?"

"No. Yes. I don't know." Bea stood, moving to the window. The midday sun made her wings visible, throwing rainbow shadows across the floor. "I want you to stay because the part of me that was human wants you near. But I also want you to go because the part of me that's becoming something else knows your presence hurts you, and hurting is a human thing I'm forgetting how to understand."

She turned to face him, and Walt saw it clearly—the wrongness. Her movements were too fluid. Her eyes blinked at the wrong interval. When she breathed, it was habit, not necessity. The wings shifted slightly, responding to a wind that didn't exist.

"The transformation is nearly complete," Bea said. Her voice had harmonics now, layers of sound that made Walt's teeth ache. "I can feel it. Soon there won't be any human left at all. Just... the pattern. what it made me."

"The creature said you'd be perfect."

"Perfect." Bea laughed, and the sound was like breaking glass. "Yes. Perfect and eternal and utterly, completely lost." She crossed the room and knelt in front of Walt's chair, taking his hands. Her touch was fever-hot. "Listen to me. Please. While I still remember how to say this clearly: you need to let me go."

"Bea—"

"I'm not your sister anymore. Not really. And soon I won't even be this close to her. I'll be something wearing her skin, singing songs you can't hear, seeing beauty in things that should horrify you." Her grip tightened. "And I'll be bound here somehow. Tied to this place by the pattern. Unable to die, unable to leave, unable to be anything but a reminder of your worst mistake."

Walt pulled his hands free and stood, needing distance. "I won't abandon you."

"You already have. You abandoned me the moment you stole that bottle and made a wish to something you didn't understand." Bea's voice wasn't angry. sad. "Now you're abandoning yourself, staying here, watching this. Go, Walt. Find a new life somewhere far from here. Let me be... whatever I'm becoming... alone."

"I can't."

"Because you love me."

"Yes."

"Then prove it." Bea stood, and the wings spread wide, filling the small room with impossible color. "Let me go. Walk out that door and don't come back. That's the kindness I need now. Not your presence. Your absence."

Walt looked at her—this beautiful, terrible stranger—and knew she was right. Knew that staying would only prolong both their suffering.

But he also knew he wouldn't leave.

"No," he said.

Bea's expression didn't change. She nodded slowly, as if she'd expected this. "Then we'll both be trapped here. You by love. Me by the pattern. Forever."

She moved to the corner of the room and sank down, wings folding against her back. For a long moment, she was silent. Then, so quietly Walt almost didn't hear: "I wish you'd let me die."

That night, Walt stared at the bottle and thought about breaking it.

The collector had warned against it. Had said it would release everything at once, transform everything nearby. But Walt was past caring about consequences. Bea was already lost. What did it matter if he followed?

His hand closed around the bottle. Warm-cold glass, familiar now. The creature inside was still, watching him.

"You know what I'm thinking," Walt said.

"Yessss." The creature's voice was soft. "You think to break the bottle. To end the pattern. To destroy us and the dying-one both."

"She's not dying anymore. You made sure of that."

"We saved her. That is what you asked."

"I asked for you to cure her. Not turn her into... that."

"Curing means unmaking the sickness. We unmade it. We made her more. Better. Eternal." The creature pressed against the glass. "If you break the bottle, she will still complete. But faster. More painful. And you will complete too. Everything in the pattern's reach will complete all at once. Is that what you want?"

Walt's grip tightened. "What I want is to undo every choice that brought me here."

"Cannot undo. Can only continue forward. Pattern moves one direction only."

"Then I'll break it. End the pattern. Maybe that's the kindness she needs."

"Breaking is not ending," the creature said. "Breaking is releasing. We would be free. The pattern would complete immediately instead of gently. The dying-one would become fully us in an instant. You would become part of the pattern. The building, the street, perhaps more—all would transform. All would sing our song. Is that mercy?"

Walt set the bottle down with shaking hands. "What about opening it? Instead of breaking?"

The creature went still. Then, slowly: "Opening is permitted. Opening is the proper way. If you open, we can emerge. The pattern will complete as intended. The dying-one will transform fully but gently. And we will be free to continue our work."

"What work?"

"The pattern is eternal. It grows. We will find other pieces, other shapes that fit. We will make more perfect things." The creature's smile was gentle. "This is what we do. What we have always done. What we will always do."

"And Bea?"

"She will be the first piece of the next pattern. Beautiful. Perfect. Eternal." The creature tilted its head. "Would you like to be the second?"

Walt stepped back, bile rising. "No."

"Then do not open the bottle. Leave it sealed. The pattern will complete anyway, but slower. The dying-one will finish her transformation over days instead of moments. You will have time to watch. Time to grieve. Time to accept."

"Or I could throw it in the river. Lock it in a chest. Bury it."

"We are already inside the dying-one," the creature said patiently. "Distance changes nothing. Burial changes nothing. The pattern will complete regardless. We are patient. We are eternal. We will finish what we start."

Walt looked at Bea, curled in the corner, wings shimmering in the lamplight. She was humming that endless melody, fingers tracing patterns in the air—spirals and angles that left glowing trails.

Three options. All of them terrible.

Break the bottle: catastrophic transformation, everything nearby changed at once.

Open the bottle: controlled completion, the creature freed, Bea fully transformed but gently.

Leave it sealed: slow torture, watching Bea fade over days while the pattern completed anyway.

"What would you do?" Walt asked. "If you were human, if someone you loved was becoming what you are, what would you choose?"

The creature considered this. "We do not love as humans do. But if we carried affection for a thing, we would complete the pattern quickly. Suffering is a human addition. We do not intend it, but we recognize it occurs. Quick completion is kinder than slow."

"You're saying I should open the bottle."

"We are saying the pattern will complete regardless. You can choose the speed, not the destination."

Walt closed his eyes. Thought of Bea as she was—laughing over stolen honey cake, gentle hands mending shirts, quiet strength through months of dying. Thought of Bea as she'd become—strange and beautiful and utterly lost.

Thought of Bea curled in moonlight, crying that she was scared while the fear faded from her eyes.

He opened his eyes and looked at the bottle.

"If I open it," he said slowly. "The transformation finishes. Bea becomes... like you. Fully. Completely. And you're free."

"Yessss."

"And there's no way to reverse it after. No way to bring her back."

"There is no bringing back. She is already changed. Opening only completes what is begun."

Walt's hand moved to the bottle. Stopped. "I need to ask her first."

"The dying-one will say yes. She is already mostly us. She wants completion now. The waiting hurts her human-parts."

"I need to ask her anyway."

Walt crossed to where Bea sat. She looked up at him, eyes full of shifting colors.

"I heard," she said before he could speak. "I've been hearing everything lately. The creature's voice, your thoughts, the conversations of people three streets over. Sound travels differently now."

"I can open it. End this quickly. Or leave it, and it happens slowly over days."

"What about breaking it?"

"That hurts everyone. Transforms everyone nearby."

Bea nodded slowly. She stood, wings spreading slightly for balance, and walked to the table. She placed one hand on the bottle, and the creature pressed its palm against hers through the glass.

"I want it to end," she said quietly. "The waiting is worse than the transformation. Every hour I lose more of myself. Soon there won't be anything left that remembers being afraid. I want..." Her voice broke. "I want to stop being aware of what I'm losing. Does that make sense?"

"Yes."

"Then open it." She looked at him, and for a moment she was Bea again—really, truly Bea—looking at her brother with fear and love and resignation. "Open it, Walt. Let it finish. I can't stand being trapped between anymore."

Walt's hand closed around the cork.

"Are you sure?"

Bea smiled. Sad and gentle and already half-OTHER. "I'm sure. Do it quick. Like pulling off a bandage."

Walt pulled the cork.
