# Chapter 9: The Pattern Complete

The first month was the hardest.

Walt leave three times. Made it as far as the street before turning back. He told himself he was staying to watch over Bea, to keep her safe, to be there if some fragment of her humanity returned.

But really, he stayed because leaving would mean accepting that she was truly lost. And he wasn't ready to accept that.

Not yet.

Maybe never.

Bea stood at the window most hours, face turned to the sky. She didn't sleep. Didn't eat. Didn't need anything Walt could provide. The wings spread from her shoulders like permanent shadows, catching light in ways that made them impossible to look at directly.

She hummed constantly—that spiral melody the creature had sung, now joined by harmonies Walt couldn't hear. Sometimes he'd wake in the dark hours to find her standing over him, eyes glowing softly, watching.

"Why are you looking at me?" he asked once.

"I am trying to remember what it meant to love you," she said. Her voice was gentle but empty. "I know I did once. I have the memory. But the feeling is... gone. I am trying to understand how human-me felt it."

"And?"

"I cannot." She turned back to the window. "It is like trying to remember how to be afraid. The concept exists, but the experience does not. I look at you and I see beauty in your pattern, perfection in your place in the design. But love?" Her head tilted. "That is a human thing. I am not human anymore."

Walt wanted to scream. To rage. To break something.

Instead, he nodded and went back to sleep.

Or tried to.

Mrs. Hadley from the candle shop came up to check on them after the first week. She took one look at Bea—at the wings, the glowing eyes, the too-perfect face—and fled down the stairs, making sounds of terror.

After that, no one visited.

Walt heard the whispers in the street. Heard people cross themselves when they passed below the window. Heard mothers warning children away from the haunted attic where something beautiful and wrong lived.

He didn't correct them.

What would he say? "Don't worry, it's my sister. She made a deal with a fairy and now she's eternally perfect and completely OTHER"?

They'd lock him up. Or worse, they'd lock Bea up. Put her in some curiosity show, charge admission to see the girl with wings who spoke in harmonics.

So Walt said nothing. Kept to himself. Bought supplies when necessary and avoided eye contact. Let the neighborhood think what it wanted.

Two months in, he came home to find Bea creating another pattern.

She'd arranged every object in the room—furniture, dishes, his spare clothes, the empty bottle, everything—into a massive spiral that filled the floor from wall to wall. She stood in the center of it, wings spread, humming.

"What are you doing?" Walt asked from the doorway.

"Practicing," Bea said without looking at him. "The creature showed me how to see patterns. Now I am learning to create them. Look—do you see the beauty in it?"

Walt looked. The arrangement made his eyes hurt, made his stomach turn. Objects he'd lived with for years suddenly seemed wrong, alien, part of something vast and terrible.

"No," he said.

"Pity." Bea turned to him, and her smile was the creature's smile—gentle and patient and completely inhuman. "The pattern is perfect. Every piece in its place. Every angle aligned. The mathematics of it are sublime."

"It's wrong."

"Wrong by human measure. But human measure is flawed." She gestured at the spiral. "This is *right*. This is how things should be arranged. If you could see it as I see it, you would weep with joy."

Walt carefully stepped over the pattern's edges and made his way to his chair. "Can you put everything back when you're done?"

"Done?" Bea laughed—a sound like breaking crystal. "I will never be done. The pattern is eternal. It grows and shifts and changes, but it never completes. That is its beauty."

Walt closed his eyes. "Please, Bea. put the furniture back so I can sleep."

Silence. Then, slowly: "You called me Bea."

"That's your name."

"Is it?" She moved closer, wings rustling. "I remember being Bea. Remember her thoughts, her fears, her loves. But I am not her anymore. I am... more. Other. Different." She knelt in front of Walt's chair, head tilting. "Perhaps you should call me something else. Something that reflects what I have become."

"No." Walt opened his eyes and looked at her. "You're Bea. You'll always be Bea. I don't care what you've become."

Something flickered in her expression—too fast to read, gone before Walt could name it. Then her face smoothed back into that terrible serenity.

"As you wish," she said. She stood and, with a gesture, the furniture rearranged itself. Not back to its original places—Bea didn't remember or care about those—but into functional positions. Chair by the window. Bed against the wall. Table in the center.

Close enough.

"Thank you," Walt said.

Bea didn't respond. She was already back at the window, face turned to the afternoon sun, humming.

Three months. Six months. A year.

Time passed, and nothing changed.

Bea remained exactly as she'd been the day the transformation completed. Eternally perfect. Eternally wrong. Eternally *there* but unreachable.

Walt aged. Lines appeared at the corners of his eyes. Gray crept into his hair. His hands developed the tremor that came with too many years of picking locks in the cold.

Bea didn't age at all.

He brought her things sometimes. Offerings, almost. The honey cakes she used to love. Flowers from the market. Once, a book of poetry she'd read to him when they were younger.

She accepted them with that gentle smile and arranged them in her patterns. The cakes turned to dust. The flowers dried and crumbled. The book's pages became part of a spiral made from torn paper and moonlight.

Nothing touched her. Nothing reached her. Nothing mattered except the patterns.

"Do you remember our parents?" Walt asked one day. He'd been thinking about them—dead for years now, buried in a pauper's grave he'd never been able to afford markers for.

Bea paused in her humming. "I remember the facts of them. Mother who sickened. Father who drank. Both dead before I was twelve. You raised me after." She looked at him. "Is that what you wanted?"

"I wanted you to remember how you *felt* about them."

"Feelings are human attachments. I am not attached anymore. I simply *am*." She turned back to the window. "Does that distress you?"

"Yes."

"Why? You saved me. I will exist forever. I will never die, never age, never fade. Is that not what you wanted?"

Walt looked at her—beautiful and terrible and so far removed from the sister he'd save. "I wanted you to live, Bea. This isn't living. This is existing. There's a difference."

"Is there?" She tilted her head. "From where I stand, existence is perfection. Life was brief and painful and full of fear. I do not miss it."

"I miss you."

Bea said nothing. After a long moment, she went back to humming.

Eighteen months after the transformation, Walt met someone.

Her name was Anna. She ran a bookshop three streets over. She had kind eyes and careful hands and absolutely no idea what waited in Walt's attic room.

He didn't tell her. How could he?

"I live alone," he lied. "In a small room above a candle shop."

She didn't pry. They took walks together. Talked about books and weather and the small happinesses of ordinary life. Once, she kissed him in the rain, and Walt felt something in his chest that wasn't grief.

Hope, maybe. Or the memory of it.

He thought about leaving then. Thought about packing his few possessions and walking away from the attic room and the sister who wasn't his sister anymore. Thought about building a life with Anna, somewhere far from here, somewhere the patterns didn't reach.

But when he came home that night, Bea was standing in the doorway.

"You smell different," she said.

Walt froze. "What?"

"Different. Like someone else's soap. Someone else's warmth." She tilted her head. "You were with a human. A female human. You touched her."

"How do you know that?"

"I can see the threads. The connections between things. You have a new thread now, running from you to her. It glows." Bea stepped closer. "Are you leaving?"

"I don't know."

"You should." Her voice was empty of emotion. "There is nothing for you here. I do not need you. I will exist with or without your presence. You should seek your own pattern."

Walt looked at her. "Do you want me to go?"

"Want is a human concept. I do not want. I simply observe what is and what could be. You staying serves no purpose. Your grief changes nothing. Your love—" She paused. "—changes nothing."

"Then I'll stay."

"Why?"

Because leaving would be admitting defeat. Because some stubborn part of him still hoped she might come back. Because he'd destroyed her trying to save her, and walking away would make that destruction meaningless.

"Because you're my sister," Walt said.

Bea looked at him for a long moment. Then, quietly: "I was your sister. That girl is gone. I am what remains."

She turned and walked to the window, wings spreading to catch the moonlight.

Walt stood in the doorway and felt the last piece of hope die.

He didn't see Anna again.

Two years. Three. Five.

Walt's hair went fully gray. His hands shook more. The stairs became harder to climb. The city changed around him—new buildings, new people, new lives unfolding in the streets below.

Bea remained unchanged.

Perfect. Eternal. Lost.

He still brought her things sometimes. Not offerings anymore. Just... habits. Bread she wouldn't eat. Tea she wouldn't drink. Words she wouldn't hear.

"I'm sorry," he said one evening. The same words he'd said a thousand times, hoping they might matter.

Bea turned from the window. In the lamplight, her wings cast impossible shadows on the walls—too many angles, too much that shouldn't exist.

"You say that often," she observed. "Why?"

"Because I did this to you. Because I couldn't let you go."

"And if you could go back? If you could choose again?"

Walt thought about it. thought. The honest answer surprised him.

"I don't know," he admitted. "I'd want to choose differently. To let you die peacefully instead of this. But I don't know if I'd actually do it. Loving someone means you can't always make the right choice. Sometimes you make the one you can live with."

"Can you live with this?"

Walt looked at her—at the stranger wearing his sister's face, the pattern wearing his sister's name, the eternity he'd bought with her humanity.

"I'm trying," he said.

Bea nodded slowly. Then she did something she hadn't done in years. She crossed the room and placed one hand on Walt's shoulder. Her touch was fever-hot, inhuman, but the gesture itself was almost familiar.

"Thank you," she said. Her voice still had no warmth, but there was something in it—an echo, maybe, of who she'd been. "For staying. For trying. For being the brother-one even when I am no longer the sister-one. That is..." She paused, searching for words. "...kind."

Then she went back to the window and resumed her humming, and Walt sat in his chair and wept silently while the moon rose and the patterns shifted and the world continued, indifferent and beautiful and exactly as terrible as it had always been.

Years later—how many, Walt had stopped counting—he would die.

Peacefully, in his sleep, old and worn and finally letting go.

And Bea would remain.

Standing at the window. Wings spread. Humming that endless melody.

Perfect and terrible and utterly, eternally alone.

The pattern the creature had promised was complete. Beautiful in its way. Tragic in every way that mattered.

In the end, the fairy had been right about one thing: Bea would never die.

She would never again be alive either.

But the fairy had never promised that.
