# Chapter 4: The Improvement

Five days later, Bea walked down the stairs without holding the railing.

Walt watched from the top step, bread and butter in hand, as she descended with easy confidence. No hesitation. No pausing to catch her breath. She moved like she used to, before the illness, with that particular grace she'd always had.

Better, even. Smoother.

"Where are you going?" he called down.

She looked up at him, smiling. "to see Mrs. Hadley. I thought I'd ask if she needed help with her mending."

"Are you sure you're ready for that?"

"Walt, I feel wonderful." And she did look it—color in her cheeks, brightness in her eyes. She'd even put on weight, though Walt couldn't figure out how. They hadn't been eating any more than usual.

He watched her disappear into the candle-maker's shop below and told himself this was good. This was what he'd wanted.

The bottle sat on the table where it always sat now. In the days since that first cure, the creature inside had been quiet. It watched. It hummed sometimes. But it didn't speak unless spoken to, and Walt had learned not to ask it too many questions.

The answers were never right.

He crossed to the window and looked down at the street. After a moment, he saw Bea emerge from the shop and walk toward Mrs. Hadley's house three buildings over. Her gait was light. Almost bouncing.

Walt frowned. Bea had never bounced, even healthy.

He turned back to the room and began tidying—a habit born of nervous energy. The beds were already made, the floor already swept, but he straightened the quilts anyway and realigned the chairs and tried not to think about how the creature's eyes tracked his movements the entire time.

When Bea came back an hour later, she was humming.

The sound stopped Walt mid-motion, cloth in hand, frozen in the act of wiping down the table. He knew that melody. He'd been hearing it for five days, drifting from the bottle when the creature thought no one was listening.

"Bea?"

She looked up, still smiling. "Hmm?"

"That song. Where did you learn it?"

Her brow furrowed. "I don't know. It's been stuck in my head all morning." She hummed a few more notes, then laughed. "I can't even remember the words."

"There are no words," Walt said quietly.

"What?"

He nodded toward the bottle. "That's its song. The creature's."

Bea went still. Her hand moved to her throat, almost unconsciously. "I must have heard it from the bottle. That's all."

"You were downstairs."

"Sound carries." But she'd stopped humming.

That night, Bea ate more than Walt had seen her eat in months. She finished her portion of bread and cheese, then asked if he was going to finish his. When he gave it to her, she ate that too.

"I was so hungry," she said, almost surprised. "I hadn't realized."

Walt wanted to be glad. She'd lost so much weight during the illness. She needed to eat, needed to regain her strength.

But watching her now, the way she tore into the bread with fingers that moved a little too quickly, a little too eagerly—something in his chest tightened.

"Bea," he said carefully. "How are you feeling? Really?"

She looked at him, mouth full, and swallowed. "Good. good. Why?"

"No pain? No weakness?"

"None." She set down the last bit of crust. "It's amazing, isn't it? I thought I'd need weeks to recover, but I feel like I was never sick at all."

"That's not how recovery works."

"Maybe not usually. But nothing about this is usual, is it?" She glanced at the bottle. The creature was pressed against the glass, watching them. "I think it's still working. The cure. I can feel it sometimes, this warmth in my chest. Like it's... fixing things."

Walt didn't like the way she said "fixing." Didn't like the distant look that came into her eyes when she talked about it.

"Maybe we should see Brennan," he said. "The apothecary. Have him examine you, make sure everything is—"

"I'm fine, Walt."

"to be safe."

Bea sighed, but she smiled too, indulgent. "Alright. If it'll make you feel better."

Brennan came the next afternoon. He was a thin man with careful hands and eyes that missed nothing. Walt had never particularly liked him, but he was competent, and he'd been treating Bea since the illness started.

He examined her in silence—listening to her breathing, checking her pulse, looking at her throat and eyes and the color of her fingernails. When he was done, he stepped back with a strange expression on his face.

"Well?" Walt asked.

"Her lungs are clear," Brennan said slowly. "Completely clear. No fluid, no inflammation. Her heartbeat is strong and regular." He paused. "I don't understand."

"She's better," Walt said. "That's good, isn't it?"

"Mr. Moore, your sister was dying. The wasting sickness doesn't just... disappear. And certainly not in less than a week." Brennan looked at Bea, who sat on the edge of the bed, swinging her legs like a child. "What changed?"

"I got lucky," Bea said lightly. "Spontaneous remission. I've heard of it."

"Not like this." Brennan's gaze found the bottle on the table. He stared at it for a long moment, at the soft glow pulsing within. "What is that?"

"A curiosity," Walt said, too quickly. "I acquired it for a collector."

"It's glowing."

"Yes."

Brennan moved closer to the bottle, reaching out. His fingers were inches from the glass when he jerked back, face draining of color.

"Don't touch it," he said, more to himself than to them. He backed away, gathering his bag with trembling hands. "I need to go."

"But Bea—"

"Your sister isn't sick anymore." Brennan's voice was tight. "But she's not... well. Not in any way I understand." He looked at Walt, and there was something almost like pity in his eyes. "I don't know what you've done, Mr. Moore. But whatever that thing is, whatever you've bargained with—I want no part of it."

He left quickly, footsteps loud on the stairs.

Walt turned to Bea, who was still sitting on the bed, expression unreadable.

"He's wrong," she said. "I'm fine."

But her eyes, when she looked at Walt, reflected the lamplight in a color that wasn't right.

The wrongness crept in gradually after that. Small things. Easy to dismiss.

Bea stopped sleeping at night. Or rather, she'd lie in bed with her eyes closed, but when Walt woke in the dark hours, he'd see her chest barely moving. Once, he watched for a full minute and didn't see her breathe at all.

When he asked about it, she smiled and said she slept soundly now. Didn't dream anymore.

She started spending hours at the window, face turned to the sky. Walt thought she was enjoying being well enough to stand, until he realized she only did it when the moon was out. She'd stand there, absolutely still, eyes fixed on the pale light as if seeing something in it that Walt couldn't.

"What are you looking at?" he asked one night.

"The moon," she said. Her voice had a distant quality. "It's singing."

"The moon doesn't sing, Bea."

"Doesn't it?" She turned to him, and for a moment, her eyes were full of something vast and strange. Then she blinked, and it was Bea again, his sister, smiling at him. "Sorry. I'm being odd. I think I'm still adjusting."

But her shadow on the wall behind her had too many fingers.

Walt told himself he was imagining things. The lamplight played tricks. Bea was recovering, and recovery was strange, everyone knew that.

He told himself this even as he watched her reorganize their small collection of household objects—cups and plates and spoons—into patterns on the table. Precise geometric arrangements that made his eyes hurt if he looked at them too long.

"What are you doing?" he asked.

Bea looked down at her work as if seeing it for the first time. "I don't know," she said slowly. "I just... felt like they should go this way. Doesn't it look nice?"

It didn't look nice. It looked wrong, in a way Walt couldn't articulate.

He scattered the arrangement with one hand. The objects clattered across the table, and Bea flinched like he'd struck her.

"Walt—"

"You're not playing with the dishes anymore," he said, harsher than he meant to. "You're twenty-one, not ten."

Bea's face went still. "You're right. I'm sorry."

But later, when Walt came back from buying bread, he found the objects arranged again. A different pattern this time, more complex, spiraling out from a central cup in a way that made Walt's stomach turn over.

The creature in the bottle hummed its approval.

On the eighth day, Walt woke to find Bea standing over him.

She was standing there, inches from his chair where he'd fallen asleep, staring down at him with eyes that caught the pre-dawn light and threw it back in shades of gold and green.

Walt jerked upright, heart pounding. "Bea? What—"

"I'm sorry," she said. Her voice was soft, uncertain. "I didn't mean to wake you. I wanted to..."

"To what?"

"To make sure you were real." She knelt beside the chair, taking his hand in both of hers. Her skin was fever-warm. "Walt, I'm scared."

Relief and fear crashed through him in equal measure. "Tell me what's wrong."

"I don't..." She stopped, swallowed. When she spoke again, her voice was barely a whisper. "I'm having thoughts that aren't mine. Knowing things I shouldn't know. Last night I spoke to Mrs. Hadley's cat, and I understood what it was saying. Not in words, but I *understood*." Her fingers tightened on his. "And when I look in the mirror, sometimes my reflection doesn't match what I'm doing. Like there's something else wearing my face."

Walt pulled her close, arms around her shoulders. She was shaking. "It's alright. We'll fix this."

"How?"

"I don't know. But we will." He looked over her head at the bottle on the table. The creature was watching them, head tilted. "I'll make it undo what it did. I'll—"

"The pattern must complete," the creature said, voice like wind chimes. "Cannot uncomplete. Can only continue."

Walt's hands balled into fists. "Then stop. Stop whatever you're doing to her."

"We are helping. Making well. Making perfect." The creature pressed against the glass. "She asked to be well. She becomes. She shall be more-than-well. She grows—"

"I didn't ask for this," Bea said, pulling away from Walt. She stood, walked to the bottle, stared at the thing inside. "I didn't ask to be whatever you're turning me into."

The creature tilted its head the other direction. "The dying-one asked. The brother-one asked. 'Make my sister well.' We are. We are making well. So well. So perfect."

"Stop it," Walt said. "Stop right now, or I'll—"

"You'll what?" The creature's smile was gentle, patient. "Break the bottle? We would not advise. Breaking releases all at once. Better slow. Better gentle. Better the pattern completes carefully."

Bea's face had gone pale. "What happens if the pattern completes?"

The creature hummed.

Walt stood, moving between Bea and the bottle. "There has to be a way to stop this. To reverse it."

"Reversal is not part of the pattern," the creature said, voice almost sad. "The pattern moves forward. Always forward. Cannot unknit what is being knitted. Can only continue to completion."

"And what happens then? When you complete your pattern?"

The creature's eyes brightened. "She will be perfect. More-than-human. Less-than-dead. She becomes—" It paused, as if searching for a word. "—*right*."

"Right how?"

But the creature had stopped speaking. It watched them with too-bright eyes and hummed its endless melody.

Walt turned to Bea. She was crying, silent tears tracking down her cheeks. "I'm sorry," he said. "God, Bea, I'm so sorry. I wanted to save you."

"I know." She wiped at her face. "I know, Walt. But I think—" Her voice broke. "I think you might have done something worse than letting me die."

Outside, the sun was rising, painting the sky in shades of pink and gold. Beautiful and terrible and completely indifferent to the small tragedy unfolding in an attic room where a brother and sister stood divided by an impossible choice already made.

The creature hummed, and Bea's voice joined it, unconscious and perfect, harmonizing with sounds no human throat should be able to make.

She didn't notice until Walt flinched.

Then she pressed both hands over her mouth and ran to the corner of the room, as far from the bottle as she could get, and tried hard not to think about how natural the harmonizing had felt.
