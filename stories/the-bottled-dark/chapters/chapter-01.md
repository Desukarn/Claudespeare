# Chapter 1: The Dying Room

The coughing started again after sunset.

Walt set down the tin cup he'd been heating over the lamp and turned toward the narrow bed pushed against the far wall. Bea's shoulders shook with each spasm, her thin frame curling inward like paper near a flame. He crossed the small attic room in three steps and knelt beside her, one hand on her back.

"Easy," he said. "breathe."

She couldn't, not yet. The coughs tore through her, wet and rattling. Walt waited, his hand making small circles between her shoulder blades, feeling each ridge of her spine through the worn cotton of her nightgown. When the fit finally passed, she sagged back against the pillows, face pale as the moth-eaten quilt pooled around her waist.

"Better?" he asked.

Bea managed a smile. Her lips had a faint blue tinge that hadn't been there last week. "Better than what?"

"Better than yesterday."

"That's a low bar, Walt."

He returned to the lamp and poured the now-warm water into a chipped teacup painted with faded roses. The apothecary had left them a powder—something for the pain, he'd said, though Walt suspected it did nothing at all. He stirred it in anyway and brought the cup to her.

She took it in both hands. Her fingers looked like bird bones wrapped in skin.

"You don't have to hover," she said, but there was no heat in it. She sipped the tea, grimaced at the bitter taste, sipped again.

Walt moved to the small window and looked out. Rain slicked the narrow street below, turning the cobblestones black and making halos around the gas lamps. Their attic room sat above a candle-maker's shop, and most evenings the smell of tallow crept up through the floorboards. Tonight it mixed with the scent of Bea's medicinal tea and the damp wool of his coat hanging by the door.

"Did you eat?" he asked.

"Some broth. Mrs. Hadley brought it up this afternoon."

"That was kind of her."

"She thinks I'm dying."

Walt's jaw tightened. He didn't turn from the window. "You're not."

"Walt—"

"You're not."

Silence, except for the rain and the occasional creak of someone moving in the room below. Bea set the teacup down on the floor beside the bed, her movements slow and deliberate.

"Come sit with me," she said.

He did, pulling the room's only chair—a rickety thing with an uneven leg—close to her bedside. In the lamplight, her face looked softer, younger. She'd turned twenty-one last month. Walt had stolen a honey cake from a baker's window sill to mark the occasion, and they'd split it, laughing at their own ridiculousness.

She wasn't laughing now.

"The apothecary came this morning," she said.

Walt nodded. He'd passed the man on the stairs, had seen the look in his eyes. The kind of look you'd give a horse with a broken leg.

"He said there's nothing more he can do." Bea's voice was quiet, matter-of-fact. "The tinctures aren't working. The powders aren't working. He said I should... prepare myself."

"He's wrong."

"He's not, though." She reached out and took Walt's hand. Her palm was cold. "I'm tired, Walt. All the time. I can barely walk from the bed to the window without needing to rest. And the coughing—"

"Will get better."

"It won't." She squeezed his hand. "It's alright. I've made my peace with it."

"Well, I haven't."

Bea sighed, but it was a fond sound. "You're stubborn."

"Runs in the family."

"Does it?" Her mouth quirked. "I seem to remember being the reasonable one."

Despite everything, Walt almost smiled. "You're the one who climb the cathedral roof when you were nine."

"I wanted to see the gargoyles up close."

"You fell and broke your arm."

"And I saw the gargoyles." She leaned back against the pillows, eyes drifting closed. "Worth it."

Walt studied her face—the dark circles under her eyes, the hollow of her cheeks, the way her chest rose and fell with each labored breath. Six months ago, she'd been well enough to work. She'd done mending for a seamstress, careful, patient work that brought in enough to keep them fed. Now she could barely hold a needle.

Six months. That's all it had taken for the illness to hollow her out.

"I'm going to fix this," he said.

Bea opened her eyes. "How?"

"I don't know yet."

"Walt—"

"There are physicians in the city. Real ones, not apothecaries. People who study at universities. They'd know what to do."

"We can't afford them."

"I'll find a way."

She looked at him for a long moment, something sad and knowing in her expression. "Don't do anything stupid."

"Would I ever?"

"Yes. Frequently."

He stood, needing to move, and busied himself tidying things that didn't need tidying—straightening the blanket at the foot of her bed, adjusting the lamp's wick, moving the teacup to the small table they used for meals. Bea watched him, but she didn't speak.

When there was nothing left to rearrange, Walt picked up his coat from its peg by the door.

"Where are you going?" Bea asked.

"Out for a bit. Need some air."

"It's raining."

"I'll manage."

She didn't argue, but her eyes followed him as he shrugged into the coat. It was still damp from earlier, cold against his shirt.

"Walt," she said.

He paused at the door.

"Don't do anything stupid," she said again, softer this time. "Please."

He looked back at her—small and pale in the lamplight, already half-shadow. "Get some rest. I won't be long."

The narrow stairs creaked under his weight. He passed through the candle-maker's shop, nodded to the owner's wife who was sweeping the floor, and stepped out into the rain.

The streets were quiet, slick and dark. Walt pulled his coat tighter and walked.

He had no plan, not really. a bone-deep certainty that he couldn't sit in that room and watch Bea fade. Wouldn't. He'd done enough watching in his life—watched their parents sicken and die when he was sixteen and Bea was eleven, watched their small inheritance disappear to creditors and landlords, watched opportunities close like doors in a hallway until theft was the only one left open.

He'd kept them alive through lean times and leaner. Kept a roof over their heads, food in their bellies. It hadn't been a good life, but it had been a life.

He wouldn't watch it end. Not like this.

The tavern on Millgate Street was warm and loud, packed with bodies seeking shelter from the weather. Walt slipped inside and found a spot near the back where he could watch the room. He had a few coins in his pocket—enough for one drink, maybe two if he nursed them.

He ordered ale and listened.

Taverns were good for listening. People talked freely when they thought no one important was paying attention. Walt had learned years ago that being unremarkable was a useful skill. You could hear a lot if you looked like the kind of person no one would bother remembering.

Tonight he heard about a shipment delayed by the rain, a merchant's daughter who'd run off with a soldier, the price of grain going up again. Nothing useful.

Then a voice cut through the others—cultivated, expensive. Out of place in this room.

"...absolutely extraordinary. The detail on the etchings alone must have taken months. And the contents! My God, the contents."

Walt shifted slightly, angling himself to see the speaker. A man in his fifties, well-dressed in dark wool and brass buttons, holding court at a corner table. A collector, by the look of him. The type who bought and sold curiosities to other wealthy eccentrics.

"What is it?" someone asked.

The collector waved a hand. "A bottle. But not any bottle—it's covered in symbols, protective wards I think, though I haven't been able to translate them all. And inside..." He leaned forward, voice dropping to a dramatic whisper. "There's something alive in there."

Murmurs around the table. Walt took a sip of his ale and look bored.

"Alive?" a woman said. "What, like a insect?"

"I don't know what it is. Something small, luminous. It moves. Sometimes I think I can hear it singing, though that's probably my imagination."

"Where'd you get it?"

"Estate sale. Previous owner died suddenly." The collector's smile was thin. "His notes were... incomplete. But from what I could piece together, the bottle has been passed down through several collections. Always changing hands. No one keeps it long."

"Why not?"

The collector shrugged. "Bad luck, perhaps. Or bad nerves. It is unsettling to look at."

"But valuable?"

"Immensely. I've already had three inquiries from potential buyers. I'm thinking of auctioning it next month."

Walt set his ale down carefully, mind working. A valuable curiosity in the hands of a collector. The type of thing that might fetch enough to pay for a real physician. Maybe several physicians.

He finished his drink and left quietly, slipping back out into the rain.

The collector's house wouldn't be hard to find. Men like that always lived in the nicer parts of the city, places where the streets had names instead of numbers and the gas lamps actually worked. Walt had done enough second-story work to know how to get in and out of a wealthy man's home.

The walk back to the attic room was longer than the walk out, or maybe it felt that way. By the time Walt climbed the stairs, his coat was soaked through and rain dripped from his hair.

Bea was asleep. Her breathing sounded rough even in rest, but her face was peaceful.

Walt hung his coat to dry and sat down in the chair beside her bed. Through the window, the rain kept falling, turning the world outside into a blur of dark water and dim light.

Tomorrow, he thought. Tomorrow he'd find the collector's house. Tomorrow he'd take that bottle and sell it to one of those inquiring buyers. Tomorrow he'd get enough money to save her.

Bea stirred in her sleep, a small sound escaping her lips. Walt reached out and pulled the quilt up to her shoulders.

"Tomorrow," he whispered to the room, to the rain, to whatever was listening. "I'll fix this tomorrow."

In the morning, he'd be a thief again. But tonight, he was her brother, keeping watch in the lamplight while she slept.

Tomorrow would come soon enough.
