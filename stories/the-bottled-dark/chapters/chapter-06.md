# Chapter 6: Point of No Return

The full moon turned the attic room silver.

Bea stood at the window, face pressed to the glass, arms spread wide as if to embrace the light. She'd been there for hours, unmoving, barely breathing. Walt had stopped trying to pull her away after the third attempt. She didn't fight him, drifted back to the window the moment he let go, drawn by something she couldn't explain and he couldn't understand.

"Bea," he tried again. "It's late. You should sleep."

"I don't need to sleep anymore." Her voice was distant, dreamy. "Sleep is for people who get tired. I'm not tired. I'm not anything."

Walt's chest tightened. "You're my sister."

She turned then, and in the moonlight her face was beautiful and terrible. Her eyes had changed color—not brown anymore, but something between gold and green, shifting like oil on water. Her skin had taken on a faint luminescence, as if she was glowing from within.

"Am I?" she asked. Not cruelly. curious, as if the answer was genuinely uncertain.

"Yes."

Bea studied him for a long moment. Then something shifted in her expression—pain breaking through the alien serenity—and she was Bea again, Bea, looking at him with eyes full of fear.

"Walt," she said, voice cracking. "I'm losing myself. I can feel it. There are moments when I don't remember who I am, when human things don't make sense anymore. I looked at my hands this morning and couldn't remember what they were for."

Walt crossed the room and took those hands in his. They were too warm, fever-hot, and when he looked closely he could see her skin had become too smooth, too perfect, like porcelain than flesh.

"We'll fix this," he said, knowing it was a lie.

"How?" Bea laughed, and the sound had harmonics that hurt to hear. "You read the books. You talked to the collector. There's no way to stop it."

"There's always a way."

"Is there?" She pulled her hands free. "Name one. Please. Because I'm running out of time to be afraid, and I want—I need to be afraid. Fear means I'm still human."

Walt looked at the bottle. The creature inside was pressed against the glass, watching them with bright eyes. It had been more active recently, moving constantly, humming louder. As if it could sense the pattern approaching completion.

"Iron," Walt said. "The books said iron disrupts Fair Folk magic."

"We're not magic," the creature said, voice like wind chimes. "We're pattern. Iron is iron. It changes nothing."

"Then I'll try it anyway." Walt pulled a knife from his belt—iron blade, old and cheap. He set it on the table beside the bottle.

Nothing happened.

The creature looked at it with what might have been amusement. "You see? These are not our rules. Your protections mean nothing."

"Salt," Walt tried next, pulling a cloth pouch from his pocket. He poured a circle of salt around the bottle, the white grains bright in the moonlight. "Salt and iron. The old wards."

The creature hummed.

Bea made a sound—half laugh, half sob. "It's not working."

"Rowan, then." Walt had brought branches of rowan wood, tied with red thread the way the books said. He placed them around the bottle, completing the traditional protective barrier.

The creature's glow brightened slightly. "Pretty," it said. "But useless. We are already inside the dying-thing. She breathes us now. Her blood carries our pattern. You cannot ward against what is already within."

Walt swept the rowan branches aside with one arm. Salt scattered across the table. The knife clattered to the floor.

"Then what?" He was shouting now, not caring who heard. "What the hell can I do?"

"Nothing," the creature said gently. "Accept. That is all. Let the pattern complete. It will be beautiful."

"I don't want beautiful!" Walt grabbed the bottle, fingers tight on the glass. "I want my sister back. I want Bea, not whatever you're making her into."

The bottle was warm-cold under his hands, temperature shifting in ways that made no sense. The creature pressed its too-many-fingered hands against his palms, separated only by glass.

"We are making her perfect," it said. "More than she was. Better than dying. Why do you grieve?"

"Because you're taking her away from me!"

"We are saving her. That is what you asked."

Walt's grip tightened. It would be so easy to throw the bottle. To smash it against the wall and end this, consequences be damned. Maybe the collector was wrong. Maybe breaking it would destroy the creature and stop the transformation.

Maybe it would make everything worse.

"Don't," Bea said quietly.

Walt looked at her. She'd moved closer, moonlight painting her in shades of silver and gold. Her eyes were full of that alien serenity again, but underneath it he could see fear.

"The collector said breaking it would release everything at once," she continued. "Everything it's been doing slowly would happen immediately. To me. To you. To everyone nearby. We'd all change."

"Maybe that would be better than this."

"No." Bea took the bottle from his hands, her touch gentle but firm. "No, Walt. I won't let you destroy yourself trying to save me. Not when there's nothing left to save."

"You're still you—"

"Am I?" She set the bottle back on the table. "I don't breathe regularly anymore. My heart beats in rhythms that shouldn't work. I can hear colors and taste moonlight and I understand what the creature is humming even though it has no words. I look at you and I see..." She stopped, swallowing hard. "I see the pattern you're part of. The shape of you. The way you fit into the design of everything. And it's beautiful, Walt, but it's not how a sister should see her brother."

Walt felt something break in his chest. "Bea—"

"I'm not going to die," she said. Her voice was soft, almost resigned. "That's what you wanted, right? For me to live? Well, I'm going to live forever. I'm going to be eternal and perfect and completely, utterly OTHER. And there's nothing either of us can do about it."

"I could—" Walt stopped, the words sticking in his throat.

Bea knew anyway. "Kill me?" She smiled, sad and knowing. "Could you? Really? Before the transformation completes?"

"If you asked me to."

"I did ask. Days ago. And you said no." She reached out and touched his face, fingers fever-warm. "You can't let me go, Walt. That's always been your problem. You hold on to things so tightly they break."

"I'm sorry."

"I know." She pulled her hand back. "I'm sorry too. I should have fought harder when you first used the bottle. Should have made you get rid of it, should have—" She stopped, shaking her head. "But I wanted to live too. I wanted it so badly. And now I'm going to live forever as something that isn't me."

Outside, clouds drifted across the moon. The room darkened, and Bea gasped—a sharp, pained sound. She stumbled backward, hand going to her chest.

"Bea?"

"The moonlight—I need—" She turned back to the window, pressing against the glass. When the moon emerged from the clouds, she sagged with relief. "I'm sorry. I can't help it. The moon feeds me now. Or the pattern does, through the moon. I don't know. I don't understand what I'm becoming."

Walt watched her, this stranger wearing his sister's face, and felt hope die a quiet death.

"What happens when it completes?" he asked. Not to Bea. To the creature. "What happens when your pattern is finished?"

The creature's eyes brightened. "She will be like us. Part of the pattern. She will sing the songs that shape the world. She will see as we see, be as we are."

"Will she remember me?"

"Yessss. Memory is part of the pattern. But understanding will change. Love will change. She will love you as we love—vast and incomprehensible and terrible in its perfection."

"That's not love."

"It is the only love that lasts forever." The creature pressed against the glass. "We do not abandon. We do not forget. We carry all things in the pattern eternally. Is that not what you wanted? For her to live? To not fade? To be?"

"I wanted her to be *human*."

"Human is dying. Human is brief. Human is dust." The creature's voice was gentle, patient. "We offer eternity. We offer perfection. We offer—"

"I don't care what you offer!" Walt's hands balled into fists. "I want my sister back."

"She is here. She will always be here. But she will be more."

Bea made a sound—low and broken. She'd slid down the wall beneath the window, arms wrapped around her knees, face hidden. Moonlight pooled around her like water.

"I'm scared," she whispered. "I'm so scared, and I can feel the fear fading. Like it's being smoothed away. Soon I won't remember how to be afraid. I won't remember how to be sad. I'll be... perfect."

Walt knelt beside her. "Tell me what to do. Please, Bea. Tell me what you need."

She looked up at him, tears on her face that caught the moonlight and glowed. "Hold me. While I still remember what that means."

He did. He wrapped his arms around her and held on while she cried, while the moon climbed higher, while the creature hummed its endless melody.

Bea's sobs gradually faded. Her breathing evened out—or stopped, Walt couldn't tell anymore. When she pulled back, her face was calm. Too calm. The fear was gone from her eyes, replaced by that alien serenity.

"It's happening," she said, and her voice was steady. "I can feel it. The transformation is accelerating. Soon I'll..." She paused, tilting her head in a gesture that mirrored the creature's. "Soon I'll complete."

"How long?"

"Hours, maybe. The next moonrise at most." She stood, movements fluid and strange. "You should leave, Walt. Go somewhere far from here before it finishes. You don't need to watch this."

"I'm not leaving you."

"You already have." She looked at him, and her eyes were full of something vast. "I'm already gone, brother. You're watching the afterimage fade."

She turned back to the window. The moonlight embraced her, and Walt watched as her shadow stretched across the floor—too many angles, too many limbs, too much that wasn't Bea.

The creature hummed louder, and Bea's voice joined it.

Perfect harmony. Perfect horror.

Walt buried his face in his hands and tried not to hear the moment when Bea's voice stopped sounding human.

When he looked up again, his sister was standing in moonlight, wings spreading from her shoulders—translucent and veined with gold—while she sang in languages older than words.

The pattern was nearly complete.

And Walt could do nothing but watch.
