# Chapter 3: First Contact

Bea woke to find Walt asleep in the chair beside her bed, his head tipped forward, chin nearly touching his chest.

The morning light coming through the window was thin and gray. Rain had started again sometime during the night, a soft patter against the glass. Bea shifted carefully, trying not to wake him, but the movement triggered a cough that she couldn't quite suppress.

Walt's eyes opened immediately. He straightened, blinking sleep away, one hand going to the small of his back.

"You should've slept in the bed," Bea said, voice hoarse.

"You were in it."

"There's room."

"You need the rest more than I do." He stood, stretching, and moved to the table where the kettle usually sat. That's when Bea saw it.

The bottle.

It sat on the table, catching what little light filtered through the window, and it was the most beautiful thing she'd ever seen.

"What is that?" she asked.

Walt turned, following her gaze. His expression shifted into something careful and neutral. "Something I found."

"You stole it."

"Yes."

Bea tried to sit up. The effort cost her, but she managed it, propping herself against the thin pillows. "Walt—"

"It's valuable. I can sell it. Get enough money to pay for proper doctors, not just old Brennan and his useless powders."

"It's glowing."

"I know."

"There's something inside it."

Walt was quiet for a moment. Then he picked up the bottle—carefully, like it might break or bite—and brought it to her. "I don't know what it is," he admitted. "The man I took it from said it was alive. I thought he was exaggerating."

Bea took the bottle from him, feeling its weight settle into her palms. The glass was warm on one side and cold on the other, temperature shifting against her skin in a way that made no sense. The symbols etched into the surface were beautiful and precise, spiraling around the bottle in patterns that seemed to move when she wasn't looking directly at them.

And inside—

"Oh," Bea breathed.

The light pulsed. Brightened. And in its glow, she saw the thing that made it.

It was small, no larger than her hand. Delicate, like something made of flower petals and spider silk. It had wings—translucent, veined with gold—and a shape that was almost human but not quite. Too many joints in the fingers. Eyes that reflected the light in colors that didn't exist.

It was looking at her.

Bea's hands trembled. Not from weakness, not this time. From something else. An instinct that went deeper than thought, older than words. Every part of her that still remembered how to survive was screaming that this was *wrong*.

"We should get rid of it," she said.

"What?"

"Whatever this is, Walt, it's not—" She stopped, trying to find words for the feeling coiling in her chest. "It's not right. Look at it. Really look at it."

Walt frowned, but he looked. Bea watched his face as he studied the creature in the bottle, watched the moment his expression shifted from curiosity to something more uncertain.

"It's strange," he said finally. "But that doesn't mean it's dangerous."

"Doesn't it?"

Before Walt could answer, the thing in the bottle moved. It pressed against the glass, fingers (too many fingers) spreading wide, and opened its mouth.

The sound that came out was like wind chimes. Beautiful and pure and wrong in a way that made Bea's teeth ache.

"We can cure the dying-thing," it said.

Bea almost dropped the bottle. Only Walt's quick hands kept it from falling, taking it from her and setting it back on the table. They both stared at it.

The creature tilted its head—too far, at an angle human necks couldn't manage—and waited.

"Did it just—" Bea started.

"Yes," Walt said.

The creature blinked, eyes reflecting the lamplight in shades of gold and green and colors Bea couldn't name. "We can help," it said in that wind-chime voice that seemed to come from everywhere and nowhere at once. "The sick-thing, the dying-one. We can unknit the sickness."

Walt's hand was on Bea's shoulder, grip tight. "Can you understand us?" he asked.

"Yessss." The word had too many sounds in it, harmonics that hurt to hear. "We understand. We can help. You want the dying-thing to live. We want—" It paused, head tilting the other direction. "—to complete the pattern."

Bea's instincts screamed louder. "No. Walt, no. Whatever it is, whatever it's offering—"

"It says it can cure you."

"It's in a bottle covered in warning symbols. In seven languages, you said the collector told you." She grabbed Walt's wrist, felt how cold her own fingers were against his skin. "This isn't medicine. This isn't—it's something else."

"The pattern must complete," the creature said, voice soft and soothing as honey. "The dying-thing fits. The right shape. The right time. We will unknit the sickness. She will be well."

"See?" Walt pulled away from Bea's grip. "It can help."

"At what cost?"

Walt didn't answer. Bea could see the war playing out on his face—hope against caution, desperation against sense. She knew which one would win. She'd known since the moment she saw the bottle.

"Walt," she tried one more time. "Please. I'm ready. I've made my peace with—"

"Well, I haven't." He turned to the bottle, to the creature inside. "You can cure her? Make her well?"

"Yesss. We will unknit the sickness. Unweave the dying. She will be... more."

"More what?"

The creature just smiled. Its mouth had too many teeth, all of them small and perfect and white as new snow.

"Don't—" Bea started.

But Walt was already speaking. "Make my sister well," he said, voice firm despite the tremor in his hands. "Cure her. Please."

The creature's eyes brightened. It pressed both hands against the glass, and the light inside the bottle grew. "Yessss," it said. "We will help. We will complete. We will unknot the dying-thing and make her well well well—"

Heat bloomed in Bea's chest.

It wasn't painful, not exactly. More like warmth spreading through cold water, ice melting under a sudden sun. It radiated from her lungs outward, following her veins, settling into her bones.

The constant ache she'd been living with for months—the tightness in her chest, the weight that made every breath a conscious effort—eased.

Not gone. But... less.

Bea drew in a breath. Then another. No cough. No rattle. Just air, clean and full, filling her lungs the way it used to.

"Bea?" Walt's voice was tight with hope. "Are you—"

"I don't know." She put a hand to her chest, feeling her heartbeat. Strong. Steady. When was the last time it had felt steady? "I feel... different."

"Good different?"

"I think so." But even as she said it, something in her—that same instinct that had screamed danger—whispered that this wasn't right. The warmth in her chest felt wrong. Not painful, but *other*. Like it didn't quite belong to her.

The creature in the bottle hummed, a sound that made Bea's vision blur at the edges. "Good," it said. "Good good good. We are unknitting. She will be well. She will be more-than-well. She will be *perfect*."

Walt knelt beside the bed, taking Bea's hands in his. "How do you feel? Really?"

"Warm," Bea said. "And... tired. But not the same kind of tired. Not sick-tired. Just tired."

"You should rest." Walt was grinning now, relief and joy bright in his face. "Sleep. Get your strength back."

Bea wanted to argue, wanted to say that this felt wrong, that they should be careful, that creatures in bottles didn't grant wishes without prices.

But she was tired. And for the first time in months, her chest didn't hurt.

She lay back against the pillows. Walt pulled the quilt up around her shoulders, tucking it in the way he had when she was little and woke from nightmares.

"Thank you," he said to the creature. "I don't know what you are or what you want, but—thank you."

The creature tilted its head. "We want to complete the pattern," it said simply. "That is all. That is everything. You have helped. We have helped. Now the pattern continues."

Walt nodded as if this made sense. He set the bottle back on the table, within sight but not within reach.

Bea watched it through half-closed eyes. The light pulsed, soft and steady. The creature moved inside its glass prison, wings fluttering, fingers tracing patterns on the inside of the bottle that left glowing trails in their wake.

It was still looking at her.

Bea closed her eyes, but she could feel its gaze. Could feel the warmth in her chest, spreading, settling, changing things in ways she didn't understand.

Sleep came quickly, faster than it should have. As she drifted off, she thought she heard singing. A voice like wind chimes, humming a melody that had no beginning and no end, just spiraling patterns of sound that wound through her dreams and made her think of things she had no names for.

When Bea woke, the light outside had changed. Afternoon, maybe. She sat up—and realized it didn't hurt.

No shortness of breath. No tightness in her chest. No need to move slowly and carefully to avoid triggering a coughing fit.

She stood up. Actually stood, without needing to brace herself on the bedframe.

Walt wasn't in the room. The bottle sat on the table, glowing softly. The creature inside was very still, watching her.

Bea walked to the window—walked, not shuffled—and looked out at the rain-darkened street below. Her reflection in the glass looked almost like she remembered. Thin, still. Pale. But standing upright. Breathing.

She brought her hand up to the window and froze.

Her reflection moved a fraction of a second after she did.

Bea lowered her hand slowly. Tried again. The reflection followed, perfectly synchronized this time. She must have imagined it. Trick of the light. She'd just woken up, after all.

Behind her, the creature hummed softly.

Bea turned to look at it. In the gray afternoon light, it seemed even more beautiful than before. Delicate. Perfect.

And so, so wrong.

"What did you do to me?" she asked.

The creature's head tilted. "We are unknitting. The sickness is being unwoven. You will be well."

"And then what?"

"Then you will be perfect." Its smile was gentle. "Do not fear, little chrysalis. We are helping. We are helping. The pattern will be so beautiful when it completes."

Bea wrapped her arms around herself. The warmth in her chest pulsed in time with the light in the bottle, and she realized she couldn't tell anymore which was her heartbeat and which was something else.

"What pattern?" she whispered.

But the creature just hummed that spiral melody and smiled its too-many-teethed smile and said nothing at all.

When Walt came back an hour later, arms full of bread and cheese and apples—things he must have spent the last of their money on—Bea was sitting at the table. She'd brushed her hair and put on a dress that hadn't fit properly in weeks.

"You're up," Walt said, and his smile could have lit the whole room. "You're really up. How do you feel?"

Bea looked at him. At her brother, who'd stolen a cursed thing from a collector's house because he couldn't let her go. Who'd traded something unknown for her life because desperation didn't leave room for caution.

She could tell him the truth. Could say that something felt deeply wrong, that her reflection moved strangely and her heartbeat had harmonics it shouldn't have and the warmth in her chest felt like it was changing her from the inside out.

But Walt looked so happy. So relieved.

And she was standing. Breathing without pain.

"Better," Bea said. "I feel better."

Walt set down his purchases and pulled her into a hug. Bea leaned into it, eyes on the bottle over his shoulder.

The creature watched them with eyes like shifting colors.

And hummed.
