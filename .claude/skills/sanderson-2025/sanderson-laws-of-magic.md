# Sanderson's Laws of Magic - Complete Magic System Design Skill

## Overview
Based on Brandon Sanderson's 2025 Lecture #7, this skill provides comprehensive guidelines for crafting compelling magic systems using Sanderson's Three Laws. While focused on magic, these principles apply to any systematic element in storytelling (technology, psion

ics, superpowers, etc.).

## Core Philosophy

**Sanderson's Principle**: "These laws are not rigid rules, but flexible guidelines that encourage writers to think deeply about magical systems, viewing them as integral parts of storytelling rather than mere decorative elements."

**The Golden Rule**: "It's okay to go big. It's okay to go epic. But be sure to go deep as well."

## THE THREE LAWS

### Law One: Understanding Equals Magic

**Full Statement**: "An author's ability to solve conflict with magic is directly proportional to how well the reader understands said magic."

#### The Spectrum: Hard vs. Soft Magic

Magic systems exist on a spectrum between two approaches:

**SOFT MAGIC SYSTEMS**
Rules are deliberately obscure, maintaining mystery and wonder.

**Characteristics**:
- Readers don't understand exact mechanisms
- Capabilities seem limitless or undefined
- Used primarily to complicate, not solve problems
- Creates sense of awe and mystery
- Reader identifies with non-magical characters

**Example - Tolkien (*Lord of the Rings*)**:
- Gandalf's powers are vague and wondrous
- We experience magic through Hobbits' eyes (non-magical)
- Magic creates problems (Saruman, Sauron) more than solves them
- When Gandalf uses magic to solve problems, it's usually off-screen or subtle
- Maintains sense of mystery and wonder

**When to Use Soft Magic**:
- High fantasy with mythic feel
- Stories where magic is background element
- Plots that don't hinge on magical problem-solving
- When sense of wonder matters more than tactics

##### Soft Magic Execution Techniques

**IMPORTANT**: Soft magic is about HOW you write it, not just what you define.

**1. Language and Description**
- Use poetic, sensory language over mechanical description
- Emphasize feeling and atmosphere over function
- Example:
  - ❌ Hard: "She channeled energy through the focal crystal"
  - ✅ Soft: "The air shivered with possibility, and she felt the forest's attention turn toward her"

**2. Maintaining Mystery**
- Replace explanations with questions and wonder
- Characters don't fully understand what's happening
- Contradictions and uncertainties are features, not bugs
- Example:
  - ❌ "The spell requires three components because..."
  - ✅ "No one knows why it requires three. Some say the number is sacred. Others whisper darker reasons."

**3. Thematic Constraints (Instead of Mechanical)**
- Magic follows thematic rules, not logical ones
- Constrained by meaning, emotion, story significance
- Example Themes:
  - **Sacrifice**: Magic requires giving up something meaningful
  - **Truth**: Magic works only when spoken with honesty
  - **Balance**: Magic demands equivalent exchange of symbolic weight
  - **Connection**: Magic flows through relationship, not power

**4. POV Strategy**
- Non-magical POV characters experience magic as mystery
- Magical POV characters don't analyze mechanisms
- Internal monologue focuses on feeling, not process
- Example:
  - ❌ "I gathered the threads of power and wove them according to the pattern..."
  - ✅ "Power moved through me—or did I move through it? I couldn't tell. The distinction seemed meaningless."

**5. Avoiding "Accidental Hardening"**
- Track what you've explained—stop before full system emerges
- When tempted to answer "how," ask "what does it mean?"
- Keep some contradictions deliberately unresolved
- Don't let characters discuss magic mechanics
- **Warning Signs**:
  - Characters analyzing "how" magic works
  - Systematic categorization emerging
  - Magic becoming predictable tool
  - Power levels or quantification appearing

**HARD MAGIC SYSTEMS**
Clear, understandable rules enable strategic problem-solving.

**Characteristics**:
- Readers understand precise powers and limitations
- Capabilities have defined boundaries
- Can be used to solve plot problems satisfyingly
- Creates tension through strategic application
- Reader understands what's possible and impossible

**Example - Sanderson (*Mistborn*)**:
- Clear metal-based powers
- Defined limitations (must ingest metals, metals burn out)
- Readers can predict what's possible
- Climax involves clever application of established rules
- Magic solves problems because we understand it

**When to Use Hard Magic**:
- Stories where magic drives plot
- Tactical/strategic narratives
- When you want readers theorizing about applications
- Superhero-style stories

**The Critical Rule**: The harder your magic (more understood), the more you can use it to solve plot problems. The softer your magic (less understood), the less you should use it to resolve conflicts.

#### Practical Application

**For Soft Magic**:
```
Magic Role: Create wonder, complicate situations
Problem-Solving: Must use non-magical solutions at climax
Reader Experience: Awe and mystery
Key Question: "How does this make readers feel?"
```

**For Hard Magic**:
```
Magic Role: Tool for characters to overcome obstacles
Problem-Solving: Can use magic at climax if rules are clear
Reader Experience: Understanding and strategizing
Key Question: "Do readers understand the rules?"
```

**Hybrid Approach** (Most Common):
- Some elements well-defined (protagonist's powers)
- Other elements mysterious (ancient magic, gods)
- Allows tactical use AND sense of wonder

**Example - Harry Potter**:
- Protagonist's spells: Well-defined (hard)
- Ancient magic, love protection: Mysterious (soft)
- Can solve problems with understood spells, while maintaining mystery with deeper magic

### Law Two: Limitations > Powers

**Full Statement**: "Limitations are more interesting than powers. A character's constraints prove more compelling than abilities."

#### The Superman Principle

**Sanderson's Insight**: "What makes Superman interesting? Two things: his code of ethics and his weakness to kryptonite."

**The Problem**: Unlimited power is boring because it removes tension.

**The Solution**: Constraints create interesting stories.

#### Types of Constraints

**1. LIMITATIONS** (Fundamental Ability Restrictions)
What the magic fundamentally cannot do.

**Examples**:
- Can only affect certain materials (Mistborn: metals only)
- Range limits (must touch target, line-of-sight only)
- Categorical restrictions (can't create food, can't resurrect dead)
- Scale limits (can lift car, not building)

**Purpose**: Define scope, prevent omnipotence

**2. WEAKNESSES** (Exploitable Vulnerabilities)
Specific things that negate or harm the magic user.

**Examples**:
- Kryptonite (Superman)
- Cold iron (Fae magic)
- Magic-dampening materials
- Vulnerabilities during casting
- Backlash from overuse

**Purpose**: Create dramatic tension, provide enemies with tools

**3. COSTS** (Negative Consequences)
Price paid for using magic.

**Types of Costs**:
- **Physical**: Exhaustion, aging, injury
- **Mental**: Sanity loss, memory degradation
- **Moral**: Corruption, soul damage
- **Resource**: Consumable components, limited fuel
- **Social**: Stigma, persecution, isolation
- **Time**: Long casting, cooldown periods

**Example - Wheel of Time**:
- Male magic users (saidin) descend into madness
- Creates tragic dimension to power
- Every use brings them closer to insanity
- Makes using power a meaningful choice, not automatic

**Purpose**: Make magic use a meaningful decision, not default action

#### Strategic Value of Constraints

Excellent limitations accomplish three objectives:

**1. Force Creative Problem-Solving**
When power is limited, characters must be clever.

**Example**:
- Unlimited teleportation → Just teleport away from any danger (boring)
- Can only teleport to places you've been → Must plan, prepare, make strategic choices (interesting)

**2. Increase Narrative Tension**
Clear limits make readers worry whether character can succeed.

**Example**:
- "Can cast 3 spells before exhausted" → Reader counts spells, worries about running out
- "Unlimited spells" → No tension about resource management

**3. Deepen Worldbuilding Authenticity**
Constraints create ripple effects in society and culture.

**Example**:
- Healing magic that drains life force → Healers are rare, respected, martyrs
- Easy healing with no cost → Healing would be commonplace, no dramatic weight

### Law Three: Expand, Don't Add

**Full Statement**: "Expand what you already have before you add something new."

#### Worldbuilder's Disease

**The Problem**: Creating increasingly complex systems without depth.

**Symptoms**:
- Magic system has 47 different types of magic
- Each barely explored
- No deep implications
- Feels like list rather than integrated system

**Sanderson's Insight**: "A brilliant magic system for a book is less often one with a thousand different powers and abilities—and is more often a magic system with relatively few powers that the author has considered in depth."

#### The Depth-Over-Breadth Principle

**Better Approach**:
- Few powers, deeply considered
- Exploration of implications
- Interconnected effects
- Cultural/societal impact

**Example - Mistborn**:
- 16 metals, each with specific power
- BUT: Explores combinations, cultural impact, historical implications
- Entire society built around who can use which metals
- Religious significance
- Economic systems
- Political structures
- Result: Feels deep despite relatively simple base

#### Implementation Strategies

**1. EXTRAPOLATE**
Ask "What happens when..." questions

**Questions to Ask**:
- What happens when this power is used creatively?
- What happens if someone masters this beyond normal limits?
- What happens when two powers combine?
- What happens to society with this power available?
- What happens economically?
- What happens militarily?
- What happens in daily life?

**Example - Telekinesis**:
- Basic: Move objects with mind
- Extrapolate Combat: Redirect arrows, throw debris, crush windpipes
- Extrapolate Society: Replace cranes, no need for elevators
- Extrapolate Economy: Telekinetics highly valued for construction
- Extrapolate Culture: Moving objects by hand seen as lower-class
- Extrapolate Creative: Intricate sculptures impossible by hand
- Result: One power, deeply explored

**2. INTERCONNECT**
Link powers thematically to core narrative

**Questions to Ask**:
- How does this magic reflect story themes?
- How does it connect to character arcs?
- How do different aspects of magic relate to each other?

**Example - Avatar: The Last Airbender**:
- Four elements (Fire, Water, Earth, Air)
- Each reflects philosophy and culture
- Bending style mirrors nation's values
- Character arcs tied to understanding their element
- Theme: Balance (between elements, within self)
- Result: Magic is inseparable from story

**3. STREAMLINE**
Consolidate magical concepts

**Instead of**:
- Pyrokinesis (fire control)
- Cryokinesis (ice control)
- Electrokinesis (lightning control)
- Aerokinesis (wind control)
- Geokinesis (earth control)
- Hydrokinesis (water control)

**Consider**:
- Elemental manipulation (all elements)
- OR: Energy manipulation (explains fire, lightning, etc.)
- OR: Matter state control (solid, liquid, gas, plasma)

**Benefit**: One concept explored deeply > Six concepts explored shallowly

## PRACTICAL MAGIC SYSTEM DESIGN

### Step 1: Establish the Emotional Core

**Before mechanics, identify**:
- What feeling should magic evoke?
- What role does magic play in story?
- What themes does magic explore?

**Examples**:
- **Wonder and Mystery**: Soft magic, unknowable
- **Strategic Problem-Solving**: Hard magic, tactical
- **Terrible Cost**: Dark magic, sacrificial
- **Harmony with Nature**: Elemental, balanced
- **Human Ambition**: Scientific, expanding

### Step 2: Define Core Mechanism

**The Central Question**: How does magic work?

**Source-Based Systems**:
- Draw from natural elements (fire, water, etc.)
- Channel from gods/spirits
- Internal energy (chi, mana)
- External energy (ley lines, moonlight)
- Genetic/bloodline ability

**Rule-Based Systems**:
- Sympathetic magic (like affects like)
- True name magic (knowing name gives power)
- Ritual magic (specific actions required)
- Verbal magic (speaking makes it real)
- Gesture magic (movements channel power)

**Cost-Based Systems**:
- Sacrifice (blood, life force)
- Resources (components, fuel)
- Time (long casting)
- Sanity/corruption (mental cost)
- Exchange (give to receive)

**Example Framework**:

### Step 4: Worldbuilding Integration

**Magic's Impact on Society**:

**Economy**:
- What jobs does magic create/eliminate?
- How is magical ability monetized?
- What's the magical economy? (spell components, services)

**Politics**:
- Who controls magic use?
- Are magic users regulated?
- Do they have special status/restrictions?
- Magic-based power structures?

**Military**:
- How is magic used in warfare?
- Magical arms race?
- Defensive countermeasures?

**Daily Life**:
- How does average person encounter magic?
- Magical conveniences?
- Magical dangers?

**Culture/Religion**:
- Is magic sacred, profane, or neutral?
- Religious attitudes toward magic?
- Cultural traditions involving magic?

**Example - Mistborn Economic Impact**:
- Nobility have Allomantic abilities (genetic)
- Creates hereditary aristocracy
- Skaa (commoners) without powers are lower class
- Half-breeds (Mistings with one power) are middle class
- Metals become valuable commodities
- Pewter and tin crucial resources
- Result: Magic creates entire social structure

### Step 5: Character Integration

**How Characters Interact with Magic**:

**Prodigies vs. Hard Workers**:
- Natural talent vs. earned skill
- Different emotional journeys

**Specialists vs. Generalists**:
- Master one ability vs. learn many
- Jack-of-all-trades vs. expert

**Ethical Users vs. Pragmatists**:
- Self-imposed restrictions
- Willing to pay any cost
- Creates character conflict

**Example Questions**:
```

Magical ability level: Novice / Intermediate / Expert / Master
Specialization: _______________
Learning style: Natural talent / Hard work / Both
Ethical stance on magic: _______________
Personal limitation beyond system: _______________
```

## ADVANCED TECHNIQUES

### Creating Satisfying Magic-Based Climaxes

**The Setup-Payoff Pattern**:
1. **Establish Rule Early**: Demonstrate magic principle in low-stakes situation
2. **Reinforce Rule**: Show it multiple times so readers internalize it
3. **Create Impossible Situation**: Present problem that seems unsolvable
4. **Apply Rule Cleverly**: Use established principle in unexpected way
5. **Reader Reaction**: "Of course! Why didn't I think of that?"

**Example - Mistborn**:
- **Establish**: Pewter enhances physical strength
- **Reinforce**: Multiple fight scenes using pewter
- **Impossible**: Character needs superhuman strength to survive
- **Clever Application**: [Spoiler - creative pewter use]
- **Result**: Satisfying because rules were clear

### Balancing Multiple Magic Types

**If Your World Has Multiple Systems**:

**Option 1: Interconnected**
All magic draws from same source, just different applications
- Example: Avatar (all bending is chi manipulation, different philosophies)

**Option 2: Separate But Balanced**
Different sources, but comparable power levels
- Example: Wheel of Time (saidin vs. saidar, different but balanced)

**Option 3: Hierarchical**
Some magic more powerful, but more costly/rare
- Example: LoTR (Valar > Maiar > Wizard's staff magic > artifact magic)

**Warning**: Each additional system requires exponential complexity consideration

### Soft Magic in Hard Magic World (Or Vice Versa)

**The Hybrid Approach**:
- Protagonist's magic: Hard (they understand, readers understand)
- Ancient magic: Soft (mysterious, unknowable)
- Antagonist's magic: Medium (understood enough to counter, mysterious enough to surprise)

**Benefit**:
- Can use hard magic for satisfying problem-solving
- Maintain sense of wonder with soft elements
- Create mystery and tension with unknowns

**Example - Harry Potter**:
- Classroom spells: Hard (clear rules, taught systematically)
- Love protection: Soft (mysterious, ancient, not fully explained)
- Horcruxes: Medium (understand concept, not all mechanics)

### Magic and Technology Interaction

**In Worlds with Both**:

**Option 1: Separate Domains**
- Magic does things technology can't
- Technology does things magic can't
- Minimal overlap

**Option 2: Competitive**
- Magic vs. technology progression
- One advancing, other declining
- Cultural conflict

**Option 3: Synergistic**
- Magic enhances technology
- Technology enables better magic use
- Magitech integration

**Example - Sanderson's Mistborn Era 2**:
- Industrial revolution + magic
- Allomantic-powered machinery
- Technological understanding of magic
- Result: Fresh take on established system

## MAGIC SYSTEM ARCHETYPES

### Elemental Systems
**Core**: Manipulation of classical elements

**Strengths**:
- Intuitive (everyone understands fire burns)
- Visual and dramatic
- Clear categories

**Challenges**:
- Well-worn territory
- Need fresh angle to stand out

**Fresh Approaches**:
- Unusual element combinations
- Cultural interpretation (Japanese 5 elements vs. Western 4)
- Scientific grounding (states of matter instead)
- Philosophical basis (elements as metaphor)

### Sympathetic Magic
**Core**: Like affects like; connections between things

**Strengths**:
- Ancient magical tradition (feels authentic)
- Allows creative applications
- Built-in limitations (need connection)

**Challenges**:
- Can become anything (too flexible)
- Need clear rules about connections

**Implementation**:
- Define what creates connection
- Establish range/strength limits
- Determine costs

### True Name Magic
**Core**: Knowing true name gives power over thing

**Strengths**:
- Knowledge-based (rewards clever characters)
- Built-in quest (discover names)
- Natural limitations (names are secret)

**Challenges**:
- Can feel arbitrary
- Why these specific names?

**Implementation**:
- Establish naming language/system
- Create hierarchy (partial names = partial power)
- Define how names are learned

### Energy/Mana Systems
**Core**: Internal or external energy pool

**Strengths**:
- Clear resource management
- Familiar to readers
- Easy to balance (numerical)

**Challenges**:
- Generic if not distinctive
- Can feel game-like

**Implementation**:
- Unique energy source
- Interesting recharge method
- Meaningful costs beyond "running out"

### Blood/Sacrifice Magic
**Core**: Power through sacrifice

**Strengths**:
- Inherently dark and dramatic
- Built-in costs
- Moral complexity

**Challenges**:
- Can be gratuitously dark
- Limits heroic use

**Implementation**:
- Define what's sacrificed and why
- Scale cost to power gained
- Explore moral implications

### Genetic/Bloodline Magic
**Core**: Inherited magical ability

**Strengths**:
- Creates social stratification
- Family drama potential
- "Chosen one" narratives

**Challenges**:
- Can seem elitist
- Limits who can be magical

**Implementation**:
- How does inheritance work?
- Can non-magical gain power?
- Social implications?

### Ritual Magic
**Core**: Specific actions produce effects

**Strengths**:
- Preparation matters
- Knowledge-based
- Room for creativity in components

**Challenges**:
- Can slow pacing
- Why these specific rituals?

**Implementation**:
- Clear ritual structure
- Logical component connections
- Failure consequences

### Verbal/Spoken Magic
**Core**: Speaking makes magic real

**Strengths**:
- Dramatic and active
- Language creates limits
- Cultural depth (magical language)

**Challenges**:
- What if silenced?
- Why these words?

**Implementation**:
- Magical language rules
- Mispronunciation consequences
- Non-verbal alternatives?

## COMMON PITFALLS AND SOLUTIONS

### Pitfall 1: Magic as Plot Convenience
**Problem**: Magic solves any problem whenever needed

**Why It Fails**: Removes tension, feels like cheating

**Solution**:
- Apply Law One (readers must understand limits)
- Apply Law Two (meaningful limitations)
- Foreshadow magical solutions early

### Pitfall 2: Inconsistent Rules
**Problem**: Magic works differently each time

**Why It Fails**: Readers can't invest if rules change

**Solution**:
- Document your rules (even if readers don't see full doc)
- Track each magic use for consistency
- If breaking rule, explain why (ritual vs. combat casting)

### Pitfall 3: Overpowered Magic
**Problem**: Magic users are unstoppable

**Why It Fails**: No tension, boring conflicts

**Solution**:
- Strong limitations (Law Two)
- Countermeasures exist
- Costs make frequent use prohibitive
- Not everyone can use it

### Pitfall 4: Undefined Soft Magic
**Problem**: "It's mysterious" becomes excuse for no rules

**Why It Fails**: Even soft magic needs internal logic

**Solution**:
- YOU know the rules (even if readers don't)
- Consistent feel/aesthetics
- Clear about what it can't do
- Don't solve major plot problems with it

### Pitfall 5: Magic Without Impact
**Problem**: Magic exists but doesn't affect world

**Why It Fails**: Feels tacked-on, not integrated

**Solution**:
- Apply Law Three (explore implications)
- Show societal impact
- Integrate with character arcs
- Make magic matter to plot

### Pitfall 6: Too Many Systems
**Problem**: 10 different unrelated magic types

**Why It Fails**: Overwhelming, shallow exploration

**Solution**:
- Apply Law Three (expand before adding)
- Consolidate similar systems
- Connect systems thematically
- Focus on fewer, deeper systems

### Pitfall 7: Explanation Dump
**Problem**: Stopping story to explain magic rules

**Why It Fails**: Kills pacing, boring

**Solution**:
- Show through use, not lectures
- Character learning = reader learning
- Reveal incrementally
- Make explanation scenes active (teaching scenes with conflict)

## REVISION CHECKLIST

### Magic System Audit

**Consistency Check**:
- [  ] Rules are applied consistently throughout
- [  ] No contradictions in how magic works
- [  ] Power levels remain consistent
- [  ] Limitations are respected

**Law One Check**:
- [  ] Reader understanding matches how magic solves problems
- [  ] Hard magic has clear rules established early
- [  ] Soft magic doesn't solve major plot conflicts
- [  ] Hybrid magic clearly separates understood/mysterious elements

**Law Two Check**:
- [  ] Clear limitations exist
- [  ] Meaningful costs for using magic
- [  ] Weaknesses create dramatic tension
- [  ] Constraints force creative problem-solving

**Law Three Check**:
- [  ] Existing powers explored deeply
- [  ] Societal implications considered
- [  ] Creative applications demonstrated
- [  ] Thematic connections established
- [  ] Resisted urge to add unnecessary complexity

**Integration Check**:
- [  ] Magic affects economy/society realistically
- [  ] Characters interact with magic meaningfully
- [  ] Magic connects to themes
- [  ] Magic serves story, doesn't overshadow it

**Climax Check**:
- [  ] Magical solution foreshadowed earlier
- [  ] Uses established rules only
- [  ] Feels clever, not convenient
- [  ] Satisfying payoff for setup

## FINAL WISDOM FROM SANDERSON

> "These laws are not rigid rules, but flexible guidelines that encourage writers to think deeply about magical systems, viewing them as integral parts of storytelling rather than mere decorative elements."

> "It's okay to go big. It's okay to go epic. But be sure to go deep as well."

> "A brilliant magic system for a book is less often one with a thousand different powers and abilities—and is more often a magic system with relatively few powers that the author has considered in depth."

> "An author's ability to solve conflict with magic is directly proportional to how well the reader understands said magic."

> "Limitations are more interesting than powers."

> "Expand what you already have before you add something new."
