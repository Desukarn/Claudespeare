# Promise, Progress, Payoff - Sanderson Plot Framework Skill

## Overview
Based on Brandon Sanderson's 2025 Lecture #2, this skill helps writers implement the Promise, Progress, Payoff framework to create engaging narratives that keep readers turning pages.

## Core Philosophy
Storytelling is a three-part dance:
1. **Promise**: Establish reader expectations (the contract)
2. **Progress**: Maintain narrative momentum (meaningful forward movement)
3. **Payoff**: Deliver satisfying conclusions (surprising but inevitable)

## How to Use This Framework

**Structure = Scaffolding, Not Straitjacket**

**Sanderson's Philosophy**: Plot frameworks are tools that "provide consistency without constraining creativity." Don't become "a cook rather than a chef"—frameworks should enhance your work, not limit it.

**Best Use**:
- **During Revision**: Use to identify weak spots and ensure reader satisfaction
- **Not First Draft**: Don't let it paralyze you during initial creation
- **For Diagnosis**: When something feels wrong, use framework to find the problem

**Remember**: "You never know which technique will unlock the next breakthrough"—study frameworks widely, but let your story breathe.

**Conflict Makes Story**: As Sanderson emphasizes, "The conflict and the struggle make the story, not the sequence of events." Focus on escalating conflict, not just moving plot pieces around.

## Skill Components

### 1. PROMISE ANALYSIS

When analyzing or creating story openings, evaluate these promise types:

#### Tone Promises
- **Humorous**: Include early jokes, playful language (e.g., Terry Pratchett)
- **Dark**: Open with shocking moments, executions, violence (e.g., Game of Thrones)
- **Whimsical**: Employ playful language, unique naming conventions

**Check**: Does the opening chapter's tone match the overall story mood?

#### Story Promises
- Communicate broader narrative direction
- Example: "The Dragon Reborn will confront the Dark One" (Wheel of Time)
- Sets expectations for the entire arc

**Check**: Is the main story question established by end of Act One?

##### The Major Dramatic Question (CRITICAL)

**Every story requires a central question** by the end of Act One that drives reader investment.

**Sanderson's Teaching**: "This question clarifies by Act One's conclusion, unifying subplots and character arcs around central conflict."

**Examples**:
- *Star Wars*: Will Luke become a Jedi and defeat the Empire?
- *Pride and Prejudice*: Will Elizabeth and Darcy overcome their pride and prejudice to be together?
- *The Lord of the Rings*: Will Frodo destroy the Ring and save Middle-earth?
- *The Hunger Games*: Will Katniss survive the Games?

**Why It Matters**:
- **Unifies** all subplots around central conflict
- **Drives** reader investment (they want the answer)
- **Guides** your plotting (everything serves answering this question)
- **Determines** your climax (must answer this question)

**Test**: Can you state your story's Major Dramatic Question in one sentence?
```
My story's Major Dramatic Question: _______________
When is it established? (Must be by end of Act One): _______________
How does the climax answer it? _______________
```

**Common Mistake**: Having multiple equally-important questions. You can have subquestions, but ONE must be primary.

**Application**:
- **Planning**: Define this before outlining
- **Discovery Writing**: Identify it in revision, ensure it's clear by Act One
- **Revision**: Check that every major scene relates to this question
- **Climax**: Must provide satisfying answer

#### Character and Conflict Promises
Opening chapters must introduce:
- **Who** the protagonist is
- **What** they want (their desire)
- **Why** they can't easily achieve it (obstacles)

**Check**: Can readers articulate these three elements after the first chapter?

#### Structural Promises
- Genre conventions shape expectations
- Epic fantasy: "Young person looking up wondering what could come next"
- Mystery: Dead body, detective introduction
- Romance: Meet-cute or initial conflict between romantic leads

**Check**: Does your opening fit or intentionally subvert genre expectations?

### 2. PROGRESS TRACKING

Progress ≠ chronological advancement. It's creating meaningful forward momentum.

#### Types of Progress

**Information-Based Progress** (Mystery/Thriller)
Track:
- Clues discovered
- Suspects eliminated
- Mysteries gradually unraveling

**Relationship Progress** (Romance/Drama)
Show:
- Initial conflict/misunderstanding
- Gradual understanding
- Connection moments alternating with setbacks
- Climactic relationship shifts

**Character Internal Progress** (Literary/Character-driven)
Demonstrate:
- Beliefs being questioned
- Personal challenges being overcome
- Skills/understanding developing

**Plot/Action Progress** (Adventure/Epic Fantasy)
- Quests advancing
- Geography traversed
- Obstacles overcome
- Alliances formed

#### Creating Escalation: Yes, But / No, And

**Sanderson's fundamental escalation technique** ensures each scene raises stakes while moving story forward.

**YES, BUT** Pattern:
- Character achieves their goal (YES)
- BUT complications arise that create new problems
- Example: Hero steals the artifact (YES) BUT triggers alarm system (complication)

**NO, AND** Pattern:
- Character fails to achieve their goal (NO)
- AND circumstances worsen beyond the initial failure
- Example: Guards catch hero (NO) AND the artifact is cursed (worsens)

**Why This Matters**:
"Each scene should move forward while simultaneously raising stakes" - this creates the feeling of progress while maintaining tension.

**Application**:
```
Scene Goal: _______________
Outcome: YES, BUT / NO, AND (circle one)
If YES, BUT: What complication arises? _______________
If NO, AND: How do circumstances worsen? _______________
Result: Stakes are now _______________ (higher than before)
```

**Common Pattern in Act 2**:
- Early Act 2: More YES, BUT (victories with complications)
- Mid Act 2: Mix of both
- Late Act 2: More NO, AND (failures that compound)
- This creates natural escalation toward darkest moment

#### The Try-Fail Cycle

**Sanderson's structural pattern** that "nearly all discovery writers employ variations of":

**Cycle Structure**:

**1. First Attempt**: Character tries obvious solution → FAILS
- **Reveals**: Character underestimated the problem
- **Reader learns**: Antagonist is more capable than expected
- **Stakes**: Problem is harder than it seemed

**2. Second Attempt**: Character tries more sophisticated approach → FAILS
- **Reveals**: Internal flaws or deeper complications
- **Reader learns**: There are layers to this problem
- **Stakes**: Character must grow to succeed

**3. Third Attempt**: Character succeeds after growth/transformation
- **Reveals**: Character has truly changed
- **Reader learns**: This victory was earned
- **Stakes**: Payoff feels satisfying because we saw the struggle

**Example Pattern**:
```
Problem: Defeat the dragon

Attempt 1 (Obvious): Attack with sword → Dragon barely notices
Reveal: Need magic, not just strength

Attempt 2 (Sophisticated): Use ancient spell → Spell backfires
Reveal: Hero's fear blocks magic; must overcome internal flaw

Attempt 3 (Transformed): Embrace courage, channel spell properly → Success
Reveal: Internal growth enabled external victory
```

**Note**: Robert Jordan exemplified this throughout *The Wheel of Time*. Each book often follows this pattern.

**Why It Works**:
- Each failure provides new information
- Raises stakes organically
- Forces character development
- Makes final success feel earned, not lucky

**Application for Different Writer Types**:
- **Discovery Writers**: This emerges naturally; be aware and enhance it in revision
- **Outliners**: Can structure this intentionally in planning
- **Both**: Ensure failures reveal something new each time

#### Signposting Techniques

Signposts are breadcrumbs showing "you're making story progress."

**Critical insight**: "A big reason that people drop off a book is that there weren't enough signposts of progress."

**Narrative Signpost Types**:
- Revealing critical information
- Introducing new obstacles
- Showing character decisions
- Shifting story direction
- Small victories that accumulate

**Chapter-Level Application**:
Treat opening paragraphs as "stealth thesis paragraphs"—subtly guide reader expectations while maintaining intrigue.

**Relationship-Driven Signposting**:
- Emotional progression matters more than action
- Characters deepening understanding
- Subtle interaction shifts
- Small victories revealing development

#### Avoiding Stagnation

Prevent narrative wheel-spinning through:
- **Pacing variation**: Speed up and slow down intentionally
- **Character reflection moments**: Slowing strategically deepens impact
- **Each section must advance**: Plot, character understanding, OR underlying conflict

### 3. PAYOFF DELIVERY

Payoffs deliver "a surprising but fulfilling answer to your promise."

#### Payoff Anatomy

**The Balance**: Simultaneously unexpected AND inevitable
- Satisfies core promises
- Subverts specific predictions
- Feels earned, not cheap

#### Understanding Twists

**Twist Mechanics** (like reframing gifts):
1. Initially promise one outcome
2. Gradually redirect desires toward something different
3. Deliver the new outcome satisfyingly

**Examples**:
- *While You Were Sleeping*: Promise one romantic partner, redirect to another
- *Into the Woods*: "Be careful what you wish for" - initial wishes granted, then consequences explored

**Twist Requirements**:
- Clear initial promise
- Gradual expectation redirection
- Resolution feeling both surprising AND true to story essence

**Goal**: Not reader deception, but delightful surprise aligned with narrative integrity

#### Payoff Approaches

**The Straightforward Payoff** (*Pride and Prejudice*)
- Promise: Characters will marry
- Sophistication: Complications enhance rather than undermine resolution
- Result: Both expected AND deeply satisfying

**The Complex Mechanism** (*Lord of the Rings*)
- Maintain belief in ultimate resolution
- Create genuine doubt (spider attack, mounting darkness)
- Deliver surprise satisfaction when payoff arrives

#### The Gandalf Promise Technique

**Setup**:
1. Character makes specific promise ("morning of the fifth day")
2. Escalate circumstances so darkly the promise is overshadowed
3. Immediate conflict becomes so consuming readers forget the promise
4. Deliver on promise precisely when hope seems lost

**The Principle**: "Make circumstances so dark that when the sun comes out, you're cheering."

**Power**: When hope arrives, readers experience catharsis beyond typical plot resolution because they'd forgotten it was coming.

#### Creating Effective Payoffs

**Create Genuine Doubt Through**:
- Real obstacles
- Apparently impossible promises
- Red herrings and escalations
- Stakes that feel genuinely high

**Provide Plausible Deniability**:
- Give readers sufficient doubt
- Ensure resolution feels earned rather than cheap
- Make the path to victory feel genuinely uncertain

**Match Payoffs to Story Essence**:
- Identify narrative soul (what's this REALLY about?)
- Make climax reflect the most important plot thread
- Emotional payoff > plot mechanics

## Application Workflow

### For Outlining (Architect Writers)

**Step 1**: Define Your Promises
```
Tone Promise: _______________________
Story Promise: _______________________
Character/Conflict Promises:
- Who: _______________________
- Want: _______________________
- Obstacle: _______________________
Structural Promise (Genre): _______________________
```

**Step 2**: Map Progress Milestones
```
Type of Progress (circle one): Information / Relationship / Internal / Plot

Act 1 Signposts:
- _______________________
- _______________________

Act 2 Signposts:
- _______________________
- _______________________
- _______________________
- _______________________

Act 3 Signposts:
- _______________________
- _______________________
```

**Step 3**: Design Payoff
```
Initial Promise: _______________________
Twist/Redirection (if any): _______________________
Final Payoff: _______________________

Will payoff be: [  ] Straightforward  [  ] Twist  [  ] Dark-then-light (Gandalf)

Check: Does this payoff satisfy the promise while surprising readers? _______
```

### For Revision (Discovery Writers)

**Pass 1: Promise Audit**
- Read first 3 chapters
- List all promises identified
- Check if promises are fulfilled by story's end
- Flag broken promises for revision

**Pass 2: Progress Check**
- Mark every scene with its signpost type
- Identify "wheel-spinning" scenes (no signposts)
- Revise or cut scenes without clear progress

**Pass 3: Payoff Evaluation**
- Does the climax answer the Major Dramatic Question?
- Is the payoff surprising?
- Is the payoff inevitable in hindsight?
- Does it feel earned?

## Implementation Examples

### Example 1: Mystery Novel

**Promises**:
- Tone: Dark, atmospheric (opening with crime scene)
- Story: Detective will solve murder
- Character: Flawed detective seeking redemption through this case
- Structural: Red herrings, clues, suspect interviews

**Progress Signposts**:
- Clue discovered (physical evidence)
- Suspect eliminated through alibi
- New connection revealed between victims
- Detective's personal flaw interferes with investigation
- Witness comes forward
- Previous assumption proven wrong

**Payoff**:
- Twist: The person we thought was protecting someone is actually the killer
- Redirection: Gradually shift sympathy toward "protector" character
- Delivery: Revelation reframes all previous evidence in shocking but logical way

### Example 2: Romance

**Promises**:
- Tone: Light, hopeful (witty banter in meet-cute)
- Story: These two will end up together
- Character A: Wants control, fears vulnerability
- Character B: Wants connection, fears abandonment
- Structural: Misunderstandings, forced proximity, grand gesture

**Progress Signposts**:
- Initial antagonism with spark
- Forced to work together
- Moment of genuine connection
- Setback due to misunderstanding
- Vulnerability revealed
- Near-miss kiss
- Dark moment of separation
- Realization of love
- Grand gesture

**Payoff**:
- Straightforward: They get together (as promised)
- Surprise: Character A makes grand gesture (they always needed control, so surrendering to love is character growth)
- Satisfaction: Both characters have grown to deserve happiness

### Example 3: Epic Fantasy

**Promises**:
- Tone: Epic, high-stakes (prologue with world-threatening event)
- Story: Chosen One will save the world from Dark Lord
- Character: Farm boy unaware of destiny
- Structural: Quest, mentor death, training montage, final battle

**Progress Signposts**:
- Call to adventure
- Mentor introduction
- Training begins
- First use of power
- Mentor death (stakes raised)
- Quest item obtained
- Betrayal by ally
- Power mastery moment
- Gathering allies
- Approaching dark fortress

**Payoff** (Using Gandalf Technique):
- Promise: Hero will face Dark Lord
- Darkness: Make final battle seem impossible, all hope lost
- Forgotten Element: Early promise of "ancient prophecy" or "hidden weakness"
- Delivery: At darkest moment, prophecy/weakness activates, enabling victory
- Reader Response: Cheering because they'd forgotten hope existed

## Common Pitfalls

### Promise Pitfalls
❌ **Too vague**: "Something interesting will happen"
✓ **Specific**: "This detective will catch the serial killer"

❌ **Promising wrong genre**: Opening like comedy, delivering tragedy
✓ **Tonal consistency**: Match opening mood to overall story

❌ **No clear protagonist desire**: Reader doesn't know what character wants
✓ **Explicit want**: Character states or demonstrates clear goal early

### Progress Pitfalls
❌ **Wheel-spinning**: Scenes that don't advance anything
✓ **Every scene matters**: Each advances plot, character, or theme

❌ **Too slow**: Taking 200 pages to make any progress
✓ **Regular signposts**: Something meaningful every 2-3 chapters

❌ **Progress without cost**: Easy victories don't engage
✓ **Yes, but / No, and**: Victories have complications; failures worsen situation

### Payoff Pitfalls
❌ **Deus ex machina**: Solving problems with unestablished element
✓ **Earned resolution**: Payoff uses only elements promised earlier

❌ **Too predictable**: Exactly what reader expected in obvious way
✓ **Inevitable surprise**: Logical in hindsight, unexpected in moment

❌ **Wrong payoff**: Delivering on minor thread, ignoring major promise
✓ **Match importance**: Biggest payoff for biggest promise

## Advanced Techniques

### Micro-Promises (Chapter Level)
Each chapter makes mini-promises:
- Chapter ends with question → Next chapter answers it
- Chapter introduces problem → Later chapter resolves it
- Chapter hints at mystery → Payoff comes later

**Application**: Treat each chapter as mini Promise-Progress-Payoff cycle

### Subverting Expectations (The Sanderson Way)
**Quote**: "Authors that do something they aren't supposed to do but make it work often garner a big following."

**His Example**: *The Way of Kings* has three prologues (traditionally discouraged)
**Why It Works**: Each serves specific narrative purpose; intentional risk with planned payoff

**Your Turn**: Identify "rules" you want to break, then justify with clear purpose and payoff plan

### Multi-Threaded Payoffs
Complex stories have multiple promise-payoff threads:
- **A-Plot**: Main story promise (defeat Dark Lord)
- **B-Plot**: Relationship subplot (romantic arc)
- **C-Plot**: Character internal (overcome fear)

**Technique**: Align all payoffs to climax in same sequence, creating compound satisfaction

### The "Forgetting" Technique (Gandalf Promise)
1. Plant promise early
2. Create mounting tension that overshadows promise
3. Reader forgets promise amid immediate crisis
4. Deliver on promise at darkest moment
5. Reader experiences unexpected relief

**Key**: Promise must be clear enough to remember when delivered, subtle enough to forget during crisis

## Checklist for Promise-Progress-Payoff

### Promise Check (First 3 Chapters)
- [  ] Tone is clear and consistent with overall story
- [  ] Major Dramatic Question is established
- [  ] Protagonist's want is clear
- [  ] Obstacles to want are established
- [  ] Genre expectations are met or deliberately subverted
- [  ] Reader knows what kind of story this is

### Progress Check (Middle Chapters)
- [  ] Every scene has at least one signpost
- [  ] Multiple types of progress (info, relationship, internal, plot)
- [  ] Pacing varies intentionally (fast action, slow reflection)
- [  ] No scenes exist that could be cut without loss
- [  ] "Yes, but" and "No, and" patterns create escalation
- [  ] Each chapter ends with reader wanting next chapter

### Payoff Check (Final Chapters)
- [  ] Climax addresses the Major Dramatic Question
- [  ] Payoff uses only elements established earlier
- [  ] Resolution feels both surprising and inevitable
- [  ] Emotional satisfaction matches promise
- [  ] All significant promises are addressed
- [  ] Reader closes book feeling satisfied

## Final Wisdom from Sanderson

> "The goal isn't to make your plot 'perfect,' but rather to create intentional risks where you understand progression and payoff mechanics, ultimately delivering narratives that resonate, surprise, and satisfy readers."

> "Don't eliminate every flaw seeking 'perfection.' Instead, create intentional risks where you understand progression and payoff mechanics."

> "If you can make circumstances so dark that when the sun comes out, you're cheering, you don't need a twist."
