# Worldbuilding Tools - Sanderson Framework Skill

## Overview
Based on Brandon Sanderson's 2025 Lectures #8 and #9, this skill provides comprehensive tools and frameworks for creating immersive, coherent worlds that serve your story.

## Core Philosophy

**Sanderson's Principle**: "Worldbuilding should always, always serve the story, and not overshadow it."

**The Coherence Priority**: "Some of the most immersive worlds in literature are not necessarily the most detailed, but the most cohesive" where every element serves the larger story.

**Depth Over Density**: "It's not about density of ideas. It's about how the ideas influence each other and the depth that you can play with those ideas."

## THE THREE COMPONENTS OF WORLDBUILDING

### 1. Physical Setting
Geography, climate, flora/fauna, and cosmology

### 2. Cultural Setting
Government, religion, technology, language, and social norms

### 3. Magic System
Rules, societal influence, and limitations (see separate Sanderson's Laws skill)

## CORE WORLDBUILDING PRINCIPLES

### Principle 1: The Iceberg Technique

**Concept**: Keep most world details beneath the surface.

**Visible (10%)**:
- Story-essential elements
- Details that create atmosphere
- Information characters naturally know
- World aspects affecting plot

**Hidden (90%)**:
- Writer's background knowledge
- Unexplored history
- Detailed systems not relevant to story
- Deep lore for consistency

**Application**:
```
VISIBLE IN STORY:
- _______________
- _______________
- _______________

AUTHOR KNOWS (Hidden):
- _______________
- _______________
- _______________

Purpose: Readers sense depth without being overwhelmed
```

**Example - Tolkien**:
- **Visible**: Hobbits, Shire, Ring quest, immediate geography
- **Hidden**: Thousands of years of history, languages, genealogies, creation myths
- **Result**: Feels vast and real without info dumps

### Principle 2: The Intersection Approach

**Don't Perfect Sequentially**: Develop world, characters, and plot simultaneously

**Why This Works**:
- Each element informs the others
- Characters shape world as much as world shapes characters
- Plot needs drive worldbuilding needs
- Prevents over-building unused elements

**Bad Process**:
1. Build entire world (6 months)
2. Create characters (2 months)
3. Plot story (1 month)
4. Realize world doesn't serve story
5. Rebuild

**Good Process**:
1. Basic world concept
2. Character concept that fits world
3. Plot concept that uses world and character
4. Deepen world to serve plot
5. Adjust characters based on deeper world
6. Refine plot based on character/world interaction
7. Iterate

### Principle 3: Worldbuilder's Disease Prevention

**The Disease**: "You could spend so long on this that you get paralyzed by the amount that you have to do."

**Symptoms**:
- Creating detailed maps you'll never use
- Documenting every king in 1000-year history
- Designing complete languages
- World becomes procrastination tool

**Cure**:
- Set deadlines
- Prioritize story relevance
- Build what you need when you need it
- Remember: Writing > Worldbuilding

**Permission to Build Later**:
- You don't need every detail before starting
- Build as you write
- Discovery during drafting is valid
- Revision can add consistency

## THE PYRAMID OF ABSTRACTION

### Grounding Abstract Concepts Through Sensory Details

**The Problem**: Abstract worldbuilding feels distant and unreal

**The Solution**: Ground concepts in sensory experience

**Instead of Explaining Mechanics, Describe How It Feels**:

**Abstract**: "The magic system works through channeling energy"
**Concrete**: "Magic tastes like copper, makes her teeth ache, and leaves a shimmer in the air like heat waves"

**Abstract**: "The city is technologically advanced"
**Concrete**: "Steam hisses from grates, automatons clank past carrying parcels, and electric lights buzz overhead"

**Sensory Checklist**:
```
WORLD ELEMENT: _______________

SIGHT: _______________
SOUND: _______________
SMELL: _______________
TASTE: _______________
TOUCH: _______________
EMOTIONAL FEEL: _______________
```

**Example - Describing a Forest**:
- Don't: "The forest was magical and ancient"
- Do: "Moss glowed faintly in the darkness. The air smelled of decay and growing things. Tree roots hummed beneath her feet, a vibration she felt in her bones. When she touched bark, it was warm, like living skin."

## WORLDBUILDING ORGANIZATION STRUCTURE

### Document Organization

Create systematic sections:

**1. SETTING**
- Geography
- Climate
- Flora/fauna
- Cosmology/astronomy
- Technology level

**2. CULTURE**
- Government structure
- Economic system
- Social hierarchy
- Religion/beliefs
- Language notes
- Customs/traditions
- Arts and entertainment

**3. MAGIC/SPECIAL SYSTEMS**
- Rules and limitations
- How it's accessed
- Who can use it
- Societal integration

**4. HISTORY**
- Major events affecting story
- Recent history (last generation)
- Legends/myths
- Conflicts and resolutions

**5. CHARACTERS**
- How they fit into world
- Cultural backgrounds
- Relationship to magic/technology
- Social positions

### Accumulation Method

**As You Write**:
- Keep worldbuilding document open
- Add ideas as they emerge
- Note contradictions to resolve later
- Build organically through discovery

**Example Entry**:
```
Date: _______________
Element: Currency system
Note: Mentioned "iron marks" as currency. Need to determine:
- Why iron? (Metal-based magic? Historical reason?)
- Exchange rates with other denominations
- Who mints them? (Centralized or regional?)
- How common people earn them
Status: To develop
```

## COMMON WORLDBUILDING PITFALLS

### Pitfall 1: Information Dumping

**Problem**: Stopping story to explain world details

**Why It Fails**:
- Kills pacing
- Bores readers
- Feels like textbook

**Solutions**:

**Technique 1: Dialog Reveals World**
Characters discuss world naturally through conflict/goals

**Bad**:
"As you know, Bob, our kingdom has been at war with the Northern Territories for fifteen years because of the trade dispute over spice routes."

**Good**:
"We can't trade through the Northern pass anymore."
"Not since the war started."
"Fifteen years of this. For what? Spices?"

**Technique 2: Action Reveals World**
Show world through character interaction with it

**Bad**:
"The city had steam-powered transportation and automatons that served in shops."

**Good**:
She flagged down a steam-cab, the automaton driver clicking acknowledgment. "Market district," she said, and it jerked into motion, pistons hissing.

**Technique 3: Sensory Immersion**
Let readers experience world through senses, not explanation

**Technique 4: POV Character Ignorance**
Use characters learning about world as vehicle for reader learning

**Example**: Harry Potter learns about wizarding world while readers do

### Pitfall 2: Inconsistency

**Problem**: World works differently in different scenes

**Why It Fails**: Breaks immersion, feels amateurish

**Solutions**:
- Document your rules
- Track mentions of world elements
- Revision pass specifically for consistency
- Beta readers can catch contradictions

**Common Inconsistencies**:
- Geography (distances change)
- Technology (appears/disappears as convenient)
- Social rules (characters break them without consequence)
- Magic rules (works differently each time)
- Currency (values fluctuate randomly)
- Weather/climate (doesn't match geography)

### Pitfall 3: Earth-Clone Syndrome

**Problem**: "Medieval Europe but with magic"

**Why It's Common**:
- Familiar to readers
- Easy shorthand
- Well-documented
- Genre expectations

**Why It's Limiting**:
- Feels generic
- Misses opportunities for freshness
- Often includes inaccurate medieval assumptions

**Solutions**:

**Option 1: Research Actual Period**
- Medieval Europe was diverse and complex
- Many misconceptions in popular fantasy
- Deeper research = more interesting details

**Option 2: Base on Different Culture**
- Thousands of real-world cultures to draw from
- Arabic, African, Asian, South American settings
- Research respectfully and thoroughly

**Option 3: Hybrid Cultures**
- Blend elements from multiple sources
- Create something new from combinations
- Avoid 1:1 mapping (not "fantasy Japan")

**Option 4: Original, But Grounded**
- Create from scratch
- But: Ground in human psychology and needs
- Example: Sanderson's Roshar (crab-based ecology, highstorms)

### Pitfall 4: Technology Inconsistency

**Problem**: Technology exists for plot convenience only

**Example Issues**:
- Communication technology when needed, absent when inconvenient
- Transportation speed varies by plot needs
- Weapons technology doesn't match societal development

**Solution**: **Technological Consistency Framework**

```
TECHNOLOGY ELEMENT: _______________

EXISTS: Yes / No
IF YES:
- How common is it? _______________
- Who has access? _______________
- Cost to use? _______________
- How does it affect plot? _______________
- Why doesn't everyone use it? _______________
- What prevents abuse? _______________

STORY IMPACT:
- How does this limit characters? _______________
- How does this enable characters? _______________
- Plot holes it creates: _______________
- How to address plot holes: _______________
```

### Pitfall 5: Worldbuilding for Worldbuilding's Sake

**Problem**: Creating details that don't serve story

**Symptoms**:
- Pages of geography never mentioned again
- Detailed history that doesn't affect plot
- Cultures characters never visit
- Languages never spoken

**Solution**: **Story-Relevance Test**

For each world element, ask:
- Does this affect plot? (Yes/No)
- Does this affect character? (Yes/No)
- Does this create atmosphere? (Yes/No)
- Does this establish theme? (Yes/No)

**If all No**: Consider cutting or simplifying

**Permission**: It's okay to know things readers don't, but prioritize what matters to story

## CULTURAL WORLDBUILDING

### Religion and Belief Systems

**Purpose in Worldbuilding**:
- Shapes values and morality
- Creates conflict (between beliefs, within believers)
- Explains cosmology
- Influences government and society

**Questions to Answer**:
```
RELIGION NAME: _______________

CORE BELIEFS:
- Creation story: _______________
- Afterlife: _______________
- Deity/deities: _______________
- Purpose of existence: _______________

PRACTICES:
- Worship style: _______________
- Sacred sites: _______________
- Rituals: _______________
- Clergy structure: _______________

SOCIAL INTEGRATION:
- Relationship to government: _______________
- Social power: _______________
- Who follows it: _______________
- Conflicts with other beliefs: _______________

STORY RELEVANCE:
- How this affects protagonist: _______________
- Conflicts it creates: _______________
- Themes it explores: _______________
```

**Avoid**:
- All good vs. all evil religions (too simple)
- Religion exists only as antagonist
- No believers are good people (unrealistic)
- Single global religion with no sects (unrealistic)

**Example - Creating Depth**:
- **Simple**: "They worship the Sun God"
- **Deep**: "They worship the Sun God through three aspects: Creator, Sustainer, Destroyer. Different sects emphasize different aspects. Morning prayers honor Creator, noon prayers Sustainer, sunset prayers acknowledge Destroyer. Political conflict exists between sects over which aspect should dominate temple hierarchy."

### Government and Politics

**Power Structures**:
- Monarchy (hereditary, elected, theocratic)
- Democracy (direct, representative, limited)
- Oligarchy (merchant, military, magical)
- Anarchy (organized or chaotic)
- Tribal/clan-based
- Other systems

**Questions to Answer**:
```
GOVERNMENT TYPE: _______________

POWER SOURCE:
- Who rules: _______________
- Why they have power: _______________
- How they maintain power: _______________

STRUCTURE:
- Levels of government: _______________
- Local vs. central power: _______________
- Checks and balances: _______________

PRACTICAL MATTERS:
- How are laws enforced: _______________
- Justice system: _______________
- Taxation: _______________
- Military: _______________

STORY RELEVANCE:
- How this creates obstacles: _______________
- Political conflicts in plot: _______________
- Character relationship to power: _______________
```

**Creating Political Conflict**:
- Competing factions
- Succession crises
- Rebellion/revolution
- External threats
- Internal corruption
- Changing power dynamics

### Economics and Trade

**Often Overlooked, But Important**:
- How do people earn livelihood?
- What's valuable and why?
- Trade routes and relationships
- Economic disparities

**Questions to Answer**:
```
ECONOMIC SYSTEM: _______________

CURRENCY:
- What is it: _______________
- Who controls it: _______________
- Exchange systems: _______________

RESOURCES:
- Valuable resources: _______________
- Scarce resources: _______________
- Trade goods: _______________

CLASSES:
- Wealthy: _______________
- Middle class: _______________
- Poor: _______________
- Mobility between classes: _______________

STORY IMPACT:
- Economic motivations: _______________
- Conflicts over resources: _______________
- Character economic status: _______________
```

**Example - Magic's Economic Impact**:
- Healing magic exists → Healers highly paid
- Only nobility can use magic → Economic monopoly
- Magic requires rare components → Component trade economy
- Magic produces goods → Traditional crafts decline

### Social Hierarchy and Class

**Questions to Answer**:
```
CLASS STRUCTURE: _______________

DIVISIONS:
- Upper class: _______________
- Middle class: _______________
- Lower class: _______________
- Outcasts/untouchables: _______________

MOBILITY:
- Can people change class: _______________
- How: _______________
- Barriers: _______________

PRIVILEGES AND RESTRICTIONS:
- What upper class can do: _______________
- What's forbidden to lower classes: _______________
- Legal differences: _______________

STORY RELEVANCE:
- Protagonist's class: _______________
- Class conflicts: _______________
- Social commentary: _______________
```

### Cultural Customs and Traditions

**Small Details, Big Impact**:
- Greetings (bow, handshake, kiss?)
- Table manners
- Courting/marriage customs
- Coming-of-age rituals
- Funeral practices
- Holidays and festivals
- Taboos and superstitions

**Example Development**:
```
CUSTOM: Greeting ritual

DESCRIPTION:
- Equals: Touch foreheads, exchange names
- Superior to inferior: Nod, inferior bows
- Intimate: Foreheads + hands on shoulders

MEANING:
- Forehead touch: "I see your thoughts clearly"
- Sharing names: Trust
- Shoulder touch: "I guard you"

BREAKING CUSTOM:
- Not touching foreheads: Insult, distrust
- Refusing to bow: Challenge to authority
- Touching shoulders uninvited: Presumptuous

STORY USE:
- Character navigates unfamiliar customs
- Political insult through deliberate break
- Intimate moment when characters finally touch shoulders
```

## PHYSICAL WORLDBUILDING

### Geography and Mapping

**How Much Detail?**
- Sketch map for your reference
- Detail areas relevant to story
- Can leave edges vague
- Readers don't need exact measurements

**Considerations**:
```
GEOGRAPHICAL FEATURE: _______________

PHYSICAL:
- Size: _______________
- Climate: _______________
- Resources: _______________
- Hazards: _______________

HUMAN IMPACT:
- Settlements: _______________
- Trade routes: _______________
- Strategic importance: _______________
- Cultural significance: _______________

STORY ROLE:
- How it affects plot: _______________
- Obstacles it creates: _______________
- Opportunities it provides: _______________
```

**Climate and Geography Consistency**:
- Desert doesn't neighbor frozen tundra (without mountains/magic explanation)
- Rivers flow downhill to sea/lake
- Trade develops along waterways
- Cities exist where resources are
- Climate affects culture (clothing, architecture, food, customs)

### Flora and Fauna

**When to Detail**:
- Creatures are plot-relevant (hunted, ridden, eaten, threatening)
- Plants have uses (medicine, magic, food, poison)
- Ecosystem is unusual and affects story

**Questions to Answer**:
```
CREATURE/PLANT: _______________

PHYSICAL:
- Appearance: _______________
- Size: _______________
- Habitat: _______________
- Diet (if creature): _______________

BEHAVIOR:
- Dangerous or benign: _______________
- Interaction with humans: _______________
- Intelligence level: _______________

USES:
- Economic value: _______________
- Cultural significance: _______________
- Story relevance: _______________
```

**Creating Believable Ecosystems**:
- Food chains exist
- Predator/prey balance
- Seasonal variations
- Environmental adaptations

**Example - Sanderson's Roshar**:
- Highstorms define everything
- Flora/fauna adapted to survive storms
- Crab-like shells on most creatures
- Plants retract during storms
- Architecture designed around storms
- Culture and religion shaped by storms
- **Result**: One core concept (highstorms) shapes entire ecosystem coherently

## NARRATIVE TECHNIQUES FOR WORLDBUILDING

### Technique 1: The Stranger Arrives

**Method**: POV character is newcomer to setting

**Advantages**:
- Natural reason to explain world
- Character discovers with reader
- Built-in perspective shift

**Examples**:
- Harry Potter entering wizarding world
- Frodo leaving Shire
- Portal fantasy protagonists

**Implementation**:
- Character genuinely doesn't know
- Other characters explain naturally
- Reader learns through character's eyes

### Technique 2: The Knowledgeable Native

**Method**: POV character knows world; reader learns through observation

**Advantages**:
- No need for explanation
- Show through character's natural interaction
- More immersive

**Challenges**:
- Must reveal world without explaining
- Requires trusting reader to infer

**Implementation**:
- Character thinks/acts according to world's norms
- Don't explain what character would already know
- Trust sensory details to convey world

**Example**:
**Don't**: "In our world, magic comes from the sun, as everyone knows."
**Do**: She turned her face to the sun, feeling power warm her skin. Midday was always best for casting.

### Technique 3: The Cultural Clash

**Method**: Characters from different cultures interact

**Advantages**:
- Reveals both cultures through contrast
- Creates natural conflict
- Allows explanation through misunderstanding

**Implementation**:
- Characters explain their ways to each other
- Misunderstandings arise from cultural differences
- Reader learns multiple perspectives

### Technique 4: Seamless Integration

**Method**: Worldbuilding woven into action and dialogue

**Principle**: Don't stop story to worldbuild; worldbuild while story continues

**Example**:

**Info Dump**:
"The Assassins' Guild had operated in the city for three hundred years, ever since Lord Vetinari legalized the profession and introduced regulations requiring licenses, approved rates, and a strict code of conduct. This had significantly reduced random murders."

**Integrated**:
The assassin handed her an embossed card. "Guild certified, licensed by Lord Vetinari himself. You'll note our rates are competitive and the contract terms clear."
She studied the card. "You people actually have business cards?"
"Professionalism is crucial in our trade."

## REVISION TECHNIQUES

### Consistency Pass

**Read specifically for**:
- Geography (distances, terrain)
- Weather (matches climate)
- Technology (used consistently)
- Social rules (followed or broken with reason)
- Magic (rules consistent)
- Timeline (events in logical sequence)

**Track Worldbuilding Elements**:
Create spreadsheet of:
- Element name
- First mention
- Key characteristics
- Subsequent mentions
- Consistency check

### Sensory Detail Pass

**For each scene, add**:
- At least 3 senses
- Emotional feel
- Cultural elements
- Environmental details

**Check**: Does world feel real and present?

### Info Dump Hunt

**Search for**:
- Paragraphs of pure description
- "As you know" dialogue
- Stopping action for explanation

**Fix by**:
- Breaking into smaller pieces
- Moving into action
- Cutting unnecessary detail
- Showing through behavior

## WRITING ACROSS DIFFERENT BACKGROUNDS

**Sanderson's Advice on Representing Diverse Cultures**:

**Research**:
- Primary sources written by people from target communities
- Multiple perspectives (individuals differ within groups)
- Historical and contemporary sources
- Cultural consultants if possible

**Sensitivity**:
- Consider potential representational harm
- Particularly important for marginalized readers
- Avoid stereotypes
- Show individuals, not monoliths

**Authenticity**:
- Don't assume all members of group are same
- Research specific region, time period, subculture
- Details matter (food, music, family structure, values)

**Get Feedback**:
- Beta readers from represented backgrounds
- Sensitivity readers
- Listen to concerns
- Be willing to revise

## WORLDBUILDING CHECKLIST

### Essential Elements
- [  ] Physical setting defined (geography, climate)
- [  ] Cultural setting established (government, religion, customs)
- [  ] Magic/technology system (if applicable)
- [  ] History (enough to ground present)
- [  ] Language considerations (names, terms, dialect approach)

### Coherence Check
- [  ] Elements interconnect logically
- [  ] Geography affects culture
- [  ] Climate affects architecture and customs
- [  ] Magic/technology integrated into society
- [  ] Economic systems make sense
- [  ] Political structures explained

### Story Service Check
- [  ] World serves plot, doesn't overshadow it
- [  ] Worldbuilding creates obstacles for characters
- [  ] Setting affects character decisions
- [  ] World details are story-relevant
- [  ] Balance between exotic and relatable

### Consistency Check
- [  ] Geography consistent (map if needed)
- [  ] Technology used consistently
- [  ] Social rules followed or broken with reason
- [  ] Magic rules consistent
- [  ] Timeline logical
- [  ] Details don't contradict

### Revelation Check
- [  ] No large info dumps
- [  ] World revealed through action/dialogue
- [  ] Sensory details ground abstract concepts
- [  ] Reader can infer without everything explained
- [  ] Information revealed when relevant

## FINAL WISDOM FROM SANDERSON

> "Worldbuilding should always, always serve the story, and not overshadow it."

> "Some of the most immersive worlds in literature are not necessarily the most detailed, but the most cohesive."

> "It's not about density of ideas. It's about how the ideas influence each other and the depth that you can play with those ideas."

> "Focus worldbuilding efforts on areas that: directly impact characters and plot, align with your creative passion, support rather than overshadow the core narrative."

> "Don't eliminate every flaw seeking 'perfection.' Create intentional choices that serve your story."
