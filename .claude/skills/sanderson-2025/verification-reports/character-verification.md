## Recommendations for Enhancement

### PRIORITY 1: Expand "Handling Restricted Characters"

**Current**: Lines 114-117 briefly mention this.

**Recommended Addition**:
```markdown
### Handling Restricted Characters (Detailed)

When characters face severe constraints (imprisonment, injury, social restrictions):

**Mental Proactivity**:
- Show internal planning and strategizing
- Character thinks through options even if can't act yet
- Mental rehearsal of future actions

**Small Acts of Agency**:
- Find micro-decisions within constraints
- Example: Prisoner organizing cell, deciding meal order, befriending guard
- These small choices maintain sense of proactivity

**Value Attempts, Not Just Success**:
- Character tries to escape and fails? Still proactive
- Planning that doesn't work out? Shows agency
- Readers respect the attempt

**Shift Pillar Focus**:
- If proactivity is externally limited, develop capability or relatability
- Example: Imprisoned character becomes more self-aware (relatability growth)
- Or learns new skill in confinement (capability growth)

**Example - Edmond Dantès (*Count of Monte Cristo*)**:
- Imprisoned for years (limited proactivity)
- Learns from Abbé Faria (capability growth)
- Plans meticulously (mental proactivity)
- Small acts: Digging tunnel, hiding evidence
- Result: Maintains engagement despite physical restriction
```

### PRIORITY 2: Elevate "Comparative Relatability" Principle

**Current**: Buried in John Wick example (lines 230-234).

**Recommended Addition**:
```markdown
### The Comparative Relatability Principle

**Core Concept**: Relatability is relative, not absolute.

**Application for Antiheroes**:
When protagonist does morally questionable things, readers root for them IF:
1. Antagonists are MORE morally questionable
2. Protagonist's motivations are MORE understandable
3. Protagonist shows MORE humanity/vulnerability

**Sanderson Quote**: "You make sure the people they're fighting are even less relatable than they are."

**Examples**:
- John Wick kills many people (questionable) vs. Villains kill innocent dog (worse)
- Dexter kills people (bad) vs. Victims are serial killers (worse people)
- Walter White cooks meth (criminal) vs. Rivals are more ruthless (worse criminals)

**Exercise**:
```

**Current**: Lines 592-609 separate them but don't contrast explicitly.

```markdown
### Antiheroes: Two Fundamentally Different Types

| Aspect | Classical Literary | Modern Pop Culture |
|--------|-------------------|-------------------|
| **Proactivity** | Low (deliberately passive) | High or Medium |
| **Capability** | Often low | Usually very high |
| **Relatability** | Low (alienating on purpose) | Moderate (via comparative relatability) |
| **Purpose** | Critique society/heroism | Provide catharsis/wish fulfillment |
| **Reader Response** | Discomfort, reflection | Rooting for them despite flaws |
| **Examples** | Emma (*Madame Bovary*), Bartleby | John Wick, Dexter, Walter White |
| **Arc Pattern** | Often static or declining | Often capable → more capable |
| **Moral Framework** | Subverts traditional heroism | Vigilante justice, "worse villain" |

**Key Distinction**: Classical antiheroes make you think about heroism. Modern antiheroes make you cheer for flawed people.
```
