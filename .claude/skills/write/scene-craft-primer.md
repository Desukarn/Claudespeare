# Scene Craft Primer

A reference guide for constructing effective scenes and avoiding cookie-cutter storytelling.

## A. Scene Construction Fundamentals

Every scene needs three elements. If a scene lacks any of these, it's weak and should be revised or cut.

### The Three Essential Elements

**1. Goal**
- What does the POV character want in THIS scene?
- Must be specific and immediate
- Examples: "Get information from the witness," "Convince her boss to approve the plan," "Escape the building"

**2. Conflict**
- What opposes the character getting what they want?
- Can be: another character, environment, internal struggle, time pressure
- NO CONFLICT = NO SCENE (just cut it and summarize)
- Examples: "The witness refuses to talk," "Boss is skeptical and resistant," "Guards block the exit"

**3. Outcome**
- What changes by the end of the scene?
- Must advance the story (new information, relationship shift, obstacle overcome/introduced, decision made)
- Use YES-BUT or NO-AND pattern (see Sanderson Framework)
- Examples: "Gets partial info BUT witness is found dead next morning," "Boss says NO AND assigns her to different case," "Escapes BUT now they know she was there"

### Good vs Weak Scenes

**WEAK SCENE:**
```
Sarah meets Marcus at a coffee shop. They discuss the case.
Marcus explains what happened at the lab. Sarah listens
and asks questions. They agree to meet again tomorrow.
```

Why weak:
- No clear goal for Sarah (just "discussing")
- No conflict (Marcus freely provides info)
- No real outcome (story hasn't moved)
- Could be summarized: "Sarah learned about the lab incident from Marcus"

**GOOD SCENE:**
```
Sarah meets Marcus at the coffee shop to find out what
really happened at the lab. Marcus is nervous, keeps
looking at the door. He starts to explain, but when Sarah
asks about the missing samples, he shuts down. "I can't
talk about that. They're watching." He leaves abruptly.
Sarah notices a black car parked across the street.
```

Why good:
- Clear goal: Sarah wants information about missing samples
- Clear conflict: Marcus is afraid and won't tell her
- Clear outcome: YES-BUT (she learns someone is watching) AND new complication (she's being surveilled too)

## B. Information Delivery Methods

How you deliver information determines whether your story feels natural or like AI slop.

### BAD: Convenient Conversations

Characters discussing plot points conveniently, with no conflict or purpose beyond delivering information to the reader.

**Examples of what NOT to do:**

**Tavern Scene Info Dump:**
```
❌ Sarah enters the tavern and sits at the bar. Two strangers
   nearby are discussing the MacGuffin she's been searching for.

   "As you know, the Crystal of Power was hidden in the Northern
   Temple three hundred years ago," one says.

   "Yes, and only someone with royal blood can claim it," the
   other replies.

   Sarah listens carefully, learning everything she needs.
```

Why this is terrible:
- Strangers conveniently discussing exactly what protagonist needs
- "As you know" exposition (telling each other things they both already know)
- No conflict—information comes for free
- Scene exists ONLY to deliver information
- Readers can smell this a mile away

**Character Thinking Encyclopedia Entries:**
```
❌ Sarah looked at the temple. She thought about its history.
   It had been built in 1723 by the Northern Kingdom as a
   repository for magical artifacts. The builders had used
   enchanted stone, which was resistant to both physical and
   magical attacks. The temple's defenses included three layers
   of wards, each requiring a different type of magic to bypass.
```

Why this is terrible:
- Character mentally reciting Wikipedia
- No one thinks in encyclopedia format
- Stops the story cold for a lecture
- Feels like the author explaining to the reader, not character thinking

### GOOD: Information Through Conflict

Information revealed through struggle, discovery, or character action.

**Information Through Active Discovery:**
```
✅ Sarah pushed open the temple door. The stone hummed beneath
   her palm—enchanted, probably. That meant wards.

   She pulled out her detection crystal. It pulsed once. Twice.
   Three times.

   "Shit. Three layers." She'd need all three types of magic
   to get through. Good thing she'd brought help.
```

Why this works:
- Character actively discovering information on-page
- Information revealed through action (using detection crystal)
- Only includes what's relevant RIGHT NOW
- Character's reaction tells us the stakes ("Shit" = this is hard)

**Information Through Argument/Conflict:**
```
✅ "You can't just walk in there," Marcus said.

   "Why not?"

   "The wards will fry you. Three layers—physical, magical,
   and something else. Nobody knows what the third one is."

   "You're saying nobody's ever gotten through?"

   "I'm saying nobody's ever gotten through alive." He grabbed
   her arm. "Don't be stupid."

   She pulled free. "Then I'll be the first."
```

Why this works:
- Information emerges from conflict (Marcus trying to stop Sarah)
- Dialogue has subtext (concern, stubbornness)
- Shows character dynamics
- Raises tension while delivering facts
- We learn both information AND character traits

**Information Through Consequences:**
```
✅ Sarah placed her hand on the ward. Heat seared through her
   palm and she jerked back, swearing. The stone glowed red
   where she'd touched it.

   Enchanted stone. Marcus was right—this wasn't going to be easy.

   She wrapped her burned hand and tried a different approach.
```

Why this works:
- Character learns through experience
- Information costs something (burned hand)
- Shows, doesn't tell
- Creates immediate stakes

## C. Dialogue Craft

Natural dialogue has subtext, conflict, and personality. AI dialogue tends to be on-the-nose and robotic.

### Subtext Over Exposition

People rarely say exactly what they mean. They hint, deflect, lie, avoid.

**AI DIALOGUE (On-the-nose):**
```
❌ "I am angry at you because you betrayed my trust when you
   told my secret to Jennifer," Sarah said angrily.

   "I am sorry. I made a mistake. I hope you will forgive me,"
   Marcus replied apologetically.
```

Problems:
- Characters state emotions explicitly
- No subtext—everything spelled out
- Dialogue tag + adverb ("said angrily," "replied apologetically")
- Sounds like robots, not humans

**NATURAL DIALOGUE (Subtext):**
```
✅ "Jennifer knows."

   Marcus went still. "What?"

   "Don't play dumb." Sarah turned away. "I told you that in
   confidence."

   "I—look, it just came up—"

   "Get out."

   "Sarah—"

   "I said get out."
```

Why this works:
- Emotion shown through action ("went still," "turned away")
- Subtext clear without stating it
- Natural speech patterns (interruptions, fragments)
- Tension builds through what's NOT said
- No dialogue tag adverbs needed

### Conflict Creates Natural Dialogue

The best dialogue has friction. Characters want different things.

**WEAK (No Conflict):**
```
❌ "We should investigate the temple," Sarah said.

   "I agree. That's an excellent idea," Marcus replied.

   "Great. Let's go tomorrow morning."

   "Sounds perfect. I'll bring supplies."
```

Why weak:
- No tension
- Characters agree too easily
- "Excellent idea" = chatbot language
- Boring—nothing at stake

**STRONG (Conflict):**
```
✅ "We're going to the temple."

   "Like hell we are."

   Sarah slammed her coffee down. "You got a better idea?"

   "Yeah. Staying alive." Marcus leaned forward. "That place
   has killed better mages than you."

   "Then I guess I'll be the exception."

   "Or you'll be dead. Fifty-fifty odds."
```

Why strong:
- Characters want different things (Sarah wants to go, Marcus wants to stop her)
- Natural disagreement
- Personalities shine through
- Tension drives the conversation

### Examples: On-the-Nose vs Subtle

**ON-THE-NOSE:**
```
❌ "I feel very nervous about this plan because it seems
   dangerous and I worry we might fail," Sarah said nervously.
```

**SUBTLE:**
```
✅ Sarah checked her gear for the third time. "You're sure
   about the guard rotation?"

   "I'm sure."

   "And the backup plan?"

   "We've been over this—"

   "Just checking." She zipped the bag shut. Unzipped it.
   Checked again.
```

The second example SHOWS nervousness through:
- Repetitive checking
- Questioning details
- Fidgeting with the bag
- No need to label the emotion

## D. Red Flags to Avoid

Watch for these patterns that signal cookie-cutter storytelling:

### 1. Convenient Conversations

**Red flag:** NPCs discussing exactly what protagonist needs to know, exactly when they need it.

**Fix:** Make information cost something. Protagonist must:
- Earn it through conflict
- Discover it through action
- Pay a price for it
- Work for it

### 2. Info-Dump Scenes

**Red flag:** Scene exists ONLY to deliver information, with no character goal or conflict.

**Test:** Could you cut this scene and just add a one-line summary?
- "Sarah learned about the temple from Marcus" = scene was just info delivery

**Fix:**
- Give scene a goal beyond information (convince Marcus to help, prove she's capable, etc.)
- Add conflict (Marcus refuses, lies, demands payment, etc.)
- Deliver info through action, not explanation
- Spread information across multiple scenes

### 3. Setup-Heavy Openings

**Red flag:** First 3+ chapters explain world, characters, and backstory before story starts.

**Test:** What happens in Chapter 1?
- If answer is "introduces characters and world," that's setup, not story

**Fix:**
- Start with inciting incident or compelling action
- Deliver setup through conflict and discovery
- Use In Medias Res or Hook First opening
- Trust reader to pick up context from action

### 4. Passive Protagonist

**Red flag:** Things happen TO protagonist. They react but don't drive action.

**Test:** Count protagonist decisions in each scene.
- If protagonist spends most scenes listening, watching, or being told things = passive

**Fix:**
- Protagonist actively pursues goals
- Protagonist makes decisions that change the story
- Protagonist acts, doesn't just react
- Aim for 60%+ scenes where protagonist is proactive

### 5. "As You Know" Exposition

**Red flag:** Characters explaining things to each other that they both already know.

**Examples:**
- "As you know, the war started in 2045..."
- "You remember when we discovered the crystal..."
- "Of course, the ritual requires three mages..."

**Why it's bad:** No one talks like this. It's author explaining to reader while pretending characters are talking.

**Fix:**
- Character discovers new information
- Character explains to someone who doesn't know
- Character argues about interpretation of facts
- Or just cut it—does reader need this right now?

### 6. Wikipedia-Style Thinking

**Red flag:** Character thinks in encyclopedia format, reciting facts.

**Example:**
```
❌ Sarah examined the crystal. She knew that crystals like
   this were formed over thousands of years through magical
   compression. The process required both ambient magic and
   specific geological conditions. Only three regions in the
   world had the right conditions: the Northern Mountains,
   the Southern Desert, and the Eastern Archipelago.
```

**Why it's bad:** No one thinks like this. Feels like author lecture.

**Fix:**
```
✅ Sarah examined the crystal. Northern make, probably.
   She'd seen similar compression patterns in the mountains.
   But this one had... something else. A shimmer she didn't
   recognize.

   Where the hell had he found this?
```

Character thinks in their voice, notices specific details relevant NOW, and has personality.

## Quick Reference Checklist

Before writing any scene, ask:

**Goal:**
- [ ] What does POV character want in this scene?
- [ ] Is it specific and immediate?

**Conflict:**
- [ ] What opposes them?
- [ ] Is there actual friction/tension?
- [ ] Does something make this difficult?

**Outcome:**
- [ ] What changes by scene end?
- [ ] Does this advance the story?
- [ ] YES-BUT or NO-AND?

**Information Delivery:**
- [ ] Is information revealed through action/conflict?
- [ ] Or is this a convenient conversation?
- [ ] Do characters already know what they're "explaining"?
- [ ] Could I show this instead of telling it?

**Dialogue:**
- [ ] Do characters have subtext?
- [ ] Are they in conflict about something?
- [ ] Does each character sound different?
- [ ] Would real people talk this way?

If you answer "no" to any critical question (Goal/Conflict/Outcome), revise the scene or cut it.

---

**Remember:** Every scene must justify its existence by advancing the story through character goals, conflict, and meaningful outcomes. If a scene is just delivering information or setting up for later, it's weak—find a way to deliver that information through conflict and action instead.
