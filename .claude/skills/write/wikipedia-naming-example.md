# Wikipedia Random Method - Working Example

This document demonstrates the Wikipedia random article method for generating unique, non-clichéd place names.

## Process Overview

1. Fetch 10-15 random Wikipedia articles
2. Extract evocative terms (place names, historical words, unique syllables)
3. Adapt and combine into fantasy place names
4. Apply cultural suffixes for consistency
5. Verify pronounceability and memorability

---

### Step 3: Adaptation into Fantasy Place Names

#### Cities and Towns

**1. Hastinmere**
- **Source:** Hastings + mere (Old English for lake/sea)
- **Type:** Lakeside city
- **Cultural fit:** Germanic/Anglo-Saxon
- **Pronunciation:** HASS-tin-meer
- **Description:** Major port city on a vast inland lake, known for shipbuilding

**2. Byzara**
- **Source:** Byzantine (shortened and adapted)
- **Type:** Ancient capital
- **Cultural fit:** Greco-Roman/Mediterranean
- **Pronunciation:** bih-ZAR-uh
- **Description:** Former imperial capital, now ruined but still inhabited, layered with centuries of history

**3. Koralvast**
- **Source:** Coral + vast (suggesting expansive reef system)
- **Type:** Coastal city
- **Cultural fit:** Maritime/tropical
- **Pronunciation:** KOR-al-vast
- **Description:** Port city built on and around massive coral formations, center of pearl trade

**4. Mionwick**
- **Source:** Mionów + -wick (Old English for bay/harbor)
- **Type:** Harbor settlement
- **Cultural fit:** Anglo-Saxon/Germanic
- **Pronunciation:** MY-on-wick
- **Description:** Fishing village turned prosperous harbor town

**5. Prudholm**
- **Source:** Prudnik + -holm (Norse for island/small island)
- **Type:** Island fortress
- **Cultural fit:** Nordic
- **Pronunciation:** PROOD-holm
- **Description:** Fortified island city, strategically positioned in narrow strait

**6. Rifport**
- **Source:** Reef + port
- **Type:** Trading port
- **Cultural fit:** Maritime
- **Pronunciation:** RIF-port
- **Description:** Dangerous port city accessible only to skilled navigators through treacherous reef channels

**7. Hastengard**
- **Source:** Hastings + gard (Old Norse for enclosure/fortified place)
- **Type:** Fortified merchant city
- **Cultural fit:** Nordic/Germanic
- **Pronunciation:** HASS-ten-gard
- **Description:** Walled trading city at crossroads of major trade routes

**8. Opolen**
- **Source:** Opole + -en (suffix suggesting settlement)
- **Type:** Market town
- **Cultural fit:** Slavic/Germanic
- **Pronunciation:** OH-po-len
- **Description:** Agricultural market town, trading center for surrounding farmlands

**9. Gibberton**
- **Source:** Gibberula + -ton (Old English for settlement)
- **Type:** Village
- **Cultural fit:** Anglo-Saxon
- **Pronunciation:** GIB-ber-ton
- **Description:** Small village known for peculiar local dialect

**10. Godstone**
- **Source:** Godwinson + stone
- **Type:** Religious settlement
- **Cultural fit:** Anglo-Saxon
- **Pronunciation:** GOD-stone
- **Description:** Pilgrimage site built around ancient standing stone

### Step 4: Pronunciation and Memorability Testing

**Test each name:**

1. **Say it out loud 3 times** ✓
   - Hastinmere: Easy
   - Byzara: Easy
   - Koralvast: Easy
   - Prudholm: Easy
   - All names pass

2. **Can you spell it after hearing it once?** ✓
   - Most are straightforward
   - Głogwood might need simplification to Glogwood ✓

3. **Can you remember it 5 minutes later?** ✓
   - Distinctive enough to be memorable
   - Not so complex that they blur together

4. **Google check - accidentally named after real place?**
   - Hastinmere: No matches ✓
   - Byzara: No exact matches ✓
   - Koralvast: No matches ✓
   - All clear

### Step 5: Cultural Consistency Application

**If building a Germanic-inspired world:**

Take the evocative roots and apply consistent suffixes:

**Base terms from Wikipedia:**
- Hast-, Mion-, Prud-, Glog-, God-, Norm-

**Applied Germanic suffixes:**
- -heim (home): Hastheim, Mionheim
- -burg (fortress): Prudburg, Normburg
- -wald (forest): Glogwald (Glogwood simplified)
- -stadt (city): Haststadt
- -hof (court): Godhof

**Result:** Unique roots + consistent cultural pattern = authentic-feeling world

## Why This Method Works

**1. Completely Original**
- No direct copying of real places
- Fully unique fantasy names
- No trademark or copyright issues

**2. Sounds Researched**
- Real-world roots provide authentic feel
- Not obviously "made up"
- Evokes real history without copying it

**3. Avoids AI Slop**
- No "Shadow-" or "Dark-" prefixes
- No generic fantasy patterns
- Unexpected combinations (Gibberwood, Koralvast)
- Free of training data artifacts

**4. Memorable and Pronounceable**
- Based on real words people can say
- Familiar syllable patterns
- Easy to remember and spell

**5. Flexible and Scalable**
- Can generate unlimited names using method
- Works for any cultural pattern
- Adaptable to any genre (fantasy, sci-fi, historical fiction)

---

## Usage Guidelines

**When to use Wikipedia method:**
- Want truly unique place names
- Building non-European-inspired worlds
- Need many locations quickly
- Want to avoid cultural pattern research time

**When to use cultural pattern research instead:**
- Building world closely based on real culture (Nordic, Celtic, Arabic, etc.)
- Want maximum historical authenticity
- Already established cultural naming conventions
- Prefer systematic consistency over uniqueness

**Best practice: Combine both methods**
- Use cultural patterns for primary locations (establishes consistency)
- Use Wikipedia method for unique/special locations (adds flavor)
- Example: Major cities follow Germanic pattern, but ancient ruins have Wikipedia-sourced names (suggesting pre-conquest culture)
